Chapter Summary

Childbirth is common in the prehospital setting. Most often, paramedics only assist in this natural process. However, obstetric emergencies can develop suddenly and have life-threatening consequences. The paramedic must be prepared to recognize and manage these events and sometimes assist in abnormal deliveries.

⚠
Two-Patient RuleEvery obstetric patient represents two patients simultaneously — the mother and the fetus. Assessment and treatment decisions must account for both.

Trimesters at a Glance
1st Trimester

Weeks 1–12. Fertilization, implantation, organogenesis. Morning sickness. Highest risk of spontaneous abortion. Ectopic pregnancy risk greatest in this window.

2nd Trimester

Weeks 13–26. Fetal growth, uterus rises to umbilicus by week 20. BP drops due to reduced peripheral resistance. Fetal movement begins.

3rd Trimester

Weeks 27–40. Rapid fetal growth, preeclampsia risk rises, cardiac output peaks at +30%. Supine hypotension risk. Term ≥37 weeks.

02 Anatomy & Physiology
REPRODUCTIVE ANATOMY • FETAL CIRCULATION • MATERNAL CHANGES
Specialized Structures of Pregnancy
Placenta

Disklike organ of exchange. Functions: gas transfer, nutrient transport, waste excretion, hormone production, barrier formation. Not a mixing of blood — diffusion only.

Amniotic Sac & Fluid

Completely surrounds embryo. Fluid produced by placenta then fetal urine. Rupture = gross or leaking. May rupture before or during labor.

Umbilical Cord

Contains 2 arteries (deoxygenated blood TO placenta) and 1 vein (oxygenated blood FROM placenta). Remember: 1 vein = oxygenated.

03 Antepartum Complications
BLEEDING • HYPERTENSIVE DISORDERS • METABOLIC • INFECTION
Bleeding Complications
ConditionKey FeaturesPrehospital Management
Spontaneous Abortion
(<20 wks)		 Most frequent cause of vaginal bleeding in pregnancy; 10–26% of pregnancies; most before 10 wks Monitor for hypovolemia; emotional support; transport; save any passed tissue
Ectopic Pregnancy Implantation outside uterus (usually fallopian tube); 1–2% of pregnancies; #1 cause of 1st-trimester maternal death; Classic triad: abdominal pain + vaginal bleeding + amenorrhea Treat for shock. Rapid transport. May have referred shoulder pain (diaphragmatic irritation from blood)
Abruptio Placentae Premature separation of normally implanted placenta (>20 wks). Sudden painful vaginal bleeding. Risk factors: HTN, preeclampsia, smoking, trauma, cocaine Prevent shock; oxygen; IV access; rapid transport
Placenta Previa Placenta covers cervical os. Painless bright red bleeding. Risk: prior C/S, advanced maternal age, cocaine use Prevent shock; NO vaginal exam; oxygen; rapid transport
Uterine Rupture Sudden abdominal pain in active labor; early shock signs; may/may not have vaginal bleeding; delivery needed <37 min IV access; oxygen; rapid transport — life-threatening for both
⚠
Abruptio vs. Previa Memory Trick
ABruptio = ABdominal pain + bleeding (painful)
PREvia = PREtty painless bleeding (no pain; bright red)
Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy
Gestational HTN

BP ≥140/90 mmHg after 20 wks without proteinuria. Resolves postpartum. May be early sign of preeclampsia.

Preeclampsia

Gestational HTN + at least one of: proteinuria, thrombocytopenia, impaired liver/renal function, pulmonary edema, visual/cerebral disturbances. After 20 wks.

Vasospasm Endothelial injury Clotting cascade
Eclampsia

Preeclampsia + seizures. Most common after 28 wks. Can occur up to 4 weeks postpartum (rare after 48 hrs). Most often in first pregnancy.

Preeclampsia/Eclampsia Management Protocol
  1. Left lateral recumbent position
  2. High-concentration oxygen; monitor SpO2
  3. IV access — NS or LR, KVO (max 80 mL/hr)
  4. Anticipate seizures; airway and circulatory support ready
  5. Seizure occurs → Magnesium Sulfate 4g (50%) IV over 10–20 min, then 1g/hr infusion
  6. Seizure persists → Midazolam, Lorazepam, or Diazepam
  7. Monitor for respiratory depression and hypotension; rapid transport
⚠
Mag Toxicity Signs: Loss of deep tendon reflexes (first), respiratory depression, cardiac arrest. Antidote: Calcium Gluconate.
Other Antepartum Conditions
ConditionKey Points
Hyperemesis GravidarumSevere nausea/vomiting, weight loss, ketosis, hypokalemia. Begins 2–5 wks post-conception; often resolves by 20 wks. Tx: antiemetics, rehydration, electrolyte replacement.
Rh SensitizationRh-negative mother + Rh-positive fetus → antibody production damages fetal RBCs in subsequent pregnancies. Prevention: RhoGAM at 26–28 wks AND within 72 hrs of delivery if baby is Rh+.
Gestational DMInsulin deficiency or placental hormone interference. Risks: large baby, difficult delivery, fetal RDS, maternal type 2 DM risk. Manage: glucose monitoring, dietary modification, insulin if needed.
TORCH InfectionsToxoplasmosis, Other, Rubella, CMV, Herpes — cross placenta; can cause fetal death or serious complications. Prevention via immunization and hygiene.
PROM / PPROMRupture of membranes before labor. PPROM = <37 wks (highest risk <34 wks). Infection (chorioamnionitis) risk rises after 24 hrs. Fever + uterine pain = infection. Tx: antibiotics; delivery is definitive.
Pulmonary EmbolismSignificant cause of maternal death. Virchow's Triad: venous stasis + hypercoagulability + vessel injury. Classic: sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, hemoptysis.
04 Labor & Delivery
STAGES OF LABOR • DELIVERY STEPS • NEWBORN ASSESSMENT • APGAR CALCULATOR
Stages of Labor
1st Stage — Early
0–3 cm dilation
  • Contractions q5–20 min, 30–45 sec
  • Primipara: 8–20 hrs
  • Multipara: 6–8 hrs
1st Stage — Active
4–8 cm dilation
  • Contractions q4–5 min, ~60 sec
  • Intense contractions begin
  • 1–2 hrs duration
1st Stage — Transition
8–10 cm dilation
  • Contractions q2–3 min, 60–90 sec
  • Most intense phase
  • 15–30 min duration
2nd Stage
Full dilation → Birth
  • Fetal head enters birth canal
  • Crowning = imminent delivery
  • Primipara: 1–2 hrs; Multipara: ~30 min
3rd Stage
Birth → Placenta
  • Placenta delivery
  • 5–60 min regardless of parity
  • Uterus contracts, cord lengthens
Signs of Imminent Delivery
Contractions q1–2 min / 45–60 sec Urge to bear down Large bloody show Crowning Mother says delivery is imminent
Delivery Assistance Steps
  • 1
    Standard Precautions — gloves, mask, eye protection, gown
  • 2
    Gentle counterpressure to fetal head during crowning — prevent explosive delivery/perineal tearing. If membranes intact, tear sac.
  • 3
    Nuchal cord check — feel around the fetal neck. If present, gently slip cord over the head.
  • 4
    Grasp head with hands over ears — support as it rotates for shoulder presentation. Most fetuses present facedown.
  • 5
    Guide head downward to deliver anterior shoulder, then upward to release posterior shoulder. Rest of baby follows quickly.
  • 6
    Grasp and support newborn with dry towel. Place on mother's abdomen skin-to-skin.
  • 7
    Clear airway with sterile gauze. Routine suctioning only if obvious obstruction.
  • 8
    Dry and warm the newborn; cover especially the head to reduce heat loss.
  • 9
    Record sex and time of birth.
  • 10
    Delayed cord clamping — clamp after 1 minute (after drying and assessing, at least 30–60 sec). Clamp 4–6 inches from newborn in 2 places; cut between clamps.
⚠
Placenta Delivery: Do NOT wait for placenta before transport. Usually delivers within 20 minutes. Transport placenta with patient in plastic bag. Initiate fundal massage. Monitor for hemorrhage.

Postpartum Emergencies
Postpartum Hemorrhage

>500 mL blood loss after delivery. Most within first 24 hrs. Causes: uterine atony (most common), retained placenta, lacerations.

Management:

  1. Uterine fundal massage until firm
  2. Encourage breastfeeding (releases oxytocin)
  3. Oxytocin 10 units in 1,000 mL IV at 20–30 mL/min (per protocol)
  4. Fluid resuscitation; blood products per direction
  5. Consider Tranexamic Acid
Postpartum Depression

Affects ~1 in 7 mothers. Symptoms lasting ≥2 weeks; begins during pregnancy or within 4 weeks of delivery.

Signs & Symptoms:

Appetite/weight changes Decreased energy Feeling worthless/guilty Difficulty concentrating Recurrent thoughts of death
⚠
Thoughts of suicide or harm to infant require immediate intervention and resource connection.
Trauma in Pregnancy
Key Considerations
  • ▹ Priorities SAME as non-pregnant patient — exsanguinating hemorrhage first
  • ▹ 30–35% blood volume loss produces minimal BP change but reduces uterine blood flow 10–20%
  • ▹ Blood shunted from uterus to preserve maternal BP — fetus is at risk before mom shows shock signs
  • ▹ Avoid vaginal exam (may increase bleeding / trigger delivery)
  • ▹ Transport: left lateral recumbent (avoid IVC compression) unless spinal injury suspected
  • ▹ Spinal precautions: logroll board 10–15° left with towel/pillow under right side
  • ▹ Fetal heart rate deceleration = decreased cardiac output and hypoxia in fetus

Cardiac Arrest Management in Pregnancy
  • 1
    Initiate high-quality CPR immediately with minimal interruptions
  • 2
    Manual uterine displacement to the left — if it does not compromise compressions
  • 3
    Defibrillate shockable rhythms; standard drug doses apply
  • 4
    If ≥20 weeks gestation (or fundal height ≥2 fingerbreadths above umbilicus): rapid transport for perimortem C-section
  • 5
    Goal: Perimortem C/S within 5 minutes of loss of pulses — saves mother AND fetus by restoring venous return
