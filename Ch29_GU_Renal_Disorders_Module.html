Overview & Anatomy Review

The urinary system is the body's master filtration and homeostatic regulation system. Understanding the anatomy and physiology of the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra is essential to recognizing and managing genitourinary emergencies in the prehospital environment.

Anatomy of the Urinary System

🫘
Kidneys (2)
Retroperitoneal organs at T12–L3. Each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons — the functional filtration units. The kidneys filter ~180 liters of blood daily, producing ~1.5L of urine.
💡 Each kidney is roughly the size of your fist and weighs ~150 grams
🔬
Nephron
The basic functional unit. Includes: Glomerulus (filtration), Bowman's capsule, Proximal convoluted tubule, Loop of Henle, Distal convoluted tubule, Collecting duct.
💡 If you uncoiled all nephron tubules from one kidney, they would stretch ~80 km
📏
Ureters (2)
Muscular tubes (~25 cm) carrying urine from renal pelvis to bladder via peristalsis. Three natural narrowing points where kidney stones commonly lodge.
💡 Ureteral peristalsis occurs 1–5 times/minute
🫧
Urinary Bladder
Muscular reservoir (detrusor muscle + trigone). Normal capacity ~400–600 mL. Micturition reflex triggered ~200–300 mL. The urothelium provides the waterproof lining.
💡 The bladder can stretch to hold over 1 liter when necessary
🚰
Urethra
Males: ~20 cm (prostatic, membranous, spongy segments) — passes through prostate, making BPH a common obstruction site. Females: ~4 cm — shorter length explains higher UTI prevalence.
💡 The male urethra doubles as the pathway for both urine and semen
⚙️
Adrenal Glands
Sit atop each kidney. Medulla → epinephrine/norepinephrine. Cortex → aldosterone (sodium/potassium regulation), cortisol, androgens. Critical to fluid and electrolyte balance.
💡 Aldosterone acts on the distal tubule to retain sodium and excrete potassium

Key Renal Functions

🧹
Filtration & Excretion
Remove metabolic waste: urea, creatinine, uric acid, drug metabolites, excess water and electrolytes. GFR = 125 mL/min normally.
⚖️
Fluid/Electrolyte Balance
Regulate Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, PO₄³⁻. Fine-tune blood volume and osmolality via tubular reabsorption and secretion.
🧪
Acid-Base Regulation
Regenerate and excrete bicarbonate. Acidify urine by secreting H⁺ ions. Primary long-term pH regulator (slower than lungs).
🩸
Erythropoiesis
JGA cells sense hypoxia → secrete EPO (erythropoietin) → stimulate RBC production in bone marrow. Explains anemia in CKD patients.
💊
Vitamin D Activation
Convert inactive 25-OH-D₃ → active calcitriol (1,25-OH₂-D₃) → promotes GI calcium absorption. CKD → hypocalcemia, renal osteodystrophy.
🩺
Blood Pressure Control
RAAS: Renin (from JGA) → Angiotensin I → ACE → Angiotensin II → aldosterone + vasoconstriction. Long-term BP regulation via volume control.
🏭
🔑 Master Analogy

Think of the kidney as a sophisticated water treatment plant for your blood. Blood enters under pressure (like raw water input), gets filtered through ultrafine membranes (glomerulus = sediment filter), then travels through multiple treatment chambers (tubules) where precious materials are reclaimed and toxins are concentrated. The final product — urine — is the plant's "effluent." When the plant breaks down, toxins back up into the bloodstream, fluid regulation fails, and every organ in the body suffers.

Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) — The Key Metric

GFR measures how much blood the kidneys filter per minute. It is the gold standard for assessing kidney function. Normal GFR: 90–120 mL/min/1.73 m². As kidney disease progresses, GFR falls — CKD is staged by GFR values.

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

AKI — formerly called Acute Renal Failure (ARF) — is an abrupt decrease in kidney function developing over hours to days, resulting in accumulation of nitrogenous waste products and fluid/electrolyte imbalances. It is a life-threatening emergency requiring rapid recognition and intervention.

Classification: Pre-Renal, Intra-Renal, Post-Renal

🩸
Pre-Renal AKI (~65%)
Cause: Reduced blood flow TO the kidneys. The kidneys themselves are intact but underperfused.

Causes: Hypovolemia (hemorrhage, burns, vomiting/diarrhea), heart failure, septic shock, anaphylaxis, NSAIDs/ACE inhibitors

Key: Kidneys try to compensate → concentrated urine (high specific gravity, low sodium)
🔬
Intra-Renal AKI (~25%)
Cause: Direct damage to kidney parenchyma — nephrons themselves are injured.

Causes: ATN (ischemia or nephrotoxins), glomerulonephritis, contrast dye, aminoglycosides, rhabdomyolysis, severe infection

Key: "Muddy brown" granular casts in urine; damaged tubules cannot concentrate urine
🚧
Post-Renal AKI (~10%)
Cause: Obstruction DOWNSTREAM from the kidneys, causing back-pressure injury.

Causes: Kidney stones, BPH, bladder cancer, strictures, blood clots, external compression (tumor)

Key: Often reversible if obstruction relieved quickly — hydronephrosis on imaging
💧
🔑 AKI Analogy

AKI is like problems with a garden fountain:
Pre-Renal = The water supply was turned off or pressure dropped (pump failure, pipe leak) — the fountain is fine, but there's no water.
Intra-Renal = The fountain pump itself is broken (motor burned out, impeller damaged).
Post-Renal = The drain pipe is clogged — water backs up and eventually damages the pump.
Identifying WHICH type determines whether you give fluids (pre-renal), avoid nephrotoxins (intra-renal), or relieve obstruction (post-renal).

Pathophysiology of ATN (Most Common Intra-Renal AKI)

ACUTE TUBULAR NECROSIS — PATHOPHYSIOLOGY CASCADE
1
Ischemic/Nephrotoxic Insult
Prolonged hypoperfusion OR nephrotoxin (aminoglycosides, contrast, myoglobin from rhabdo) directly damages tubular epithelial cells
2
Tubular Cell Death
Proximal tubule cells most vulnerable (highest metabolic demand). ATP depletion → cell swelling → necrosis → sloughing into tubular lumen
3
Tubular Obstruction
Dead cells and casts block tubular lumens → back-pressure → reduced filtration (GFR drops precipitously)
4
Backleak & Vasoconstriction
Filtrate leaks back through damaged tubule walls. Afferent arteriole constriction further reduces GFR. Inflammatory mediators worsen perfusion.
5
Accumulation of Toxins
BUN, creatinine, K⁺, H⁺, phosphate accumulate → uremia, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis → multi-organ effects
6
Recovery Phase (if reversible)
Surviving tubular cells regenerate over 1–3 weeks. Polyuric phase: tubules recover before concentrating ability → massive urine output → watch for dehydration!

Clinical Presentation of AKI

Critical Signs & Symptoms
  • Oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hr or <400 mL/day) or anuria
  • Edema (periorbital, peripheral, pulmonary)
  • Hypertension (fluid overload) or hypotension (pre-renal)
  • Nausea, vomiting, anorexia (uremic symptoms)
  • Altered mental status, confusion (uremic encephalopathy)
  • Pericardial friction rub (uremic pericarditis)
  • Seizures (electrolyte imbalances)
  • Dyspnea (pulmonary edema from fluid overload)
Dangerous Electrolyte Consequences
  • Hyperkalemia: K⁺ >5.5 mEq/L → peaked T-waves, widened QRS, ventricular fibrillation
  • Metabolic acidosis: Impaired H⁺ excretion → Kussmaul respirations
  • Hyponatremia: Fluid retention dilutes sodium
  • Hyperphosphatemia: Impaired excretion
  • Hypocalcemia: Impaired Vit D activation → tetany
  • Uremia: BUN >100 mg/dL → encephalopathy, coagulopathy, immune suppression
⚡
CRITICAL — Hyperkalemia ECG Changes
AKI-associated hyperkalemia is immediately life-threatening. Know the progression: Peaked T-waves → PR prolongation → widened QRS → sine wave pattern → PEA/VF. Treat at K⁺ >6.0 or any ECG changes regardless of level.

💡 Hyperkalemia Treatment Analogy

Think of the hyperkalemia treatment protocol as a three-step potassium management system:
Step 1 — Calcium Gluconate = Fire extinguisher. It doesn't fix the problem but prevents the house from burning down (stabilizes the heart). Acts in minutes.
Step 2 — Insulin + D50 / Bicarbonate / Albuterol = Moving the furniture to a safer room. K⁺ is shifted INSIDE cells (not eliminated). Buys 2–6 hours.
Step 3 — Dialysis / Kayexalate = Actually removing the furniture from the house. TRUE potassium elimination. Requires hospital.

📋 Case Study — Acute Kidney Injury
A 58-year-old male with a history of diabetes and hypertension calls 911 for severe weakness, decreased urine output for 3 days, and leg swelling. He has been taking ibuprofen 800mg TID for knee pain for 2 weeks.
BP168/104 mmHg
HR88 bpm
SpO₂92% (RA)
RR22/min — deep
ECGPeaked T-waves
Urine Output~50 mL last 24hr
🔑 What type of AKI is most likely and what is the contributing medication?
Most likely intra-renal AKI precipitated by NSAID (ibuprofen) use in a high-risk patient. NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins which normally dilate afferent arterioles. In diabetics and those with HTN/baseline CKD, this prostaglandin-mediated vasodilation is critical for maintaining GFR. Blocking it causes profound afferent arteriolar constriction → ischemia → ATN. This is a common cause of iatrogenic AKI in the ED and prehospital setting.
⚠️ How do you interpret the peaked T-waves on ECG?
Hyperkalemia is present until proven otherwise. With severe oliguria/anuria and AKI, potassium cannot be excreted. K⁺ accumulates rapidly. The peaked (tall, narrow, symmetric) T-waves are the FIRST ECG sign of hyperkalemia. Immediate intervention: Calcium Gluconate 1g IV to stabilize cardiac membrane, followed by Insulin/D50 to shift K⁺ intracellularly. Call a MAYDAY alert — this patient needs the ED NOW.
🫁 Why is the RR 22 and breathing deep?
Kussmaul respiration — a physiologic response to severe metabolic acidosis. The kidneys cannot excrete H⁺ ions, causing metabolic acidosis. The respiratory center compensates by increasing depth and rate to blow off CO₂ and raise pH. This is a sign of severe, decompensated AKI.

✅ Hyperkalemia is the most immediately life-threatening complication of AKI. While all options are serious, hyperkalemia can cause VF/asystole within minutes if K⁺ is severely elevated. Continuous cardiac monitoring is mandatory for all AKI patients.

Chronic Kidney Disease & ESRD

CKD is a progressive, irreversible decline in kidney function over ≥3 months. End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) represents complete or near-complete kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplantation. Over 37 million Americans have CKD — making it extremely common in prehospital practice.

Pathophysiology of CKD Progression

CKD PROGRESSION — THE NEPHRON LOSS CASCADE
1
Initial Injury / Risk Factors
Diabetes (#1 cause — 44%), Hypertension (#2 — 28%), glomerulonephritis, PKD, recurrent UTIs, NSAIDs, contrast nephropathy. Leads to irreversible nephron loss.
2
Hyperfiltration Compensation
Remaining nephrons work harder (hyperfiltration) to compensate for lost nephrons. This INCREASES intraglomerular pressure — initially maintains GFR but accelerates glomerular scarring (glomerulosclerosis).
3
Progressive Nephron Loss
Glomerulosclerosis → tubulo-interstitial fibrosis → more nephrons lost → vicious cycle. GFR declines ~2–4 mL/min/year in uncontrolled DM or HTN.
4
Uremic Syndrome Develops
GFR <15 → uremic toxins accumulate (BUN, creatinine, β2-microglobulin, uremic acids) → multi-organ dysfunction: pericarditis, neuropathy, encephalopathy, coagulopathy.
5
ESRD — Dialysis Required
GFR <10–15 → kidney can no longer sustain life without external support. Hemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis, or renal transplantation becomes necessary for survival.
🏗️
🔑 CKD Progression Analogy

CKD is like a company progressively losing employees: You start with 2 million nephrons (1M per kidney). As some retire early (diabetes damages them), the remaining employees work overtime (hyperfiltration) to cover the workload. But overworked employees burn out faster, causing more attrition. Eventually you lose so many workers that the company can't function — no matter how hard the remaining ones work. Dialysis is like hiring a temporary staffing agency to do the essential functions while you wait for a permanent replacement (transplant).

Multi-System Effects of ESRD/Uremia

Cardiovascular (Leading Cause of Death)
  • Hypertension — RAAS overactivation, fluid overload
  • LV Hypertrophy — chronic pressure overload
  • Accelerated atherosclerosis — uremic toxins damage endothelium
  • Pericarditis/Pericardial Effusion — uremic toxin irritation of pericardium; friction rub is pathognomonic
  • Hyperkalemia-induced arrhythmias
  • Pulmonary edema — fluid overload between dialysis sessions
Hematologic & Metabolic
  • Normocytic anemia — reduced EPO; may present with severe fatigue, dyspnea, angina
  • Platelet dysfunction — uremic toxins impair platelet aggregation → bleeding tendency (uremic bleeding)
  • Renal osteodystrophy — impaired Ca/Phosphate regulation → secondary hyperparathyroidism → bone disease
  • Metabolic acidosis — impaired H⁺ excretion
  • Hypertriglyceridemia
Neurologic — Uremic Encephalopathy
  • Early: Fatigue, impaired concentration, sleep disturbance
  • Intermediate: Confusion, asterixis (flapping tremor — toxic encephalopathy sign), personality changes
  • Late: Seizures, coma, death if untreated
  • Peripheral neuropathy — "burning feet," restless leg syndrome, glove-stocking sensory loss
GI & Integumentary
  • Nausea/vomiting — uremic gastritis; key presenting complaint
  • Uremic fetor — ammonia-like breath odor (urea converted to ammonia by oral bacteria)
  • Uremic frost — white crystalline deposits on skin (rare, very high BUN)
  • Pruritus — calcium/phosphate deposits in skin, uremic toxins
  • Sallow (yellowish) skin — urochrome pigment accumulation

Dialysis & Complications

Over 550,000 Americans receive dialysis for ESRD. Paramedics frequently respond to dialysis-related emergencies — many of which are immediately life-threatening. Understanding dialysis modalities and their unique complications is essential for prehospital care.

Hemodialysis (HD) — How It Works

🔄
🔑 Hemodialysis Analogy

Hemodialysis is like an external kidney bypass machine. Blood is pumped out of the body through an access point (AV fistula or catheter), passed through a dialyzer (artificial kidney with semipermeable membranes), where waste products and excess fluid are removed by diffusion and ultrafiltration across the membrane into dialysate fluid, then the cleaned blood is returned. It's essentially a "blood car wash" — in dirty, out clean. Typically performed 3x/week for 3–4 hours each session.

Vascular Access Types

💪
AV Fistula (Gold Standard)
Surgically created connection between artery and vein (usually radial artery + cephalic vein). Takes 6–8 weeks to mature. Palpable "thrill" + audible "bruit." Longest lifespan, lowest infection risk. NEVER use for IV access or BP measurement.
🔧
AV Graft
Synthetic tube (PTFE) bridging artery and vein. Used when native vessels unsuitable. No wait time. Higher clotting/infection risk than fistula. Also has thrill and bruit. NEVER use for routine IV access.
🔌
Tunneled Central Catheter
Dual-lumen catheter in subclavian/jugular/femoral vein. Used when fistula/graft not available. Highest infection risk. Can be used for IV access in true emergencies — consult protocol/medical direction first.

Life-Threatening Dialysis Complications

1. Dialysis Disequilibrium Syndrome (DDS)

Pathophysiology: Rapid removal of urea from blood during dialysis creates an osmotic gradient — urea equilibrates more slowly in the brain than in the blood. Water shifts INTO brain cells (cellular swelling) → cerebral edema. Most common during FIRST dialysis sessions.

Presentation: Headache → nausea/vomiting → confusion → seizures → coma (in severe cases). Occurs DURING or within hours AFTER dialysis.

Treatment: Stop dialysis, supportive care, consider hypertonic saline (NaCl 3%) or mannitol to reduce cerebral edema, airway management, anticonvulsants for seizures.

🧪
💡 DDS Analogy

DDS is like deflating a balloon too quickly: The dialyzer rapidly removes urea from the blood (outside the brain), but urea trapped inside brain cells acts as an osmotic magnet, drawing water in faster than it can equilibrate. The brain swells within its rigid skull — causing the neurological symptoms. Slower, more gradual first dialysis sessions prevent this.

2. AV Fistula/Graft Complications

Thrombosis (Clotted Access)

Most common complication. Loss of thrill/bruit on auscultation. Patient may notice: no "buzzing" feeling at access site. Requires urgent thrombectomy/thrombolysis — patient will miss dialysis.

Infection

Erythema, warmth, swelling, purulence at access site. Bacteremia/sepsis risk extremely high. Staphylococcus aureus most common. Risk for endocarditis.

AV Fistula Hemorrhage ⚠️

LIFE-THREATENING! Spontaneous rupture or traumatic injury to a high-flow fistula can cause massive hemorrhage. Treatment: direct pressure. If uncontrolled: proximal BP cuff inflated above systolic as tourniquet. Maintain pressure throughout transport.

Steal Syndrome

High-flow fistula "steals" blood from distal extremity → hand ischemia → pain, pallor, paresthesia, pulselessness distal to access site.

3. Post-Dialysis Hypotension

Most common intradialytic complication (20–30% of sessions). Rapid fluid removal (ultrafiltration) depletes intravascular volume faster than refilling from interstitial space. Vasodilatory response, eating before dialysis, antihypertensives, anemia, and autonomic neuropathy all contribute.

Presentation: Dizziness, nausea, muscle cramps, syncope during/after dialysis. BP drops 20+ mmHg.

Treatment: Trendelenburg position, IV NS bolus (250–500 mL), reduce/stop ultrafiltration rate. Vasopressors rarely needed prehospital but have midodrine (oral alpha agonist) prescribed by some centers.

4. Air Embolism

Rare but catastrophic. Air enters dialysis circuit through loose connections, empty IV bags, or accidental line disconnection → air bolus to right heart/pulmonary vasculature → obstructive shock, respiratory failure, or cerebral air embolism.

Presentation: Sudden onset dyspnea, chest pain, cyanosis, "mill wheel" murmur (churning sound over precordium), neurological deficits, cardiovascular collapse.

Treatment: Immediately clamp dialysis line, Left lateral decubitus + Trendelenburg position (air floats to right ventricle apex, away from RVOT), 100% O₂, CPR if needed. Aspiration of air via central line in hospital.

⚠️
Air Embolism Position
Left lateral decubitus (Durant's maneuver) + Trendelenburg = air rises to right ventricular apex (away from pulmonary outflow tract), preventing complete outflow obstruction. This is the ONLY specific positioning intervention in resuscitation with a defined anatomical rationale.

Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)

Uses the peritoneal membrane as a natural dialyzer. Dialysate is instilled into the peritoneal cavity through a permanent catheter, dwells for a set time (solutes diffuse across peritoneum), then drained. Can be performed at home continuously (CAPD) or nocturnally (CCPD).

🦠
Peritonitis — Most Feared PD Complication
Bacterial contamination of peritoneal cavity. Presentation: cloudy PD effluent (key sign!), abdominal pain/tenderness, fever, nausea. Can rapidly progress to septic shock. Pathogens: S. epidermidis, S. aureus, gram-negatives. Treatment: IV antibiotics, urgent hospital transport. Peritonitis is the leading cause of PD technique failure and patient mortality.
🧠

UTI & Pyelonephritis

Urinary tract infections range from uncomplicated cystitis (lower UTI) to life-threatening urosepsis. Pyelonephritis (upper UTI) involves kidney infection and represents a potential surgical and systemic emergency requiring aggressive prehospital assessment.

Anatomy-Based Classification

💧
Cystitis (Lower UTI)
Infection limited to bladder. Most common UTI type. Presentation: Dysuria, urgency, frequency, suprapubic pain, cloudy/malodorous urine, hematuria. NO fever typically. Mostly E. coli (80%).
🔥
Pyelonephritis (Upper UTI)
Infection of renal parenchyma and pelvis. Classic triad: Fever (often >38.5°C/101.3°F), flank pain/CVA tenderness, nausea/vomiting. May have cystitis symptoms too. Can progress to sepsis.
⚡
Urosepsis
UTI causing systemic inflammatory response. SIRS criteria + UTI source. Signs: high fever or hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnea, altered mental status, hypotension. Septic shock: mortality >25%. Aggressive resuscitation required.

Pathophysiology — Ascending Infection

UTI → PYELONEPHRITIS → UROSEPSIS PROGRESSION
1
Bacterial Entry & Colonization
E. coli (with P-fimbriae virulence factor) or other uropathogens ascend from periurethral area → urethra → bladder. Women: shorter urethra → higher risk. Sexual activity, urinary catheters, urinary stasis all increase risk.
2
Bladder Invasion (Cystitis)
Bacteria adhere to bladder urothelium via fimbriae, invade epithelial cells. Inflammatory response → dysuria, frequency, urgency, hematuria. Pyuria (WBCs in urine) is the hallmark.
3
Ascending to Kidney (Pyelonephritis)
Bacteria ascend via ureters (vesicoureteral reflux facilitates this) → reach renal pelvis → invade renal cortex. Inflammatory cytokines cause fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness.
4
Bacteremia & SIRS
Bacterial LPS and inflammatory mediators enter bloodstream → systemic inflammatory response → fever, tachycardia, vasodilation, capillary leak → sepsis.
5
Septic Shock
Distributive shock: massive vasodilation + capillary leak → relative hypovolemia + poor tissue perfusion → multi-organ dysfunction → death if not treated promptly.

Special Populations — Atypical Presentations

Elderly Patients — CRITICAL!
  • May present with ONLY altered mental status — new confusion in elderly = UTI until proven otherwise
  • Minimal or absent dysuria/frequency complaints despite serious infection
  • Afebrile despite significant infection (blunted immune response)
  • Urinary incontinence as chief complaint
  • Falls due to AMS/weakness → UTI the underlying cause
  • High risk for bacteremia/urosepsis with minimal prodrome
Males with UTI — Red Flags
  • UTI in males under 50 = anatomically unusual → further workup required
  • Suspect prostatitis: perineal pain, fever, obstructive voiding symptoms
  • Suspect sexually transmitted infection (gonorrhea, chlamydia)
  • BPH causing urinary stasis → reservoir for bacteria
  • Uncircumcised men: subpreputial colonization increased risk
  • Any male with UTI + retention = emergency

CVA (Costovertebral Angle) Tenderness Assessment

Technique: Murphy's Kidney Punch Test

Position patient seated or in lateral recumbent position. Place your non-dominant hand flat over the costovertebral angle (intersection of the 12th rib and paravertebral muscles, just below the 12th rib at the flank). Strike your hand with the ulnar aspect of your dominant hand with a firm (not forceful) blow. Positive test = pain with percussion = pyelonephritis highly likely. This test differentiates musculoskeletal back pain (negative) from pyelonephritis (positive) and is reproducible on each side.

⚠️ Important: BILATERAL CVA tenderness with fever and hemodynamic compromise → think bilateral pyelonephritis, perinephric abscess, or septic emboli.

Kidney Stones (Nephrolithiasis)

Nephrolithiasis affects ~11% of men and 6% of women in the United States, with a lifetime recurrence rate of 50%. The pain of renal colic is famously described as one of the most severe pains a person can experience — paramedics must recognize and aggressively manage it.

Stone Types & Formation

Calcium Oxalate (75–80% of stones)

Most common stone type. Formed when calcium combines with oxalate in urine. Risk factors: low fluid intake, high oxalate foods (spinach, nuts, chocolate), hyperparathyroidism, hypercalciuria, vitamin D excess. Radiopaque on plain X-ray.

Uric Acid Stones (5–10%)

Associated with gout, high purine diet (red meat, shellfish), metabolic syndrome, acidic urine. Radiolucent on X-ray (not seen without CT). Uniquely DISSOLVABLE with urine alkalinization (potassium citrate, sodium bicarbonate).

Struvite Stones (10–15%)

Also called "infection stones." Formed from ammonium magnesium phosphate. Caused by urease-producing bacteria (Proteus mirabilis, Klebsiella) that alkalinize urine. Can form rapidly into massive "staghorn calculi" filling the entire renal pelvis. Associated with recurrent UTIs, especially in women.

Cystine Stones (1–3%)

Caused by rare genetic disorder (cystinuria) — defective renal transport of cystine → precipitation. Appear in childhood/young adults. Poorly responsive to conservative treatment. Require specialized management.

Pathophysiology of Renal Colic Pain

⚡
🔑 Renal Colic Analogy

Imagine a garden hose with a marble stuck inside it: Water pressure (urine) builds up behind the blockage. The hose (ureter) tries desperately to push the marble forward with waves of muscular contractions (peristalsis). Each contraction creates intense spasmodic pain (colic). Unlike appendicitis (constant pain), colic comes in waves — 20–60 second peaks separated by partial relief. Additionally, the buildup of pressure above the stone stretches the renal capsule (which has pain fibers) and triggers prostaglandin-mediated inflammation — worsening both pain AND ureteral spasm. This explains why NSAIDs and opioids are the two-pronged attack for stone pain.

The Three Points of Natural Ureteral Narrowing

Stones typically lodge at three anatomical narrowing points where passage is most difficult. The location of pain gives a clue to where the stone is.

1️⃣
Ureteropelvic Junction (UPJ)
Most common location for stones >1 cm. Pain radiates to flank/back. Most stones >6mm at UPJ require intervention (lithotripsy or ureteroscopy).
2️⃣
Crossing Iliac Vessels
Mid-ureter compressed by iliac vessels. Pain radiates to lower abdomen, anterior hip, medial thigh. Stone usually needs intervention if stuck here.
3️⃣
Ureterovesical Junction (UVJ)
Most stones <5mm pass spontaneously from here. Pain radiates to groin, labia/scrotum, inner thigh. Dysuria and urinary urgency mimic UTI symptoms (distal stone compresses bladder trigone).

Clinical Presentation & Prehospital Assessment

Classic Presentation
  • Sudden onset severe flank pain — 10/10, colicky (waves)
  • Radiation pattern — flank → lower abdomen → groin → scrotum/labia → inner thigh
  • Hematuria — present in 85% (may be microscopic)
  • Restlessness — cannot find comfortable position (distinguishes from peritoneal irritation where patients lie still)
  • Nausea/vomiting (reflex from ureteral spasm)
  • Urinary frequency/dysuria (distal stones)
  • Diaphoresis from pain severity
⚠️ Dangerous Mimics — Rule Out First!
  • Aortic Aneurysm (AAA/Dissection) — most dangerous mimic! Severe flank pain + hypotension = AAA until proven otherwise. Never assume "just a kidney stone" without considering vascular emergency.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy — women of childbearing age with lower quadrant/flank pain + vaginal bleeding
  • Ovarian Torsion — acute onset unilateral pelvic pain in women
  • Appendicitis — RLQ pain, but constant not colicky
  • Infected Stone with Obstruction — fever + renal colic = urologic emergency (obstructed pyelonephritis → sepsis)
🚨
CRITICAL: Infected Obstructed Stone = Surgical Emergency
Fever + flank pain + history of stones = obstructed pyelonephritis until proven otherwise. The obstruction traps bacteria above the stone → rapidly progresses to renal abscess, bacteremia, and septic shock. This requires emergent urology consultation and decompression (stenting or nephrostomy tube). This combination has mortality rates up to 30%. Treat as sepsis + high-priority transport.

Urinary Retention & BPH

Acute urinary retention (AUR) is the sudden, painful inability to urinate despite a full bladder. It is one of the most common urological emergencies, predominantly affecting men over 60 years of age due to benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH).

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) — Pathophysiology

🍩
🔑 BPH Analogy

The prostate is a donut, and the urethra runs through the hole. As men age, DHT (dihydrotestosterone) drives prostate cell proliferation. The gland grows — but not outward — it grows inward, compressing the urethral "hole" of the donut. The bladder now has to squeeze harder to force urine through the narrowed channel. Over time, the bladder muscle hypertrophies (trying harder). Eventually, the muscle fatigues and decompensates → acute urinary retention. The bladder is full, can't empty, and the patient is in agony.

BPH → ACUTE URINARY RETENTION PROGRESSION
1
Prostate Growth (DHT-driven)
After age 40, DHT accumulation promotes both glandular and stromal hyperplasia of the prostate transition zone (surrounds urethra). Gland enlarges from ~20g to sometimes >100g.
2
Bladder Outlet Obstruction (BOO)
Enlarged prostate + increased alpha-1 adrenergic tone → compress prostatic urethra → increased voiding resistance → LUTS: weak stream, hesitancy, frequency, nocturia, incomplete emptying.
3
Detrusor Compensation → Decompensation
Detrusor muscle hypertrophies to overcome resistance (compensation) → trabeculation, diverticula. Eventually muscle fatigues and loses contractility (decompensation) → large post-void residual → retention.
4
Acute Urinary Retention Trigger
Precipitants: constipation, cold weather (sympathetic tone ↑), alcohol, anticholinergic medications, alpha agonists (decongestants like pseudoephedrine), UTI, anesthesia → bladder completely unable to empty.
5
Complications if Untreated
Bladder rupture (rare), hydroureter/hydronephrosis → obstructive AKI, bladder stone formation, UTI/urosepsis. Post-obstructive diuresis after catheterization can cause dangerous fluid/electrolyte shifts.

Clinical Presentation & Assessment

Acute Urinary Retention Presentation
  • Severe suprapubic/lower abdominal pain — usually 8–10/10
  • Urge to void but inability to urinate — pathognomonic
  • Distended, palpable, tender suprapubic mass (full bladder)
  • Restlessness and anxiety from pain/urge
  • May have mild tachycardia from pain
  • History: older male, BPH diagnosis, anticholinergic meds, alcohol, constipation
  • Ask: "When did you last urinate?" — may be 12–24+ hours
Chronic Urinary Retention (Silent)
  • Large residual urine (>300 mL) without acute pain
  • Bladder is overstretched and insensate
  • Overflow incontinence — small dribbling despite large bladder
  • Signs of obstructive uropathy: bilateral hydronephrosis, elevated creatinine
  • Risk of post-obstructive diuresis after catheterization (electrolyte loss)
  • Found incidentally or after a fall/AMS workup reveals elevated creatinine

Definitive treatment is urethral catheterization (16–18 Fr Foley catheter) — typically a hospital intervention. Other Causes of Urinary Retention

Non-BPH Causes to Consider

Neurogenic Bladder

Disruption of normal bladder innervation. Causes: spinal cord injury (T6+ complete lesions → autonomic dysreflexia + retention), multiple sclerosis (demyelination of sacral pathways), diabetes mellitus (autonomic neuropathy), Parkinson's disease.

Urethral Stricture

Narrowing of urethra from prior infection (gonorrhea), instrumentation, trauma, or radiation. Common in men. Gradual onset poor stream → acute retention.

Clot Retention

Blood clots from bladder cancer, trauma, or vigorous anticoagulation obstruct bladder outlet. Requires 3-way catheter irrigation. Ask about hematuria history.

Autonomic Dysreflexia (SCI)

EMERGENCY! In T6+ SCI patients: bladder distension triggers massive sympathetic discharge → severe hypertension (BP may reach 200–300 mmHg), bradycardia, pounding headache, diaphoresis above lesion. Immediate catheterization/bladder drainage is the treatment.

Testicular Emergencies

Testicular emergencies are time-sensitive surgical conditions. Failure to diagnose testicular torsion within the critical time window results in irreversible testicular loss. These conditions require a high index of suspicion and urgent transport.

Testicular Torsion — Surgical Emergency

⏰
TIME CRITICAL: The "Golden 6 Hours"
Testicular salvage rates by time to detorsion: <6 hours = 90–100% salvage | 6–12 hours = 50% | 12–24 hours = <10% | >24 hours = nearly 0%. Every minute counts. Do NOT delay transport for diagnostic uncertainty. Treat as torsion until proven otherwise.
TESTICULAR TORSION PATHOPHYSIOLOGY
1
Bell Clapper Deformity (Predisposing Anatomy)
In 12% of males, the tunica vaginalis inserts abnormally high → testis "hangs" freely like a bell clapper, able to rotate freely within the scrotal sac. Bilateral in 40% — high recurrence risk in the contralateral testis.
2
Twisting of the Spermatic Cord
Spontaneous rotation (often during sleep, exercise, or cold exposure) twists the spermatic cord. Usually 270–720° rotation. Sudden onset SEVERE unilateral testicular pain is the result.
3
Vascular Occlusion
Venous outflow obstructed first (low pressure) → venous congestion, edema → arterial inflow compromised → ischemia. Testicular ischemia begins within 4–6 hours.
4
Infarction and Necrosis
Without detorsion: testicular infarction → necrosis → testis becomes non-viable. Necrotic tissue triggers immune response → anti-sperm antibodies may affect the contralateral testis.
Torsion — Clinical Features
  • Sudden onset SEVERE unilateral scrotal pain — often wakes from sleep
  • High-riding testis — cord shortens as it twists, pulling testis superiorly
  • Horizontal testicular lie — testis lies horizontal rather than vertical
  • Absent cremasteric reflex — most sensitive sign (92%)
  • Scrotal swelling and erythema (may be absent early)
  • Nausea/vomiting (common — testicular sympathetics)
  • NO relief with scrotal elevation (Prehn's sign NEGATIVE)
  • Peak incidence: 12–18 years (newborns are second peak)
Epididymitis — Differentiating Features
  • Gradual onset pain (hours to days, not sudden)
  • Usually in sexually active males or older men with UTI/BPH
  • Fever and dysuria common (infectious etiology)
  • Cremasteric reflex PRESENT
  • Prehn's sign POSITIVE — pain relieved by scrotal elevation (testis still has blood supply)
  • Normal testicular position (not high-riding)
  • Pyuria on urinalysis
  • Pathogens: STI (chlamydia/gonorrhea in <35), E. coli/Pseudomonas (in >35)
🌀
🔑 Torsion vs. Epididymitis Analogy

Torsion is like strangling the blood supply with a tourniquet — it happens suddenly and completely. There's nothing gradual about it; one moment the blood flows, the next it doesn't. This is why the pain is SUDDEN, SEVERE, and doesn't improve with position changes.

Epididymitis is like a slowly worsening muscle injury — inflammatory, gradual, and directional (the pain source is posterior/inferior where the epididymis sits). Blood still flows to the testis, which is why Prehn's sign is positive (less tension on the vascular supply when lifted).

Manual Detorsion (Rare Prehospital Intervention)

Manual Detorsion — "Opening a Book" Technique

May be attempted if surgical care significantly delayed and operator is trained. The testis usually twists INWARD (medial rotation). Manual detorsion: rotate testis OUTWARD (laterally) — like opening a book — in 180° increments.

Success indicators: Sudden relief of pain, testis descends to normal position, return of Doppler flow (hospital). Success rate 70% when performed correctly. Even if partially successful, expedite to OR — surgical orchiopexy still required.

⚠️ Caution: If pain acutely worsens with rotation, you may be rotating in the wrong direction — rotate the opposite direction. Administer adequate analgesia/anxiolysis before attempt.

Other Scrotal Emergencies

Torsion of the Testicular Appendage

Torsion of a small embryological remnant (appendix testis) attached to the upper pole of testis. Most common cause of acute scrotal pain in boys 7–12 years. Less severe than testicular torsion. "Blue dot sign" — small blue/black dot visible through thin scrotal skin at upper pole (ischemic appendage). Treatment: conservative (NSAIDs, scrotal support). Cremasteric reflex present.

Fournier's Gangrene

Life-threatening necrotizing fasciitis of perineum/scrotum. Polymicrobial (aerobic + anaerobic synergy) infection. Risk: diabetes, immunocompromise, obesity, alcoholism. Presentation: Rapid progression of scrotal/perineal pain, swelling, erythema → skin necrosis, crepitus (gas in tissue), septic shock. Treatment: emergent surgical debridement + broad-spectrum antibiotics. Mortality 20–40%. High-flow O₂, IV access, fluids, antibiotics, immediate transport.

Male GU Emergencies

Several male-specific genitourinary conditions require rapid prehospital recognition and management. These include priapism, phimosis, paraphimosis, and Peyronie's disease complications. While embarrassing for patients to discuss, these are legitimate emergencies requiring professional, nonjudgmental assessment.

Priapism — Prolonged, Painful Erection

Definition & Types

Priapism is a persistent penile erection lasting >4 hours, unrelated to sexual arousal, and failing to resolve after orgasm. Left untreated, it leads to permanent erectile dysfunction within 24–36 hours due to ischemic damage.

Ischemic (Low-Flow) Priapism — EMERGENCY!

Most common (95%). Failure of blood to drain from the corpora cavernosa → compartment syndrome → ischemia → fibrosis. Painful, fully rigid. Corpora is hypoxic, acidotic. Causes: Sickle cell disease (#1 cause in children), medications (PDE5 inhibitors like sildenafil, trazodone, antipsychotics, cocaine), spinal cord injury, leukemia.

Non-Ischemic (High-Flow) Priapism

Unregulated arterial inflow (usually post-traumatic fistula). Painless, partially rigid. Corpora is well-oxygenated. Not a true emergency but requires urologic evaluation. Treated differently (arterial embolization vs. aspiration for ischemic).

🔒
🔑 Ischemic Priapism Analogy

Ischemic priapism is a penile compartment syndrome. Just as muscle tissue in a tight forearm compartment becomes ischemic from venous outflow obstruction, the corpora cavernosa become ischemic when venous drainage is blocked. Blood stagnates, oxygen depletes, CO₂ and lactic acid accumulate, pH drops. Without surgical decompression (aspiration/shunt), the smooth muscle of the corpora dies and is replaced by fibrotic tissue — permanently preventing erection. Time-critical: treat within 4–6 hours of onset.

Prehospital Management of Priapism
  • Professional, non-judgmental approach — these patients are often embarrassed and in significant pain
  • Document time of onset (critical for prognosis)
  • Ask about medications: sildenafil, trazodone, antipsychotics, illicit drugs
  • Sickle cell disease patients: IV hydration, oxygen — reduce sickling-related stasis
  • Ice packs NOT recommended (vasoconstriction worsens ischemia in low-flow)
  • Pain management: IV opioids (morphine, fentanyl)
  • Rapid transport — definitive treatment is aspiration/irrigation ± intracavernosal injection of phenylephrine (sympathomimetic → vasoconstriction → detumescence)
  • If sickle cell patient: oxygen + hydration may resolve stuttering priapism

Paraphimosis — True Urological Emergency

⚠️
Paraphimosis vs. Phimosis — Critical Distinction
Phimosis: Foreskin CANNOT be retracted forward (foreskin stuck OVER glans) — not usually an emergency unless causing urinary obstruction. Paraphimosis: Retracted foreskin becomes TRAPPED BEHIND the glans — creates a tourniquet effect → venous congestion → arterial compromise → glans ischemia. TRUE EMERGENCY — can lead to gangrene of the glans within hours.
PARAPHIMOSIS PATHOPHYSIOLOGY
1
Foreskin Trapped Behind Glans
Retracted prepuce forms a tight band (paraphimotic ring) posterior to the coronal sulcus. Usually occurs after catheterization, urologic procedures, or sexual activity when foreskin not returned forward.
2
Venous/Lymphatic Obstruction
Tight band obstructs venous and lymphatic drainage → progressive edema of the glans and prepuce → glans swells → the band becomes even tighter (positive feedback loop).
3
Arterial Compromise
As edema worsens, arterial inflow is eventually compromised → ischemia of the glans penis.
4
Necrosis (if untreated)
Hours to days without treatment → gangrene of the glans penis. Requires surgical resection. Prevention of reaching this stage is the goal.
Prehospital Reduction Attempt

Manual Reduction: Compress the glans with both thumbs while pushing the foreskin forward with the index fingers (for several minutes). This may reduce edema enough to slide the foreskin forward. Significant lubrication is essential. May require topical anesthetic gel (lidocaine) or parenteral pain management first.

Granulated Sugar Technique (if available): Apply generous amount of granulated sugar to the glans and wrap with gauze. Osmotic effect draws fluid from edematous tissue over 15–30 minutes → reduces edema → manual reduction attempted.

If reduction fails: emergent transport — urologist will perform dorsal slit or circumcision. Document time of onset.

Penile Fracture

Rupture of the Tunica Albuginea

Mechanism: Traumatic bending of an erect penis (usually during sexual activity) → audible "pop" or "crack" → immediate rapid detumescence → penile swelling, ecchymosis, deviation to opposite side of tear ("eggplant deformity").

Pathophysiology: Tear in the tunica albuginea (fibrous sheath surrounding corpora cavernosa) → blood extravasates into surrounding tissue → massive hematoma. 30% also injure the urethra (concurrent urethral injury) → blood at urethral meatus, inability to void.

Treatment: Supportive (ice, splinting, pain management), immediate surgical consultation. NEVER attempt to straighten or manually reduce a penile fracture. This is a true surgical emergency — better outcomes with early repair (<24 hours).

Rhabdomyolysis

Rhabdomyolysis is the rapid breakdown of damaged skeletal muscle cells, releasing their intracellular contents — including myoglobin — into the bloodstream. Myoglobin is directly nephrotoxic, causing acute kidney injury in up to 50% of severe cases.

Causes of Rhabdomyolysis — "The 5 T's + Crush"

🏋️
Traumatic/Crush Injury
Building collapse, entrapment, motor vehicle crashes. The classic mechanism — prolonged pressure on muscle groups releases massive myoglobin load on reperfusion. "Crush syndrome" when extricated from entrapment.
🌡️
Toxins & Drugs
Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors — especially in combination with fibrates), cocaine, amphetamines, alcohol, carbon monoxide, snake envenomation, malignant hyperthermia from succinylcholine.
💪
Exertion
Extreme exercise (ultramarathon, military training, CrossFit), status epilepticus (prolonged seizure activity), exertional heat stroke. Even moderate exercise in untrained individuals under heat stress.
🌡️
Temperature Extremes
Hyperthermia (heat stroke — >40°C/104°F) and hypothermia both damage muscle directly. Malignant hyperthermia from anesthetic agents is a pharmacogenetic cause.
⚡
Electrolyte Disorders
Severe hypokalemia and hypophosphatemia impair muscle cell membrane function → breakdown. Often seen in anorexia nervosa, chronic alcoholism, refeeding syndrome.
🦠
Infectious/Inflammatory
Viral myositis (influenza, COVID-19, EBV), severe sepsis, inflammatory myopathies (polymyositis, dermatomyositis). Bacterial toxins directly damage sarcolemma.

Pathophysiology of Myoglobin-Induced AKI

RHABDOMYOLYSIS → ACUTE KIDNEY INJURY CASCADE
1
Muscle Cell Injury
Damaged sarcolemma → massive influx of Ca²⁺ → activates proteases, phospholipases → cell lysis. Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase failure → cell swelling → membrane rupture. Intracellular contents released: myoglobin, CK, K⁺, phosphate, uric acid, LDH.
2
Myoglobin Enters Bloodstream
Myoglobin (17,000 Da protein containing heme iron) freely filtered by glomeruli (small enough). Normal urine = clear/yellow. With rhabdomyolysis: urine turns dark "tea-colored" or "cola-colored" from myoglobin.
3
Three Mechanisms of AKI
① Direct tubular toxicity: heme iron generates free radicals → lipid peroxidation → tubular cell necrosis (ATN). ② Tubular obstruction: myoglobin precipitates in acid urine + Tamm-Horsfall protein → casts → lumenal blockage. ③ Renal vasoconstriction: hypovolemia + direct myoglobin toxicity → afferent constriction → reduced GFR.
4
Systemic Complications
Hyperkalemia (K⁺ from lysed cells + impaired excretion) → cardiac arrhythmias. Metabolic acidosis. Hypocalcemia (calcium sequesters into damaged muscle). DIC in severe cases. Compartment syndrome (third-space edema).
☕
🔑 Rhabdomyolysis Analogy

Rhabdomyolysis is like dumping raw sewage (myoglobin + cellular debris) into a pristine water treatment plant (kidneys): The filtration membranes (tubules) become clogged with the toxic sludge. The heme iron acts like bleach being poured on the filter membranes — directly oxidizing and destroying them. The plant (kidneys) breaks down trying to process the overload. The solution: flood the system with clean water (aggressive IV hydration) to dilute the sludge, speed its passage, and alkalinize the water (sodium bicarbonate) to prevent myoglobin from precipitating into crystal chunks that block the filters.

Clinical Presentation — The Classic Triad

💪
Muscle Pain & Weakness
Myalgias, tenderness to palpation over affected muscle groups, weakness. May be diffuse or localized to the injured/exercised muscle group. May be subtle or severe.
☕
Dark "Tea/Cola" Urine
Classic sign — myoglobin in urine. Urine dipstick tests positive for "blood" (cross-reacts with myoglobin heme) but microscopy shows NO red blood cells. This distinction is key.
🔬
Elevated CK
CK >1,000 U/L (rhabdo). CK >10,000 U/L = severe rhabdo. CK peak occurs 24–72 hours post-injury. Half-life ~36 hours. Serial CK levels guide management.
⚡
Crush Syndrome — Prehospital Critical Protocol
"Start the IV before the extrication!" — Reperfusion of ischemic limbs releases massive amounts of myoglobin, K⁺, and acids. Starting aggressive IV fluid resuscitation BEFORE releasing the crush injury buffers the reperfusion toxemia. Establish IV, begin NS @ 1–1.5 L/hour BEFORE extrication in prolonged entrapment (>4 hours). Anticipate hyperkalemia-induced cardiac arrest immediately post-extrication — have calcium gluconate, sodium bicarbonate, and defibrillator ready.

You respond to a building collapse. A 35-year-old male has been entrapped under concrete debris for approximately 5 hours with his right leg and left arm compressed. Extrication will take another 20–30 minutes.
BP88/60 mmHg
HR128 bpm
ECGPeaked T-waves
SpO₂96%

🔑 Why must IV fluids be started BEFORE the debris is lifted?
Releasing a crush injury without pre-treatment is called "second hit" or "reperfusion injury." When the pressure is released: 1) Massive myoglobin flood → immediate nephrotoxicity, 2) Massive K⁺ release → hyperkalemia → VF/cardiac arrest, 3) Acidotic blood from ischemic tissue floods systemic circulation → cardiovascular collapse. Starting NS 1–1.5 L/hour BEFORE extrication dilutes these toxins as they enter the bloodstream, buffers the K⁺ and acid load, and maintains BP. Some EMS systems carry bicarbonate specifically for this scenario.
⚠️ The ECG shows peaked T-waves. What is your immediate management?
Hyperkalemia is already present from the ischemic muscle releasing K⁺ — and it will get much worse upon extrication. Immediate: Calcium gluconate 1g IV to stabilize the cardiac membrane (onset 1–3 min). Continue aggressive fluid resuscitation. Pre-draw: insulin/D50. Prepare for potential VF at time of extrication — defibrillator charged, pads on. Brief the receiving ED: "Crush syndrome, hyperkalemia with ECG changes, starting fluids prior to extrication — ETA 30 minutes."

Nephrotic & Nephritic Syndromes

Glomerular diseases are classified by their pathological pattern: nephrotic syndrome (protein loss) vs. nephritic syndrome (inflammation with hematuria). Both require recognition and appropriate hospital referral, as they represent serious renal pathology often encountered after complications arise.

Nephrotic Syndrome — "Protein Leak"

🚰
🔑 Nephrotic Syndrome Analogy

Nephrotic syndrome is like a leaky water main with no pressure. The glomerular filtration barrier (normally impermeable to protein) develops holes — massive amounts of albumin (and other proteins) leak into the urine. Without albumin, blood oncotic pressure falls. Plasma water is no longer held in the vascular space by osmotic forces — it floods into the interstitium → generalized edema (anasarca). Meanwhile, the liver goes into overdrive synthesizing proteins, inadvertently also producing excess lipoproteins → hyperlipidemia and lipiduria. The kidneys, sensing low oncotic pressure, activate RAAS → more fluid retention → worsening edema.

Nephrotic Syndrome — Defining Features
  • Massive proteinuria — >3.5g/24 hours (foamy urine)
  • Hypoalbuminemia — <3.5 g/dL (reduced oncotic pressure)
  • Generalized edema (anasarca) — periorbital edema classic (especially children), dependent edema, ascites, pleural effusions
  • Hyperlipidemia — elevated LDL/triglycerides (liver overproduction)
  • Lipiduria — oval fat bodies on microscopy
  • Hypercoagulable state — antithrombin III lost → DVT/PE risk
  • Increased infection risk — immunoglobulins lost in urine
Nephritic Syndrome — Defining Features
  • Hematuria — "cola-colored" or "smoky" urine, RBC casts in microscopy (pathognomonic for glomerulonephritis)
  • Hypertension — fluid and sodium retention
  • Oliguria — reduced GFR
  • Mild proteinuria — <3.5g/24hr (less than nephrotic)
  • Azotemia — elevated BUN/creatinine
  • Periorbital edema (less than nephrotic)
  • Examples: post-streptococcal GN (most common in children), IgA nephropathy, RPGN
💡
Memory Tool: Nephrotic vs. Nephritic
"Nephrotic = Protein" (Otic = O = Osmosis/protein): Protein loss, edema, hypoalbuminemia — NO blood, NO inflammatory signs.
"Nephritic = Inflammatory" (Itic = Inflammation): Hematuria, HTN, RBC casts — the glomerulus is INFLAMED and leaking blood. You can have overlap syndromes, but conceptually these help categorize disease.

GU Assessment & Prehospital Management

A systematic approach to genitourinary emergencies integrates history-taking, physical examination, and clinical reasoning to rapidly identify life threats and initiate appropriate interventions before reaching definitive care.

Focused GU History — Key Questions

OPQRST + GU-Specific Questions
  • Urinary Symptoms: Frequency, urgency, dysuria, hematuria (gross blood), color, odor, volume (oliguria/polyuria), incontinence, hesitancy, nocturia
  • Last urination: Time + amount — oliguria (<400mL/day) vs. anuria (<50mL/day)
  • Flank/pelvic pain: Location, radiation pattern, colicky vs. constant
  • Sexual history: STI risk, recent sexual activity (torsion, priapism triggers)
  • Genital symptoms: Scrotal pain/swelling (ALWAYS ask males)
  • Dialysis history: Last session, access site, missed sessions
Critical Medical History
  • Diabetes mellitus (autonomic neuropathy, neurogenic bladder, susceptibility to UTI/AKI)
  • Hypertension (renal vascular disease)
  • Known CKD/ESRD and dialysis history
  • Kidney stones (recurrent lithiasis)
  • BPH diagnosis (retention risk)
  • Sickle cell disease (priapism, renal medullary ischemia)
  • Spinal cord injury (neurogenic bladder, autonomic dysreflexia)
  • Medications: NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, statins, anticholinergics, alpha agonists, anticoagulants

📋 Capstone Case Study — Multi-system GU Emergency
You respond to a 64-year-old male with known ESRD on dialysis (3x/week) who missed his last two sessions because he "didn't feel well." He now presents with shortness of breath, confusion, and leg swelling. His wife says he has been taking ibuprofen for pain and drinking "not much" for the past several days.
BP182/110 mmHg
HR98 bpm, irregular
SpO₂88% RA
RR26/min, labored
ECGWide QRS, peaked T
GCS12 (confused)
🔑 What life threats do you identify and prioritize?
SIMULTANEOUS LIFE THREATS:
1. Severe hyperkalemia (peaked T-waves + wide QRS in ESRD patient who missed dialysis — K⁺ rising without dialysis) → CARDIAC ARREST RISK
2. Pulmonary edema (SpO₂ 88%, labored breathing, missed dialysis = fluid overload, no ultrafiltration) → RESPIRATORY FAILURE
3. Hypertensive emergency with encephalopathy (BP 182/110, AMS)
4. Uremic encephalopathy (confusion, missed 2 dialysis sessions)
Priority 1: Airway/breathing — NRB mask 15LPM, consider CPAP if available. Priority 2: Cardiac — IV access, calcium gluconate 1g IV NOW for hyperkalemia. Followed by insulin/D50. Priority 3: Immediate transport with ALS notification to ED — this patient needs emergent dialysis.
⚠️ What role did the ibuprofen play in this deterioration?
NSAIDs in ESRD patients worsen fluid retention (sodium retention), can cause additional renal vasoconstriction, and impair prostaglandin-mediated renal perfusion — worsening already compromised renal function. They also worsen fluid overload by reducing renal sodium excretion. Additionally, with already impaired platelet function from uremia, NSAIDs add antiplatelet effects → bleeding risk. NSAIDs are absolutely contraindicated in ESRD/CKD patients.
