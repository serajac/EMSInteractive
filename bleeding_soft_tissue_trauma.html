Types of Hemorrhage
Origin, Recognition & Clinical Significance
Andragogy Principle 3: Prior Experience

You have already seen these bleeds on scene. We are naming what you already recognize intuitively and deepening your clinical reasoning around each type.

Arterial bleeding arises from disruption of a high-pressure vessel carrying oxygenated blood directly from the heart. It is characterized by bright red, pulsatile spurting that is synchronous with the cardiac cycle.

⚠️
Critical Fact A severed femoral artery can exsanguinate an adult in as little as 3–5 minutes. Do not delay hemorrhage control for any reason at a scene with arterial involvement.

Pathophysiology Bridge

Arterial pressure averages 120 mmHg systolic. When the vessel wall is breached, blood is ejected with each ventricular contraction. The body's initial compensatory response — vasoconstriction via catecholamine release — may temporarily reduce visible bleeding, creating a dangerous false sense of control.

Clinical Analog

Think of a garden hose with a hole under full pressure. Kinking the hose (tourniquet or proximal compression) stops the spray far more effectively than attempting to patch the hole (direct pressure alone on arterial wounds).

CharacteristicArterial
ColorBright red (oxygenated)
Flow patternPulsatile, spurting
RateRapid — life-threatening immediately
Control methodTQ if extremity; direct pressure + packing otherwise
Spontaneous controlRare without intervention

Venous hemorrhage originates from the low-pressure venous system. Blood is dark red (deoxygenated) and flows in a steady, non-pulsatile stream. While less dramatic than arterial bleeding, large venous injuries — particularly involving the femoral, subclavian, or jugular veins — can be immediately life-threatening.

💡
Clinical Pearl Jugular vein injuries carry a unique risk: air embolism. The negative intrathoracic pressure created during inhalation can draw air into the venous system if a neck vein is open. Occlusive dressings are critical.

Venous vs. Arterial: The Field Distinction

In low-light or high-stress scenes, color differentiation may be difficult. Use flow pattern: Does it pulse? Pulsatile = arterial. Steady = venous. Both require immediate control.

Capillary bleeding is slow, oozing, and typically self-limiting. It appears as a diffuse weep from abrasions or superficial lacerations. While rarely life-threatening in isolation, in anticoagulated patients or those with clotting disorders it may be significant.

Internal Hemorrhage — The Hidden Killer

Internal hemorrhage is blood loss into a body cavity with no external sign. A hemothorax can hold 2–3 liters; a fractured pelvis up to 4 liters; intra-abdominal hemorrhage can be limitless. Your only tools are:

  • Mechanism of injury analysis
  • Signs of hypoperfusion (shock index, skin color/temp/moisture)
  • Abdominal rigidity, guarding, or distension
  • Pelvic instability on palpation
  • Unilateral decreased breath sounds (hemothorax)
  • Rapid transport to definitive care — your hospital is the treatment
🚨
Transport Decision Internal hemorrhage cannot be controlled in the field. Permissive hypotension (target SBP 80–90 mmHg in penetrating trauma) prevents clot disruption during transport. Load and go. Do not delay for IV access at scene.

Quick Check · Module 1
Test your foundational understanding
A patient with a femoral laceration has bright-red, pulsatile hemorrhage. Which mechanism best explains why direct pressure alone is likely insufficient as a definitive control method?
  • A. Venous pooling prevents adequate clot formation at high-pressure sites.
  • B. Arterial pressure exceeds the compressive force achievable by hand pressure alone over a large vessel.
  • C. Bright-red blood indicates deoxygenated flow that resists coagulation.
  • D. Direct pressure is contraindicated when bone fractures are suspected.
✅ Correct. The femoral artery carries blood at systolic pressures (70–120 mmHg). Manual compression may slow but rarely fully occlude a severed large-caliber artery. A tourniquet applied proximal to the injury provides circumferential compression that eliminates distal arterial flow entirely.
You arrive to find a patient with an open abdominal wound with protruding intestines. Your first action should be:
  • A. Gently replace the viscera into the abdominal cavity to prevent further contamination.
  • B. Apply a dry, sterile dressing and tape all four sides.
  • C. Cover with a moist (saline-moistened) occlusive dressing and prevent desiccation.
  • D. Insert a nasogastric tube to decompress the bowel before transport.
✅ Correct. Eviscerated bowel must never be reduced in the field — there is no sterile environment and reduction can cause peritoneal contamination, vascular kinking, or obstruction. A moist occlusive dressing prevents desiccation (drying kills exposed tissue) and protects from further contamination.

Hemorrhage Control: The Hierarchy
TECC/TCCC & Hartford Consensus Integrated
🎯
MARCH Protocol (Tactical/Field) Massive hemorrhage → Airway → Respirations → Circulation → Hypothermia. Hemorrhage control is FIRST — not third. This is a paradigm shift from traditional ABC prioritization.
Step 1: Direct Pressure
Apply firm, continuous pressure with gloved hand or hemostatic gauze. Minimum 3–5 minutes without releasing. Works for most venous and minor arterial wounds not involving large proximal vessels.
Step 2: Wound Packing
For deep, irregular wounds or wounds in junctional regions (neck, axilla, groin). Pack gauze deep into the wound cavity, apply firm direct pressure for minimum 3 minutes. Hemostatic gauze (Combat Gauze / ChitoGauze) preferred for HART/junctional hemorrhage.
Step 3: Tourniquet Application
For life-threatening extremity hemorrhage not controlled by pressure. Apply 2–3 inches proximal to wound. Tighten until hemorrhage stops (not just slows). Note time of application. Acceptable ischemia time: 2 hours before definitive care required.
Step 4: Junctional Hemorrhage Devices
Pelvis, groin, axilla — areas where TQ cannot be applied. Devices: Combat Ready Clamp (CRoC), Junctional Emergency Treatment Tool (JETT), SAM Junctional Tourniquet. These apply direct compression to major junctional vessels.

Tourniquet: Evidence-Based Decision Making

Contemporary evidence from military and civilian trauma strongly supports early tourniquet application for life-threatening extremity hemorrhage. The fear of ischemic complications — historically overemphasized — is substantially less clinically significant than exsanguination.

⚠️
Never Remove a Tourniquet in the Field Once applied, a tourniquet should only be removed by a physician in a controlled surgical environment with blood products immediately available. "Converting" a tourniquet to a pressure dressing in the field has caused preventable deaths.

Correct Technique (CAT Tourniquet)

  • Place 2–3 inches proximal to the wound (or as proximal as anatomically possible)
  • Thread tail through buckle, pull tight — doubly secure
  • Turn windlass rod until bleeding stops COMPLETELY (not just slows)
  • Lock windlass rod in keeper
  • Write application time directly on tourniquet or patient's forehead
  • Cover patient (hypothermia prevention)

Field Management of Penetrating Neck Wounds Apply occlusive dressing immediately (air embolism risk from jugular injury). Do NOT apply circumferential compression (bilateral carotid occlusion → stroke). Use gloved fingers or folded gauze with direct pressure on the specific wound. Maintain c-spine precautions only if mechanism suggests hyperextension/hyperflexion — penetrating trauma alone does NOT mandate rigid c-collar.

Crush Syndrome (Rhabdomyolysis)

Prolonged entrapment (>1 hour) causes myocyte death with release of myoglobin, potassium, phosphate, and creatine kinase into systemic circulation. The clinical risk arrives after extrication, not during.

💊
Pre-Extrication Protocol Begin aggressive IV crystalloid (normal saline, NOT LR — lactate in hyperkalaemia is dangerous) at 1–1.5 L/hour BEFORE release. Alkalinize urine with sodium bicarbonate per protocol. Monitor for reperfusion dysrhythmias post-release. Hyperkalemia → cardiac arrest risk is real.

Traumatic amputations present a viscerally disturbing scene that can distract from life-threatening hemorrhage. Your priority is always: patient stabilization first, limb preservation second.

Amputated Part Preservation

  • Rinse part gently with sterile saline — remove gross contamination only
  • Wrap in sterile saline-moistened gauze
  • Place in sealed sterile bag or container
  • Place bag in a second container with ice and water (NOT direct ice contact)
  • Transport with patient — label container clearly
  • Viable preservation window: 4–6 hours for digit; 4 hours for proximal limb (more muscle = less ischemic tolerance)
💡
Degloving Injuries Avulsions removing a sleeve of skin and subcutaneous tissue (degloving) — common in rollovers and industrial machinery. Moist dressings, preserve all tissue, transport priority. These often have intact neurovascular structures despite dramatic appearance.

Special Burn Types

Electrical burns are the most deceptive soft tissue injury in EMS. The entry and exit wounds may appear trivial while internal tissues along the electrical pathway have sustained massive injury.

  • Current follows path of least resistance (blood vessels, nerves)
  • Entry wound: small, charred; exit wound: explosive (more tissue damage)
  • Cardiac dysrhythmia risk (ventricular fibrillation is primary death mechanism)
  • Rhabdomyolysis: treat as crush injury
  • Delayed presentation of internal injuries is common — all electrical exposures require hospital evaluation regardless of appearance
  • High voltage (>1000V): blast, arc flash, thermal burns are also present

Acid Burns

Cause coagulation necrosis — a dense eschar forms that limits deeper penetration. Generally more contained than alkali burns.

Alkali Burns

Cause liquefaction necrosis — fat saponification allows continued deep penetration. More dangerous with progressive ongoing tissue destruction. Common alkalis: lye, cement, bleach.

Field Management

  • Ensure scene safety and provider PPE first
  • Brush off dry chemical before irrigation
  • Copious water irrigation for minimum 20 minutes (30 for eye exposure)
  • Remove all contaminated clothing and jewelry
  • Exception: hydrofluoric acid (HF) requires calcium gluconate gel application

In the above scenario (dark red, steady flow), you apply direct pressure × 3 minutes without success. Your next intervention should be:
  • A. Apply ice and elevate the limb to reduce venous pressure.
  • B. Pack the wound with hemostatic gauze and apply firm direct pressure.

Apply your complete knowledge set

Patient Prioritization (START Triage)

START Category: IMMEDIATE (Red)

Exsanguinating hemorrhage from traumatic amputation. Hypotension (88/60) and tachycardia (138) indicate Class III hemorrhagic shock (30–40% blood volume lost).

Management Priority Sequence

0–30 seconds: Tourniquet
Apply CAT TQ 2–3 inches proximal to amputation. Tighten until all hemorrhage stops. Document time.
30–90 seconds: Wrap amputated part
Moist gauze → sterile bag → second container with ice/water mixture. Transport with patient.
En route: IV/IO access × 2
Warm isotonic fluids (or blood products if available). Permissive hypotension target: SBP 80–90. Keep warm.

START Category: DELAYED (Yellow) — with upgrade potential

Currently hemodynamically stable with intact perfusion. However, 2-hour entrapment with bilateral leg involvement = significant rhabdomyolysis risk.

⚠️
Pre-Extrication Protocol is Critical Begin 1 L NS (NOT LR) IV before release. Consider bicarb (50 mEq NaHCO3) per protocol. Have defibrillator immediately available. Release can trigger K+ surge → cardiac arrest. Monitor ECG continuously after extrication.

START Category: DELAYED (Yellow)

Hemodynamically stable. Mild respiratory distress warrants close monitoring for pneumothorax or hemothorax development.

Impaled Object Management

  • DO NOT REMOVE the rebar — removal may release tamponaded hemorrhage
  • Stabilize object in place using bulky dressings secured around the impaled object
  • Cut object down to manageable length if necessary for transport
  • Exception to non-removal: if object prevents CPR or airway management and patient is in arrest — clinical judgment required
  • Monitor breath sounds bilaterally en route — if absent unilaterally, needle decompression per protocol
  • Maintain supine or position of comfort — do not rotate or flex the shoulder

Fluid Resuscitation: Current Evidence

Permissive Hypotension

Target SBP 80–90 mmHg for penetrating trauma without TBI. Aggressive crystalloid administration disrupts forming clots, causes hemodilution, and worsens the "lethal triad": acidosis + hypothermia + coagulopathy.

Lethal Triad Management

ComponentWhy It KillsField Intervention
HypothermiaEnzymatic coagulation cascade requires 37°C — cold = coagulopathyBlankets, warm fluids, remove wet clothing, heated ambulance
AcidosisImpairs platelet function and clotting factor activityOptimize perfusion; reduce ischemic burden; limit normal saline (hyperchloremic acidosis)
CoagulopathyClotting factor dilution + activation = inability to form stable clotsHemostatic agents; blood products (1:1:1 pRBC:FFP:PLT); TXA within 3 hrs of injury
💊
Tranexamic Acid (TXA) 1g IV/IO over 10 minutes within 3 hours of injury for significant hemorrhagic trauma. Inhibits fibrinolysis (clot breakdown). Reduces mortality by ~15% in major trauma. Avoid if injury occurred >3 hours prior — may increase mortality.
