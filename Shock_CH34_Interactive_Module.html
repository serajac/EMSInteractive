Foundational Science
Perfusion Physiology

Shock is not simply "low blood pressure" — it is cellular hypoxia: the failure to deliver adequate oxygen to tissues for aerobic metabolism. Understanding the physiology of oxygen delivery is the key to understanding every type of shock.

MAP = CO × SVR  |  CO = HR × SV  |  Normal MAP = 70–100 mmHg
🫀
Cardiac Output (CO)
The product of heart rate × stroke volume. Normal: 4–8 L/min. Determined by preload, afterload, and contractility (Starling's law).
📦
Preload
Ventricular volume at end of diastole — how much blood the ventricle has to work with. Think of it as "venous return." Governed by the Frank-Starling mechanism: more stretch = more contraction, up to a point.
🔩
Afterload (SVR)
The total resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood. Determined mainly by arteriolar tone. Arterioles can change diameter up to fivefold, making them the dominant regulator of blood flow.
💪
Contractility (Inotropy)
The intrinsic force of myocardial contraction. Normal ejection fraction: 50–70%. Borderline: 41–49%. Below 40% indicates significant impairment.
🫁
Oxygen Content
Blood must carry adequate O₂. Requires patent airway, adequate ventilation, SpO₂ ≥94%, and sufficient hemoglobin to transport oxygen to cells.
🔬
Microcirculation
The capillary–cellular interface where gas exchange occurs. In shock, arteriolar narrowing reduces capillary flow → anaerobic metabolism → lactic acid accumulates → metabolic acidosis.
🚿
Clinical Analogy — The Container Model
Think of the cardiovascular system as a closed-loop plumbing system. The heart is the pump, the blood vessels are the pipes, and blood is the fluid. Shock occurs when: (1) the pump breaks down, (2) the fluid leaks out, or (3) the pipes dilate beyond the fluid's ability to fill them. Each type of shock maps to one of these failure modes.

Type I
Hypovolemic Shock
Hypovolemic Hemorrhagic & Non-Hemorrhagic ▼

Inadequate circulating blood volume → decreased preload → decreased cardiac output → cellular hypoxia. The most common cause is hemorrhage; others include dehydration, burns, vomiting, diarrhea, and third-space loss (peritonitis).

🪣
Analogy — The Leaky Bucket
Imagine a pump connected to a bucket with a hole in it. The pump (heart) is perfectly functional, but as the bucket (vascular system) loses fluid faster than it fills, pressure drops and flow fails. Plugging the hole (hemorrhage control) is more important than pouring more water in (fluid resuscitation) if the leak is still active.

03
Type II
Cardiogenic Shock
Cardiogenic Pump Failure ▼

Severe compromise in cardiac output despite adequate circulating blood volume. The pump fails → blood backs up into the pulmonary circulation → pulmonary edema + forward hypoperfusion simultaneously. Myocardial infarction is the most common cause.

⚙️
Analogy — The Broken Pump
Imagine a water distribution system where the pump is seized. The reservoir is full, the pipes are intact, but the pump cannot push fluid forward. Water backs up behind the pump (pulmonary edema/JVD) while everything downstream dries out (systemic hypoperfusion). Adding more water to an already-full reservoir makes the backup worse — hence why fluid must be used cautiously.

Common Causes
Acute MI (most common), severe dysrhythmias (complete heart block, VT/VF), myocarditis, myocardial contusion, acute valvular failure (acute aortic regurgitation), dilated cardiomyopathy end-stage
Classic Presentation
Hypotension + JVD + pulmonary edema (rales/crackles) + cool, clammy, diaphoretic skin + narrow pulse pressure. May have chest pain, dyspnea, ECG changes (ST elevation, new LBBB, dysrhythmias)
Hemodynamic Profile
↓ CO · ↑ SVR · ↑ Preload (PCWP)

The vicious cycle: low CO → reflex ↑ SVR → ↑ afterload → worsens CO further

Fluid Caution: Unlike hypovolemic shock, aggressive IV fluids can worsen pulmonary edema in cardiogenic shock. Give cautious fluid challenges (250 mL) while monitoring lung sounds closely. The priority is improving pump function, not filling the tank.
04
Type III
Obstructive Shock

Inability to produce adequate cardiac output despite normal intravascular volume and myocardial function. A mechanical obstruction blocks flow. The pump works, the fluid is there — something is physically blocking the circuit.

Obstructive Cardiac Tamponade ▼
🫀
Analogy — The Strangled Heart
Imagine wrapping a rubber band tightly around a rubber squeeze-ball. You can squeeze as hard as you want, but the rubber band prevents the ball from ever fully expanding to refill. The heart cannot fill during diastole because the pericardial fluid compresses from the outside. No fill = no output.

Treatment
Aggressive fluid boluses to maintain preload (bridge therapy). Definitive: pericardiocentesis in-hospital. Rapid transport is essential.
Obstructive Tension Pneumothorax ▼
🫧
Analogy — The One-Way Valve
Air enters the pleural space through a one-way valve mechanism with each breath but cannot escape. Like inflating a balloon inside a box — as the balloon expands, it shoves everything else aside. The mediastinum shifts, the SVC kinks, venous return drops to near zero, and the heart has nothing to pump.

Classic Signs
Absent/decreased unilateral breath sounds · Tracheal deviation (AWAY from tension side, late sign) · JVD · Hypotension · Hypoxia · Tachycardia
Treatment
Needle Decompression: 2nd ICS, MCL (or 4th/5th ICS, MAL). Converts tension PTX → simple PTX. Follow with chest tube in-hospital. Do NOT delay for X-ray — this is a clinical diagnosis!
Obstructive Massive Pulmonary Embolism ▼
🚫
Analogy — The Blocked Exit Ramp
Blood flowing through the right heart is like traffic heading to a highway. A massive PE is like a multi-car crash blocking the on-ramp. The right ventricle keeps pumping against the blockage, pressure builds (acute right heart failure/cor pulmonale), the RV dilates, the interventricular septum shifts left, and the LV starves for preload.
Risk Factors (Virchow's Triad)
Stasis (long immobilization, post-op) · Endothelial injury (fractures, surgery) · Hypercoagulability (malignancy, OCP, post-COVID). Also: long-bone fractures (fat embolism).
Signs & Symptoms
Sudden dyspnea · Pleuritic chest pain · Tachycardia · Signs of right heart failure (JVD, S3) · Hypoxia · Possible syncope. Massive PE = shock + hypoxia.
Treatment
Supportive O₂, IV access. In-hospital: Fibrinolytic therapy (tPA), surgical or catheter-directed embolectomy for massive PE. Prehospital: supportive care, rapid transport.
05
Type IV
Distributive Shock

Peripheral vasodilation causes a decrease in systemic vascular resistance → the vascular "container" expands faster than the available blood volume can fill. Uniquely presents as "warm shock" — warm, flushed skin from vasodilation (unlike the cold, clammy skin of hypovolemic/cardiogenic shock).

Neurogenic Neurogenic Shock (Vasogenic) ▼

Disruption of the spinal cord at T6 and above interrupts sympathetic pathways → unopposed parasympathetic tone → massive vasodilation + bradycardia. Unique: the only form of shock with bradycardia as a feature (all others cause tachycardia).

🔌
Analogy — City-Wide Power Failure
Every traffic signal in a city suddenly goes dark. Normally the sympathetic nervous system acts like traffic control — keeping vessels at an appropriate tone. When the spinal cord is severed, all vessels below the injury lose their "signal" simultaneously, relaxing completely. The container expands dramatically with no compensatory response possible from below the injury level.
Classic Presentation
BRADYCARDIA + HYPOTENSION + WARM/FLUSHED SKIN

This triad distinguishes neurogenic from all other shock states. MAP target: 85–90 mmHg for spinal cord perfusion.
Common Mechanism
High cervical or thoracic spine fracture/dislocation. Also: epidural anesthesia (iatrogenic spinal block), transverse myelitis. Distinguished from spinal shock (flaccid paralysis without hemodynamic instability).

Anaphylactic Anaphylactic Shock ▼

The most extreme form of anaphylaxis: IgE-mediated mast cell and basophil degranulation releases histamine, tryptase, and leukotrienes → systemic vasodilation + bronchospasm + increased capillary permeability → distributive shock with airway compromise.

🚨
Analogy — The Fire Alarm System Gone Haywire
The immune system's "emergency response" (IgE on mast cells) detects what it wrongly identifies as a mortal threat. Like a fire alarm system that activates all sprinklers simultaneously for a small kitchen fire — the immune response is so overwhelming that it causes more damage than the trigger. Every blood vessel opens, capillaries leak, and bronchioles slam shut, all at once.

Septic Septic Shock ▼

Sepsis = SIRS + known/suspected infection. Septic shock = sepsis + profound circulatory/cellular/metabolic abnormalities refractory to fluid resuscitation. The cytokine storm (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) drives endothelial dysfunction, massive vasodilation, and myocardial depression simultaneously.

🌪️
Analogy — The Immune System Riot
Normal infection response is like a targeted SWAT team. Septic shock is a full city riot — every branch of the immune system fires simultaneously. Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) act like inflammatory gasoline: vessels dilate uncontrollably, capillary membranes become like sieves leaking protein-rich fluid, and the heart itself begins to fail from cytokine-mediated myocardial depression — a triple-threat hemodynamic catastrophe.
SIRS Criteria (2 of 4)
TEMP: >38°C or <36°C
HR: >90 bpm
RR: >20/min or PaCO₂ <32
WBC: >12,000 or <4,000
Common Infection Sites
Lungs (pneumonia — most common) · Urinary tract (UTI/pyelonephritis) · GI tract (peritonitis, cholangitis) · Skin/Soft tissue (cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis) · Catheter-associated infections

Prehospital sepsis tool: ① Altered mental status · ② RR ≥22/min · ③ SBP ≤100 mmHg
Score ≥2 → high suspicion for sepsis → early hospital notification for antibiotic initiation

Progression
Stages of Shock
Compensated
EARLY · REVERSIBLE
Body maintains near-normal BP through catecholamine release (↑ HR, vasoconstriction, ↑ contractility). Catecholamines also stimulate gluconeogenesis. Skin: pale, cool, diaphoretic. CO₂ rises slightly (from hyperventilation compensation). SpO₂ may still be maintained.
Uncompensated
PROGRESSIVE · TREATABLE
Compensatory mechanisms overwhelmed. SBP drops (diastolic may rise first due to vasoconstriction, causing narrow pulse pressure). Anaerobic metabolism, lactic acidosis. Ischemia threatens myocardium — cardiodepressant substances released from ischemic pancreas worsen contractility. AMS, oliguria.
Irreversible
TERMINAL · FATAL
Cellular ischemia and necrosis. Membrane pump failure → cells swell and die. DIC. MODS (multi-organ dysfunction syndrome). Even with return to normal perfusion, patients do not survive — organelle breakdown is irreversible. Death inevitable.
👶
Age Matters: Children compensate very well for longer periods than adults (strong cardiovascular reserve), then decompensate suddenly and catastrophically. Older adults compensate poorly from the start (reduced cardiac reserve, comorbidities). Beta-blockers can mask tachycardic compensation — always review medications!
07
Clinical Practice
Assessment of Shock
🩸 Control Exsanguination FIRST
In trauma, identify and control life-threatening external hemorrhage before the airway. Use direct pressure, wound packing, or tourniquet for extremity wounds. This precedes the traditional ABCDE sequence.
💨 Airway & Breathing
Confirm airway patency. Respiratory pattern reflects adequacy of ventilation and offers clues to shock etiology (e.g., Kussmaul breathing = metabolic acidosis).
💓 Circulation Assessment
Rate, character, and location of pulses. Narrow pulse pressure = impending uncompensated shock. Skin: color, moisture, temperature, and capillary refill (most useful in pediatrics).
🧠 Disability (Neuro)
LOC assessment = indirect cerebral perfusion indicator. Restlessness and agitation are early signs of cerebral ischemia. AVPU or GCS serially.

Resuscitation Science
Fluid Management
Flow ∝ r⁴/L  |  Short + Large Bore = Maximum Flow Rate
Isotonic Crystalloids
Normal Saline (0.9%): 154 mEq/L Na⁺. No buffering. Can cause hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis in large volumes.

Lactated Ringer's: Balanced; lactate buffered by liver → combats acidosis. One-third remains intravascular after 1 hour. More physiologic choice for hemorrhagic shock.
Colloids
Large protein molecules create oncotic pressure → stay intravascular longer. Albumin (natural), Hetastarch/Hespan (synthetic).

Synthetics: expensive, risk of allergic reaction, antiplatelet effects, may prolong bleeding time. Less commonly used prehospital.
Blood Products
pRBCs restore O₂-carrying capacity. Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP) replaces clotting factors. Balanced ratio (1:1:1 pRBC:FFP:Platelets) for massive transfusion.

Type and cross-match when possible. Plasma can be given without ABO compatibility concern.
IV Access Strategy
Poiseuille's Law: flow ∝ r⁴/length. Use short, large-bore catheters (16-18g preferred). Pressure bags at 300 mmHg maximize rate. Intraosseous (IO) access delivers equivalent flow when IV not obtainable.
