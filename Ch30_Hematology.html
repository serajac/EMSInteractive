Section 01 — Foundation

Blood & Its Components

Blood is the body's transport network — a living fluid carrying oxygen, nutrients, waste products, immune cells, and clotting factors to every tissue in the body.

Overview: What Is Blood?

Blood is a specialized connective tissue composed of formed elements (cells and cell fragments) suspended in plasma. The average adult has approximately 5–6 liters of blood, representing about 7–8% of body weight. Blood performs three essential functions: delivery of metabolic substrates to tissues, defense against microorganisms, and maintenance of acid–base balance.

🔬 Clinical Analogy

Think of blood as a city's highway system: Plasma is the road itself, RBCs are oxygen-delivery trucks, WBCs are the police/fire department, and platelets are the road-repair crew. Each component has a distinct job — and when one fails, the whole city feels it.

Plasma — The Liquid Matrix

Plasma is approximately 92% water and contains salts, metals, inorganic compounds, and three critical proteins:

ProteinPrimary FunctionClinical Significance
AlbuminMaintains oncotic pressure; keeps water in bloodstreamLow albumin → edema, third-spacing; checked in liver/kidney disease
GlobulinsTransport proteins; provide immunity (immunoglobulins)Gamma globulins = antibodies; elevated in infection/autoimmune disease
FibrinogenPrecursor to fibrin; essential for clot formationConsumed in DIC; replaced with FFP/cryoprecipitate
📌 Key Distinction
Plasma minus fibrinogen and clotting factors = Serum. Serum is what remains after blood clots; plasma still contains fibrinogen. This is why chemistry panels are run on serum, while coagulation studies require plasma with anticoagulant.
Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)

RBCs are the most abundant cells in the body and the primary oxygen carriers. Their unique biconcave disc shape maximizes surface area for gas exchange while allowing them to deform and squeeze through capillaries smaller than their own diameter.

🔴 Lifespan: ~120 days
🔬 No nucleus or organelles
🏭 Produced: Bone marrow
♻️ Destroyed: Spleen & liver
🧬 Hemoglobin carries O₂
📊 Normal: 4.5–6M/μL (M), 4–5.5M/μL (F)
🔬 Clinical Analogy

Hemoglobin is like a four-seat car — each molecule has 4 heme groups, each capable of carrying one oxygen molecule. A fully loaded car (oxyhemoglobin) is bright red; an empty car (deoxyhemoglobin) is dark red/purple. In carbon monoxide poisoning, CO permanently occupies a seat, preventing O₂ from boarding — like a passenger who refuses to leave and blocks the fare-paying customers.

White Blood Cells (Leukocytes)

WBCs are the body's immune defense force. They are produced in bone marrow and lymph glands and migrate to sites of infection or injury. A normal WBC count is 4,500–11,000/μL — elevation (leukocytosis) often signals infection or leukemia; depression (leukopenia) indicates immunocompromise.

Platelets (Thrombocytes)

Platelets are small, sticky cell fragments derived from megakaryocytes in the bone marrow. They play a central role in hemostasis by adhering to damaged vessel walls and forming a physical plug. Normal count: 150,000–400,000/μL.

⚠️ Critical Threshold
Platelet count <50,000/μL → increased bleeding risk with procedures. <20,000/μL → risk of spontaneous bleeding (petechiae, ecchymosis, mucosal bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage). <10,000/μL → transfusion typically indicated.
🔬 Clinical Analogy

Platelets are like emergency patch kits for broken pipes. When a blood vessel is damaged, platelets rush to the scene and stick together to form a temporary plug — like duct-taping a leaky pipe until a permanent repair (fibrin clot) can be made. Without enough platelets, even small cuts become dangerous leaks.

Hemostasis

The body's elegant four-stage process for stopping hemorrhage — from the initial vessel spasm to permanent fibrin sealing — and how medications can augment or disrupt this cascade.

The Four Stages of Hemostasis

Hemostasis is the physiologic response to vessel injury that causes bleeding to cease. It occurs in four sequential, overlapping phases:

STAGE 1Vascular Spasm
Injured vessel constricts immediately → reduces blood flow
→
STAGE 2Platelet Plug
Platelets adhere to collagen → activate → aggregate → temporary plug
→
STAGE 3Coagulation Cascade
Intrinsic/extrinsic pathways → thrombin → fibrin meshwork
→
STAGE 4Fibrous Repair
Fibrous tissue grows into clot → permanent vessel sealing
The Coagulation Cascade

The cascade is a series of enzymatic reactions in which each factor activates the next, producing a massive amplification of the initial signal. Two pathways converge on a common pathway:

Extrinsic Pathway (Fast — seconds)
Triggered by tissue factor (TF) released from damaged tissue outside the vessel. TF + Factor VII → activates Factor X. This is the primary pathway for hemostasis after injury. Measured by PT/INR.
Intrinsic Pathway (Slower — minutes)
Triggered by contact with damaged vessel collagen. Involves Factors XII, XI, IX, VIII. More important for amplifying clot formation. Measured by aPTT. Deficient in hemophilia A (Factor VIII) and B (Factor IX).
📌 Common Pathway
Both pathways converge at Factor X activation. Factor Xa + Factor V → activates prothrombin → thrombin → converts fibrinogen into fibrin threads → fibrin meshwork traps RBCs and platelets to form the stable clot.
🔬 Clinical Analogy

The coagulation cascade is like a nuclear reactor chain reaction — one neutron triggers 2, which trigger 4, which trigger 8... an exponential amplification. Factor XIII is the "cross-linking agent" that ties the fibrin threads together like cross-stitching a wound — without it, the clot is like a pile of loose rope instead of a knot.

Anemia

A reduction in oxygen-carrying capacity — not a disease, but a symptom. Understanding the cause is critical to treating the effect. The cells are empty, broken, or simply too few.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Anemia is defined as a reduction in the concentration of hemoglobin or the number of erythrocytes below normal for age and sex. The result is impaired oxygen delivery to tissues. The body compensates by increasing heart rate, respiratory rate, and redistributing blood flow — until it can't.

🔬 Clinical Analogy

Anemia is like running a city with too few delivery trucks. Even if the roads are clear and fuel is available, not enough oxygen reaches the cells. The city (body) compensates by sending trucks on more routes (tachycardia) and speeding them up (increased cardiac output) — but eventually, fatigue and failure set in.

Iron-Deficiency Anemia — The Most Common Form

Iron deficiency prevents the bone marrow from synthesizing adequate hemoglobin, producing microcytic, hypochromic RBCs (small and pale — low MCV, low MCHC). It is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide.

CAUSEBlood loss (menstrual), poor intake, malabsorption
→
EFFECTDepleted iron stores → ↓ ferritin
→
RESULT↓ Hemoglobin synthesis → small pale RBCs
→
OUTCOMEReduced O₂ delivery → fatigue, pallor, tachycardia

Common risk factors: Heavy menstrual bleeding, pregnancy/breastfeeding, inflammatory bowel disease, peptic ulcer disease, bariatric surgery, vegetarian/vegan diet without supplementation, excessive cow's milk in infants.

Hemolytic Anemia — Premature RBC Destruction

Hemolytic anemia occurs when RBCs are destroyed faster than the bone marrow can replace them. The spleen removes damaged cells, leading to splenomegaly. Bilirubin from RBC breakdown can cause jaundice.

Inherited / Intrinsic Causes
• Hereditary spherocytosis (abnormal cell membrane → trapped in spleen)
• G6PD deficiency (enzyme lack → oxidative stress destroys RBCs)
• Sickle cell disease (discussed in Section 8)
• Thalassemia (abnormal hemoglobin chains)
Acquired / Extrinsic Causes
• Autoimmune hemolytic anemia (warm/cold antibody types)
• Microangiopathic hemolysis (TTP, HUS, DIC — mechanical destruction)
• Infection (malaria, Clostridium — organisms directly lyse RBCs)
• Drug-induced (penicillin, methyldopa)
⚠️ EMS Clue
Hemolytic anemia triad: Pallor + Jaundice (scleral icterus) + Splenomegaly. Ask about recent infections, new medications, or family history. Dark urine (hemoglobinuria) indicates intravascular hemolysis — a red flag.

Sickle Cell Disease

A genetic mutation transforms round, flexible oxygen carriers into rigid crescent-shaped missiles that jam blood vessels, causing agonizing pain crises, organ damage, and potentially fatal complications.

Genetics & Epidemiology

Sickle cell disease (SCD) is an autosomal recessive inherited disorder caused by a point mutation in the beta-globin gene on chromosome 11. A single amino acid substitution (valine for glutamic acid at position 6) produces hemoglobin S (HbS) — a defective hemoglobin with inferior oxygen-carrying capacity.

📌 Epidemiology
Primarily affects individuals of sub-Saharan African, South American, Caribbean, Central American, Saudi Arabian, Indian, and Mediterranean descent. 1 in 13 African American babies is born with the sickle cell trait (HbAS). Full disease (HbSS) = both copies abnormal. The sickle cell trait is thought to confer some protection against malaria.
Pathophysiology — The Sickling Cascade
TRIGGERLow O₂, dehydration, cold, acidosis, infection, stress
→
MUTATIONHbS polymerizes under deoxygenation → RBCs deform into rigid sickle shape
→
OCCLUSIONSickle cells jam capillaries → vaso-occlusion → tissue ischemia
→
CRISISExcruciating pain, organ damage, hemolytic anemia
🔬 Clinical Analogy

Normal RBCs are like round, flexible coins that flow effortlessly through a coin slot. Sickle cells are like crumpled, rigid pieces of foil — they jam the slot, pile up, and block everything behind them. The backup creates pressure (pain), starves tissues of oxygen, and the cells themselves are so fragile they break apart (hemolysis), causing chronic anemia.

Polycythemia

When the body makes too many red blood cells, blood thickens like ketchup — increasing clot risk, hypertension, and stroke. Primary (bone marrow disease) vs. secondary (physiologic response) have very different etiologies.

Primary Polycythemia (Polycythemia Vera — PV)

A myeloproliferative neoplasm in which the bone marrow overproduces RBCs autonomously, without the normal erythropoietin signaling. Caused in 95% of cases by a JAK2 V617F mutation. Primarily affects individuals >50 years old.

🔬 Clinical Analogy

Think of normal blood as water flowing through a pipe. In polycythemia vera, so many RBCs are packed into the bloodstream that blood becomes thick like ketchup. Ketchup doesn't flow well — it clogs pipes (vessels), builds pressure (hypertension), and creates chunks (clots). The JAK2 mutation is like a thermostat stuck on "make more RBCs" — it never gets the off signal.

🔴 Red/purple complexion
🤲 Red hands and feet
🫁 Headache, dizziness
👀 Blurred vision
💢 Hypertension
🩺 Splenomegaly
😣 Generalized itching (aquagenic pruritus)
🩸 Clot risk (DVT, PE, stroke)
TreatmentDrug/ProcedureGoal
First-linePhlebotomyRemove blood to reduce hematocrit to <45%; decreases blood viscosity
CytoreductionHydroxyureaSuppresses bone marrow overproduction of RBCs; used when phlebotomy alone insufficient
Clot preventionAspirin 81mg/dayAnti-platelet; reduces thrombotic risk (strokes, MI, DVT)
Alternative cytoreductionRuxolitinib (Jakafi)JAK1/JAK2 inhibitor — directly targets the pathologic mutation; for hydroxyurea-resistant PV
Secondary Polycythemia — Physiologic Compensation

Secondary polycythemia is a normal compensatory response to chronic hypoxia. The kidneys detect low O₂ and release erythropoietin (EPO), which stimulates the bone marrow to make more RBCs — trying to carry more oxygen with each heartbeat.

Chronic Hypoxia Causes
• High altitude (thin air → lower PO₂)
• COPD / chronic lung disease
• Obstructive sleep apnea
• Heavy cigarette smoking (CO displaces O₂)
• Congenital heart disease with right-to-left shunt
Non-Hypoxic Causes (Inappropriate EPO)
• Renal cell carcinoma (tumor secretes EPO)
• Hepatocellular carcinoma
• Benign renal cysts
• EPO doping (athletes using synthetic EPO — "blood doping")
📌 Treatment of Secondary Polycythemia
Treat the underlying cause (CPAP for sleep apnea, O₂ therapy for COPD, smoking cessation). The body will down-regulate EPO when oxygen delivery improves and RBC count will normalize. Phlebotomy is occasionally used for symptom relief if hematocrit is dangerously elevated.

Leukemia

A cancer of blood-forming cells — abnormal WBCs flood the bone marrow, crowding out normal production of all blood lines. When the factory makes defective products in bulk, the whole system shuts down.

Pathophysiology — The Bone Marrow Takeover

Leukemia occurs when a single abnormal WBC undergoes malignant transformation and proliferates uncontrollably in the bone marrow. These leukemic cells:

ORIGINGenetic mutation in myeloid or lymphoid stem cell
→
EXPANSIONMalignant clone proliferates → crowds out normal marrow
→
FAILURE↓ RBCs (anemia) + ↓ platelets (bleeding) + ↓ normal WBCs (infection)
→
SPREADLeukemic cells spill into blood, organs → organ dysfunction, death
🔬 Clinical Analogy

Imagine a car factory where one rogue assembly line starts producing defective cars that don't work and never stop coming. These defective cars (leukemic cells) fill up all the parking lots (bone marrow space) so normal cars (RBCs, platelets, WBCs) can't be assembled or parked. Eventually, normal production stops entirely — leading to anemia, bleeding, and infection simultaneously.

Lymphoma

Cancer of the lymphatic system — when the body's immune checkpoints go rogue. Two major families: Hodgkin (typically curable) and Non-Hodgkin (highly variable, many subtypes).

The Lymphatic System — Brief Review

The lymphatic system is a network of nodes, ducts, and lymphoid organs (spleen, thymus, tonsils, bone marrow) that filter tissue fluid and house immune cells. Lymph nodes are strategic checkpoints where lymphocytes inspect for foreign invaders.

🔬 Clinical Analogy

Lymph nodes are like airport security checkpoints. Normal lymphocytes are trained security agents who screen passengers (antigens) and respond to threats. In lymphoma, one security agent clones themselves uncontrollably — filling the checkpoint with copies of one agent, blocking real traffic, and eventually spreading to other checkpoints (lymph node chains) and airports (organs) throughout the country (body).

Hodgkin Lymphoma

Characterized by the presence of Reed-Sternberg cells — giant, binucleated B-cells that are the pathological hallmark. Bimodal age distribution: young adults (15–35) and older adults (>55). Generally the most curable of all lymphomas when caught early.

Signs & Symptoms
• Painless, progressive lymph node enlargement (neck, axillae, groin)
• "B symptoms": Fever, drenching night sweats, >10% weight loss in 6 months
• Fatigue
• Pruritus (generalized itching)
• Splenomegaly
• Alcohol-induced pain at enlarged lymph node sites (pathognomonic)
Staging & Treatment
Ann Arbor Staging (I–IV):
Stage I: Single lymph node region
Stage II: 2+ regions, same side of diaphragm
Stage III: Both sides of diaphragm
Stage IV: Organ involvement (liver, bone marrow)

Treatment: ABVD chemotherapy (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) ± radiation; 5-year survival >85% for early disease
Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma (NHL)

A large, heterogeneous group of >60 lymphoid malignancies. Most common in adults >60. Graded by aggressiveness: indolent (low-grade), aggressive (intermediate), or highly aggressive (high-grade). B-cell NHLs are more common than T-cell NHLs.

Multiple Myeloma

A plasma cell malignancy where one rogue antibody-producing cell clones itself into a bone-destroying army. CRAB is the key — hyperCalcemia, Renal failure, Anemia, Bone lesions.

Pathophysiology

Multiple myeloma is a malignancy of plasma cells (differentiated B-cells that produce antibodies). A single malignant plasma cell clones itself, accumulating in the bone marrow and producing large amounts of a monoclonal immunoglobulin (M-protein) — a useless antibody that serves no immune function but damages organs.

🔬 Clinical Analogy

Imagine a factory worker who goes rogue, clones themselves thousands of times, and the clones all make the same defective product (M-protein) while destroying the factory walls (bones). The defective products pile up in the kidneys (renal failure), clog the blood (hyperviscosity), and the factory (bone marrow) collapses, unable to make normal products — causing anemia and immune failure.

📌 CRAB Criteria — Myeloma Defining Features
C — hyperCalcemia (bone destruction releases calcium into blood)
R — Renal insufficiency (M-protein clogs renal tubules; Bence Jones proteins in urine)
A — Anemia (marrow crowding → ↓ RBC production)
B — Bone lesions (osteolytic "punched out" lesions on X-ray; pathologic fractures)
Signs & Symptoms
🦴 Bone pain (back, ribs)
💔 Pathologic fractures
😴 Fatigue (anemia)
🩺 Weakness
🧠 Confusion (hypercalcemia)
🚱 Kidney failure
⚖️ Weight loss
🤒 Frequent infections
🩸 Bleeding (platelet crowding)
👁️ Blurred vision (hyperviscosity)
⚠️ EMS Pearl — Spinal Cord Compression
Vertebral collapse from lytic lesions can cause acute spinal cord compression — a true emergency. Signs: back pain + sudden bilateral leg weakness/numbness, urinary/bowel incontinence. This requires immediate hospital transport and high-dose dexamethasone — delays cause permanent paralysis.

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

The ultimate paradox of hemostasis — a catastrophic disorder where the body clots and bleeds simultaneously. The coagulation system burns through all its resources, leaving the patient defenseless against hemorrhage.

Pathophysiology — The Two-Phase Catastrophe
🔬 Clinical Analogy

DIC is like a fire sprinkler system gone haywire. Phase 1: Every sprinkler in the building activates simultaneously (clotting everywhere) — the system depletes all its water (clotting factors) responding to a massive false alarm. Phase 2: When a real fire breaks out (actual bleeding), there's no water left (consumptive coagulopathy) — the patient hemorrhages uncontrollably from every site.

Phase 1 — Thrombotic Phase
Free thrombin circulates systemically → massive fibrin deposition throughout microvasculature → microthrombi in small vessels → multi-organ ischemia (kidney, liver, lungs, brain). Platelets consumed forming clots. Clotting factors consumed. The "clot and run out" phase.
Phase 2 — Hemorrhagic Phase
Clotting factors and platelets now depleted → the body CANNOT form new clots. Even minor vessel damage causes uncontrolled hemorrhage. Fibrinolysis activates to dissolve the microthrombi → D-dimers rise → fibrin degradation products further inhibit remaining clotting. Dual organ failure from ischemia AND bleeding.
Triggers — What Activates DIC?
🤕 Severe trauma / crush injury
🦠 Sepsis (gram-negative bacteremia)
🤰 Obstetric emergencies (abruption, amniotic fluid embolism)
🐍 Snake envenomation
🔥 Severe burns
💉 Massive transfusion reactions
☠️ Malignancy (especially AML-M3)
🫀 Cardiac arrest / prolonged shock
Signs & Symptoms
💨 Dyspnea (microemboli in lungs)
🩸 Bleeding from all sites (IV sites, wounds, mucosae)
💢 Hypotension, shock
👄 Gingival/mucosal bleeding
🩺 Petechiae and purpura
🧠 Altered mental status
🚱 Oliguria (renal microthrombi)
📊 Falling platelet count + prolonged PT/INR/aPTT

Hemophilia

An inherited inability to form a firm clot — the coagulation cascade works, but a critical catalyst is missing. Bleeding continues unchecked into joints, muscles, and the brain until the missing factor is replaced.

Genetics & Classification

Hemophilia is an X-linked recessive disorder — the gene is on the X chromosome. Females with one abnormal X are carriers (usually asymptomatic); males with one abnormal X develop the disease. This is why hemophilia predominantly affects males.

🔬 Clinical Analogy

Imagine cement that never hardens. Normal blood clotting is like mixing cement (fibrinogen) with water — add the catalyst (clotting factors) and it hardens into a plug. In hemophilia, the catalyst is missing. The cement (fibrin) never sets — it stays liquid and keeps flowing. The temporary platelet plug forms fine (primary hemostasis is intact), but it washes away without the solid fibrin scaffold behind it. Every cut, bruise, or joint movement becomes a prolonged bleed.

Hemophilia A — Factor VIII Deficiency
Most common form (~80% of cases). Factor VIII is essential for the intrinsic pathway (amplification). Severity classified by Factor VIII level:
• Severe: <1% activity (spontaneous bleeding)
• Moderate: 1–5% (bleeding with minor trauma)
• Mild: 5–40% (bleeding only with major trauma/surgery)
Hemophilia B — Factor IX Deficiency
"Christmas Disease" (first described in Stephen Christmas, 1952). Factor IX works with Factor VIII in the intrinsic pathway. Clinically indistinguishable from Hemophilia A — differentiated only by factor assay. Same X-linked inheritance, same severity classification.

Thrombocytopenia

Too few platelets — the repair crew is gone. When the patch kits run out, even small vessel injuries lead to catastrophic, uncontrolled leaks. Two major types: ITP (immune destruction) and TTP (pathologic consumption).

Pathophysiology — Three Mechanisms
🔬 Clinical Analogy

Think of platelets as a city's emergency patch kits for broken water pipes. Thrombocytopenia occurs three ways: (1) the factory stopped making patch kits (decreased production), (2) the city is using up patch kits faster than they're made (increased consumption/destruction), or (3) all the patch kits are sitting in a warehouse and can't be distributed (splenic sequestration). When leaks happen — cuts, microinjuries — there aren't enough kits to fix them.

MechanismExamplesKey Feature
Decreased ProductionAplastic anemia, leukemia infiltrating marrow, chemo, radiation, B12/folate deficiency, alcohol toxicityAll cell lines affected (anemia + leukopenia + thrombocytopenia = pancytopenia)
Increased DestructionITP (immune), TTP (consumptive), DIC, HIT (heparin-induced), massive transfusionOnly platelets low (or platelets + RBCs in TTP/DIC); marrow compensates by making more
Splenic SequestrationCirrhosis with hypersplenism, portal hypertension, storage diseasesUp to 90% of platelets trapped in enlarged spleen; total body platelets normal but circulation low
ITP — Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura

ITP is an autoimmune disorder where the immune system generates antibodies (primarily IgG anti-GPIIb/IIIa) that coat platelets, marking them for phagocytosis by splenic macrophages. The bone marrow compensates by producing more megakaryocytes and platelets, but destruction outpaces production.

Acute ITP (Pediatric)
Typically follows viral infection (1–4 weeks prior). Self-limited in 80% of children. Often resolves spontaneously within 6 months. Presents with petechiae and purpura after a "viral illness."
Chronic ITP (Adult)
Persists >12 months. More common in women 20–40 years. Often insidious onset without preceding illness. Requires treatment if symptomatic or platelets <30,000/μL.

TTP — Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura

TTP is a life-threatening emergency caused by deficiency or inhibition of ADAMTS13 — the enzyme that normally breaks down ultra-large vWF multimers. Without ADAMTS13, vWF accumulates → platelets clump on it → microthrombi form throughout the body → platelet consumption and RBC shearing.

⚠️ TTP Pentad — Classic (not always complete)
1. Thrombocytopenia (platelet clumping)
2. Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes on smear)
3. Neurological symptoms (confusion, seizure — microthrombi in brain)
4. Renal impairment (microthrombi in kidneys)
5. Fever
Treatment: Emergent plasmapheresis (plasma exchange) removes the ADAMTS13 antibody and replaces normal ADAMTS13. Mortality WITHOUT treatment: >90%. WITH treatment: ~10–20%.

Assessment & Management of Hematologic Emergencies

Hematologic patients often arrive to EMS knowing more about their disease than anyone else in the room. Respect that knowledge, perform a systematic assessment, and provide expert supportive care on the way to definitive treatment.

Scene Safety & Standard Precautions

Many hematologic patients are severely immunocompromised (chemotherapy, bone marrow transplant, functional asplenia). Standard precautions protect both the patient AND you. Use PPE not just against blood — but to protect the patient from YOU. A simple respiratory illness can be life-threatening for a patient with a WBC of 500.

⚠️ Reverse Isolation Consideration
For profoundly immunocompromised patients (neutrophil count <500/μL), consider reverse isolation precautions — mask the patient for transport to prevent hospital-acquired infection. Notify receiving hospital so they can place the patient in a negative-pressure or isolation room immediately.

Specifically ask ALL hematologic patients about: recent dizziness or syncope, difficulty breathing, palpitations or irregular heartbeat, and new or worsening pain. These may indicate anemia-related cardiovascular decompensation.
