Endocrinology

The endocrine system is the body's silent chemical messenger network — a collection of ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream to regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, and response to stress. Dysfunction anywhere in this system can produce life-threatening emergencies demanding rapid field assessment and intervention.

Diabetes Mellitus DKA HHS Thyroid Storm Myxedema Coma Cushing Syndrome Addison Disease

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, the paramedic student will be able to:

  • Describe how hormones maintain homeostasis through feedback mechanisms
  • Explain pancreatic anatomy and glucose regulation
  • Identify and manage hypoglycemia, DKA, and HHS in the field
  • Recognize thyroid emergencies including thyroid storm and myxedema coma
  • Describe Cushing syndrome and Addison disease and their prehospital implications

The Big Picture — Feedback Loops

🔁 Analogy — Thermostat System

Think of hormonal feedback loops like a home thermostat. Negative feedback is the normal thermostat: when blood glucose rises, insulin is released to bring it back down. When glucose normalizes, insulin secretion stops. Positive feedback pushes the system further from normal — like a car alarm that keeps blaring louder instead of stopping. Most endocrine regulation uses negative feedback to maintain homeostasis.

🔵 Negative Feedback

Goal: restore homeostasis.
High blood glucose → pancreas secretes insulin → glucose enters cells → blood glucose falls → insulin secretion stops. Self-correcting loop.

🔴 Positive Feedback

Goal: amplify a response.
Less common in endocrinology. Example: oxytocin during labor. The stimulus continues until an endpoint is reached. Can become pathologic.

Anatomy & Physiology

The Pancreas & Glucose Regulation

The pancreas serves a dual role: exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine (blood glucose regulation). Its endocrine function through the Islets of Langerhans is central to every diabetic emergency paramedics encounter.

Islets of Langerhans — Cell Types

🏭 Analogy — The Chemical Factory

Think of the pancreas as a chemical manufacturing facility. The Islets of Langerhans are special "production departments" within the factory — each team (cell type) produces a specific hormone. The beta cell team makes insulin (the key to open cell doors), the alpha cell team makes glucagon (emergency fuel release), and the delta cell team acts as a foreman, keeping both teams in check so glucose doesn't swing wildly.

🟡 Beta Cells → Insulin

Released when blood glucose rises. Allows glucose to enter cells. Lowers blood glucose. Stored in liver as glycogen. Antagonized by glucagon and growth hormone.

🔵 Alpha Cells → Glucagon

Released when blood glucose falls. Triggers glycogenolysis (break down stored glycogen) and gluconeogenesis (make new glucose from fats/amino acids). Raises blood glucose.

🟣 Delta Cells → Somatostatin

The "modulator." Inhibits both insulin and glucagon secretion. Prevents rapid swings in blood glucose. Also inhibits growth hormone and TSH from the pituitary.

Insulin — The Master Key

🔑 Analogy — Lock & Key

Glucose is like fuel that needs to enter a locked car. Insulin is the key that unlocks the car door (cell membrane receptor). Without the key, the fuel just sits outside — circulating in the blood — and the car (cell) runs out of energy. In Type 1 diabetes, there are no keys. In Type 2, the locks are broken (insulin resistance) or there aren't enough keys being made.

Insulin's Four Major Functions

↑ Glucose Transport Into Cells

Insulin binds cell receptors, opening glucose channels (GLUT-4). Without insulin, cells cannot absorb glucose from circulation.

↑ Glucose Metabolism

Increases the rate at which cells burn glucose for energy (glycolysis). Promotes efficient energy production.

↑ Liver Glycogen Storage

Signals liver and muscles to store excess glucose as glycogen for future short-term energy needs.

↓ Blood Glucose

Net effect: removes glucose from circulation → serum glucose falls → negative feedback stops more insulin release.

Diabetic Emergency

Hypoglycemia

Blood glucose below 70 mg/dL. The brain runs out of fuel — rapidly producing neurological signs that can mimic intoxication, psychiatric emergencies, or stroke. Time-sensitive: untreated severe hypoglycemia causes irreversible brain damage and cardiac arrest.

<70 mg/dL Minutes to Onset 8th Leading Cause of Death
⛽ Analogy — Car Running Out of Gas

Imagine the brain as a high-performance engine that only runs on one fuel: glucose. It can't switch to diesel (fat), unlike muscles. When blood glucose drops below critical levels, the engine starts sputtering — first strange behavior (the dashboard warning lights), then stalling (seizure or coma). The body releases epinephrine as a "distress signal," producing the classic adrenergic signs. Every minute of severe hypoglycemia is neurological damage accumulating — treat it like a code.

1

Insulin Excess / Glucose Deficit

Too much insulin (injection, oral agent), too little food, excess exercise, or alcohol depleting glycogen stores. Blood glucose falls below 70 mg/dL.

2

Adrenergic Compensatory Response

Body senses glucose deprivation → releases epinephrine and glucagon as emergency hormones. This causes trembling, diaphoresis, pallor, tachycardia, and anxiety.

3

Neuroglycopenia

Brain cells starved of glucose → altered cognition, confusion, slurred speech, bizarre behavior. Patient may appear intoxicated. Beta-blockers can mask adrenergic signs!

4

Seizure, Coma, Cardiac Arrest

If untreated: seizures occur as neurons fire erratically without fuel. Complete neurological shutdown leads to coma. Profound hypoglycemia triggers fatal dysrhythmias.

🚨
Signs appear within minutes. Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) mask the adrenergic signs — patient on beta-blockers may only show neurological signs with NO sweating or tachycardia. Always check glucose!

1

Assess Blood Glucose with Glucometer

Always check glucose before administering glucose. Confirm hypoglycemia (<70 mg/dL). Document reading.

2A

Alert & Able to Swallow → Oral Glucose

15–20g of fast-acting carbohydrate: glucose gel, OJ, regular soda, glucose tablets. "15-15 rule": give 15g, recheck in 15 min.

2B

D50W: 25g (50 mL) IVP slow. D10W preferred for pediatric/elderly. Repeat per protocol if glucose remains low. Glucagon IM/IN if no IV access (1 mg adult).

3

Some patients (simple insulin error, alert, can eat) may be treated on scene. Sulfonylurea OD patients MUST be transported — rebound hypoglycemia risk. Document everything.

📋
Field Tip: After glucose administration, have the patient eat a complex carbohydrate meal (crackers, cheese, bread) to prevent rebound hypoglycemia. The IV dextrose wears off — food provides sustained glucose.

Diabetic Emergency

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

An absence of or resistance to insulin forces the body to burn fat for fuel, producing acidic ketone bodies. The classic triad: hyperglycemia + acidosis + ketonemia. Predominantly a Type 1 emergency but can occur in Type 2 under extreme stress.

Type 1 Primarily 12–48 Hour Onset Fruity Breath Kussmaul Respirations
🏚️ Analogy — The Empty House with Full Warehouse

Imagine cells as houses that need food (glucose) to function. The city streets (bloodstream) are flooded with grocery trucks (glucose), but the doors to every house are locked — because there's no key (insulin). The families inside are starving despite abundance outside. In desperation, they start burning the furniture (fat) for warmth. The smoke from burning fat (ketones) fills the neighborhood, making everything acidic. Meanwhile, the flooded streets (hyperglycemia) create an osmotic effect, pulling water out of every house (dehydration). Chaos.

1

No Insulin / Insulin Resistance

Trigger: missed insulin dose, infection, stress, new-onset T1DM. Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells.

2

Hyperglycemia Develops

Glucose accumulates in blood. Glucagon, epinephrine, cortisol, and GH increase glucose production further (stress hormones antagonize insulin effects).

3

Fat Catabolism → Ketone Formation

Starving cells burn fatty acids. Liver converts fatty acids into ketone bodies: acetone, acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate. Ketones are acids → metabolic acidosis.

4

Osmotic Diuresis → Dehydration

High blood glucose exceeds kidney reabsorption threshold → glucose spills into urine → osmotic diuresis → massive fluid and electrolyte losses (esp. Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺).

5

Compensatory Kussmaul Respirations

Body attempts to blow off CO₂ to compensate for metabolic acidosis. Results in deep, labored, rapid breathing — classic Kussmaul pattern. Acetone on breath = "fruity odor."

🩺 Cardinal Signs & Symptoms

  • Polyuria (excessive urination)
  • Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
  • Polyphagia (excessive hunger)
  • Fruity/acetone breath
  • Kussmaul respirations
  • Warm, dry skin; dry mucous membranes
  • Tachycardia, thready pulse
  • Postural hypotension
  • Weight loss
  • Abdominal pain, N/V/D
  • Gradual altered mental status

🔬 Lab Values (In-Hospital)

  • Blood glucose: typically 250–600 mg/dL
  • pH <7.3 (metabolic acidosis)
  • Bicarbonate <15 mEq/L
  • Positive ketones (blood/urine)
  • Elevated BUN/creatinine (dehydration)
  • K⁺ initially HIGH (shifts out of cells) then drops with treatment
  • Na⁺ falsely LOW (diluted)
⚠️
Kussmaul Respirations vs. Hyperventilation: Kussmaul respirations are deep, regular, and labored — not fast and shallow like anxiety-related hyperventilation. The depth is the distinguishing feature. This is the body's attempt to eliminate carbonic acid via CO₂.
🏥
Prehospital Role: Definitive DKA treatment (insulin infusion, electrolyte replacement, bicarbonate) is hospital-based. Your job: recognize it, establish IV access, begin fluid resuscitation, monitor, and transport rapidly.

🚑 Prehospital Management

Airway / Breathing
Support if Kussmaul respirations are insufficient. Monitor SpO₂. Consider intubation if deteriorating.

IV Access + Fluids
Large-bore IV × 2. Begin NS (normal saline) — initial bolus 500–1000 mL. Corrects dehydration, dilutes glucose.

Cardiac Monitoring
Watch for dysrhythmias from hyperkalemia (peaked T-waves, wide QRS). K⁺ shifts dramatically during treatment — dysrhythmia risk is REAL.

Do NOT Give Glucose!
Glucose contraindicated in hyperglycemia. Check BGL — if unsure, check twice. Document and transport.

🏥 Definitive (In-Hospital) Treatment

  • Regular insulin IV infusion (0.1 units/kg/hr)
  • Aggressive IV fluid replacement (3–6L in 24h)
  • Electrolyte replacement (especially K⁺ — K⁺ falls with insulin treatment!)
  • Identify and treat precipitating cause (infection, MI, etc.)
  • Continuous glucose, electrolyte, and ABG monitoring

💊 Medication Issues

  • Missed insulin doses
  • Inadequate insulin dose
  • Pump failure/occlusion
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors (euglycemic DKA)

🦠 Infection / Illness

  • Infection (most common trigger)
  • Pneumonia, UTI, cellulitis
  • COVID-19 (new T1DM trigger)
  • Acute pancreatitis

⚡ Physiological Stress

  • MI / stroke / trauma
  • Surgery
  • Pregnancy
  • Alcohol / cocaine / olanzapine
  • Emotional stress
🦠 Why Infection Causes DKA

Infection causes the body to release massive amounts of counter-regulatory hormones (cortisol, glucagon, epinephrine). These hormones antagonize insulin — even if the patient took their insulin dose, illness can overwhelm it. This is why diabetic patients deteriorate rapidly during infections and why "sick day rules" for insulin management exist.

Diabetic Emergency

Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

Extreme hyperglycemia (often >600 mg/dL) without significant ketoacidosis. The blood becomes so concentrated it pulls water from brain cells. Predominantly affects older Type 2 diabetics. Insidious onset over days to weeks. High mortality — up to 15%.

Type 2 / Elderly Glucose >600 mg/dL No Ketoacidosis 15% Mortality
🧂 Analogy — The Over-Salted Ocean

HHS is like soaking a raisin in saltwater — the raisin (your cells) shrinks as water is pulled out by osmosis. The blood becomes so thick and concentrated (hyperosmolar) from sky-high glucose that water is literally drawn out of every cell, including brain cells. Unlike DKA, there's enough residual insulin to prevent fat breakdown and ketone production — but not enough to keep glucose under control. The result is a severely dehydrated patient with a glucose level so high, the blood is nearly syrup. The brain doesn't function in concentrated brine.

Signs & Symptoms

🔵 Early Signs

  • Polyuria, polydipsia (days to weeks)
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Dry mucous membranes
  • Tachycardia
  • Lethargy, confusion
  • Gradual weakness

🔴 Late / Severe Signs

  • Profound dehydration (skin tenting)
  • Altered mental status → coma
  • Seizures (focal or generalized)
  • Hypotension / shock
  • Tachycardia
  • No Kussmaul, no fruity breath

Prehospital Management

🚑 HHS Field Treatment

  • Large-bore IV access × 2 — these patients are profoundly dehydrated
  • NS fluid resuscitation — aggressive but controlled; target 1L/hr initially
  • Cardiac monitoring — dysrhythmia risk from electrolyte derangement
  • Airway management — protect airway in obtunded patient
  • Do NOT give insulin in the field — too rapid glucose correction can cause cerebral edema
  • Rapid transport to appropriate facility
🚨
Neurological Complications: HHS can cause focal neurological deficits mimicking stroke. Do not assume these patients have had a stroke without checking blood glucose first. BGL >600 can look exactly like a right MCA stroke. The glucose level tells the story.
Thyroid Emergencies

Thyrotoxicosis & Thyroid Storm

The thyroid gland, a butterfly-shaped structure at the base of the neck, is the body's metabolic throttle. Hyperthyroidism keeps the engine revving too fast — and thyroid storm is when the engine blows. This is an adrenergic hyperdrive state demanding immediate intervention.

Graves Disease Life-Threatening Adrenergic Excess
⚡ Analogy — Stuck Accelerator

The thyroid gland is like the accelerator pedal for your body's metabolism. Normally, it receives precise signals from the pituitary (via TSH) to give just the right amount of gas. In Graves disease, rogue antibodies (TSI) act as a stuck accelerator — pressing it to the floor regardless of what the pituitary signals. The engine (metabolism) races: heart beats faster, temperature soars, thoughts race, weight drops. Thyroid storm is when the stuck pedal causes the engine to seize — a full system crisis from adrenergic overdrive that can kill within hours.

🟢 Normal Thyroid Function

Hypothalamus releases TRH → Pituitary releases TSH → Thyroid releases T3/T4. Negative feedback: high T3/T4 suppresses TSH. Regulates metabolic rate, body temperature, heart rate, mood.

🔴 Hyperthyroidism

Excess T3/T4 production. Most common cause: Graves disease (autoimmune, TSI antibodies mimic TSH). Also: toxic goiter, thyroiditis, excess iodine, thyroid adenoma.

🚨
Thyroid storm carries a mortality rate of 10–30% even with treatment. It is precipitated by stress: surgery, trauma, infection, iodine contrast, MI, pregnancy. Most cases occur in patients with undiagnosed or undertreated hyperthyroidism.

🔴 Signs & Symptoms — Thyroid Storm

  • Hyperthermia (fever >104°F / 40°C)
  • Severe tachycardia (HR >140, Afib common)
  • Heart failure (high-output cardiac failure)
  • Cardiac dysrhythmias
  • Shock (hypotension despite tachycardia)
  • Restlessness, agitation, paranoia
  • Delirium → coma
  • Abdominal pain, diarrhea

⚡ Why All These Symptoms?

Thyroid hormones amplify adrenergic receptors. Excess T3/T4 = body responds to its own epinephrine as if it's receiving a constant stress dose.

  • ↑ HR → cardiac output overwhelms heart → failure
  • ↑ Metabolic rate → generates heat → hyperthermia
  • ↑ CNS excitability → agitation, psychosis, seizure
  • ↑ GI motility → diarrhea, vomiting

🚑 Prehospital Management — Thyroid Storm

  • ABC Management: Airway protection, supplemental O₂
  • Rapid transport — this is a hospital emergency
  • Cooling: Remove clothing, cool packs to axilla/groin/neck, fan, cool IV fluids
  • IV access for fluid replacement and drug administration
  • Cardiac monitoring — treat Afib, tachyarrhythmias per protocol
  • DO NOT give aspirin — displaces thyroid hormone from proteins, worsening storm

🏥 In-Hospital Management

  • Propylthiouracil (PTU) or Methimazole — block new hormone synthesis
  • Potassium iodide — blocks thyroid hormone release (give AFTER PTU!)
  • Beta-blockers (propranolol) — control adrenergic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor)
  • Corticosteroids (dexamethasone) — reduce peripheral T4→T3 conversion
  • Acetaminophen for fever (NOT aspirin!)
⚠️
Aspirin is Contraindicated in Thyroid Storm: Salicylates displace thyroid hormones from their protein carriers (thyroid binding globulin), suddenly increasing free (active) T3/T4 and worsening the crisis. Use acetaminophen only.

Thyroid Emergency

Myxedema & Myxedema Coma

The opposite of thyroid storm — the body's engine has almost completely stalled. Profound hypothyroidism causes accumulation of mucinous proteins in skin and tissues, leading to the classic "myxedema" appearance. Myxedema coma is a rare but life-threatening emergency.

Hypothyroidism Women 40+ Gradual Onset Hypothermia
🐌 Analogy — The Slowing Engine

If thyroid storm is a stuck accelerator, myxedema is the engine slowly dying from no fuel. Everything slows: heart rate drops, metabolism crawls, the brain moves in slow motion. The body becomes swollen as mucinous proteins accumulate under the skin (like a waterlogged mattress). Myxedema coma is when the engine shuts off almost completely — the patient is hypothermic, bradycardic, and barely conscious. A single trigger (cold exposure, infection, sedative medication) can push a poorly controlled hypothyroid patient over the edge.

Myxedema: Signs & Symptoms Progression

🧊 Clinical Features of Myxedema

  • Facial edema (periorbital puffiness)
  • Cool, dry, thickened skin
  • Coarse, brittle hair and nails
  • Sensitivity to cold
  • Bradycardia
  • Weight gain
  • Weakness and fatigue
  • Depression, slowed speech/thought
  • Constipation
  • Low blood pressure

❄️ Myxedema Coma — Precipitants

  • Exposure to cold (most common)
  • Infection (especially pulmonary)
  • Heart failure
  • Trauma or surgery
  • Sedatives, hypnotics, anesthetics
  • Stroke
  • Internal hemorrhage
  • Hypoxia or hypercapnia
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hypoglycemia

Prehospital Management

🚑 Myxedema Coma — Field Treatment

  • Airway management — intubation may be required (hypercapnic respiratory failure)
  • Ventilatory support as needed (CO₂ retention common)
  • Passive warming — blankets, warm environment, warm IV fluids. Avoid aggressive external warming (peripheral vasodilation can cause cardiovascular collapse)
  • IV access — treat hypoglycemia if present (BGL check!)
  • Cardiac monitoring — expect bradycardia; watch for heart blocks
  • Rapid transport — definitive treatment is IV levothyroxine (in-hospital)
💊
Medication Caution in Myxedema: Hypothyroid patients metabolize drugs extremely slowly. Normal doses of sedatives, opioids, and anesthetics can cause profound and prolonged respiratory depression. Reduce doses significantly and monitor closely.
Adrenal Gland Disorders

Cushing Syndrome

Caused by chronically elevated cortisol levels. Cortisol, the body's primary stress hormone, in excess becomes destructive — redistributing fat, weakening muscles, raising blood pressure, elevating glucose, and destabilizing mood. Predominantly affects women aged 30–50.

Cortisol Excess Women 30–50 Moon Face / Buffalo Hump Rare Condition
⚗️ Analogy — Too Much of a Good Thing

Cortisol is like fertilizer — a little bit makes things grow and flourish. Your stress response needs it to mobilize energy, reduce inflammation, and maintain blood pressure under pressure. But too much fertilizer burns the garden. Excess cortisol for months or years redistributes fat to the trunk and face ("moon face," "buffalo hump"), breaks down muscle protein, causes hyperglycemia (cortisol is anti-insulin), weakens bones, and turns the immune system against itself. The patient looks like they've been on high-dose steroids — because essentially, they have been.

Causes of Cushing Syndrome

💊 Exogenous (Most Common)

Iatrogenic from long-term corticosteroid use (prednisone, dexamethasone) for conditions like asthma, RA, IBD, organ transplant. This is the #1 cause you'll see in practice.

🧠 Cushing's Disease

Pituitary adenoma secreting excessive ACTH → stimulates adrenal cortex to overproduce cortisol. Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. Treated with pituitary surgery.

🦠 Ectopic ACTH

Non-pituitary tumor (small cell lung cancer, carcinoid) secreting ACTH. Often rapid onset with severe hypokalemia. Poor prognosis.

Signs & Symptoms

👤 Characteristic Appearance

  • Moon face — round, plethoric facial fullness
  • Buffalo hump — fat pad on posterior neck
  • Supraclavicular fat pads
  • Central obesity (truncal) with thin limbs
  • Purple/violaceous striae (stretch marks)
  • Acne
  • Easy bruising, thin skin
  • Increased body/facial hair (hirsutism)

⚕️ Systemic Effects

  • Hypertension (cortisol causes Na/water retention)
  • Hyperglycemia / diabetes mellitus
  • Osteoporosis (cortisol inhibits osteoblasts)
  • Muscle wasting and weakness
  • Psychiatric disturbances (depression, paranoia)
  • Insomnia
  • Decreased immune function
  • Amenorrhea (females), erectile dysfunction (males)

Prehospital Management & Medications

🚑 Prehospital Role — Mainly Supportive

Cushing syndrome itself is rarely an acute prehospital emergency. Your role is to recognize it, manage associated complications (HTN crisis, hyperglycemia, adrenal crisis from sudden steroid withdrawal), and facilitate appropriate referral.

⚠️
Steroid Withdrawal Risk: Patients taking long-term exogenous corticosteroids may develop iatrogenic Cushing's — AND if they suddenly stop taking steroids, they will develop adrenal crisis (see Addison Disease). Never abruptly stop steroids in these patients.

Adrenal Gland Disorders

Addison Disease & Adrenal Crisis

The opposite of Cushing — Addison disease is a deficiency of cortisol and aldosterone. Without these hormones, the body cannot maintain vascular tone, electrolyte balance, or stress responses. Adrenal crisis is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate corticosteroid replacement.

Cortisol Deficiency Aldosterone Deficiency Skin Hyperpigmentation Adrenal Crisis
🏗️ Analogy — No Safety Net

Cortisol and aldosterone are your body's emergency support structures. Cortisol is the crisis manager — it mobilizes glucose during stress, maintains vascular tone, and modulates inflammation. Aldosterone is the salt-keeper, preventing sodium from escaping in urine. Without these two, every physiological stressor — infection, trauma, surgery, even emotional distress — becomes catastrophic. The body has no safety net. Sodium pours out (hyponatremia), potassium builds up (hyperkalemia), blood pressure collapses, and glucose drops. A sick Addison patient without their steroid replacement can go from walking to shock within hours.

🔴 Cortisol Deficiency Effects

  • Inability to mount stress response
  • Hypoglycemia (no gluconeogenesis)
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Hypotension (cortisol maintains vascular sensitivity to catecholamines)
  • GI disturbances (N/V/D)
  • Pituitary ↑ ACTH → ↑ melanin → skin hyperpigmentation

🟠 Aldosterone Deficiency Effects

  • Salt wasting (↓ Na⁺ reabsorption)
  • Hyponatremia → weakness, confusion
  • Hyperkalemia → dysrhythmia risk!
  • Volume depletion → hypotension
  • Craving for salty foods (classic sign)

Types of Adrenal Insufficiency

Primary (Addison Disease)

Adrenal cortex itself is destroyed. Causes: autoimmune (70%), tuberculosis, metastatic cancer, bilateral adrenalectomy, hemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome from meningococcemia). Both cortisol AND aldosterone deficient.

Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency

Adrenal cortex is intact but pituitary produces insufficient ACTH. Most common cause: abrupt discontinuation of exogenous glucocorticoids (suppresses pituitary ACTH). Only cortisol deficient — aldosterone usually intact (regulated by renin, not ACTH).

⬇️ Classic Signs & Symptoms

  • Progressive weakness (profound fatigue)
  • Progressive weight loss
  • Anorexia
  • Skin hyperpigmentation — even in sun-unexposed areas (palmar creases, buccal mucosa, scars). Caused by excess ACTH stimulating MSH production.
  • Hypotension / orthostatic hypotension
  • Hyponatremia → confusion, weakness
  • Hyperkalemia → peaked T-waves, dysrhythmias
  • Hypoglycemia
  • GI disturbances (N/V/D, abdominal pain)
  • Salt craving

🎨
Skin Hyperpigmentation — The Diagnostic Clue: When cortisol levels fall, the pituitary pumps out massive amounts of ACTH to try to stimulate the adrenal gland. ACTH shares a precursor (POMC) with MSH (melanocyte-stimulating hormone). Excess ACTH cross-activates MSH receptors → paradoxically dark skin even in areas never exposed to the sun. This is pathognomonic for PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency — not seen in secondary.
🚨
Adrenal Crisis = Cardiovascular Collapse. This is a true emergency. Cortisol is required for vessels to respond to catecholamines — without it, epinephrine and norepinephrine cannot maintain vascular tone. No cortisol = fluid resuscitation alone will not fix the hypotension. Must give steroids.

⚡ Precipitants of Adrenal Crisis

  • Infection or sepsis
  • Physiologic or emotional stress
  • Trauma or surgery
  • Sudden discontinuation of corticosteroids
  • Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage
  • MI or stroke
  • Failure to double/triple steroid dose when sick

🩺 Signs of Adrenal Crisis

  • Severe hypotension / shock
  • Acute abdominal pain
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Fever
  • Altered mental status
  • Hyperkalemia → dysrhythmias
  • Profound weakness, collapse

🚑 Prehospital Management — Adrenal Crisis

  • IV access × 2 — large bore
  • Aggressive NS bolus — 1–2L (fluid replacement for volume depletion)
  • Dextrose if hypoglycemic (check BGL!)
  • Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV if available per protocol — this is the definitive prehospital treatment
  • Cardiac monitoring — hyperkalemia dysrhythmia risk
  • Rapid transport — hospital steroids, electrolyte correction

Adrenal Insufficiency Medications

GLUCOCORTICOID
Hydrocortisone
Solu-Cortef, Cortef
Gold standard for adrenal crisis. 100 mg IV immediately. Has both glucocorticoid AND mineralocorticoid activity. Daily oral replacement: 15–25 mg/day split doses.
GLUCOCORTICOID
Dexamethasone
Decadron
4 mg IV if hydrocortisone unavailable. Doesn't interfere with cortisol stimulation test (ACTH stim) — useful if diagnosis uncertain. No mineralocorticoid activity.
MINERALOCORTICOID
Fludrocortisone
Florinef
Oral mineralocorticoid replacement. Replaces aldosterone function — retains sodium, excretes potassium. Essential for primary adrenal insufficiency.
