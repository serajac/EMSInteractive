Section 01
🧠 Foundation

Anatomy & Physiology of the Nervous System

The master control system of the body — coordinating everything from a heartbeat to a thought. Understanding the blueprint is essential before diagnosing what's broken.

🔌 Big Picture Analogy

"Think of the nervous system as the internet infrastructure of the body. The brain is the data center, the spinal cord is the fiber-optic backbone, and the peripheral nerves are the last-mile cables delivering signals to every home (organ). The CNS is the protected server room — encased in bone, it's your most hardened infrastructure. The PNS is everything outside that building."

🔬 The Neuron — Basic Unit of the Nervous System

Every neuron has three key parts: the cell body (with a large nucleus — the "brain" of the neuron), dendrites (branching projections that receive signals — like antenna arrays), and an axon (the long single projection that sends signals — like a fiber-optic cable).

Sensory (Afferent)

Carry signals TO brain/cord from the body. React to touch, sound, pain, light.

Motor (Efferent)

Carry signals AWAY from brain/cord. Stimulate muscle contraction and gland activity.

Interneurons

Connect neurons within the CNS. Process and relay information between sensory and motor neurons.

⚡ Impulse Transmission & Myelination

Neurons in resting state are positively charged outside, negatively charged inside. When stimulated, an action potential depolarizes the membrane and travels down the axon.

🏎 Myelination Analogy

"Unmyelinated axons are like a dirt road — signals travel slowly, every inch. Myelinated axons have a superhighway system with nodes of Ranvier as off-ramps, letting signals leap from node to node (saltatory conduction). This is why demyelinating diseases like MS are so devastating — it's like tearing up the highway and replacing it with gravel."

🔗 Synapses — Where Neurons Talk

A synapse is the junction between neurons: presynaptic terminal → synaptic cleft → postsynaptic membrane. Synaptic vesicles in the presynaptic terminal release neurotransmitters into the cleft, which bind to receptors and continue the signal.

💡Key neurotransmitters: Dopamine (movement, reward), Acetylcholine (neuromuscular junction), Serotonin (mood, vasoconstriction), GABA (inhibitory — calms neural activity), Glutamate (excitatory). Imbalances in these drive many neurologic diseases.
🩸 Cerebrovascular Supply

The brain receives blood from two sources: the vertebral arteries (entering via foramen magnum, supplying cerebellum, uniting to form the basilar artery) and the internal carotid arteries (entering via carotid canals, giving rise to anterior cerebral arteries supplying frontal lobes).

⭕Circle of Willis — The brain's built-in redundancy system. Anterior cerebral arteries connect via the anterior communicating artery, forming a ring that ensures collateral flow if one vessel is blocked. However, distal vessels deep in the brain have no collateral circulation — occlusion there means infarction.

⚡ Critical Concepts

Neurologic Pathophysiology

What happens when pressure builds, blood flow fails, or the brain is starved of oxygen and glucose. These are the mechanisms that kill neurons — understand them and you understand your interventions.

20%of body O₂ used by brain
25%of body glucose used by brain
60–80Normal CPP (mmHg)
5–15Normal ICP (mmHg)
🧲 The Rigid Container Analogy (Monro-Kellie Doctrine)

"Picture your skull as a sealed, rigid water balloon filled to capacity with three things: brain tissue (80%), blood (10%), and CSF (10%). If you add more of anything — tumor, bleed, edema — something else must leave, or pressure rises. When ICP climbs high enough, it compresses brain tissue and shuts off blood flow. This is the Monro-Kellie Doctrine: the total intracranial volume is fixed."

🧮 The CPP Formula — The Number That Matters
CPP = MAP − ICP
Cerebral Perfusion Pressure = Mean Arterial Pressure − Intracranial Pressure

CPP 60–80 mmHg = Normal. CBF is autoregulated when CPP is 50–160 mmHg.

If CPP drops below 50 mmHg → cerebral blood flow critically declines → neurons starve and die.

If ICP rises to match MAP → CPP = 0 → brain death.

⚠️Cushing's Reflex (Triad): Late sign of critically elevated ICP. Hypertension + Bradycardia + Irregular respirations. The body raises BP to try to perfuse the brain. This is a pre-herniation emergency — act immediately.
📉 The Cascade of Elevated ICP
Stage 1
ICP begins to rise → CSF displaced from cranial vault to spinal subarachnoid space (compensation)
Stage 2
Venous blood displaced from cranial vault → MAP rises to maintain CPP
Stage 3
Compensatory mechanisms fail → CPP falls → cerebral vasodilation → more blood volume → more ICP (vicious cycle)
Stage 4 — HERNIATION
Brain tissue shifts through tentorium or foramen magnum → death without immediate intervention

💊 Prehospital ICP Management Principles
🔬 ICP-Related Medications (Hospital Context — Know Mechanism)
Mannitol (Osmitrol)
Osmotic diuretic. Pulls fluid out of brain tissue into bloodstream via osmosis. Temporarily reduces ICP. Used in-hospital for herniation syndromes.
Hypertonic Saline (3%)
Creates osmotic gradient, drawing water from edematous brain cells. Often preferred over mannitol in trauma. May be used prehospital per protocol.
Dexamethasone (Decadron)
Corticosteroid. Reduces vasogenic edema (especially around tumors). NOT recommended for traumatic brain injury — only for tumor-related or infectious edema.
💡Prehospital Rule: Target SpO₂ ≥94%, ETCO₂ 35–40 mmHg. Hyperventilation temporarily reduces CO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → brief ICP reduction. Only used for herniation signs. Chronic hyperventilation causes dangerous cerebral ischemia.

⚠️GCS ≤8 = Severe TBI. Consider advanced airway. GCS 9–12 = Moderate. GCS 13–15 = Minor. Always document the individual component scores (e.g., E3V4M5 = 12) not just the total.
🏋 Abnormal Posturing — Severity Spectrum

Decorticate Posturing

Arms flex at elbows + adduct to chest, legs extend. Damage to thalamus or cerebral hemispheres. Cortex is disconnected — midbrain still working.

Memory trick: "deCORTicate = CORe arms — hugging inward"

Decerebrate Posturing

Arms extend + internally rotate, legs extend. Implies cerebral AND midbrain dysfunction. More ominous — brainstem is failing.

Memory trick: "deCEREBRate = extends outward like a rigid soldier"

⛔Flaccidity (no tone, no posturing) usually indicates brainstem or spinal cord dysfunction — worst prognosis. The brain has stopped sending any motor commands.

💡 Pupil Rule of Thumb

"Equal pupils = equal blame on both sides (toxic/metabolic). Unequal pupils = one side is being compressed (structural). Think of it as: if both eyes are equally affected, the cause is systemic (a bad drug or low sugar). If one eye is different, something physical is pressing on one side of the brain."

💤 Altered LOC

Coma

An abnormally deep state of unconsciousness — the patient cannot be aroused by external verbal or physical stimuli. Requires systematic, rapid cause identification.

💻 The Crashed Computer Analogy

"Coma is like a computer that won't boot. The hardware might be physically damaged (structural coma — tumor, bleed, herniation) or it might be running the wrong software or missing fuel (toxic/metabolic coma — low glucose, opioids, electrolyte chaos). Your first job is figuring out which type it is, because the fix is completely different. You don't treat a hardware crash with a software patch."

💊 Prehospital Coma Reversal Medications
Dextrose (D50W / D10W)
Mechanism: Replaces glucose substrate. If coma is from hypoglycemia, glucose is the literal "fuel" the brain needs. Always check BGL first — D50 in a euglycemic patient is not harmful short-term but unnecessary. In pediatrics, use D10W or D25W. Dose: 25g D50W IV (1 amp) for documented hypoglycemia.
Naloxone (Narcan)
Mechanism: Competitive opioid receptor antagonist (mu, kappa, delta). Displaces opioids from receptors. Watch for acute withdrawal/agitation. Short half-life — patient may re-narcotize before naloxone wears off. Dose: 0.4–2mg IV/IM/IN; infusion may be needed.
Thiamine (Vitamin B1)
Mechanism: Essential cofactor for glucose metabolism in neurons. Without thiamine, dextrose administration can precipitate Wernicke's encephalopathy (ataxia, ophthalmoplegia, confusion). Always give thiamine before or with dextrose in alcoholic/malnourished patients. Dose: 100mg IV/IM.
Flumazenil (Romazicon)
Mechanism: Benzodiazepine antagonist. Reverses benzo-induced coma. Caution: May precipitate seizures in chronic benzo users/poly-drug overdose. Use selectively and per protocol. Short-acting — re-sedation is possible.
🚑 Prehospital Management Priorities
Priority 1
Airway — unconscious patients cannot protect their airway. Consider advanced airway if GCS ≤8 or inability to maintain patency.
Priority 2
Ventilation — supplemental O₂, avoid hyperventilation unless herniation signs present. Target SpO₂ ≥94%, ETCO₂ 35–40 mmHg.
Priority 3
BGL — rapid glucose check. Hypoglycemia is the most easily reversible cause of coma. Treat immediately if <60 mg/dL.
Priority 4
IV access — for medication administration and fluid resuscitation. Do NOT delay transport for IV access in stroke or critical patients.
Priority 5
Consider reversible causes — naloxone for suspected opioid overdose, thiamine + dextrose for hypoglycemia in malnourished patients.
📋 Case Study
You respond to a 58-year-old male found unresponsive by his wife at 0700. He was fine at 2300 the night before. He is a known alcoholic with DM. GCS = 6 (E1V2M3). Skin is cool, diaphoretic. Pupils equal and reactive. RR = 8, irregular. BP = 110/70. BGL = 28 mg/dL.
What is your most likely diagnosis and immediate treatment sequence?
Diagnosis: Hypoglycemic coma (toxic/metabolic — symmetric signs, equal pupils, gradual onset).

Treatment sequence:
1. Open/position airway, consider NPA or OPA
2. BVM ventilation — RR of 8 is inadequate
3. Thiamine 100mg IV (alcoholic patient — give BEFORE glucose)
4. D50W 25g IV → reassess BGL and LOC every 5 min
5. Establish second IV, continuous monitoring
6. Transport — even with glucose correction, determine why BGL was so low at night

Key point: Symmetric exam + equal pupils + BGL = toxic/metabolic, not structural stroke. Hypoglycemia can perfectly mimic stroke — always check the glucose!

🩸 Time-Critical Emergency

Stroke & Intracranial Hemorrhage

1.9 million neurons die every minute a large stroke goes untreated. The 5th leading cause of death in the US. Your speed from dispatch to door defines survival and functional outcome.

87%Ischemic strokes
13%Hemorrhagic strokes
4.5 hrstPA window (ischemic)
20%TIA → stroke within days
🐟 The "Fish Without Water" Analogy

"Brain cells are like fish in a tank — they need oxygenated blood (water) to survive. In an ischemic stroke, the pump breaks and water stops flowing. There's a dead zone (infarct core — neurons already dead) surrounded by a gasping zone (penumbra — neurons that are struggling but ALIVE if you restore flow fast enough). Your job is to get that patient to a facility where the pump can be restarted before the gasping zone also dies. Every minute = 1.9 million neurons lost."

🔴 Types of Stroke

Ischemic Stroke (87%)

Thrombotic: Atherosclerotic plaque ruptures, clot forms in-situ in cerebral vessel. Associated with long history of vascular disease (HTN, DM, smoking).

Embolic: Clot travels from elsewhere (heart — A-fib; carotid plaque) and lodges in cerebral vessel.

Large Vessel Occlusion (LVO): Major cerebral vessel involved → higher mortality, catastrophic deficit. Requires thrombectomy, not just tPA.

Hemorrhagic Stroke (13%)

Intracerebral hemorrhage: Vessel ruptures within brain parenchyma — most common cause is chronic hypertension (weakens vessel walls).

Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH): Often from ruptured aneurysm or AVM. Classically: "worst headache of my life" — sudden, thunderclap onset.

Key: tPA is contraindicated in hemorrhagic stroke. This is why CT must precede fibrinolytics.

⏱ The 8 Ds of Stroke Management
8 Ds of Stroke Chain of Survival

DData — CT scan ≤25 min, results ≤45 min

📏 Prehospital Stroke Scales

Score ≥4 → likely LVO. Easy to perform, helps triage to thrombectomy centers.

Score >0 = stroke. Designed to identify LVO with higher sensitivity than other scales.

💊 Stroke Pharmacology — Know These
Alteplase (tPA)
Class: Fibrinolytic / thrombolytic. Mechanism: Tissue plasminogen activator — converts plasminogen to plasmin, which dissolves the fibrin clot. Window: ≤4.5 hours from symptom onset (or last-known-well time). Contraindications: Hemorrhagic stroke, recent surgery, anticoagulation, uncontrolled HTN >185/110. Your role: Establish PRECISE symptom onset time — this determines eligibility.
Aspirin (ASA)
Class: Antiplatelet. Do NOT give prehospital — must rule out hemorrhagic stroke first. Hospital gives 325mg after CT confirms ischemic stroke and tPA is not given.
Labetalol / Nicardipine
Class: Antihypertensives. Blood pressure management in stroke is nuanced: permissive hypertension is allowed prehospital (the brain is trying to perfuse through stenotic vessels). Only treat BP if >220/120 prehospital or per protocol. Avoid dropping BP precipitously.
Glucose (D50W)
Always check BGL — hypoglycemia is the #1 stroke mimic. BGL <60 mg/dL causing neuro deficits is a hypoglycemic emergency, not a stroke. Treat with dextrose.
🩸 Ischemic Stroke — Infarct Core & Penumbra (MCA Occlusion) Animated
🚨Posterior Circulation Strokes: More subtle, easier to miss. May present as dizziness, vertigo, ataxia, diplopia, dysphagia — without classic FAST symptoms. Always consider stroke in sudden-onset dizziness with neurologic symptoms. These patients need the same urgency.
📋 Case Study — Stroke
You're called to a 67F at 1400. Family reports she was "fine at lunch" at 1200 and was found at 1345 slumped in her chair with left-sided facial droop, left arm weakness, and slurred speech. BP 196/108, HR 88, BGL 104 mg/dL. GCS = 13 (E3V4M6). LAMS score: face 1, grip 0, arm strength 0 = total 1.
Identify the stroke scale results, calculate the tPA window, and outline your management priorities.
Stroke scales: Cincinnati CPSS = positive (facial droop + arm drift + speech). LAMS = 1 (low — LVO less likely based on LAMS alone, but RACE would be more predictive). The exam should also assess for neglect and gaze deviation.

tPA window: Last-known-well = 1200. Current time = ~1400. Window = 2 hours elapsed, within the 4.5-hour window — patient is potentially eligible IF CT rules out hemorrhage.

Management:
1. Activate hospital code stroke NOW via radio
2. Airway — she's managing it (GCS 13) but monitor closely
3. O₂ only if SpO₂ <94% — avoid routine O₂
4. Position: supine, HOB 15° (not if suspected hemorrhagic)
5. IV access — do not delay transport
6. NO aspirin, NO aggressive BP lowering prehospital
7. Document exact last-known-well time — critical for tPA decision
8. Transport to comprehensive stroke center given potential LVO

Key point: DO NOT allow anything by mouth (aspiration risk). Monitor for deterioration (rising ICP, seizure, airway compromise).

⚡ Electrical Storm

Seizure Disorders

Abnormal, synchronized electrical discharges in the brain — from brief absence spells to life-threatening status epilepticus. Recognition, safety, and targeted pharmacology are your tools.

⚡ The Electrical Surge Analogy

"A seizure is like a power surge in the brain's electrical grid. Normally, neurons fire in an organized, regulated rhythm. In a seizure, a group of neurons begins firing uncontrollably and synchronously — like shorting out a circuit. The surge can stay in one area (focal seizure) or spread across the whole system (generalized). Status epilepticus is like a surge protector that doesn't trip — the overload continues and begins to melt the wiring (neuronal injury, hyperthermia, acidosis, death)."

⚗️ Pathophysiology — Ion Channel Chaos

Normal neuronal excitability depends on balanced sodium (Na⁺) and potassium (K⁺) ion channels. Seizures result from structural lesions or metabolic derangements that alter membrane permeability — neurons become hyperexcitable and synchronously depolarize.

The reticular activating system normally modulates consciousness. When a seizure generalizes to involve both hemispheres, consciousness is lost.

🚨 Status Epilepticus — The Emergency Within the Emergency
⚠️Definition: Continuous seizure activity ≥5 minutes OR two or more seizures without return to baseline between episodes. Every minute of untreated status = neuronal injury, hyperthermia, metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, and increasing drug resistance.
0–5 min
Active seizure. Protect patient. Loosen clothing. Time the seizure. Prepare medications.
5 min — Status Epilepticus
Administer first-line benzodiazepine. Establish IV/IO. Check BGL. Airway management priority.
10–20 min — Refractory
Repeat benzo + second-line agent (levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, valproate). Prepare for RSI.
>20 min — Super-Refractory
Anesthetic doses of propofol, ketamine, or barbiturates (hospital). Continuous EEG monitoring needed.
💊 Seizure Pharmacology
Midazolam (Versed) IM
Class: Benzodiazepine. Mechanism: Enhances GABA (the brain's inhibitory neurotransmitter) at GABA-A receptors → increases Cl⁻ influx → membrane hyperpolarization → neuron can't fire. Route advantage: IM 10mg is faster than IV access in a seizing patient. Now preferred first-line in many EMS systems. Monitor: Respiratory depression — have BVM ready.
Lorazepam (Ativan) IV
Class: Benzodiazepine. Same mechanism as midazolam. Longer duration of action. Preferred when IV is established. Dose: 0.1mg/kg IV (max 4mg per dose, repeat once).
Diazepam (Valium) IV/Rectal
Class: Benzodiazepine. IV or rectal gel (Diastat) for home/caregiver use in pediatric epilepsy. Longer half-life — good sustained coverage once seizure breaks.
Levetiracetam (Keppra)
Class: Anticonvulsant. Mechanism: Binds SV2A protein, modulates neurotransmitter release. Second-line agent for status epilepticus. Fewer drug interactions than phenytoin. Increasingly used in EMS for refractory SE.
Fosphenytoin (Cerebyx)
Class: Prodrug of phenytoin. Mechanism: Blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels → stabilizes neuronal membranes → prevents repetitive firing. Second-line SE. Given IV slowly — cardiac monitoring required (can cause hypotension, dysrhythmias).
Dextrose (D50W)
Always check BGL — hypoglycemia is a common and reversible cause of seizures. Treat aggressively if BGL <60 mg/dL.

🤕 Pain Assessment

Headaches

The vast majority are benign — but every paramedic must recognize the red flags that signal a headache is actually a life threat in disguise.

🚨ALWAYS Rule Out Secondary Causes First: Thunderclap headache (SAH), headache + fever + nuchal rigidity (meningitis), headache + focal neuro deficit (stroke), headache + altered LOC (increased ICP), new headache in immunocompromised patient, or "worst headache of my life." These require emergent workup.

🔑 Migraine Pathophysiology Analogy

"A migraine is like a two-phase electrical storm in the brain. Phase 1 (aura): a slow wave of neural excitation spreads across the cortex (cortical spreading depression) → neurons fire then go silent → visual aura, tingling, speech changes. Phase 2 (headache): trigeminal nerve activation releases inflammatory neuropeptides → serotonin imbalance → meningeal vessels dilate and become inflamed → the pounding, light-sensitive pain. Triptans work by stimulating serotonin receptors to reverse the vasodilation and block pain signals at the trigeminal nucleus."

💊 Headache Pharmacology
Sumatriptan (Imitrex)
Class: Triptan / 5-HT1B/1D agonist. Mechanism: Selectively constricts cranial blood vessels and blocks trigeminal pain signal transmission. Gold standard for acute migraine. Caution: contraindicated in coronary artery disease, hemiplegic migraine, or basilar migraine. Routes: oral, SQ, nasal.
Ondansetron (Zofran)
Class: 5-HT3 antagonist / antiemetic. EMS Use: Treats migraine-associated nausea without the extrapyramidal side effects of older antiemetics. Also works as adjunct — reduces migraine pain (likely via central serotonin modulation).
Ketorolac (Toradol)
Class: NSAID. Mechanism: COX inhibition → reduces prostaglandin-mediated neurogenic inflammation. Parenteral NSAID — effective for moderate migraine or cluster without the rebound risk of opioids. Often used IM in EMS.
Prochlorperazine (Compazine)
Class: Phenothiazine / D2 antagonist. Effective antiemetic AND analgesic for migraine. Risk of extrapyramidal symptoms (akathisia, dystonia) — pretreat or have diphenhydramine available.
O₂ (100%) High-Flow
15 L/min via NRB for 15–20 min → aborts 60–70% of cluster headaches. Mechanism: cerebral vasoconstriction + direct effect on trigeminovascular system. Simple, effective, no side effects. First-line for cluster headache prehospital.

🦠 Infectious Emergencies

CNS Infections

Bacterial meningitis kills in hours. Encephalitis can cause permanent disability. These infections carry higher morbidity and mortality than most other organ systems — speed and PPE are non-negotiable.

🛡 The Meninges as Bubble Wrap Analogy

"The meninges are three layers of protective bubble wrap around the brain (dura mater → arachnoid → pia mater). Meningitis is when that bubble wrap becomes infected and inflamed. The CSF space between the arachnoid and pia (subarachnoid space) fills with pus, bacteria, and inflammatory mediators. This triggers headache (swelling), fever (systemic immune response), and nuchal rigidity (meningeal irritation — the meninges pull taut when the neck flexes). The classic triad is how the bubble wrap screams for help."

🌡 Classic Triad of Meningitis
🤒

Fever

Sudden high fever from bacterial sepsis / inflammatory response

🤕

Severe Headache

Increased ICP + meningeal irritation. Usually sudden onset.

🦴

Nuchal Rigidity

Stiff neck — inability to flex chin to chest due to meningeal irritation. Kernig's and Brudzinski's signs positive.

⚠️Additional Signs: Photophobia, phonophobia, altered mental status, seizures. Meningococcal meningitis may show a non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash — systemic septicemia, extremely high mortality without immediate antibiotics.
🛡 PPE — Protect Yourself
⛔Bacterial meningitis and some forms of encephalitis can be spread via droplets (saliva, nasal secretions, respiratory droplets). Both the patient AND crew must wear masks. Add standard + droplet precautions. Notify the receiving facility so they can prepare isolation.
💊 CNS Infection Pharmacology (Hospital — Understand the Mechanism)
Ceftriaxone (Rocephin)
Class: 3rd generation cephalosporin. Mechanism: Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis. Broad-spectrum coverage for N. meningitidis and S. pneumoniae. First-line empirical coverage for bacterial meningitis. Time is critical — early administration significantly reduces mortality.
Vancomycin
Class: Glycopeptide antibiotic. Added to cover resistant S. pneumoniae. Inhibits cell wall synthesis by binding D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan. Penetrates blood-brain barrier in inflamed meninges.
Dexamethasone (Decadron)
Class: Corticosteroid. Given BEFORE first antibiotic dose in bacterial meningitis. Reduces inflammatory cytokine release → decreases meningeal swelling → reduces sensorineural hearing loss (major complication). Does NOT reduce effectiveness of antibiotics.
Acyclovir (Zovirax)
Class: Antiviral (nucleoside analog). First-line for HSV encephalitis. Mechanism: inhibits viral DNA polymerase. Given empirically when encephalitis is suspected — HSV encephalitis is treatable but devastating if delayed.

🧬 Neoplasms

CNS Tumors & Spinal Cord Compression

Brain tumors produce symptoms through mass effect, increased ICP, and local tissue destruction. Your prehospital role is supportive management and recognizing when a "simple headache" is something far more ominous.

🧠 Brain Tumors — What You Need to Know

Brain tumors (neoplasms) may be primary (arising from brain tissue — gliomas, meningiomas, pituitary adenomas) or metastatic (spreading from lung, breast, melanoma, renal). Primary CNS tumors are the most common solid tumors in children and the most common cause of cancer death in children.

Effects depend on size, location, growth rate, and presence of edema or hemorrhage.

PresentationLikely Tumor Location
Headache (worse AM, supine), N/V, papilledemaAny location — increased ICP
Seizures (focal → generalized)Motor cortex, temporal lobe
Personality change, frontal lobe signsFrontal lobe
Visual field defectsOccipital lobe or pituitary
Language problems (aphasia)Dominant temporal/frontal lobe (Broca's/Wernicke's)
Ataxia, coordination problemsCerebellum
🦴 Spinal Cord Compression

Tumors, abscesses, herniated discs, or trauma can compress the spinal cord. Epidural spinal cord compression (ESCC) from metastatic cancer is an oncologic emergency.

⚠️ESCC Symptoms: Progressive back pain (often nocturnal, worse lying down) → weakness/paresthesia below the level → bowel/bladder dysfunction. New-onset back pain in a cancer patient = ESCC until proven otherwise. Untreated → paraplegia within hours to days.
💊 Tumor/Edema Management
Dexamethasone (Decadron)
High-dose corticosteroid for tumor-related vasogenic cerebral/spinal edema. Mechanism: stabilizes blood-brain barrier, reduces capillary permeability around tumor. Typical dose: 10mg IV bolus then 4mg q6h. Also first-line for ESCC.
Anticonvulsants
If tumor presents with seizures: benzodiazepines acutely (see seizure section), then prophylactic anticonvulsants (levetiracetam preferred — fewer interactions with chemotherapy).
🚑 Prehospital Management of CNS Tumor Emergencies

Prehospital care ranges from comfort and emotional support during transport to full resuscitation for acute decompensation. Common acute presentations include:

• Seizure: Manage per seizure protocol. Note that tumor patients may have breakthrough seizures despite anticonvulsant medications.

• Herniation signs (Cushing's triad): Elevate HOB 30°, consider controlled hyperventilation (ETCO₂ 30–35), contact medical direction.

• Altered LOC: Airway, breathing, circulation — the fundamentals always apply regardless of underlying diagnosis.

🔄 Chronic Conditions

Degenerative & Other Neurologic Diseases

Chronic, often progressive conditions that shape your patient's baseline. Know these diseases to understand what "normal" looks like for your patient — and when they're decompensating.

🧠 Dementia & Alzheimer's Disease
📁 Hard Drive Analogy

"Dementia is like a hard drive that's been corrupting from the outside in. Recent memories (RAM) go first — patients can't form new memories but may vividly recall events from 50 years ago (long-term storage). Alzheimer's specifically destroys the cortex — the entire filing system eventually fails. Confabulation (making up plausible stories to fill memory gaps) is the hard drive trying to run despite corrupted sectors."

Alzheimer's: Abnormal protein deposits (amyloid plaques, tau tangles) → cortical neuron death → brain atrophy. 1 in 3 older adults die with this disease — higher than breast and prostate cancer combined.

💡EMS Pearl: NEVER assume an altered Alzheimer's patient doesn't understand you — they may comprehend more than they can express. Speak directly, calmly, and respectfully. Also never assume altered LOC is baseline — delirium superimposed on dementia is common and has a reversible cause (UTI, hypoglycemia, medication toxicity).
FeatureDementiaDelirium
OnsetGradual (months-years)Acute (hours-days)

ConsciousnessUsually clearClouded, fluctuating

EMS actionSupport, comfort, transportFind and treat the cause
🧲 Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
🔌 Stripped Wire Analogy

"MS is an autoimmune disease where the immune system mistakes myelin (the insulation around nerve axons) for a foreign invader and destroys it. This is like stripping the plastic coating off electrical wires — signals short circuit, slow down, or fail entirely. The brain tries to compensate by finding alternate routes (like detouring around a closed road), but eventually the axon itself is damaged and the road closes permanently."

Symptoms vary widely based on which white matter tracts are demyelinated: fatigue, vertigo, ataxia, spasticity, incontinence, blurred/double vision, optic neuritis, slurred speech, numbness. Diagnosed by clinical criteria + MRI (plaques in white matter).

💊 MS Management
Methylprednisolone (Solu-Medrol)
High-dose IV corticosteroids for acute exacerbation/relapse. Reduces inflammation and shortens duration of relapse. Does not change the long-term course.
Interferon Beta / Glatiramer
Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) — reduce relapse frequency. Not used acutely. Self-injected or infused periodically.
Baclofen
Antispastic agent (GABA-B agonist). Reduces spasticity. May cause hypotension if stopped abruptly.

🔴 Parkinson's Disease
⚙️ Brake Without Gas Analogy

"The basal ganglia is like a fine-tuning system for movement — it uses dopamine (the 'gas') and acetylcholine (the 'brake') to coordinate smooth motion. In Parkinson's, dopamine-producing cells in the substantia nigra degenerate → the dopamine/acetylcholine ratio shifts → the brake dominates without gas → tremor, rigidity, slow movement (bradykinesia). Treatment gives back the gas (dopamine precursors/agonists) or removes some of the brake (anticholinergics)."

Cardinal signs: TRAP — Tremor (resting, pill-rolling), Rigidity (cogwheel), Akinesia/bradykinesia (slow movement), Postural instability. Leading cause of neurologic disability in people >60. Progresses over 10–15 years.

💊 Parkinson's Medications
Levodopa/Carbidopa (Sinemet)
Gold standard. Levodopa crosses blood-brain barrier → converted to dopamine. Carbidopa prevents peripheral conversion (reduces N/V, allows lower dose). Long-term use → "wearing off" fluctuations and dyskinesias.
Dopamine Agonists (Ropinirole, Pramipexole)
Directly stimulate dopamine receptors. Used earlier in disease or adjunct. Fewer motor complications than levodopa.
MAO-B Inhibitors (Selegiline, Rasagiline)
Block monoamine oxidase B → prevent dopamine breakdown → extend dopamine activity. Used as adjunct or early monotherapy.
Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)
Surgical implantation of electrodes in subthalamic nucleus/globus pallidus. Delivers electrical pulses to block abnormal signals. Dramatically reduces tremor/rigidity in advanced Parkinson's unresponsive to meds. Patients carry a programmer — check for device in unresponsive Parkinson's patients.

📡 Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)
🔄 Friendly Fire Analogy

"GBS is autoimmune friendly fire — after a viral or GI infection (often Campylobacter jejuni), the immune system produces antibodies that cross-react with myelin on peripheral nerves. It's like a soldier getting back from a mission and accidentally attacking his own base. The demyelination travels from feet upward (ascending paralysis) over days to weeks. The critical risk is when it reaches the respiratory muscles — the patient stops being able to breathe."

Ascending weakness and paralysis starting in legs, spreading upward. Usually follows respiratory/GI illness by 2–4 weeks. Most patients recover but may need ventilatory support during nadir. Autonomic instability (wide BP swings, dysrhythmias) is a major concern.

⚠️Respiratory Monitoring is Critical: Measure inspiratory force (NIF) — rapid decline signals impending respiratory failure. Intubate early rather than late in rapidly ascending GBS.
⚡ ALS (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis / Lou Gehrig's Disease)
📡 Silent Motor Analogy

"ALS selectively destroys the motor neurons in the brain and spinal cord — both upper and lower motor neuron tracts. It's like cutting all the control cables to a robotic system while leaving the cameras and sensors intact. The mind remains sharp, but the body progressively loses the ability to move, speak, swallow, and breathe. ALS spares the senses — patients are trapped in a failing body with a fully functioning mind. This distinction drives the emotional and ethical weight of ALS care."

Progressive weakness starting in hands/arms + fasciculations → dysphagia, dysarthria → respiratory failure. Death typically within 5 years of diagnosis. Many patients use ventilators (BiPAP → full vent) and feeding tubes. Prehospital care: Know the patient's advance directives — many have DNR/AND orders. Airway support when appropriate, excellent emotional support always.
