Chapter 32 · Section I

Introduction & Toxic Syndromes

Toxidromes are constellations of signs and symptoms that point to a specific class of poison. Mastering these patterns is your fastest clinical shortcut.

📞

Poison Control Centers

▾

There are 55 poison control centers in the United States, most based in major medical centers. They provide 24/7 consultation from a database of over 447,000 toxic substances — drugs, chemicals, plants, animals, cosmetics, and hazardous materials.

National Poison Control Number: 1-800-222-1222
Accessible toll-free nationwide, linked into 9-1-1 systems. Always call early — before transport when possible.
Think of Poison Control as your real-time toxicology consultant — a searchable library of every dangerous substance on Earth, available in seconds. They don't just tell you what's toxic; they walk you through management step by step, factoring in the dose, the patient's weight, and your local protocols.

Poison control responsibilities include: treatment consultation, professional education, data collection, public education, research, and regional EMS protocol development.

🧬

The Seven Core Toxidromes

▾

A toxidrome (toxic + syndrome) is a recognizable pattern of symptoms caused by a specific category of poison. Identifying the toxidrome guides treatment before you know the exact agent.

🐞 Cholinergic

SLUDGE / DUMBELS

Overstimulation of acetylcholine receptors — think the body's "garden hose is stuck open." Everything secretory is turned MAX UP.

Bradycardia SLUDGE Miosis Bronchospasm

🌵 Anticholinergic

MAD AS A HATTER

Blocks acetylcholine — the body "dries out and speeds up." Opposite of cholinergic. Common in meds and certain plants.

Tachycardia Dry Skin Mydriasis Hyperthermia

👁️ Opioid

THE CLASSIC TRIAD

Respiratory depression + decreased LOC + miosis. The "3 M's": Miosis, Muteness (unresponsive), Minimal respirations.

Resp Depression Pinpoint Pupils Naloxone reverses

⚡ Sympathomimetic

FIGHT OR FLIGHT × 10

Massive norepinephrine/epinephrine release. Everything stimulated: heart, lungs, brain. Think cocaine or amphetamines.

Hypertension Tachycardia Dilated Pupils Paranoid Delusions

😴 Sedative-Hypnotic

CNS SHUTDOWN

CNS depression without the pupil changes seen in opioids. Benzos, barbiturates — especially dangerous with alcohol.

CNS Depression Ataxia Resp Risk w/ ETOH

🎭 Hallucinogen

SENSORY OVERLOAD

Variable CNS stimulation and/or depression. LSD, PCP, peyote, psilocybin. Patient may be combative; safety is paramount.

Delusions Tachycardia Seizure risk

🌡️ Serotonin Syndrome

TRIPLE THREAT

Agitation + autonomic instability + neuromuscular changes. Usually from drug combinations — SSRIs + triptans, or MDMA.

Hyperthermia Clonus Diaphoresis

Chapter 32 · Section II

Poisoning by Ingestion

77.7% of poisonings reported to US poison control centers involve ingestion. Most ingested poisons require supportive care — know when antidotes and decontamination apply.

🔍

Assessment & GI Decontamination

▾

Focus your assessment on the three vital systems most likely to cause death: cardiovascular, respiratory, and CNS.

Key History Questions

  • What was ingested?
  • When was it ingested?
  • How much was taken?
  • Alcohol or other substances?
  • Was vomiting induced?
  • Any antidote or charcoal given?
  • Psychiatric history / suicidality?

Activated Charcoal

Inert, nontoxic product of high-temperature heated wood material. Works by adsorbing (binding) toxins in the GI tract.

Activated charcoal is most effective within 1 hour of ingestion. It is CONTRAINDICATED in caustic/corrosive ingestion, altered mental status (aspiration risk), and when the airway is unprotected.
Think of activated charcoal as a "molecular sponge" — millions of microscopic pores absorb toxin molecules before they can be absorbed by the gut lining.
🧪

Acids & Alkalis (Caustics/Corrosives)

▾
Acids cause coagulation necrosis — like a cauterizing iron, they sear the tissue and create a protective eschar that limits depth. Damage usually completes within 1–2 minutes.

Alkalis cause liquefaction necrosis — they dissolve tissue like drain cleaner on a clog, continuing to penetrate deeper for minutes to hours. Far more destructive.
Acid vs. Alkali analogy: An acid burn is like a hot pan briefly touching skin — it hurts and chars the surface. An alkali burn is like dissolving sugar in water — it keeps dissolving deeper and deeper until diluted or neutralized. That's why alkali burns are more dangerous despite "looking less severe."
Toilet bowl cleaners (HCl) Rust remover (H₂SO₄) Drain cleaner (NaOH) Ammonia Bleach
DO NOT induce vomiting (re-exposes esophagus). Dilution with water/milk ONLY if conscious and per medical direction. Secure airway — laryngeal edema can develop rapidly. Transport priority.
🧴

Toxic Alcohols: Methanol, Ethylene Glycol & Isopropanol

▾

Methanol (Wood Alcohol)

Methanol itself is mildly toxic; it's the metabolites that kill. Alcohol dehydrogenase converts methanol → formaldehyde → formic acid. Formic acid destroys the optic nerve and causes profound metabolic acidosis.
Methanol is like a trojan horse — it enters innocuously (even smells faintly sweet), but is converted by the same enzyme that processes beer into a metabolic bomb (formic acid) that blinds and acidifies.

Sources: windshield washer fluid, Sterno, paint remover

Signs: visual disturbance ("snowfield" vision), severe metabolic acidosis, CNS depression

Fomepizole (4-MP) — blocks alcohol dehydrogenase, stopping conversion to formic acid. Hemodialysis for severe cases. Correct metabolic acidosis with NaHCO₃.

Ethylene Glycol (Antifreeze)

EG → glycolic acid → oxalic acid, which binds calcium to form calcium oxalate crystals. These crystals deposit in the renal tubules → acute kidney failure. The sweet taste makes accidental pediatric ingestion common.

Stage 1

30 min – 12 hr
Neurologic: drunk-like, ataxia, seizure, coma

Stage 2

12 – 24 hr
Cardiopulmonary: tachycardia, pulmonary edema

Stage 3

24 – 72 hr
Renal failure — calcium oxalate crystals destroy kidneys

Stage 4

6 – 15 days
Delayed neurological sequelae
Fomepizole (first-line antidote). Thiamine to convert glycolic acid to a non-toxic metabolite. Benzodiazepines for seizures. Hemodialysis for severe cases.

Isopropanol (Rubbing Alcohol)

Isopropanol is twice as potent as ethanol as a CNS depressant. Unlike methanol and EG, it is NOT converted to as dangerous a metabolite — it breaks down to acetone. However, it still causes significant CNS depression, and the acetone produces a characteristic fruity odor.
If methanol is a poison disguised as a drink, isopropanol is just a very strong drink — it'll get you drunk fast and hard, but won't usually burn out your eyes or kidneys. Management is supportive.

The "Mad Hatter" in Alice in Wonderland wasn't fictional — 19th-century hat makers used mercury nitrate to shape felt, breathing in fumes that caused tremors, paranoia, and dementia. Mercury doesn't just poison; it systematically dismantles the nervous system.
💊

Acetaminophen Overdose — The Silent Liver Destroyer

▾
Normal acetaminophen metabolism: 90% is conjugated (harmless). About 5% is converted by CYP2E1 to NAPQI (highly hepatotoxic), normally neutralized by glutathione. In overdose, glutathione stores are depleted → NAPQI accumulates → massive hepatocellular necrosis — especially around the central hepatic vein (zone 3).
Think of glutathione as a fire extinguisher and NAPQI as a small fire. Normal doses — plenty of extinguisher to handle the flame. In overdose, you pour gasoline — the extinguisher runs dry and the whole building (your liver) burns down. This is why acetaminophen overdose is deceptively dangerous: you may feel fine for 24 hours while your liver is silently being destroyed.

Phase I

0–24 hours
Nausea, vomiting, malaise — patient may feel "just sick"

Phase II

24–72 hours
Apparent improvement; LFTs rising secretly

Phase III

72–96 hours
Hepatic necrosis peaks — jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy

Phase IV

4 days – 2 weeks
Recovery OR fulminant hepatic failure and death
Toxic adult dose: >150 mg/kg or >12 g. At toxic levels, the patient may feel fine for 24 hours — this is the "Walking Time Bomb" phenomenon. Always check acetaminophen levels in any intentional overdose!
N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) — replenishes glutathione stores. Most effective if given within 8–10 hours of ingestion. Can still help up to 24 hours. Activated charcoal if within 1 hour and patient is alert. IV fluids and supportive care.
❤️

Cardiac Medication Toxicity

▾

Beta-Blocker Toxicity

Beta-blockers competitively antagonize catecholamines at β₁ and β₂ receptors. Overdose causes profound bradycardia, hypotension, and heart block by removing the sympathetic "gas pedal" from the heart.
Beta-blockers in overdose are like pressing the brake pedal while cutting the throttle — the heart has no way to speed up even when the body needs it. The AV node slows, contractility plummets, and cardiac output crashes.

Examples: atenolol, metoprolol (end in "-olol")

Atropine (may be ineffective), glucagon (bypasses the blocked receptor — works differently!), calcium, high-dose insulin therapy. Activated charcoal if mental status intact.

Calcium Channel Blocker Toxicity

Calcium influx is required for cardiac and smooth muscle contraction. CCB overdose blocks calcium entry → negative inotropy, chronotropy, dromotropy, and vasodilation. Can produce fatal bradydysrhythmias and refractory shock.
If the heart is a door, calcium is the key that opens it for contraction. CCB overdose jams every lock simultaneously — the heart can't contract properly AND the peripheral vessels dilate, causing a double hit of hypotension.

Examples: diltiazem, verapamil, amlodipine

IV Calcium (chloride or gluconate), high-dose insulin/glucose therapy, vasopressors, lipid emulsion therapy for severe cases. Intralipid can act as a "lipid sink" to sequester fat-soluble CCBs.
Chapter 32 · Section III

Poisoning by Inhalation

Inhaled toxins are categorized by mechanism. Recognizing which type you're dealing with determines your intervention speed and airway strategy.

🌬️

Three Categories of Inhaled Toxins

▾

Simple Asphyxiants

Displace oxygen — they're not toxic themselves, just crowd out O₂. In a confined space, even "harmless" gases become lethal.

MethanePropaneCO₂Inert gases

Chemical Asphyxiants

Don't displace O₂ — they prevent the body from using oxygen at the cellular level. Far more dangerous because O₂ is present but inaccessible.

COCyanideH₂S

Irritants / Corrosives

Cause direct cellular destruction upon contact with moist mucous membranes. Damage depends on water solubility — soluble ones hit upper airways; insoluble ones penetrate deep into alveoli.

ChlorineAmmoniaPhosgene
Water solubility and where toxins hit: Highly water-soluble gases (like ammonia) dissolve immediately in the moist upper airway — causing instant eye/nose/throat burning. This warns people to escape. Low water-solubility gases (like phosgene) bypass the upper airway with little warning and deliver their damage deep in the alveoli — causing delayed pulmonary edema hours later. The "silent" ones are the most deadly.
💀

Carbon Monoxide (CO) — The Silent Killer

▾
CO binds hemoglobin with 240× greater affinity than oxygen, forming carboxyhemoglobin (COHb). This does two things: (1) blocks oxygen-carrying capacity and (2) shifts the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve LEFT (remaining O₂ is held tighter and not released to tissues). CO also binds myoglobin and cytochrome oxidase (blocking mitochondrial electron transport) → true cellular asphyxia.
CO is the ultimate identity thief — it steals hemoglobin's "seat" by fitting in 240× better than oxygen. But O₂ is still in the blood; the patient looks pink (cherry red). Standard pulse oximetry reads falsely normal because it can't distinguish oxyhemoglobin from carboxyhemoglobin. You need CO-oximetry to detect it.
Standard SpO₂ is UNRELIABLE in CO poisoning. Use a CO-oximeter or end-tidal CO monitor. Patients appear pink or cherry red — NOT cyanotic. Headache + flu-like symptoms without fever in multiple family members = CO poisoning until proven otherwise.

Symptoms by COHb Level

  • 10–20%: Headache, nausea, dyspnea on exertion
  • 20–40%: Severe headache, confusion, syncope
  • 40–60%: Seizures, coma, cardiac dysrhythmias
  • 60–70%: Respiratory failure, death
100% High-Flow O₂ via NRB mask. High-flow oxygen reduces CO half-life from ~5 hours (room air) to ~60–90 minutes. Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) reduces it further to ~20 minutes and is used for severe cases: COHb >25%, neurological symptoms, pregnancy, cardiac involvement.
☠️

Cyanide Poisoning

▾
Cyanide binds cytochrome oxidase (Complex IV) in the mitochondrial electron transport chain, blocking the last step of aerobic respiration. Cells switch to anaerobic metabolism → massive lactic acidosis. Oxygen is delivered to tissues but CANNOT be used — classic "histotoxic hypoxia." Venous blood appears bright red because cells can't extract O₂.
Cyanide is like a key that fits perfectly into the engine but doesn't turn — it blocks the ignition of cellular metabolism. With cyanide, your cells are like a car with a full gas tank, engine cranking, but the fuel line is kinked. Everything is present for energy production, but nothing fires.
In a structural fire, assume cyanide poisoning if: rapid loss of consciousness, persistent lactic acidosis despite adequate oxygenation, and the fire involved nylon, polyurethane, or plastics. CO + CN poisoning together are common and synergistic.
Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) — preferred antidote. Binds cyanide to form cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12), which is safely excreted. Dose: 5g IV over 15 min. May be combined with sodium thiosulfate. Avoid amyl nitrite (methemoglobin formation is risky in CO + CN).
🌫️

Ammonia & Hydrocarbon Inhalation

▾

Ammonia (NH₃)

Highly water-soluble alkaline gas. Immediate intense irritation to eyes, nose, and upper airways. In high concentrations, causes chemical bronchitis, pulmonary edema, and destruction of respiratory mucosa. Associated with large-scale agriculture and refrigeration systems.
Remove from environment immediately. High-flow O₂. Positive-pressure ventilation for respiratory failure. Bronchodilators (albuterol) for bronchospasm. Diuretics if pulmonary edema. Eye irrigation for exposed eyes.

Hydrocarbon Inhalation

Most toxic via inhalation of halogenated or aromatic hydrocarbons (benzene, toluene, carbon tetrachloride). CNS depression is primary concern. Sensitizes myocardium to catecholamines → "Sudden Sniffing Death" — fatal dysrhythmia triggered by adrenaline rush, even on first exposure.
"Sudden Sniffing Death" — an adolescent inhales a hydrocarbon (huffing), gets a catecholamine surge (fear, excitement), and the sensitized heart fibrillates. Death can occur on the very first exposure. Epinephrine is CONTRAINDICATED — will worsen dysrhythmia.
Chapter 32 · Section IV

Poisoning by Injection — Envenomation

Venom is a highly evolved biological weapon. Unlike manufactured poisons, venoms contain complex mixtures of enzymes, proteins, and neurotoxins designed specifically to immobilize or kill prey.

🕷️

Spider Envenomation

▾

Black Widow (Latrodectus)

α-Latrotoxin triggers massive release of neurotransmitters (acetylcholine and norepinephrine) at nerve terminals → sustained, painful muscle cramps. The nervous system is in overdrive. Only the female is venomous (has red hourglass marking).
Black widow venom is like permanently holding down the "send signal" button on every nerve in your body simultaneously — your muscles can't stop contracting. The abdomen can become board-rigid, mimicking a surgical emergency.

Signs: Initially painless pinprick → severe muscle cramps (especially abdominal rigidity), diaphoresis, tachycardia, hypertension, nausea.

Benzodiazepines for muscle spasm and pain. Opioids for analgesia. Black widow antivenom available (used for severe cases, elderly, children). Calcium gluconate historically used (limited evidence).

Brown Recluse (Loxosceles)

Sphingomyelinase D enzyme attacks cell membranes at the bite site → progressive necrosis. Destroys RBCs (hemolysis) and activates complement → local tissue destruction that can expand dramatically over days. The "volcano lesion" — central necrosis with surrounding erythema.
Brown recluse venom is like a biological wrecking ball at the cellular level — the enzyme specifically dissolves the "mortar" (sphingomyelin) holding cell membranes together, causing cells to literally fall apart from the inside out.

Found in: dry, abandoned spaces; violin-shaped marking on cephalothorax ("fiddle-back"). Initial bite often painless; necrosis develops over 1–2 days.

Wound care, antibiotics if infected. Dapsone has been used (controversial). Surgical debridement for large necrotic wounds. Systemic involvement: steroids, blood transfusion for hemolysis.
🐍

Snakebite Envenomation

▾

Pit Vipers (Crotalidae)

Includes rattlesnakes, cottonmouths (water moccasins), and copperheads. Identified by triangular head, heat-sensing pit organ between eye and nostril, and elliptical pupils.

Pit viper venom contains hemotoxins (destroy blood cells, disrupt coagulation) and cytotoxins (tissue destruction). The venom immobilizes prey by destroying vascular integrity — massive local edema, hemorrhage, and tissue necrosis. Some species also contain neurotoxins.
Pit viper venom is a sophisticated digestive enzyme delivered in advance — the snake injects its "stomach juices" to begin breaking down tissue before eating. Local tissue literally liquefies as the venom dissolves structural proteins and destroys blood vessel walls.
Immobilize extremity at heart level. Do NOT apply tourniquet, ice, or suction. Antivenom (CroFab or Anavip) in ED. Treat coagulopathy. Mark the edge of swelling every 15–30 min to track progression.

Coral Snakes (Elapidae)

Mnemonic: "Red on yellow, kill a fellow; red on black, friend of Jack."

Coral snake venom contains neurotoxins that block neuromuscular junction (post-synaptic) → ascending paralysis similar to myasthenia gravis. Unlike pit viper bites, there is minimal local tissue reaction — the damage is systemic and neurological.
Coral snake bite may appear minor locally but causes delayed (6–12 hours) progressive bulbar paralysis and respiratory failure. A patient who appears fine after a coral snake bite must be observed in the hospital for 24 hours.
Coral snake antivenom. Supportive respiratory care. Intubation/ventilation for respiratory failure (paralysis of respiratory muscles). No significant local wound care needed.
🦂

Arthropod Stings & Tick Diseases

▾

Hymenoptera (Bees, Wasps, Ants)

Venom contains histamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, dopamine, melittin, and phospholipases. In sensitized individuals, even a single sting can trigger anaphylaxis — the most immediately dangerous complication. Honey bees leave their stinger behind (barbed) — scrape/flick it off, do NOT squeeze (injects more venom).

For anaphylaxis: IM Epinephrine (EpiPen equivalent), diphenhydramine, corticosteroids, albuterol for bronchospasm, IV fluids. Scrape (not squeeze) stingers. Monitor for biphasic anaphylaxis (second wave 4–12 hrs later).

🌊

Marine Envenomation

▾

Coelenterates (Jellyfish)

Nematocysts (stinging cells) inject venom on contact. Venom causes pain, dermatitis, and potentially cardiac dysrhythmias in severe stings (box jellyfish).

Vinegar (4–6% acetic acid) or baking soda slurry for 30+ seconds to inactivate nematocysts. Remove tentacle fragments with forceps (NOT bare hands). Hot water immersion for pain relief (denatures venom proteins). Do NOT use freshwater (causes nematocysts to fire).

Stingrays

Retroserrated spine on tail delivers venom. Both local (intense burning pain, tissue necrosis) and systemic (hypotension, dysrhythmias, seizures) effects possible.

Irrigate wound with seawater. Remove visible spine fragments (large ones need surgical removal). Hot water immersion (45°C/113°F) to denature heat-labile venom proteins. Pain management. Observe for systemic effects.

Poisoning by Absorption — Organophosphates & Carbamates

Absorbed poisons penetrate through skin and mucous membranes. Organophosphates and carbamates represent the most clinically significant absorbed poisons in EMS.

🌿

Organophosphates & Carbamates

▾
Organophosphates (OPs) and carbamates inhibit acetylcholinesterase (AChE) — the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine at nerve synapses. When AChE is inhibited, ACh accumulates excessively at ALL cholinergic synapses:

• Muscarinic effects (smooth muscle, glands): SLUDGE + bradycardia, bronchospasm
• Nicotinic effects (skeletal muscle, ganglia): MTWHF — Muscle weakness, Twitching, Weakness, Hypertension, Fasciculations
• CNS effects: Anxiety → seizures → coma
Acetylcholinesterase is the OFF switch for nerve signals. Organophosphates DISABLE the OFF switch — so every nerve fires continuously, like a car alarm that won't stop. SLUDGE is your wet, secretory emergency. Muscle fasciculations occur because the neuromuscular junction is also stuck in "ON." Eventually muscles exhaust (flaccid paralysis) — respiratory muscles fail → death.
Scene Safety First! OP-contaminated patients can contaminate responders via their clothing, skin, and vomitus. Don PPE (level B at minimum). Remove patient's clothing and decontaminate with large amounts of water BEFORE transport if possible.

Key Distinguishing Feature: OPs vs. Carbamates

Organophosphates
Bind AChE irreversibly (covalent bond — "ages"). Time-dependent — if not treated quickly, the bond becomes permanent. Examples: malathion, parathion, sarin, VX nerve agent.
Carbamates
Bind AChE reversibly (spontaneously dissociates). Less severe and self-limiting. Examples: carbaryl (Sevin), aldicarb. Pralidoxime is controversial/less effective.

Treatment Protocol

Step 1 — Atropine: Blocks muscarinic receptors. Give LARGE doses — the endpoint is drying of secretions, NOT heart rate. Doses can be massive (2–4 mg every 5–10 min, escalating). "Drying of secretions" not tachycardia is the goal.

Step 2 — Pralidoxime (2-PAM/Protopam): Reactivates AChE by displacing the OP from the enzyme. MUST be given before "aging" (irreversible bond formation). More effective for nicotinic/muscle effects than atropine. Not indicated for pure carbamate poisoning.

Step 3 — Benzodiazepines (midazolam preferred): For seizure control. Phenytoin is ineffective in OP seizures — benzos are the only effective class.
Atropine in OP poisoning is like covering your ears so you can't hear the alarm — it blocks the signal from reaching the organ (muscarinic receptors). Pralidoxime actually fixes the alarm system itself — it removes the OP "wrench" jammed in the AChE machinery.
Chapter 32 · Section VI

Substance Use Disorder & Drug Overdose

SUD affects every demographic. Approach with clinical precision and cultural humility — these are medical emergencies, not moral failures.

💉

Opioid Overdose Crisis

▾
Opioids bind μ (mu), κ (kappa), and δ (delta) opioid receptors throughout the CNS and periphery. Respiratory depression is the primary lethal mechanism — opioids suppress the brainstem respiratory centers (pre-Bötzinger complex), reducing hypercapnic and hypoxic drive. Death results from apnea and hypoxia.
The opioid triad (miosis + respiratory depression + CNS depression) is your clinical fingerprint. Think of μ-receptors as volume knobs on the brainstem's "breathing drive." Opioids turn the knob to zero — the patient stops responding to CO₂ buildup, stops breathing, and turns blue. Miosis (pinpoint pupils) occurs because μ-receptors in the Edinger-Westphal nucleus cause unopposed parasympathetic pupillary constriction.

The Three Waves of the Opioid Epidemic

Wave 1 (1999)

Prescription Opioids
OxyContin, hydrocodone overprescription → prescription OD epidemic

Wave 2 (~2010)

Heroin
Cheap, available heroin as prescription opioids became harder to get

Wave 3 (~2013)

Synthetic Fentanyl
Illicitly manufactured fentanyl — 100× morphine potency — drives current death toll
Fentanyl Scene Safety: Fentanyl is NOT absorbed through intact skin at ambient concentrations encountered at OD scenes. However, powdered fentanyl poses inhalation risk if aerosolized. Use standard precautions (gloves, eye protection). Reserve respiratory PPE for large powder/liquid fentanyl scenes.

Naloxone — The Life-Saving Antidote

Naloxone (Narcan) — pure opioid antagonist with NO agonist activity. Competitively displaces opioids from receptors. Routes: IV/IO (most rapid), IM, intranasal (IN), ET, SQ.

Dose: 0.4–2 mg IV/IM/IN; repeat every 2–3 min PRN. For fentanyl/synthetic opioids — may require higher doses or continuous infusion.

⚠️ Duration: Naloxone's half-life (30–90 min) is SHORTER than most opioids. Patients can re-narcotize after naloxone wears off — monitor closely and consider infusion.
Precipitation of Withdrawal: High-dose naloxone in opioid-dependent patients causes acute withdrawal — agitation, vomiting, severe pain. In non-cardiac-arrest situations, titrate low (0.1–0.4 mg) to restore respiratory drive without inducing full withdrawal. Goal: breathing spontaneously, NOT fully alert.
⚡

Stimulant Overdose: Cocaine & Amphetamines

▾
Cocaine blocks reuptake of dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin at presynaptic terminals — massive sympathetic surge. Also blocks fast sodium channels (local anesthetic effect) in cardiac cells → dysrhythmias and widened QRS. The combination of sympathetic excess + sodium channel blockade creates the "deadly duo" of cocaine cardiotoxicity.
Cocaine is like jamming the accelerator AND cutting the brake lines simultaneously. The sympathomimetic effect floors the pedal (hypertension, tachycardia, coronary spasm), while the sodium channel blockade makes the heart's electrical system unpredictable — wide complex rhythms can deteriorate to VF without warning.
AVOID beta-blockers in cocaine chest pain! Blocking β₂ receptors leaves α receptors unopposed → severe coronary vasospasm and hypertension. Benzodiazepines are first-line for cocaine hypertension/tachycardia.

Methamphetamine

Far longer-acting than cocaine (half-life ~10 hrs vs ~60 min). Causes direct neurotoxicity — destroys dopaminergic and serotonergic neurons. Chronic users develop permanent cognitive impairment, "meth mouth," severe skin excoriation (formication = sensation of bugs crawling), and psychosis.

Phencyclidine (PCP)

PCP is a dissociative anesthetic — blocks NMDA glutamate receptors. Creates a dissociative state in which users feel detached from reality and pain. Has BOTH stimulant AND depressant properties simultaneously, plus analgesic effects. Low dose: dissociation + agitation. High dose: coma.

PCP psychosis can mimic paranoid schizophrenia and may persist days after last use. Management: calm environment, minimal stimulation in low-dose toxicity; protect airway in high-dose toxicity (coma). Benzodiazepines for agitation/seizure. Antipsychotics (haloperidol) for psychosis.

🧠

Tricyclic Antidepressants & Other CNS Drugs

▾
TCAs block multiple receptor types simultaneously: (1) fast sodium channels → widened QRS, dysrhythmias; (2) muscarinic receptors → anticholinergic syndrome; (3) alpha-1 adrenergic receptors → hypotension; (4) GABA-A modulation → seizures. The combination makes TCA overdose one of the most dangerous in toxicology.
TCA overdose is like throwing a wrench into every gear of the cardiac machine simultaneously — sodium channels stop the conduction system, alpha blockade drops blood pressure, anticholinergic effects cause tachycardia and agitation, and then seizures can push a wide-QRS patient directly into VF.
QRS >100ms in TCA overdose = high risk for ventricular dysrhythmias and seizures. Treatment of choice: Sodium Bicarbonate IV bolus. NaHCO₃ works by (1) alkalinizing blood (sodium channel binding is pH-dependent — alkalosis improves conduction) and (2) providing sodium to overcome sodium channel blockade.

Lithium Toxicity

Lithium has a narrow therapeutic window (0.6–1.2 mEq/L). At toxic levels, it disrupts sodium transport across neuronal membranes. Early signs: tremor, diarrhea, polyuria. Severe: ataxia, confusion, seizures, coma. Treatment is supportive — hemodialysis for severe toxicity.

MAO Inhibitors

Block breakdown of monoamines (NE, dopamine, serotonin). Overdose causes serotonin-like toxicity. Dangerous dietary interactions (tyramine-containing foods → hypertensive crisis). Drug interactions with SSRIs, meperidine, and dextromethorphan can be fatal.

Chapter 32 · Section VII

Alcohol Use Disorder & Emergencies

AUD is the most prevalent substance use disorder in the US. The physiological effects of chronic alcohol use and withdrawal create some of the most complex presentations in emergency medicine.

Alcohol Physiology & Chronic Effects

▾

Ethanol is absorbed 80–90% within 30 minutes (20% stomach, rest small intestine). It's a potent CNS depressant that enhances GABA (inhibitory) and inhibits NMDA glutamate (excitatory) receptors.

Chronic Alcohol → Neuroadaptation: The brain adapts to constant GABA enhancement by DOWNREGULATING GABA receptors and UPREGULATING NMDA receptors. In withdrawal, when alcohol is suddenly removed, GABA inhibition crashes and NMDA excitation surges → CNS hyperexcitability → tremors, seizures, and delirium tremens.
Think of the CNS as a see-saw with GABA (calm) on one side and glutamate (excite) on the other. Chronic alcohol sits heavy on the GABA side. The brain compensates by lightening the GABA side and adding weight to the glutamate side. When alcohol is suddenly removed — the GABA weight disappears, and the glutamate side SLAMS down. That crash is alcohol withdrawal.

Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome

▾

Minor Withdrawal

6–8 hours after last drink
Tremor, anxiety, diaphoresis, tachycardia, nausea, insomnia, hypertension

Alcoholic Hallucinosis

12–24 hours
Visual/auditory/tactile hallucinations. Vital signs relatively normal. Patient knows they're real (unlike DTs)

Withdrawal Seizures

12–48 hours
Usually single tonic-clonic seizure. Can be multiple. Status epilepticus rare but possible.

Delirium Tremens (DTs)

48–96 hours
Full-blown autonomic storm: fever, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, global confusion, tremor. 5–15% mortality if untreated.
DTs represent the peak of the CNS "rebound" after alcohol removal. The entire autonomic nervous system is in hyperdrive — imagine your body simultaneously having a fever, a panic attack, a seizure, and complete cognitive disintegration. Without treatment (benzodiazepines), the hemodynamic stress and hyperthermia can be fatal.
Benzodiazepines are first-line for ALL stages of alcohol withdrawal — they substitute at GABA receptors, preventing CNS hyperexcitability. Diazepam or lorazepam preferred. Thiamine 100 mg IV/IM before any glucose. IV fluids for dehydration. Electrolyte replacement (Mg²⁺ often deficient). Phenobarbital for refractory seizures.

Disulfiram (Antabuse) Reaction

Disulfiram inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase → accumulation of acetaldehyde. Even small amounts of alcohol cause flushing, tachycardia, nausea, hypotension, and dysrhythmias within 15–30 minutes. Treat with IV fluids and supportive care.

Disulfiram turns the "sipping a beer" experience into the worst hangover of your life — compressed into 15 minutes. Acetaldehyde accumulation causes the exact same symptoms as a severe hangover: flushing, pounding pulse, nausea, vomiting, and dread. It's pharmacological aversion therapy.
