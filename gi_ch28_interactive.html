Abdominal & Gastrointestinal Disorders

The abdomen is the most diagnostically challenging region in emergency medicine. Dozens of organ systems share the same real estate — and their distress signals overlap, radiate, and mimic each other. A systematic approach to assessment, combined with deep pathophysiology knowledge, is the paramedic's most powerful tool.

GI BleedingAppendicitis PancreatitisCholecystitis Peptic UlcerEsophageal Varices IBD / Crohn'sBowel Obstruction

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Identify abdominal organs and describe their functions
  • Distinguish visceral, somatic, and referred abdominal pain
  • Perform a systematic prehospital abdominal assessment using OPQRST
  • Recognize and manage GI bleeding, appendicitis, pancreatitis, cholecystitis, esophageal varices, IBD, and bowel obstruction
  • Identify high-risk patient populations for abdominal emergencies

The Abdomen at a Glance — Types of Pain

🔵 Visceral Pain

Poorly localized, cramping or colicky. Comes from autonomic nerve fibers around hollow organs. Often near the umbilicus. Associated with nausea, diaphoresis, tachycardia. Examples: early appendicitis, intestinal obstruction, pancreatitis.

🔴 Somatic Pain

Sharp, localized, constant. From peritoneal irritation — bacterial or chemical. Worsens with movement. Involuntary guarding, rebound tenderness. Examples: perforated ulcer, ruptured appendix, peritonitis.

🗺️ Analogy — Referred Pain

Referred pain is like a faulty GPS signal — the pain message from a visceral organ travels up the spinal cord and merges onto the same highway as skin sensation signals. The brain can't tell which exit the pain came from, so it pins it on a body surface location far from the actual source. Classic example: gallbladder pain felt at the right shoulder tip; cardiac pain felt in the left arm; kidney stones felt in the groin.

GI Anatomy & Physiology

The GI Tract — Structure & Function

The gastrointestinal tract is a 25-foot continuous tube from mouth to anus, supported by accessory organs. Each segment has a specialized role, and understanding their locations predicts where pain will appear when they fail.

Major GI Organs

🔵 Esophagus

Muscular tube carrying food from pharynx to stomach. Lower esophageal sphincter (LES) prevents acid reflux. Portal hypertension can cause variceal enlargement of its veins.

🔵 Stomach

Stores and mixes food with HCl and pepsin. The gastric mucosa is protected by a mucus layer — NSAID/H. pylori damage breaks this barrier, causing ulcers.

🔵 Small Intestine

Duodenum (ulcers here), jejunum, ileum. Site of most nutrient absorption. Crohn's disease commonly affects the terminal ileum. 20 feet long.

🟠 Large Intestine

Cecum, ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid colon, rectum. Absorbs water and electrolytes. Diverticulosis forms here. UC inflames the mucosal lining.

🟠 Liver

Largest abdominal organ — metabolism, detox, clotting factors, bile production. Cirrhosis from chronic hepatitis or alcohol → portal hypertension → varices.

🟠 Pancreas

Dual function: exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine (insulin, glucagon). Pancreatitis = enzymes escaping and digesting the gland and surrounding tissue.

🟢 Gallbladder & Biliary Tree

The gallbladder stores bile produced by the liver. Bile is released into the duodenum via the common bile duct to aid fat digestion. Gallstones (cholelithiasis) can obstruct the cystic duct → cholecystitis, or the common bile duct → cholangitis/pancreatitis. Clay-colored stools indicate biliary obstruction preventing bile from reaching the intestine.

Prehospital Assessment

Assessing the Acute Abdomen

Abdominal pain is one of the most diagnostically challenging presentations in EMS. The paramedic's systematic approach — OPQRST, physical exam sequence, and recognition of peritoneal signs — forms the foundation for all GI emergencies.

📋 OPQRST — Your GI Detective Framework

Think of OPQRST as a six-question diagnostic interview. Each letter unlocks a different dimension of the pain story. A sharp, sudden-onset epigastric pain that worsened after eating fatty food tells a very different story than a gradual, crampy, periumbilical pain that migrated to the RLQ over 12 hours. The history IS the diagnosis in GI emergencies.

O — Onset

Sudden = vascular (AAA rupture, mesenteric ischemia) or perforation. Gradual = inflammation (appendicitis, cholecystitis). With meals = gallbladder, PUD.

P — Provocation

Worse with movement = peritonitis (somatic pain). Better with position = pancreatitis (leaning forward). After fatty meals = cholecystitis.

Q — Quality

Burning = PUD/GERD. Cramping/colicky = obstruction, renal colic. Tearing = AAA, aortic dissection. Constant/severe = peritonitis.

R — Region/Radiation

RUQ → right shoulder = gallbladder. Epigastric → back = pancreatitis. Periumbilical → RLQ = appendicitis. Flank → groin = kidney stone.

S — Severity

Rate 0–10. Pain out of proportion to exam findings suggests mesenteric ischemia. "Worst pain of my life" + tearing = AAA until proven otherwise. Track changes over time.

T — Time

Constant = inflammation or obstruction. Colicky (comes and goes) = hollow organ spasm (bowel obstruction, kidney stone, biliary colic). Duration guides urgency.

⚠️
Sequence Matters: Always use Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation (IAPP). Palpation last because it can alter bowel sounds and cause guarding that makes subsequent steps unreliable.

👁️ Inspection

  • Patient position (fetal = peritonitis)
  • Abdominal distention (obstruction, ascites)
  • Scars (previous surgeries — adhesions risk)
  • Bruising: Cullen's sign (periumbilical ecchymosis) = retroperitoneal hemorrhage; Grey Turner's sign (flank ecchymosis) = pancreatitis/AAA
  • Skin color, facial expression

🔊 Auscultation

  • Must come BEFORE palpation
  • Hyperactive bowel sounds = gastroenteritis, early obstruction
  • Hypoactive/absent = peritonitis, paralytic ileus
  • High-pitched, tinkling = mechanical obstruction
  • Bruit over aorta = AAA

👋 Palpation (Last)

  • Start AWAY from area of pain
  • Note: voluntary vs. involuntary guarding
  • Involuntary guarding (board-like rigidity) = peritonitis
  • Rebound tenderness = peritoneal irritation
  • Murphy's sign = RUQ inspiration pain = cholecystitis
  • McBurney's point tenderness = appendicitis

💊 Diagnostics (Field)

  • 12-lead ECG (rule out inferior MI mimicking GI pain)
  • Blood glucose (Addison crisis, DKA can mimic acute abdomen)
  • Serum lactate (shock, mesenteric ischemia)
  • FAST ultrasound (free fluid, AAA) where available
  • EtCO₂ monitoring

1

Airway & Ventilation

Protect airway in vomiting or obtunded patients. High-flow O₂. Have suction ready — GI bleeds and vomiting are aspiration risks.

2

IV Access & Fluid Resuscitation

Large-bore IV ×2 for suspected GI bleeds. Initiate normal saline or LR for hypovolemia. Target MAP ≥65 mmHg. Avoid over-resuscitation in suspected varices (can worsen bleeding).

3

Monitor

12-lead ECG (rule out cardiac cause). Continuous SpO₂, EtCO₂. Serial vital signs — orthostatic changes indicate significant blood loss. Document BGL and lactate per protocol.

4

Analgesia & Antiemetics

Morphine or fentanyl per protocol. Modern evidence shows analgesics do NOT mask important exam findings. Ondansetron (Zofran) or promethazine for nausea/vomiting. Position of comfort (often fetal/lateral).

5

Rapid Transport

Most abdominal emergencies require definitive surgical or endoscopic care. Document findings thoroughly, provide warm handoff with pertinent positives and negatives.

🚨
High-risk groups present atypically and decompensate rapidly. Maintain a low threshold for aggressive intervention in any of the following populations.

👶 Women of Childbearing Age

Any abdominal pain requires consideration of ectopic pregnancy (life-threatening). Rule in/out pregnancy first. Ovarian torsion, PID, and ruptured ovarian cysts also cause acute abdomen.

🧓 Older Adults

Pain may be minimal or absent despite serious pathology (perforated viscus with minimal guarding). Appendicitis presents atypically. Higher risk for mesenteric ischemia, AAA, MI presenting as abdominal pain.

🏥 Post-Surgical Patients

Adhesions from prior surgery cause bowel obstruction. Recent bariatric surgery patients have unique anatomy and complication profiles (leaks, volvulus). Infection, hemorrhage always possible.

🛡️ Immunocompromised

HIV, chemotherapy, transplant immunosuppression, long-term steroids. Normal inflammatory signs may be blunted or absent. Opportunistic infections (CMV colitis, Candida esophagitis) present atypically. Always suspect the worst.

Upper GI

Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

A breakdown of the gastric mucosal barrier allowing acid and digestive enzymes to erode the lining of the stomach or duodenum. The two main culprits: H. pylori infection and NSAID use. Perforation or hemorrhage can transform a chronic nuisance into a surgical emergency.

H. pyloriNSAIDsPerforation RiskEpigastric Pain

Pathophysiology

🏰 Analogy — The Castle Wall

The stomach lining is a castle wall protecting against an acid moat. The mucus layer is the wall's mortar — constantly repaired by prostaglandins. H. pylori is a tiny saboteur that drills through the mortar and weakens the wall from within. NSAIDs block prostaglandin synthesis — like stopping all the repair crews. Without mortar production or repair crews, the acid eventually eats through the wall itself, causing an ulcer. A perforated ulcer is when the wall completely breaches — gastric acid spills into the peritoneal cavity, causing immediate, severe somatic pain.

1

Mucosal Barrier Disruption

H. pylori secretes urease → creates ammonia → neutralizes acid around bacteria → damages mucus layer. NSAIDs block COX-1 → suppress prostaglandins → ↓ mucus and bicarbonate secretion.

2

Acid Erosion

Unprotected epithelium exposed to HCl and pepsin → superficial erosion → deepens through mucosa → submucosa → muscularis = full ulcer. Duodenal ulcers (most common) typically occur in the first portion of the duodenum.

3

Complications: Bleed or Perforate

Erosion into a blood vessel → GI hemorrhage (hematemesis, melena). Erosion through all layers → perforation → gastric contents spill into peritoneum → immediate severe somatic pain → peritonitis → sepsis.

Signs, Symptoms & Management

🩺 Classic Presentation

  • Burning/gnawing epigastric pain — typically 1–3 hours after meals (gastric) or when empty (duodenal)
  • Pain relieved by food, antacids, or vomiting
  • Nocturnal pain (wakes patient at 2–3 AM)
  • Nausea, belching, bloating
  • Hematemesis or melena if bleeding
  • Board-like rigidity if perforated

🚑 Prehospital Management

  • Obtain pertinent history (NSAID/alcohol use, prior PUD, H. pylori tx)
  • Assess for hemodynamic instability (BP, HR, orthostatics)
  • Large-bore IV ×2 if bleeding suspected
  • Fluid resuscitation for shock
  • Position of comfort; NPO
  • Transport — endoscopy or surgical repair in hospital

Upper GI — Vascular Emergency

Esophageal Varices

Dilated, tortuous veins at the lower esophagus, caused by portal hypertension from liver disease. When they rupture, the result is massive, life-threatening hematemesis with mortality of 20–30% per episode.

Portal HypertensionCirrhosis20–30% MortalityBright Red Hematemesis
🚰 Analogy — The Overpressured Plumbing

Imagine the portal vein system as the city's main water main. Cirrhosis scars the liver, creating a dam in the main line — pressure backs up throughout the system (portal hypertension). Blood needs an escape route, so it reroutes through tiny collateral veins in the esophagus that were never designed for high-pressure flow. These veins balloon out like garden hoses crimped with extreme pressure. One day a violent retch or an increase in pressure snaps one of these engorged hoses — the patient is suddenly vomiting bright red blood by the cupful. No warning, nothing to clamp externally, and the airway is immediately at risk.

Pathophysiology Flow

1

Chronic Liver Disease → Fibrosis

Alcohol, viral hepatitis B/C, fatty liver disease → hepatocyte death → fibrosis and cirrhosis → liver architecture distorted → resistance to portal blood flow.

2

Portal Hypertension Develops

Normal portal pressure: 5–10 mmHg. Cirrhosis → portal pressure >12 mmHg → blood backs up → collateral circulation develops in esophagus, stomach (fundal varices), umbilicus (caput medusae), rectum (hemorrhoids).

3

Variceal Rupture

Triggered by increased intra-abdominal pressure (vomiting, straining), increased portal pressure (alcohol binge), or erosion. Bleeding is typically profuse — 50% of patients with cirrhosis who develop varices will bleed.

4

Hemorrhagic Shock + Airway Compromise

Massive blood loss → hemorrhagic shock. Vomiting blood → aspiration risk → respiratory failure. Hepatic encephalopathy from blood in GI tract → altered mental status worsens airway protection.

EMS Management

🚨 Immediate Priorities

  • Airway is #1 — patient vomiting large volumes of blood
  • Hard-tip (Yankauer) suction IMMEDIATELY
  • Position: left lateral, head of bed elevated (Fowler)
  • Intubation for uncontrolled airway or loss of consciousness
  • Large-bore IV ×2 — fluid resuscitation (avoid over-resuscitation)
  • Rapid transport — endoscopic banding is definitive treatment

💊 Hospital Pharmacology

  • Octreotide — reduces portal venous pressure (vasoconstriction of splanchnic vessels). Drug of choice for acute variceal bleed
  • Vasopressin / Terlipressin — vasopressin analogue, reduces portal pressure
  • Propranolol — for prophylaxis of first bleed/rebleed (lowers portal pressure)
  • Ceftriaxone — prophylactic antibiotics in all variceal bleeds (prevents SBP)
  • Endoscopic band ligation (EVL) — definitive acute treatment
  • TIPS procedure — transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt
🚨
Mallory-Weiss Syndrome: Another cause of hematemesis — longitudinal mucosal tear at the gastroesophageal junction from violent, repeated vomiting. Classic history: prolonged vomiting (alcohol binge) → sudden bright red blood in vomitus. Usually self-limited but can be massive.
GI Emergency

Gastrointestinal Bleeding

GI bleeding ranges from a slow, chronic ooze causing iron-deficiency anemia to a massive, immediately life-threatening hemorrhage. The location determines the presentation — upper vs. lower — and guides resuscitation priorities.

Assessing Severity

📊 Signs of Significant Blood Loss

  • Tachycardia (earliest sign — HR >100)
  • Orthostatic hypotension (>20 mmHg drop on standing)
  • Cool, clammy, pale skin
  • Altered mental status
  • Hypotension (late sign — >30% blood volume lost)
  • Pallor, weakness, dyspnea on exertion (chronic bleeding)

🚑 Prehospital Priorities

  • Airway management (vomiting blood = aspiration risk)
  • High-flow O₂
  • Large-bore IV ×2 — begin fluid resuscitation
  • Measure serum lactate if available
  • Measure BGL (hepatic dysfunction → hypoglycemia)
  • Cardiac monitor — dysrhythmias from acute anemia/shock
  • Rapid transport — endoscopy/surgery required
☕ Clinical Pearl — Coffee Ground Emesis

When blood sits in the stomach and contacts hydrochloric acid, hemoglobin is oxidized to methemoglobin and other compounds — producing the characteristic dark brown, grainy appearance of "coffee grounds." This tells you the bleeding is upper GI and relatively slow — blood had time to be digested before being vomited. Bright red hematemesis = brisk, active upper GI bleed — the blood is arriving too fast to be digested. Melena (tarry, black, foul stools) = 200+ mL of blood digested as it transited the GI tract.

Infectious/Inflammatory

Gastroenteritis — Acute & Chronic

Inflammation of the stomach and intestines causing rapid onset of vomiting and diarrhea. Up to 179 million episodes occur yearly in the US. Most are self-limited, but severe cases cause life-threatening dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.

NorovirusSalmonellaDehydrationFecal-Oral Route
🏭 Analogy — The Factory Shutdown

The GI tract is a sophisticated processing factory. When a pathogen invades (bacterial toxin, viral infection), the body's defense system triggers an emergency shutdown: the factory expels everything as fast as possible — vomiting clears the stomach, diarrhea clears the intestines. This is actually protective, but the cost is enormous fluid and electrolyte loss. In healthy adults, the body can compensate. In the elderly, infants, or immunocompromised, the fluid losses become life-threatening within hours. Norovirus is so efficient it can shut down an entire cruise ship's "factory" simultaneously — just 18 viral particles are enough to cause infection.

🦠 Acute Gastroenteritis — Key Facts

  • Top US pathogens: Norovirus (>50%), Shigella, Salmonella
  • Transmission: fecal-oral, contaminated food/water, person-to-person
  • Settings: long-term care, schools, cruise ships, disaster areas
  • Symptoms: sudden N/V/D, cramping, low-grade fever
  • Treatment: supportive — IV fluids, antiemetics, rest
  • Bacterial causes may need antibiotics

🔄 Chronic Gastroenteritis

  • Prolonged inflammation → long-term mucosal damage
  • Causes: H. pylori, chronic NSAID/alcohol use, hyperacidity
  • Signs: epigastric pain, N/V, fever, anorexia, mucosal bleeding
  • Severe cases → hypovolemia and shock
  • Treatment: dietary regulation, medications, IV fluids

Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Ulcerative Colitis & Crohn Disease

Two distinct but related autoimmune inflammatory conditions causing chronic GI tract destruction. Both produce debilitating symptoms, require lifelong management, and can cause acute exacerbations requiring emergency intervention.

AutoimmuneRelapsing-RemittingBloody DiarrheaYoung Adults

UC vs Crohn — The Critical Comparison

🔥 Analogy — Two Types of Fire

Ulcerative Colitis is like a brush fire that burns only the surface layer — it affects only the mucosal lining of the colon, spreading continuously from the rectum upward. Crohn's disease is like a deep underground fire — it burns through ALL layers of the bowel wall (transmural) and can appear anywhere in the GI tract from mouth to anus, often with skipped "normal" segments between diseased areas. Crohn's deep burns create tunnels (fistulas) and scarring (strictures) that UC surface fires cannot.

EMS Management & Medications

🚑 Prehospital Care

  • Mild flare: supportive care, transport for evaluation
  • Severe flare: airway, IV access, fluid resuscitation for hypovolemia
  • Assess for toxic megacolon (massively dilated colon — surgical emergency)
  • Bowel sounds may be hyperactive or absent
  • Check vitals for dehydration/sepsis signs
  • Emotional support — depression is common in IBD patients

💊 IBD Medications to Know

  • Mesalamine (5-ASA) — first-line for UC; anti-inflammatory in colon
  • Prednisone — corticosteroid for acute flares; significant side effects with long-term use
  • Azathioprine / 6-MP — immunomodulators for maintenance
  • Infliximab (Remicade) — TNF-α blocker; biologic for moderate-severe disease
  • Metronidazole — for perianal Crohn's disease
  • Loperamide — antidiarrheal (use cautiously; avoid in toxic megacolon)
🚨
Toxic Megacolon: Life-threatening complication of UC (less common in Crohn's). The colon dilates massively (>6 cm), loses motility, and risks perforation and sepsis. Presentation: severely ill patient, distended abdomen, absent bowel sounds, fever, tachycardia. Do NOT give anticholinergics, opioids, or antidiarrheal agents — surgical emergency.
Surgical Emergency

Appendicitis

The most common abdominal surgical emergency — affecting 7% of the US population. The natural history is predictable: obstruction → distention → infection → gangrene → perforation → peritonitis. Early recognition prevents rupture.

Ages 10–19McBurney's PointRebound TendernessRupture = Peritonitis
🚰 Analogy — The Clogged Dead-End Pipe

The appendix is a dead-end pipe branching off the cecum. When its opening gets blocked — by a fecalith (hardened stool), lymph node swelling (from viral infection), or foreign body — the appendix continues secreting mucus with nowhere to go. Pressure builds like a water balloon filling past capacity. Bacteria multiply in the stagnant environment, inflammation spreads to the wall, blood supply is cut off (ischemia), the wall dies (gangrene), and finally the balloon pops — spilling infected contents into the peritoneal cavity. That's when diffuse peritonitis and sepsis develop.

Classic Pain Migration Pattern

1

Early: Periumbilical / Diffuse Pain (Visceral)

Obstruction → distension → visceral nerve stimulation → poorly localized, crampy, periumbilical pain. Patient cannot point to one spot. N/V, anorexia, low-grade fever. Pain intensity: mild-moderate.

2

6–12 Hours: RLQ Migration (McBurney's Point)

Inflammation extends to peritoneum → somatic nerve activation → pain localizes to RLQ at McBurney's point (1/3 distance from ASIS to umbilicus). Now sharp, constant, worsened by movement. Rebound tenderness, guarding.

3

Perforation: Temporary Relief → Diffuse Peritonitis

Rupture → brief pain relief as pressure releases → then diffuse pain throughout abdomen as contents spread → board-like rigidity, absent bowel sounds, sepsis. Temperature >38.5°C, leukocytosis. Septic shock can follow.

Special Presentations & Management

⚠️ Atypical Presentations

  • Elderly: minimal pain, fever absent, perforation rate higher — diagnose late
  • Pregnant: appendix displaced upward by uterus → RUQ or flank pain
  • Children under 5: rapid perforation; often misdiagnosed as gastroenteritis
  • Retrocecal appendix: pain more in flank; psoas sign positive
  • Rovsing's sign: LLQ palpation → RLQ pain = appendicitis

🚑 Prehospital Management

  • Position of comfort (often right side, knees flexed)
  • IV access; NPO
  • Analgesia per protocol (does NOT mask findings in modern care)
  • Antiemetics
  • No heat packs to abdomen — may increase perforation risk
  • Rapid transport — appendectomy is curative; delay = perforation
Mechanical Emergency

Bowel Obstruction

Occlusion of the intestinal lumen blocking normal flow of intestinal contents. Small bowel obstruction is most common. The most feared complication is perforation leading to fecal peritonitis and septic shock.

Adhesions #1 CauseColicky PainHigh-Pitched Bowel SoundsDistention
🚧 Analogy — The Highway Gridlock

The intestine is a high-traffic highway for digestive contents. An obstruction is a multi-car pileup that blocks all lanes. Traffic (intestinal contents, gas, fluid) backs up in every direction. The backup creates pressure and distention, which triggers increasingly desperate, powerful muscle contractions (colic) as the bowel tries to force the blockage open. Bowel sounds become high-pitched and hyperactive early (the bowel "screaming"), then go quiet late as the muscle exhausts itself or the bowel perforates — like a highway that eventually goes ghost-town silent because everyone turned around. A perforated bowel is the highway barrier finally crashing down — catastrophic spillage into the surrounding area.

🔍 Causes

  • Adhesions — #1 cause of small bowel obstruction (prior surgery)
  • Hernias — incarcerated or strangulated hernia
  • Volvulus — bowel twisting on its mesentery
  • Intussusception — bowel telescoping into itself (pediatric)
  • Tumor — #1 cause of large bowel obstruction
  • Fecal impaction — elderly, constipated patients
  • Paralytic ileus — functional (post-op, electrolyte disturbance)

🩺 Presentation

  • Crampy, colicky abdominal pain (intermittent waves)
  • Abdominal distention
  • Nausea and vomiting (bilious early; feculent late = distal obstruction)
  • Obstipation (absence of flatus and stool)
  • High-pitched, tinkling bowel sounds = early mechanical obstruction
  • Absent bowel sounds = late (ileus, ischemia, perforation)
  • Fever, leukocytosis, peritoneal signs = strangulation/perforation

🚑 Prehospital Management

  • IV access; fluid resuscitation for dehydration
  • Analgesia and antiemetics per protocol
  • NG tube for vomiting/decompression if available and indicated
  • Cardiac monitoring — electrolyte abnormalities from repeated vomiting can cause dysrhythmias
  • NPO — surgery is likely
  • Rapid transport — strangulated bowel → ischemia → infarction within hours
Colonic Disorder

Diverticulosis & Diverticulitis

Diverticulosis (pouches in the colon wall) is extremely common in Western populations — present in over 50% of Americans by age 60. Most are asymptomatic, but complications — diverticulitis (inflammation) and hemorrhage — can be serious.

Low-Fiber DietLLQ Pain#1 Lower GI Bleed CauseAges 40+
🎈 Analogy — Inner Tube Herniation

The colon wall has inner (mucosal) and outer (muscular) layers. A low-fiber diet means the colon has to work harder to move dry, hard stool — generating high pressure with every contraction. Over years, this high pressure blows small bubbles (diverticula) through weak points in the muscular layer, like patches on an inner tube bulging through a worn tire wall. Each diverticulum is a potential trap — stool can get caught in it, causing inflammation (diverticulitis), or a blood vessel running along the base can erode and rupture (diverticular hemorrhage — the #1 cause of lower GI bleeding).

📋 Diverticulosis (No Inflammation)

  • Usually asymptomatic — discovered incidentally on colonoscopy
  • Mild symptoms: irregular bowel habits, bloating, cramping
  • Significant lower GI bleeding — bright red rectal bleeding, often painless
  • Treatment: high-fiber diet, stay hydrated

🔥 Diverticulitis (Inflammation)

  • Fecal material trapped in diverticulum → bacterial overgrowth → inflammation
  • LLQ pain (sigmoid colon most affected) — "left-sided appendicitis"
  • Fever, chills, leukocytosis, nausea, vomiting
  • Complications: abscess, fistula, stricture, perforation, peritonitis
  • Treatment: antibiotics, IV fluids, bowel rest; surgery for complications
Organ Emergency

Pancreatitis

Inflammation of the pancreas triggered by premature activation of digestive enzymes. The pancreas literally begins digesting itself and surrounding tissues. Can range from mild and self-limited to severe, multi-organ failure with 20–30% mortality.

Gallstones #1 CauseAlcohol #2Epigastric Pain → BackGrey Turner / Cullen Signs
💣 Analogy — Explosives Factory on Fire

The pancreas is an explosives factory that manufactures digestive enzymes (protease, lipase, amylase) — all kept safely inactivated until they reach the small intestine. In pancreatitis, a spark ignites the factory from within — enzymes activate prematurely inside the gland. Trypsin triggers a cascade, activating other enzymes that begin destroying the gland's own tissue (autodigestion), blood vessels (hemorrhage), and fat cells in surrounding tissue (fat necrosis). The fire can spread: systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), ARDS, acute kidney injury, and septic shock can all result from enzymes and inflammatory mediators released into the bloodstream.

Causes & Pathophysiology

🪨 Gallstones (40–70%)

Common bile duct obstruction → bile backs up into pancreatic duct → activates pancreatic enzymes prematurely. Classic: acute onset after fatty meal in overweight female.

🍺 Alcohol (30%)

Direct toxic effect on acinar cells + spasm of sphincter of Oddi → enzyme stasis. Usually requires years of heavy use. Chronic pancreatitis = recurrent acute episodes → scarring → exocrine/endocrine failure.

⚡ Other Causes

Hypertriglyceridemia (TG >1000 mg/dL), hypercalcemia, trauma, ERCP procedure, medications (thiazides, steroids, azathioprine), scorpion venom, idiopathic (10%).

Signs, Symptoms & Management

🩺 Clinical Presentation

  • Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back — hallmark
  • Pain relieved slightly by leaning forward (reduces pressure on celiac plexus)
  • Nausea and vomiting (often not relieved by vomiting)
  • Fever, tachycardia in moderate-severe disease
  • Cullen's sign (periumbilical ecchymosis) — hemorrhagic pancreatitis
  • Grey Turner's sign (flank ecchymosis) — hemorrhagic pancreatitis
  • Abdominal guarding, distention
  • Hypotension/shock in severe cases

🚑 Prehospital & Hospital Management

  • Large-bore IV ×2; aggressive fluid resuscitation (LR preferred)
  • Analgesia (fentanyl IV per protocol)
  • Antiemetics
  • NPO — bowel rest
  • NG tube if vomiting is uncontrolled
  • Cardiac monitoring — electrolyte abnormalities common
  • Watch for ARDS, AKI, DIC in severe disease
  • Hospital: IV fluids, pain control, treat underlying cause (ERCP for stones)

Biliary Emergency

Cholecystitis & Biliary Disease

Inflammation of the gallbladder, almost always from gallstone obstruction. One of the most common GI emergencies — more than 750,000 cholecystectomies are performed in the US annually. The classic "4 Fs" patient: Fat, Female, Forty, Fertile.

4 FsRUQ PainMurphy's SignPost-Fatty Meal
🪨 Analogy — The Rock in the Drain

The gallbladder is a small reservoir that stores bile, releasing it through the cystic duct when a fatty meal arrives. Gallstones form when bile becomes supersaturated with cholesterol — like mineral deposits forming in a hard-water pipe. When a stone rolls into the cystic duct it's like a rock blocking the drain. The gallbladder contracts against the obstruction, causing intense biliary colic (episodic cramping RUQ pain). If the stone stays lodged, bile backs up, bacteria colonize the stagnant bile, and the gallbladder wall becomes inflamed (cholecystitis). The gallbladder swells, and the surrounding peritoneum becomes irritated — triggering Murphy's sign.

🩺 Presentation

  • RUQ pain — onset typically 30–60 minutes after fatty meal
  • Pain radiates to right shoulder or subscapular area
  • Fever, chills, leukocytosis
  • Murphy's sign — inability to inspire during RUQ palpation
  • Nausea, vomiting (often bile-stained)
  • Jaundice if CBD obstruction (Charcot's triad: fever + jaundice + RUQ pain = cholangitis)

🚑 Prehospital Management

  • IV access — fluids for dehydration
  • Analgesia per protocol (ketorolac works well — anti-inflammatory + analgesic)
  • Antiemetics for nausea
  • NPO — surgery likely
  • Transport for imaging (ultrasound is gold standard for gallstones)
  • Rule out: MI (inferior wall), PUD, pancreatitis, right-sided pneumonia
⚠️
Charcot's Triad vs Reynolds' Pentad: Charcot's Triad (fever + jaundice + RUQ pain) = cholangitis (bile duct infection). Reynolds' Pentad adds altered mental status + septic shock = ascending cholangitis — a life-threatening emergency requiring urgent biliary decompression.

Liver Disease

Acute Hepatitis & Liver Emergencies

Hepatitis — inflammation of the liver — is the leading cause of liver disease worldwide. The spectrum ranges from mild, self-limited illness to fulminant hepatic failure. The liver's central role in metabolism, coagulation, and detoxification means liver disease produces systemic consequences far beyond the RUQ.

Clinical Features & EMS Approach

🩺 Signs & Symptoms

  • Prodrome: malaise, fatigue, anorexia, nausea, fever
  • RUQ pain / hepatomegaly
  • Jaundice (yellowing of skin/sclera) — bilirubin >2.5 mg/dL
  • Dark urine (bilirubin) + pale/clay-colored stools (absent bile)
  • Pruritus (from bile salts in skin)
  • Signs of chronic liver disease: spider angiomas, palmar erythema, asterixis, caput medusae

🚑 EMS Management

  • Strict BSI — Hep B/C transmitted by blood contact; ensure gloves/eye protection
  • Support ABCs; assess mental status (hepatic encephalopathy)
  • IV access; fluid support
  • Glucose check (impaired gluconeogenesis → hypoglycemia)
  • Watch for coagulopathy signs (liver makes clotting factors)
  • All EMS personnel must be vaccinated against Hep B
  • Document exposure if needlestick — post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) available for HBV/HIV
💉
Hepatic Encephalopathy: Liver failure → ammonia not cleared → central nervous system toxicity → confusion, asterixis (flapping tremor), slurred speech, and ultimately coma. Common precipitants: GI bleeding (blood = protein = ammonia), infection, constipation, sedatives. Treat with lactulose (traps ammonia in gut) and rifaximin (reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria).
