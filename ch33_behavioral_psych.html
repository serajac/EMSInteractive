Understanding Behavioral Emergencies
Biologic, Psychosocial & Sociocultural Causes
Core Concept

A behavioral emergency is any situation in which a patient's mood or behavior presents an imminent risk of harm or death to self or others requiring immediate attention. Approximately 1 in 5.6 people have mental illnesses that severely limit their daily activities — and about 15% of ED visits relate to mental health or substance use disorders.

🔬 Pathophysiology — The Three Root Causes
Biologic: Physical or biochemical brain disturbances — genetic factors, prenatal/postnatal injury, neurotransmitter imbalance (dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, GABA). These are true medical conditions, not character flaws.

Psychosocial: Personality, coping mechanisms, and life experiences — childhood trauma, abuse, dysfunctional family structure. The brain's "wiring" shaped by experience.

Sociocultural: How a person balances emotions in society — personal relationships, economic status, social cohesion, belief systems. When this balance shifts rapidly, emotional turmoil can trigger a crisis.
💡 Clinical Analogy
Think of the brain as a complex sound system. Biologic causes are hardware failures — broken speakers or a damaged amplifier. Psychosocial causes are bad recordings that only play distorted sounds. Sociocultural causes are an unstable power supply — everything works in the right conditions but fluctuates under stress. Most psychiatric disorders involve some combination of all three.
⚕ Paramedic Pearl
Always consider organic (medical) causes before assuming a purely psychiatric etiology. Hypoglycemia, hypoxia, head trauma, drug toxicity, and sepsis can all produce psychiatric-appearing symptoms. Your differential diagnosis must rule out the killers first.

Neurocognitive Disorders
Delirium · Dementia · Neurodevelopmental Disorders
Delirium
ICD-10: F05 | Acute Organic Brain Syndrome
Acute · Reversible · Life-Threatening
🔬 Deep Pathophysiology
Delirium results from diffuse cerebral dysfunction caused by systemic insults rather than primary brain disease. Multiple mechanisms converge: neuroinflammation (cytokine release disrupts blood-brain barrier), neurotransmitter dysregulation (excess dopamine, deficient acetylcholine — the "too much gas, not enough brakes" problem), oxidative stress, and disrupted sleep-wake cycles mediated by the reticular activating system. The hippocampus and prefrontal cortex (attention, memory, executive function) are particularly vulnerable to these metabolic insults.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Computer Analogy
Delirium is like a computer running too many background processes while the power supply is unstable. The hardware (brain) is intact, but the operating system is crashing. Fix the power supply issue (the underlying medical cause) and the computer returns to normal. This is why delirium is reversible — unlike dementia where the hardware itself degrades.

Key distinctions: ACUTE onset (hours to days), FLUCTUATING course (waxes and wanes), INATTENTION as hallmark feature, often reversible with treatment of underlying cause.

Signs & Symptoms
Acute disorientation
Inattention (cardinal sign)
Fluctuating consciousness
Visual/tactile hallucinations
Disorganized thinking
Loss of judgment
Psychomotor agitation
Memory impairment
⚠ High-Risk Populations
Elderly patients, critically ill children, post-surgical patients, alcohol/drug withdrawal, poor nutritional status, vision/hearing impairment, pre-existing depression.

EMS Management: Target the underlying cause — correct hypoglycemia, ensure oxygenation, establish IV access, monitor vitals. Sedatives (benzodiazepines) may be required to reduce dangerous agitation. Avoid physical restraint alone — it worsens agitation and can cause positional asphyxia.

Dementia (Major Neurocognitive Disorder)
ICD-10: F03 | Chronic, Progressive, Irreversible
Chronic · Progressive · Irreversible
🔬 Deep Pathophysiology — The Four Main Types
Alzheimer Disease (60-70%): Accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques between neurons and tau protein tangles inside neurons. Progressive loss of acetylcholine-producing neurons in the nucleus basalis. The hippocampus (memory formation) degrades first, then spreads to temporal, parietal, and frontal cortex.

Vascular Dementia (15-20%): Cumulative ischemic damage from multiple small strokes. "Stroke by a thousand cuts" — each infarct destroys more cognitive reserve.

Lewy Body Dementia: Alpha-synuclein protein deposits in cortical neurons. Often presents with parkinsonian features, visual hallucinations, and dramatic sleep disturbances. Paradoxically, antipsychotics can be fatal in Lewy body patients.

Frontotemporal Dementia: Tau protein accumulation in frontal/temporal lobes. Personality changes and disinhibition precede memory loss — patients may appear to have a personality disorder.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Library Analogy
Dementia is like a library where the books are slowly being destroyed, starting from the newest acquisitions (recent memories) and working backward toward the oldest (remote memories, procedural skills). The librarian (working memory) eventually loses the ability to catalog or retrieve information. Unlike delirium, you cannot restore the burned books — management focuses on preserving what remains and preventing dangerous behaviors.
⚕ Paramedic Pearl — Delirium vs. Dementia
Use the "DELIRIOUS" heuristic: Delirium = acutELY onset, fLUCTUATING, Inattention, Usually Reversible. Dementia = Insidious onset, Stable progression, NOT primarily inattention, Ultimately irreversible. A demented patient can develop superimposed delirium — always assess for this in any dementia patient who "suddenly seems worse."
Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
Neurodevelopmental | Variable Severity
Neurodevelopmental
🔬 Pathophysiology
ASD involves disrupted neural connectivity — particularly between the frontal cortex (social cognition, executive function) and other brain regions. Abnormal synaptic pruning during development leads to hyper-connectivity in local circuits but reduced long-range connectivity. Mirror neuron system dysfunction impairs social mirroring and empathy processing. Many ASD patients have altered sensory gating, making ordinary stimuli overwhelming — their nervous system cannot filter out irrelevant sensory input like a neurotypical brain does.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The High-Speed WiFi Problem
Imagine having every WiFi network in the building broadcasting at full strength simultaneously, with no ability to filter or prioritize. For an ASD patient in a busy EMS scene — sirens, lights, strangers, unfamiliar smells, physical touch — each stimulus competes equally for processing. This sensory overload, not stubbornness or defiance, drives behavioral crises.

EMS Strategies: Remain calm and move slowly. Examine from toe to head. Remove sensory distractions. Use simple, clear language. Minimize adhesives and tactile contact. Ask caregivers for calming techniques. Recognize pain may manifest as laughing, singing, or disrobing.

⚠ Critical EMS Point
About 30% of ASD patients engage in self-injurious behaviors. These are not suicide attempts — they are regulatory behaviors to manage overwhelming internal states. Treat the injury; address the underlying sensory or emotional trigger.

Schizophrenia Spectrum Disorders
Thought Disorders · Psychosis · Dopamine Hypothesis
Schizophrenia
ICD-10: F20 | Onset typically 16–30 years
Chronic · Treatable · Multifactorial
🔬 Deep Pathophysiology — The Dopamine Hypothesis & Beyond
Dopamine Dysregulation: The mesolimbic pathway (VTA → limbic system) shows dopamine HYPERACTIVITY → positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions). The mesocortical pathway (VTA → prefrontal cortex) shows dopamine HYPOACTIVITY → negative/cognitive symptoms (flat affect, poverty of speech, cognitive deficits). This is why treating only dopamine excess can worsen negative symptoms.

Glutamate Hypothesis: NMDA receptor hypofunction in the prefrontal cortex impairs GABAergic interneurons, which normally regulate dopamine release. This creates a "disinhibited" dopamine system — explaining why PCP and ketamine (NMDA antagonists) can produce schizophrenia-like states.

Structural Changes: Enlarged ventricles, reduced gray matter in temporal and prefrontal cortex, abnormal thalamic gating (the brain's "sensory filter" fails, flooding consciousness with unfiltered information).
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Broken Spam Filter
The thalamus normally acts as a spam filter for consciousness — irrelevant sensory data gets blocked before reaching awareness. In schizophrenia, this filter breaks. Internal thoughts get tagged as external voices. Background patterns become meaningful signals. Random events become coordinated conspiracies. The brain isn't lying — it's receiving and processing real signals; they just aren't originating where the patient believes they are.

Anxiety, OCD & Trauma-Related Disorders
Panic Disorder · Phobia · OCD · PTSD
Panic Disorder
ICD-10: F41.0 | Sympathetic Storm
Anxiety Spectrum
🔬 Deep Pathophysiology — The False Alarm System
The amygdala's fear circuit fires a massive sympathetic response without any real threat. Norepinephrine surges, activating the locus coeruleus (the brain's "alarm center"). Simultaneously, the prefrontal cortex (which would normally evaluate and suppress the alarm) is overwhelmed, creating a positive feedback loop: physiologic symptoms → catastrophic interpretation → more fear → more symptoms.

The disorder involves hypersensitivity of chemoreceptors (CO₂ receptors), causing patients to physiologically "hyperventilate" their own panic into escalation. Serotonin deficiency in the amygdala reduces the threshold for fear activation.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Smoke Alarm in a Fireproof Room
Panic disorder is like a hypersensitive smoke alarm that triggers full building evacuation every time someone makes toast. The threat assessment (prefrontal cortex) knows there's no fire, but the alarm system (amygdala) has already put the body on full emergency alert. The racing heart, shortness of breath, and chest pain are real physiologic responses to a false alarm signal.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
ICD-10: F42 | Cortico-Striato-Thalamo-Cortical Loop
OCD Spectrum
🔬 Pathophysiology — The Stuck Record
OCD involves hyperactivity of the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) circuit — a loop that normally processes behavioral patterns and habits. In OCD, this circuit gets "stuck" — the orbitofrontal cortex generates an alarm signal (obsession), the thalamus amplifies it, and the caudate nucleus (which should inhibit the loop) fails to shut it off. Serotonin is critical for caudate function; SSRIs (which boost serotonin) are the primary pharmacological treatment. Related disorders (hoarding, body dysmorphic disorder, trichotillomania) share this neural substrate.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Error-Checking Loop
Imagine a computer program with a bug in its error-checking routine — it keeps running the same error check infinitely because it can't get a "clear" signal. The patient knows intellectually that their hands aren't contaminated after the 20th washing, but the brain's error-checking system keeps insisting something is wrong. The compulsion temporarily resets the error flag, but the bug remains.
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
ICD-10: F43.1 | HPA Axis Dysregulation
Trauma & Stressor Related
🔬 Deep Pathophysiology — The Permanent Alarm State
PTSD involves structural and functional changes in three key areas:
Amygdala: Hyperactive — triggers fear responses to trauma-related cues, even when safe. The "smoke alarm" remains permanently on high sensitivity.
Hippocampus: Reduced volume from cortisol-mediated neurotoxicity — impairs context processing, so memories aren't stored as "past" — they feel present and real during flashbacks.
Prefrontal Cortex: Hypoactive — reduced ability to inhibit the amygdala or reality-test. The "supervisor" has lost authority over the alarm system.
HPA axis dysregulation leads to abnormal cortisol rhythms. Norepinephrine hypersensitivity explains hyperarousal, startle responses, and sleep disturbances.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Trauma Scar
Normal memory is stored like a properly filed document with a timestamp. Traumatic memory in PTSD is stored without a timestamp — the hippocampal damage means the brain cannot mark it as "past." During a flashback, the nervous system experiences the event as happening NOW, generating an identical physiologic response to the original trauma. The wound doesn't have a scar — it keeps reopening.

Four Symptom Clusters: Intrusion (flashbacks, nightmares), Avoidance (trauma reminders), Arousal/Reactivity (hypervigilance, irritability), Cognition/Mood (negative beliefs, emotional numbing). EMS responders are at high PTSD risk — know your own triggers and use peer support resources.

⚠ EMS Provider Risk
EMS personnel are subject to PTSD as a direct result of occupational trauma exposure. Recognize that PTSD is a neurobiologic injury, not weakness. Seek peer support, CISM debriefings, and professional counseling when needed.

Mood Disorders
Depression · Bipolar Disorder · Suicide Risk Assessment
Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
ICD-10: F32 | Monoamine Deficiency + Neuroinflammation
Most Common Psychiatric Disorder
🔬 Deep Pathophysiology — Beyond "Low Serotonin"
The classic monoamine hypothesis (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine deficiency) is necessary but insufficient. Modern understanding includes:
Neuroinflammation: Elevated cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) reduce neurogenesis in the hippocampus and disrupt monoamine synthesis. This explains the strong link between systemic illness (MI, stroke, diabetes, cancer) and depression.
HPA Axis Hyperactivation: Chronic cortisol elevation causes hippocampal atrophy — destroying the very structure needed for memory and contextual processing.
Default Mode Network (DMN): Hyperactive DMN produces excessive self-referential, ruminating thought — the "hamster wheel" of depressive cognition.
Glutamate Dysregulation: NMDA receptor overactivation (the mechanism targeted by ketamine's rapid antidepressant effect within hours rather than the weeks needed by SSRIs).
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Dead Battery
Depression is like a phone with a dying battery and a corrupted operating system. The hardware works, but the battery drains faster than it charges (anhedonia, fatigue) and the OS keeps running a loop that tells the user the phone is worthless and irreparable (hopelessness, worthlessness). SSRIs don't "add" serotonin — they prevent its reuptake, giving the brain more time to use what little it's producing, like using power-saving mode to extend a dying battery.

Bipolar Disorder
ICD-10: F31 | Neurochemical Pendulum
Biphasic · Cyclic · Lithium-Responsive
🔬 Pathophysiology — The Sensitization Model
Bipolar disorder involves dysregulation of second messenger systems (cAMP, phosphoinositol) in neurons, leading to unstable mood states. The kindling model explains escalating episode frequency over time — each mood episode sensitizes the brain to future episodes, like how a broken bone heals with calcium deposits that make re-fracture more likely at the same site. Lithium works by stabilizing these second messenger cascades, preventing both poles of the mood swing.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Thermostat with a Broken Setpoint
Imagine a building thermostat that oscillates between -10°F and 120°F with no stable setpoint. Mania is the 120° state — everything running at maximum: talking, spending, thinking, sexual activity. Depression is the -10° state — nothing moving at all. Lithium repairs the thermostat's setpoint mechanism, maintaining a stable range. Without it, the oscillations tend to get wider and more frequent over time.

Mania Hallmarks: Impulsive activities, excessive elation, talkativeness, flight of ideas, distractibility, accelerated speech, decreased need for sleep, delusions of grandeur. DIGFAST mnemonic: Distractibility, Impulsivity/Indiscretion, Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity increase, Sleep decrease, Talkativeness.

Suicide Risk Assessment
Critical Life-Threatening Emergency
⚠ HIGH PRIORITY — Every Call
⚠ Epidemiology — Know the Numbers
45,000+ suicides annually in the US. Suicide was the 9th leading cause of death ages 10–64 (2020). For every completed suicide, 275 people seriously considered it. Firearms used in >50% of suicides. Men die from suicide at 4× the rate of women. Highest risk: non-Hispanic White and American Indian/Alaska Native males over 85.
📚 Suicide Risk Assessment — Ask These Three
1
Does the patient have a PLAN to commit suicide?
2
Is the plan LETHAL/INTENDED to be successful?
3
Does the patient have the MEANS/METHOD to follow through?

Direct questions to ask: "Do you have thoughts about killing yourself or others?" "Have you ever tried to kill yourself?" Direct questioning does NOT increase suicide risk — it opens a therapeutic dialogue.

Additional Risk Factors: History of prior attempts (strongest predictor), family history of suicide, recent loss or grief, financial setback/job loss, chronic illness, social isolation, alcohol/drug use, major depression, bipolar, schizophrenia.

⚕ Paramedic Pearl — Six Steps to Reduce Suicide Risk
1) Provide honest assurance about well-being. 2) Ensure physical AND emotional security. 3) Listen without requiring answers to every statement. 4) Determine support systems. 5) Encourage and reassure during the crisis. 6) Transport to appropriate facility. Scene safety FIRST — request law enforcement before approaching.

Other Psychiatric Disorders
Somatic · Dissociative · Eating · Personality Disorders
Somatic Symptom & Factitious Disorders
Real Suffering, No Organic Cause
Functional Disorders
🔬 Pathophysiology — When the Brain Creates Real Symptoms
Somatic symptom disorders arise from disruptions in the brain-body communication network. The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and insula, which process pain and interoception, show abnormal activity — generating genuine pain, weakness, and sensory changes without peripheral pathology. In Conversion Disorder, emotional conflict is "converted" into neurologic symptoms (paralysis, blindness, seizures) via suppression of motor/sensory cortex activity by the ACC. The symptom is not faked — it represents a real but functional (not structural) disruption of neural pathways.
💡 Clinical Analogy — Software vs Hardware
Conversion disorder is a software bug in a hardware system that's perfectly intact. The motor cortex could activate the leg, but the software instructions are blocked by an emotional "firewall." This is why symptoms don't follow anatomic distributions and why they can disappear suddenly with psychological treatment — you're patching the software, not the hardware.

Munchausen Syndrome (Factitious Disorder): Patient deliberately feigns or induces illness to assume the sick role and gain attention/sympathy. Munchausen By Proxy: patient causes illness in others (usually a child) to gain sympathy. Document findings objectively without confrontation — your PCR may be evidence in a legal investigation.

⚕ EMS Management Principle
ALWAYS treat somatic symptoms as real because you cannot differentiate functional from organic disease in the field. The patient experiencing conversion disorder has real functional impairment. Dismissing them risks missing a true organic emergency AND traumatizes an already vulnerable patient.
Dissociative Disorders
ICD-10: F44 | Consciousness Fragmentation
Trauma-Related
🔬 Pathophysiology — The Mind's Emergency Exit
Dissociation is the brain's protective mechanism during overwhelming trauma — a functional disconnection between normally integrated mental processes. Neuroimaging shows reduced activity in the prefrontal cortex (the "narrator" of self) and altered thalamic gating during dissociative episodes. In Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID), different neural networks can dominate consciousness with distinct memory, affect, and behavioral patterns — formerly called "multiple personality disorder."
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Emergency Bulkhead
When a ship is flooding, emergency bulkheads seal off compartments to prevent total sinking. Dissociation seals off traumatic memories or aspects of identity to prevent psychological flooding. The problem is that the bulkheads remain sealed even after the storm — creating isolated compartments of memory and identity that cannot communicate with each other.

Key Types: Dissociative Amnesia (blocking vital personal information, typically trauma-related), Dissociative Identity Disorder (distinct identity states), Depersonalization Disorder (feeling detached from one's own body/mind — "watching yourself from outside").

Eating Disorders
Anorexia Nervosa · Bulimia Nervosa
Life-Threatening Metabolic Emergencies
🔬 Pathophysiology — The Broken Body Map
Eating disorders involve disruption of the brain's interoceptive representation of the body. In Anorexia Nervosa, the insula (body map) shows a distorted representation — patients genuinely perceive their body as larger than it is (not a cognitive distortion but a perceptual one). Starvation amplifies serotonin signaling in the hypothalamus, paradoxically reducing hunger — creating a positive feedback loop where not eating reduces anxiety. In Bulimia, dopamine dysregulation creates binge-purge cycles analogous to other impulse control disorders.
⚠ Critical EMS Complications
Anorexia: electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia → dysrhythmia), hypoglycemia, hypothermia, cardiac failure. Bulimia: hypokalemia from purging → QTc prolongation and Torsades de Pointes, esophageal tears (Mallory-Weiss), dental erosion, Russell's sign (calluses on dorsum of hand). The HIGHEST mortality of any psychiatric disorder belongs to Anorexia Nervosa (up to 10%).
Personality Disorders
DSM-5 Cluster A, B & C
Enduring Patterns | Developmental
🔬 Overview — DSM-5 Cluster Classification
Cluster A (Odd/Eccentric): Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal — characterized by social isolation, magical thinking, and perceptual distortions. Genetically linked to schizophrenia spectrum.

Cluster B (Dramatic/Erratic): Antisocial (ASPD), Borderline (BPD), Histrionic, Narcissistic — dysregulated emotion, impulsivity, interpersonal dysfunction. BPD is associated with childhood trauma and HPA axis dysregulation.

Cluster C (Anxious/Fearful): Avoidant, Dependent, Obsessive-Compulsive — pervasive anxiety and fear. High overlap with anxiety disorders.
💡 Clinical Analogy — Personality as a Cognitive Lens
Everyone views the world through a cognitive "lens" shaped by genetics and experience. Personality disorders are like a severely distorted lens — the patient consistently misinterprets interactions through their particular distortion. You cannot correct the lens in the field. Your job is to avoid triggering the distortion while providing care. A patient with Borderline PD splitting you as "all good" then "all bad" within one call is not manipulating you — it's their neurobiologic reality.

Violent Patients & Restraint
Hyperactive Delirium · De-escalation · Chemical & Physical Restraint
Delirium with Agitated Behavior (Hyperactive Delirium)
Formerly "Excited Delirium" — Acute Life-Threatening Emergency
⚡ LIFE-THREATENING — Immediate Management Required
🔬 Pathophysiology — Dopamine Storm + Hyperthermia
Hyperactive delirium with severe agitation represents a massive catecholamine surge — dopamine and norepinephrine release at levels that exceed the body's regulatory capacity. Stimulant intoxication (cocaine, methamphetamine) blocks dopamine reuptake, creating dopamine toxicity in the limbic system. The resulting extreme agitation generates tremendous heat through sustained isometric muscle activity, while the hypothalamic thermostat is simultaneously impaired. This combination — catecholamine storm + hyperthermia + metabolic acidosis + physical struggle — creates a perfect storm for sudden cardiac death via ventricular fibrillation.
💡 Clinical Analogy — The Engine Running Without Oil
Hyperactive delirium is like an engine redlining at maximum RPM without oil or cooling. The sustained muscle activity burns glucose rapidly, produces dangerous lactate, raises core temperature to levels that denature proteins, and ultimately pushes the heart — also under extreme catecholamine load — into fatal arrhythmia. Rapid sedation is the equivalent of cutting the ignition before the engine seizes. Every minute of continued struggle is organ damage.

⚠ Avoid Prone Restraint
Prone restraint + agitation + obesity + excited delirium = positional asphyxia risk. After restraint, monitor airway, breathing, and circulation continuously. Reposition to lateral or supine position whenever possible. Never leave a restrained patient unmonitored.
De-escalation — Ten Domains
Communication as Intervention
Evidence-Based De-escalation
1. Respect Personal Space — Stay ≥2 arm lengths from patient
2. Avoid Provocation — Unclenched hands visible, calm body language
3. Establish Verbal Contact — ONE person talks; introduce yourself; address by name
4. Be Concise — Short sentences, non-clinical words, repeat key messages
5. Identify Wants & Feelings — Determine patient's top priority
6. Active Listening — Full attention, validate emotions
7. Agree or Agree to Disagree — Find common ground; agree with truth, principle, or odds
8. Set Clear Limits — Calmly state what is and is not acceptable
9. Offer Choice & Optimism — Propose alternatives to violence; no false promises
10. Debrief — Explain all restraint actions to patient, family, and crew afterward

⚠ Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)
Life-threatening reaction to antipsychotics: FEVER (high-grade), RIGIDITY ("lead-pipe" muscle rigidity), ALTERED MENTAL STATUS, AUTONOMIC INSTABILITY (tachycardia, BP lability, diaphoresis). Mortality up to 20% if untreated. Stop the antipsychotic. Cool the patient aggressively. IVF. Rapid transport. Treatment: dantrolene, bromocriptine.
Antidepressants
DrugClassOverdose Alert
Fluoxetine, Sertraline, CitalopramSSRISerotonin syndrome; QTc↑ (citalopram >40 mg). Treat serotonin syndrome with cyproheptadine, benzos, cooling.
Venlafaxine, DuloxetineSNRIHypertension, tachycardia in overdose. Discontinuation syndrome: "brain zaps"
Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline, ImipramineTCA⚠ LETHAL in OD: QRS widening, wide-complex arrhythmia, seizures, coma. Treat with NaHCO₃ 1–2 mEq/kg IV.
Phenelzine, TranylcypromineMAOI⚠ Hypertensive crisis with tyramine (aged cheese, wine), fatal with sympathomimetics (epinephrine, dopamine). DO NOT give meperidine (Demerol) — can be fatal.
