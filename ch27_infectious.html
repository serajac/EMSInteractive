disease progression

Stages of Infectious Disease

Stage 1
Incubation
Pathogen invades; no symptoms yet. Ranges from hours (influenza) to years (HIV). Patient is contagious but unaware.
Stage 2
Prodromal
Nonspecific early symptoms: fatigue, low fever, malaise. Highly contagious stage. Patient often still ambulatory.
Stage 3
Illness
Peak symptoms specific to the disease. Highest immune response. Body attempting to eliminate pathogen.
Stage 4
Convalescence
Recovery phase. Symptoms subside; energy gradually returns. Some diseases remain contagious.
EMS operational knowledge

Key Concepts

🛡️
PPE Hierarchy
Standard: Gloves, gown
Droplet: + Surgical mask, eye protection
Airborne: + N95/PAPR, negative pressure room
Contact: + Dedicated equipment
🔬
Decontamination Levels
Cleaning: Remove gross contamination
Disinfection: Kill most microorganisms
Sterilization: Destroy ALL organisms including spores
Spaulding Classification guides level needed.
📋
DICO System
Designated Infection Control Officer manages agency exposure protocols, post-exposure prophylaxis coordination, medical evaluation referrals, and OSHA bloodborne pathogen compliance.
📢
Exposure Reporting
Report immediately after any significant exposure to blood/body fluids. Document source patient information. Initiate PEP within 2 hours for HIV exposure for best efficacy. Ryan White Act protections apply.
💉
Host Defenses
Non-specific: Skin, mucous membranes, fever, phagocytes
Specific (adaptive): B-cells (antibodies), T-cells (cell-mediated). Compromised by immunosuppression, malnutrition, extremes of age.
⚠️
Antibiotic Resistance
MRSA, C. diff, CRE, and drug-resistant gonorrhea represent major threats. Contact precautions for MRSA/C. diff. Overuse and incomplete antibiotic courses drive resistance evolution.

SIRS, Sepsis & Septic Shock

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Rapid recognition and intervention are critical.

screening tool

qSOFA Score (≥2 = High Suspicion for Sepsis)

GCS ≤13
Altered mental status
SBP ≤100
Hypotension (mmHg)
RR ≥22
Tachypnea (breaths/min)
diagnostic criteria

SIRS Criteria (2 of 4 required)

Temperature
>38.3°C (100.9°F) or <36°C (96.8°F)
Heart Rate
>90 bpm
Respiratory Rate
>20 breaths/min or PaCO₂ <32 mmHg
WBC
>12,000/mm³ or <4,000/mm³ or >10% bands
disease progression

Sepsis Continuum

SIRS
Systemic inflammatory response. May be non-infectious.
→
Sepsis
SIRS + confirmed/suspected infection + organ dysfunction.
→
Severe Sepsis
+ Hypoperfusion, lactate >2 mmol/L, or organ failure.
→
Septic Shock
Refractory hypotension despite 30 mL/kg IVF. High mortality.
🔥 Clinical Analogy
Sepsis is like a wildfire response gone catastrophically wrong. The fire (infection) triggers emergency services (immune system), but instead of containing only the fire, the response starts burning down the surrounding city — blood vessels dilate uncontrollably, clotting systems activate everywhere, and organs shut down from the collateral damage.
prehospital management
Immediate Actions
1
High-flow O₂; airway management as needed
2
IV/IO access × 2; obtain blood cultures if possible
3
Fluid resuscitation: 30 mL/kg crystalloid (LR/NS) bolus
4
Monitor lactate trend if available
5
Vasopressors per protocol if hypotension persists
Sepsis Bundle (Hour-1)
Hospital targets within 1 hour:
  • Measure lactate level
  • Blood cultures before antibiotics
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • 30 mL/kg crystalloid if hypotensive or lactate ≥4
  • Norepinephrine if MAP <65 after fluids
