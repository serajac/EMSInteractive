Why this module exists — and why it matters tonight, not "someday"

The Real Consequences

Thoracic trauma is directly responsible for 20–25% of all trauma deaths in the United States, and contributes to another 25% as a secondary factor. That's roughly one in every four patients your trauma team loses.

But here is the sharp edge: the majority of preventable chest-trauma deaths in the prehospital environment come from three specific conditions — tension pneumothorax, airway obstruction, and uncontrolled external hemorrhage. All three are field-correctable in under 60 seconds by a prepared paramedic. Miss the finding, and the patient codes en route. Catch it, and you hand over a live patient at the trauma bay.

Every shift you work, you will encounter chest pain, chest wall tenderness, or a seat-belt sign. The question is whether your pattern recognition fires fast enough.

What This Module Assumes You Already Know

You are not a civilian reading a first-aid pamphlet. You are a credentialed EMT (or AEMT) preparing to carry the paramedic patch. You bring with you:

  • BLS airway and ventilation competence
  • Cardiopulmonary anatomy from A&P
  • Primary survey & scene safety principles
  • BVM and supraglottic airway proficiency
  • Basic 3-lead ECG interpretation

We will build on those foundations. We will not rehash them. Where you already have instincts, we will sharpen them. Where gaps exist, we will surface them honestly — ideally in a case, not in a cardiac arrest.

The Thoracic Architecture — Knowing What's in the Box

Principle 3 · Prior Experience You already know A&P — we link structure to failure mode

You cannot decompress what you cannot locate. Every landmark in this section exists because there is a needle, a seal, or a stethoscope that is going to be placed there under pressure, in the dark, on a patient who may be agonal. Memorize the anatomy by intervention, not by dissection.

The Thoracic Cage — The Body's Crumple Zone

The Bony Skeleton

  • 12 pairs of ribs. Ribs 1–7 are "true" (costosternal articulation), 8–10 are "false" (cartilage to rib 7), 11–12 are "floating."
  • The sternum — manubrium, body (gladiolus), xiphoid. The sternal angle (angle of Louis) marks the 2nd rib and the carina underneath.
  • The clavicles — the overpass protecting the subclavian vessels and brachial plexus. A fractured clavicle means those structures were in the line of fire.
  • The thoracic vertebrae (T1–T12) — forming the rigid posterior wall and the origin of the ribs.

Clinical bridge: Fracturing a rib takes roughly 3,300 N of force. Fracturing ribs 1, 2, or 3 takes nearly twice that — so when you see those fractured, assume catastrophic underlying energy. Look for aortic injury and tracheobronchial disruption.

The Pleura — A Potential Space, Not a Room

  • Visceral pleura hugs the lung itself — it is pain-insensitive.
  • Parietal pleura lines the inside of the chest wall — it is richly innervated and exquisitely painful when inflamed or torn.
  • Between them sits a thin film of serous fluid (5–15 mL) and a negative pressure of roughly −5 cm H₂O at rest, dropping to −8 cm H₂O during inspiration.

This negative pressure is everything. It is the vacuum that holds the lung open. Puncture it, and the lung collapses the way a balloon collapses when you release the neck — because the elastic recoil of lung tissue is stronger than atmospheric pressure pushing the chest wall out.

The Mediastinum — The High-Value Real Estate

The mediastinum is the central compartment of the thorax, bounded by the sternum anteriorly, the vertebral column posteriorly, and the parietal pleura laterally. It is the most densely packed region of the human body. Everything in it, if disrupted, kills you quickly.

The Landmarks You Will Touch Tonight

2nd Intercostal Space, Midclavicular

The classical needle decompression site. Still on many protocols. Locate by walking down from the sternal angle (2nd rib) to the space below it. Midclavicular line = midpoint of the clavicle dropped vertically.

4th–5th Intercostal, Midaxillary

The preferred site per ATLS 10th edition (2018) and widely adopted in military TCCC. Thinner chest wall in most adults; less risk of IMA injury; patient can be supine with arm abducted. Needle: 3.25-inch, 14–10 gauge catheter.

Triangle of Safety (for Chest Tubes)

Bounded by the anterior border of the latissimus dorsi, the lateral border of the pectoralis major, and a horizontal line at the level of the nipple. Not your job as a paramedic (in most jurisdictions) — but you need to keep chest seals off of it so the ED team can work.

The Anatomy Trap The patient you treat tonight is not going to have the neatly-spaced ribs of your textbook. They will be obese, or pregnant, or barrel-chested, or hypothermic and rigor-mottled. Land on the landmark by counting ribs with your fingers, not estimating by eye. A failed needle decompression is almost always a needle placed too high (above the 2nd rib), too short (did not reach the pleural space), or too medial (inside the internal thoracic artery).

Mechanisms of Injury — Reading the Scene

Principle 5 · Problem-Centered Orientation We organize by what you saw on arrival, not textbook category

Mechanism tells you what to look for before you put your hands on the patient. The goal of MOI analysis is not to predict the injury with certainty — it is to lower your threshold for aggressive assessment. A missed chest injury is almost always a missed clue from the scene.

Blunt Thoracic Trauma

Roughly 80% of chest trauma in the U.S. is blunt. The energy transfer can be deceiving — external signs are often minimal while internal structures tear, contuse, or decelerate independently of the chest wall.

The Three Forces of Blunt Chest Trauma

  • Direct impact (crush): Rib fractures, sternal fracture, pulmonary contusion directly under the point of impact. Think steering wheel or dashboard.
  • Compression: Sudden increase in intrathoracic pressure against a closed glottis — alveolar rupture (pneumothorax), traumatic asphyxia (capillary rupture above the chest).
  • Deceleration (shear): The heart and great vessels keep moving when the chest stops. Aortic transection at the ligamentum arteriosum. Atrial avulsion at the pulmonary vein insertion. Commotio cordis at the instant of ventricular repolarization.

High-Energy Blunt Scene Clues

  • Intrusion > 12 in (patient side) or > 18 in (any side)
  • Ejection (full or partial) from vehicle
  • Death in same passenger compartment
  • Falls > 20 ft adult / > 10 ft or 2–3× height in children
  • Auto-pedestrian at > 20 mph
  • Motorcycle crash > 20 mph
  • Rollover with unrestrained occupant
  • Steering wheel deformity
  • Seat belt sign across the chest

Penetrating Thoracic Trauma

The remaining 20% — but disproportionately lethal, particularly in urban and military environments. With penetrating trauma, the trajectory question is more important than the entry question. A knife wound at the 4th intercostal space on the left is a cardiac injury until proven otherwise.

The Cardiac Box Any penetrating wound in the region bounded superiorly by the clavicles, inferiorly by the costal margins, and laterally by the midclavicular lines — the "cardiac box" — must be treated as a cardiac injury until imaging proves otherwise. Mortality without intervention approaches 90% for cardiac penetration. Mortality with prompt pericardiocentesis or thoracotomy at a trauma center can drop to 40–50%. Your job is to get them there alive.

Compression and Crush

A separate category that deserves its own mention. Industrial accidents, building collapses, prolonged entrapment, farm equipment rollovers — these produce sustained intrathoracic pressure. The result is traumatic asphyxia: the chest and face above the clavicles appear deeply cyanotic, petechial, and suffused, because the compressive force drove deoxygenated blood retrograde through valveless SVC tributaries into the capillary beds of the head and neck. Below the clavicles, the patient may look normal. It is a striking and diagnostic sign.

The 12 Chest Injuries You Must Own

Principle 1 · Need to Know · Principle 4 · Readiness Each injury kills if missed Each shows up on your pager weekly

Organized by urgency, not by anatomy. The first six are the "lethal six" — field-correctable life threats you must identify in the primary survey. The next six are the "hidden six" — potentially lethal injuries discovered in the secondary survey or en route.

The Lethal Six — Primary Survey Kills

Life Threat · < 60 sec

1 · Tension Pneumothorax

Pathophys: A one-way valve — air enters the pleural space on inspiration but cannot exit on expiration. Intrapleural pressure rises above atmospheric, collapsing the ipsilateral lung, shoving the mediastinum toward the uninjured side, kinking the SVC/IVC at the cavoatrial junction, and collapsing venous return.

  • Signs: Absent/diminished breath sounds on the affected side, hyperresonance to percussion, tracheal deviation away (late), JVD, hypotension, tachycardia progressing to bradycardia + PEA
  • Intervention: Needle thoracostomy immediately — 14-ga, 3.25-in at 4th–5th ICS midaxillary (preferred) or 2nd ICS midclavicular
  • Do not wait for imaging. This is a clinical diagnosis.
Life Threat

2 · Open Pneumothorax

Pathophys: Chest wall defect ≥ ⅔ tracheal diameter allows air to preferentially enter through the wound rather than the trachea on inspiration ("sucking chest wound"). The "chest sucks" because the hole is the path of least resistance.

  • Signs: Audible sucking on inspiration, frothy blood at wound, diminished breath sounds
  • Intervention: Vented chest seal (Halo, HyFin Vent, Russell) placed on dry skin; if no vented seal available, place occlusive dressing and monitor for tension
  • Burp the seal if tension physiology develops after placement
Life Threat

3 · Massive Hemothorax

Pathophys: ≥ 1,500 mL blood in the pleural space (or ≥⅓ of the patient's blood volume). Sources: intercostal arteries, internal thoracic artery, pulmonary vessels, great vessels. The collapsed lung adds hypoxia to hypovolemia.

  • Signs: Dull to percussion (vs. hyperresonant in tension pneumo), absent breath sounds, hypotension, flat neck veins (unlike tension)
  • Field management: Large-bore IV × 2, permissive hypotension (SBP target 80–90 for non-TBI), TXA within 3 hrs of injury if < hrs and protocol allows, rapid transport to trauma center for tube thoracostomy
Life Threat

4 · Flail Chest

Pathophys: ≥ 2 adjacent ribs fractured in ≥ 2 places — the segment is mechanically disconnected from the rest of the thoracic cage. Paradoxical motion (in on inspiration, out on expiration) is the hallmark, but the killer is the underlying pulmonary contusion, not the paradox itself.

  • Signs: Visible paradoxical segment, crepitus, severe pain splinting respirations
  • Intervention: Aggressive pain control (fentanyl or ketamine), supplemental O₂, BVM/NPPV if hypoxic, intubation & positive-pressure ventilation if decompensating
  • Do not tape or splint the segment — worsens ventilation on the good side
Life Threat

5 · Cardiac Tamponade

Pathophys: Blood accumulates in the pericardial sac faster than the sac can stretch. As little as 150–200 mL of acute blood collection can tamponade a normal heart. The right ventricle — the lowest-pressure chamber — is compressed first, failing forward flow. Cardiac output plummets.

  • Beck's Triad: Hypotension + JVD + muffled heart tones (present in <30% of cases — do not wait for all three)
  • ECG: Low voltage, electrical alternans, sinus tachycardia progressing to PEA
  • Field: Volume to maintain preload, rapid transport, prepare for ED/OR pericardiocentesis or thoracotomy. Some paramedic systems authorize field pericardiocentesis — know your protocol.
Life Threat

6 · Airway Obstruction / Tracheobronchial Tear

Pathophys: Direct disruption of the trachea or mainstem bronchi — almost always within 2 cm of the carina in blunt trauma. Massive air leak, persistent pneumothorax despite decompression, subcutaneous emphysema of the neck and chest.

  • Signs: Crepitus of the neck/chest, hemoptysis, persistent large air leak, pneumomediastinum on imaging, Hamman's crunch on auscultation
  • Field: Selective mainstem intubation may be needed; sometimes intubation worsens the leak; call for air medical/surgical airway backup

The Hidden Six — Secondary Survey & En Route

Urgent

7 · Simple Pneumothorax

Small-to-moderate pleural air without mediastinal shift. May be stable for hours. Watch for progression to tension, especially under positive-pressure ventilation.

  • Diminished but not absent breath sounds
  • Supplemental O₂, serial reassessment, anticipate tube thoracostomy at hospital
Urgent

8 · Pulmonary Contusion

Parenchymal bruising — alveolar hemorrhage and edema. The most common potentially lethal chest injury. Worsens over 24–48 hours as edema develops. Classic "blossoming" on serial CT.

  • Hypoxia out of proportion to visible injury
  • Judicious fluids (over-resuscitation worsens the contusion), supplemental O₂, PEEP if intubated
Urgent

9 · Blunt Cardiac Injury

Spectrum from clinically silent contusion to free wall rupture. Most commonly affects the right ventricle (anterior structure). Dysrhythmias are the #1 complication — PVCs, sinus tach persisting despite adequate resuscitation, right bundle branch block.

  • 12-lead ECG on every suspected BCI
  • Monitor continuously; treat dysrhythmias per ACLS
Urgent

10 · Traumatic Aortic Disruption

Deceleration injury — the descending aorta tethers at the ligamentum arteriosum while the aortic arch decelerates, shearing the intima and media. ~85% die at the scene. The other 15% have a contained rupture and hours to minutes to reach a surgeon.

  • Clues: Widened mediastinum on CXR (retrospectively), BP differential L vs R arm > 20 mmHg, "pseudocoarctation" (UE HTN with lower BP in LEs), severe interscapular pain
  • Permissive hypotension (SBP 90), no aggressive fluid, rapid transport
Urgent

11 · Diaphragmatic Rupture

~75% on the left (the liver protects the right). Abdominal contents herniate into the chest. Easily missed — initial CXR is normal in up to 50%. Stomach may be auscultable in the chest.

  • Bowel sounds in the chest, scaphoid abdomen, respiratory distress worse in supine
  • Position of comfort for transport; anticipate surgical repair
Urgent

12 · Esophageal Injury

Rare but catastrophic. More common with penetrating than blunt. Gastric contents in the mediastinum → mediastinitis with > 50% mortality if repair delayed > 24 hrs.

  • Pain out of proportion to exam, odynophagia, subcutaneous emphysema of the neck without pneumothorax, Hamman's crunch
  • NPO, transport; definitive diagnosis is hospital-based (esophagram, endoscopy)

Three Special Presentations You Must Recognize

Commotio Cordis — Sudden Cardiac Arrest from a Blunt Chest Blow

A precordial blow delivered during the 10–20 ms window of ventricular repolarization (the upslope of the T wave) induces ventricular fibrillation in a structurally normal heart. Most commonly seen in adolescent baseball, softball, lacrosse, and hockey players.

Field: Treat as a primary cardiac arrest — high-quality CPR and rapid defibrillation. Survival rates have historically been poor (~15%) but are approaching 60% in communities with immediate AED availability. This is why your tournament medical plan matters.

Traumatic Asphyxia — The Violet Crush Patient

Prolonged severe compression of the thorax (trapped under a vehicle, crushed in a crowd surge, industrial entrapment) drives deoxygenated blood retrograde through valveless SVC tributaries into the cervicofacial circulation. The face, neck, and upper chest become cyanotic, petechial, and markedly swollen. Conjunctival hemorrhage is universal. The head and neck look almost plum-colored while the chest below the clavicles appears normal.

Field: Release the compression, high-flow O₂, elevate the head, treat concomitant injuries — the appearance is dramatic but the prognosis is good if the underlying hypoxia was not prolonged.

Impaled Objects — The "Do Not Remove" Rule and Its Exceptions

Standard teaching: stabilize in place, do not remove. The object may be tamponading a vessel; removal can precipitate uncontrolled hemorrhage into the pleural space.

Exceptions:

  • Object interferes with CPR — cut down or remove to allow chest compressions
  • Object in the cheek compromising airway — remove if it can be done without further tissue damage
  • Short or superficial foreign bodies that do not threaten deep structures

Otherwise, stabilize with bulky dressings, minimize motion, and let the surgeon remove it in a controlled OR environment.

Decision Anchor When you see a chest trauma patient, run this mental loop in order: Are they moving air? Is air moving evenly? Is blood moving forward? Airway → breathing (both sides equal?) → circulation (hypotension with JVD? without JVD?). That sequence distinguishes tension pneumothorax (JVD +) from massive hemothorax (JVD –) from cardiac tamponade (JVD + with muffled tones and electrical alternans) — the three obstructive-shock look-alikes that kill paramedic boards and kill patients.

Physical Exam Findings — What Each One Means

Inspection

  • Paradoxical motion → flail chest
  • Seatbelt sign → deceleration injury, high suspicion for aortic & BCI
  • Sucking wound → open pneumothorax
  • Tripoding, accessory muscle use → respiratory failure imminent
  • JVD → tension pneumo or tamponade (not hemothorax)
  • Cervicofacial cyanosis + petechiae → traumatic asphyxia

Auscultation — Read Both Sides

  • Absent unilateral → tension pneumo OR massive hemothorax
  • Diminished bilateral → bilateral injury, tracheobronchial tear, or airway problem
  • Crackles unilateral → pulmonary contusion
  • Hamman's crunch (crackling synchronous with heartbeat) → pneumomediastinum from tracheobronchial or esophageal injury
  • Bowel sounds in chest → diaphragmatic rupture
  • Muffled heart tones → tamponade (limited sensitivity)

Percussion (often skipped, shouldn't be)

  • Hyperresonant → air (pneumothorax)
  • Dull → fluid (hemothorax) or consolidation (contusion)

Practical tip: Percuss apices (upright patient) for air, bases for fluid. In a supine patient, both collect posteriorly.

Palpation

  • Subcutaneous emphysema ("Rice Krispies") → any pneumothorax or tracheobronchial injury
  • Crepitus → rib fractures
  • Point tenderness → fracture site
  • Flail segment → paradoxical motion under your hand
  • Tracheal deviation → palpate above the sternal notch (not at the notch — you'll feel the normal offset)

Needle Thoracostomy — The Most Commonly Screwed-Up Paramedic Skill

Studies consistently show field needle decompression is unsuccessful 15–65% of the time depending on site, catheter length, and operator. The most common reasons for failure:

  1. Wrong site (too high, too medial, over a rib not under one)
  2. Catheter too short (chest wall thickness at 2nd ICS MCL averages 4.5 cm in adult males; many standard IV catheters are 3.25 cm)
  3. Needle kinked or dislodged during transport
  4. Catheter clogged with blood or tissue
  5. Misdiagnosis (patient didn't have tension pneumo — they had something else)

Needle Decompression Procedure — CAMP EMS Preferred Sequence

  1. Confirm clinical signs of tension pneumothorax (hypotension + JVD + absent breath sounds on affected side ± tracheal deviation)
  2. Expose chest; clean site with chlorhexidine or alcohol
  3. Locate 5th intercostal space at midaxillary line (ATLS-preferred) — or 2nd ICS MCL if protocol-dictated. Arm abducted if possible.
  4. Use a 14-gauge, 3.25-inch (8 cm) over-the-needle catheter
  5. Walk the needle over the top of the rib below (intercostal vessels & nerve run along the inferior rib margin)
  6. Advance perpendicular to the chest wall until a pop and rush of air is appreciated
  7. Advance catheter, retract needle, secure in place
  8. Reassess — breath sounds, BP, JVD. If no improvement, consider wrong diagnosis or inadequate decompression — second needle may be indicated.
  9. Document time, site, catheter size, response

Chest Seal Application

For open pneumothorax ("sucking chest wound"). Modern preference is vented chest seals (e.g., HyFin Vent, Halo Vent, Russell) — they allow air to exit the pleural space while preventing atmospheric air from entering, reducing the risk of tension pneumothorax formation under the seal.

  • Dry the skin (gauze, towel) before application — blood and sweat defeat adhesive
  • Place directly over the wound, not off-center
  • Monitor continuously for tension physiology; if vented seal clogs or non-vented seal is placed, "burp" the seal by lifting one edge
  • For large wounds where a single seal will not cover the defect — overlap two seals, or use an ASHERMAN/three-sided dressing as a field expedient
  • Assume the wound went through the patient — check the back

Ventilation Strategy

When to Assist Ventilation

  • Respiratory rate < 10 or > 30 with inadequate tidal volume
  • SpO₂ < 90% despite high-flow O₂
  • Exhaustion — accessory muscle use fading
  • Altered mental status with respiratory compromise

Caution: Positive-pressure ventilation increases intrathoracic pressure and can convert a simple pneumothorax into a tension pneumothorax in minutes. Prepare to decompress.

Intubation Considerations

  • DSI (delayed sequence intubation) or RSI per protocol
  • Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV for induction preserves hemodynamics
  • Avoid high PEEP initially in hypovolemic patients — reduces venous return
  • Confirm tube with continuous waveform capnography, not just auscultation
  • Right mainstem is common — pull back 1–2 cm if breath sounds asymmetric post-intubation (then reconsider pneumothorax)

Fluid Resuscitation — Less Is More

The era of "two large-bore IVs wide open" is over for blunt and penetrating trauma without TBI. Modern resuscitation is balanced and restrictive:

  • Permissive hypotension: SBP target 80–90 mmHg (or palpable radial pulse + mentation) for patients without suspected TBI. The goal is to preserve perfusion without popping clots.
  • Blood over crystalloid: If your system carries whole blood or PRBCs, prefer these over LR/NS. Massive crystalloid causes dilutional coagulopathy, hypothermia, and acidosis — the "trauma triad of death."
  • TXA (tranexamic acid): 1 g IV/IO within 3 hours of injury in patients with significant hemorrhage. CRASH-2 and MATTERs data support early administration; benefit is lost after 3 hrs.
  • Warm everything. Hypothermic blood doesn't clot. Fluid warmer, blankets, heated ambulance compartment.

Question 1 of 100
The normal intrapleural pressure at rest in a healthy adult is approximately:
Correct Answer: C. -5 cm H2O
Normal intrapleural pressure at rest is approximately -5 cm H2O, which drops further to -8 cm H2O during inspiration. This negative pressure is what holds the lung expanded against its natural elastic recoil. Puncture this potential space and the negative pressure is lost, allowing the lung to collapse.
Question 2 of 100
Which of the following ribs are considered 'floating' because they do not articulate with the sternum or costal cartilage anteriorly?
Correct Answer: C. Ribs 11-12
Ribs 11 and 12 are floating ribs — they do not connect anteriorly. Ribs 1-7 are 'true' ribs with direct costosternal articulation. Ribs 8-10 are 'false' ribs that connect indirectly via the costal cartilage of rib 7.
Question 3 of 100
The ligamentum arteriosum is clinically significant in chest trauma because:
Correct Answer: B. It tethers the aortic arch, making it a common site of deceleration aortic injury
The ligamentum arteriosum (remnant of the fetal ductus arteriosus) tethers the aortic arch to the descending aorta. During rapid deceleration, the mobile arch continues forward while the tethered descending portion stops, creating shear forces that transect the aorta at this point — the classic mechanism of traumatic aortic injury.
Question 4 of 100
The parietal pleura differs from the visceral pleura in that it:
Correct Answer: B. Is richly innervated with somatic pain fibers
The parietal pleura lines the inside of the thoracic cavity and is richly innervated with somatic pain fibers — making it exquisitely painful when inflamed or injured. The visceral pleura covers the lung surface and is pain-insensitive. This is why pleuritic pain feels sharp and localized.
Question 5 of 100
The carina is located approximately at the level of:
Correct Answer: B. The sternal angle (angle of Louis) / T4-T5
The carina sits at the level of the sternal angle (angle of Louis), corresponding to the T4-T5 vertebral level. This is the bifurcation of the trachea into the right and left mainstem bronchi. The right mainstem is steeper and straighter — which is why aspirated foreign bodies and misplaced endotracheal tubes most often end up on the right.
Question 6 of 100
The mediastinum contains all of the following EXCEPT:
Correct Answer: C. The lungs
The lungs are lateral to the mediastinum, not within it. The mediastinum is the central thoracic compartment containing the heart, great vessels, trachea, esophagus, thymus, lymph nodes, and the phrenic and vagus nerves.
Question 7 of 100
Approximately how much force is required to fracture an adult rib?
Correct Answer: C. ~3,300 N
Approximately 3,300 N (about 742 lb-force) is required to fracture a typical adult rib. This is why rib fractures imply significant energy transfer — particularly fractures of ribs 1, 2, or 3, which require nearly double that force and signal catastrophic underlying injury such as aortic or tracheobronchial disruption.
Question 8 of 100
The diaphragm originates inferiorly at approximately which rib level during full expiration?
Correct Answer: B. 6th-8th ribs (nipple line anteriorly)
During full expiration, the right dome of the diaphragm can rise as high as the 4th or 5th intercostal space and the left dome to the 5th or 6th. This means any penetrating wound at or below the nipple line anteriorly (4th ICS) must be considered a potential abdominal injury — the diaphragm rises that high during normal breathing.
Question 9 of 100
Which structure passes through the aortic hiatus of the diaphragm?
Correct Answer: C. The descending aorta, thoracic duct, and azygos vein
The aortic hiatus (at T12) transmits the descending aorta, thoracic duct, and azygos vein. The esophagus passes through the esophageal hiatus at T10, and the IVC through the caval hiatus at T8. The phrenic nerves pass anterior to the diaphragm.
Question 10 of 100
The 'cardiac box' extends from:
Correct Answer: B. The clavicles to the costal margins, between the midclavicular lines
The cardiac box is bounded superiorly by the clavicles, inferiorly by the costal margins, and laterally by the midclavicular lines. Any penetrating injury to this zone is considered a cardiac injury until proven otherwise, with untreated mortality approaching 90% for cardiac penetration.
Question 11 of 100
Blunt chest trauma accounts for approximately what percentage of chest injuries in the United States?
Correct Answer: C. 80%
Approximately 80% of chest trauma in the US is blunt, with penetrating injuries making up the remaining 20%. In urban and military settings, penetrating trauma may be more prevalent. Blunt injuries can be deceiving — external findings are often minimal while internal structures are severely injured.
Question 12 of 100
A deceleration mechanism is most classically associated with which injury?
Correct Answer: B. Aortic transection at the ligamentum arteriosum
Rapid deceleration (high-speed MVC, fall from height) creates shear forces between tethered and mobile structures. The aortic arch decelerates, but the descending aorta is tethered by the ligamentum arteriosum — the shear force transects the aorta at this junction. Approximately 85% of these patients die at the scene.
Question 13 of 100
Which mechanism is most likely to produce a simultaneous pneumothorax and traumatic asphyxia?
Correct Answer: C. Sudden severe compressive crush with closed glottis (e.g., trapped under vehicle)
Sudden severe chest compression against a closed glottis dramatically raises intrathoracic pressure, which can rupture alveoli (pneumothorax) and simultaneously drive deoxygenated blood retrograde through valveless SVC tributaries into the head and neck (traumatic asphyxia). The classic presentation is cervicofacial cyanosis, petechiae, and subconjunctival hemorrhage.
Question 14 of 100
A 22-year-old ejected unrestrained driver from a rollover at highway speed has a Glasgow Coma Scale of 15 and no obvious external injuries. Your suspicion for significant thoracic injury should be:
Correct Answer: C. High — ejection is a high-energy mechanism regardless of external appearance

Question 15 of 100
A high-velocity rifle round causes tissue damage well beyond the direct bullet track primarily because of:
Correct Answer: B. Temporary cavitation from the pressure wave
High-velocity projectiles create a temporary cavity that can be 10-30 times the diameter of the bullet itself. Question 16 of 100
Primary blast injury affects air-filled organs most severely because:
Correct Answer: B. The overpressure wave compresses and then re-expands air, rupturing alveoli and tympanic membranes
Primary blast injury from an overpressure wave preferentially damages air-containing organs (lungs, bowel, middle ear) because the gas within them is compressed by the wave, then rapidly re-expands as the wave passes — rupturing alveolar walls, tympanic membranes, and bowel mucosa. The first three ribs are heavily protected by the clavicle, scapula, and shoulder girdle. Fracturing them requires approximately twice the force of lower rib fractures. Question 19 of 100
The hemodynamic collapse of tension pneumothorax is primarily caused by:
Correct Answer: B. Mediastinal shift kinking the SVC/IVC and collapsing venous return
While hypoxia contributes, the proximal cause of hemodynamic collapse in tension pneumothorax is mechanical obstruction of venous return. As intrapleural pressure rises, the mediastinum shifts toward the contralateral side, kinking the SVC and IVC at their cavoatrial insertions. Without venous return, there is no cardiac output — PEA follows in minutes.

The ATLS 10th-edition preferred site for needle thoracostomy in an adult is:
Correct Answer: B. 4th or 5th intercostal space, midaxillary line
ATLS 10th edition (2018) preferences the 4th-5th ICS midaxillary approach because chest wall thickness at that site averages significantly less than at the 2nd ICS midclavicular site (especially in adult males), making successful pleural entry more reliable. The correct catheter for adult needle thoracostomy is:
Correct Answer: C. 14-gauge, 3.25-inch (8 cm)
A 14-gauge, 3.25-inch (8 cm) over-the-needle catheter is standard. The intercostal neurovascular bundle (vein, artery, nerve — from superior to inferior, 'VAN') runs along the INFERIOR margin of each rib. To avoid it, the needle is walked OVER the top of the rib below the chosen intercostal space. This orientation minimizes vessel and nerve injury.
Question 23 of 100
Which sign of tension pneumothorax is LATE and should not be relied upon to make the diagnosis?
Correct Answer: D. Tracheal deviation away from the injured side
Tracheal deviation is a late finding in tension pneumothorax. By the time it is palpable, the patient is often peri-arrest. Diagnosis should be made clinically on the combination of unilateral absent breath sounds + hypotension + JVD + distress — do not wait for tracheal deviation.
Question 24 of 100
A patient with known simple pneumothorax is placed on a BVM with vigorous positive-pressure ventilation. What is the most concerning immediate risk?
Correct Answer: B. Progression to tension pneumothorax
Positive-pressure ventilation drives atmospheric air into the pleural space through the existing defect, rapidly increasing pleural pressure and converting a simple pneumothorax into a tension pneumothorax — often within minutes. Anticipate this and have decompression equipment ready.

Following a successful needle thoracostomy, reassessment should include all EXCEPT:

The catheter should remain in place until replaced by a chest tube at the hospital. Removing a successful decompression catheter invites re-accumulation of air and re-tension. Secure the catheter with tape and avoid patient manipulation that could kink it.

Hyperresonance to percussion indicates air in the pleural space — pathognomonic for pneumothorax. Both tension pneumothorax and tamponade can present with JVD and hypotension, but hyperresonance points to air, while dull heart sounds and electrical alternans point to tamponade.

Question 28 of 100
A patient with a penetrating chest wound has a 3-sided dressing in place from a bystander. During transport, the patient becomes progressively hypotensive with worsening breath sounds on the injured side. The most appropriate intervention is:
Correct Answer: B. Remove or lift (burp) the dressing to allow accumulated air to escape
Tension physiology developing under an occlusive or partially-vented dressing is released by 'burping' — briefly lifting one edge to let trapped air escape. Taping the fourth side (fully sealing) would WORSEN the tension. Vented chest seals are designed to vent automatically, but may clog with blood.
Question 29 of 100
An open pneumothorax ('sucking chest wound') causes respiratory compromise because:
Correct Answer: B. The chest wall defect preferentially draws air through the wound instead of the trachea on inspiration
When a chest wall defect is approximately 2/3 or greater the diameter of the trachea, air flows more easily through the wound than through the normal airway. This 'sucking' effect prevents effective ventilation of the ipsilateral lung. The preferred management is a vented chest seal.
Question 30 of 100
A vented chest seal differs from a non-vented (fully occlusive) chest seal in that:
Correct Answer: B. It allows air to exit the pleural space while preventing atmospheric air from entering
Vented chest seals (HyFin Vent, Halo Vent, Russell) have one-way valves or vent channels that allow air to leave the pleural space during exhalation while preventing inflow during inhalation. This substantially reduces the risk of tension pneumothorax developing under the seal compared to a fully occlusive dressing.

Blood, sweat, and moisture are the leading causes of chest seal adhesive failure. Drying the skin with gauze or a towel before application is more important than sterilization in the field — an un-adhered seal is a failed seal. Some seals include pre-attached cloth to help with drying.

Every penetrating wound warrants inspection for an exit. Log-roll the patient to examine the back, axilla, gluteal folds, and other 'hidden' areas. Missed exit wounds are a common source of preventable morbidity. The trajectory between entry and exit can cross multiple compartments.

For defects larger than a single seal, overlap two seals to cover the full wound, ensuring good adhesive contact at the perimeter. It requires surgical evaluation because sources include great vessels, intercostal arteries, and the internal thoracic artery — structures that often require thoracotomy for control.
Question 36 of 100
A key physical finding that distinguishes massive hemothorax from tension pneumothorax on the same side is:
Correct Answer: C. Dull percussion note in hemothorax vs. hyperresonance in pneumothorax
Percussion is key. Hemothorax = dull (fluid). Pneumothorax = hyperresonant (air). Both may show absent breath sounds and tracheal deviation when severe. JVD is typically present in tension pneumothorax but ABSENT (flat veins from hypovolemia) in massive hemothorax.
Question 37 of 100
In massive hemothorax, jugular venous distention is typically:
Correct Answer: B. Absent or flat due to hypovolemia
Because the patient has lost large volumes of blood, they are hypovolemic — neck veins are flat. This is an important distinguishing feature from tension pneumothorax and tamponade, both of which present with JVD despite hypotension because the problem is obstructive, not hypovolemic.
Question 38 of 100
The most likely source of bleeding in isolated rib fractures is:
Correct Answer: B. Intercostal arteries and veins
The intercostal neurovascular bundle runs along the inferior margin of each rib. Displaced rib fractures commonly injure these vessels, producing substantial hemothoraces — especially in anticoagulated patients. Larger hemothoraces (>1500 mL) usually indicate great vessel or parenchymal injury.
Question 39 of 100
For a patient with suspected massive hemothorax, the appropriate field fluid resuscitation strategy is:
Correct Answer: B. Permissive hypotension with SBP target 80-90 mmHg and rapid transport, TXA if within 3 hours
Permissive hypotension (SBP 80-90) allows perfusion of vital organs while minimizing the disruption of formed clots and dilutional coagulopathy. TXA within 3 hours reduces mortality (CRASH-2 trial). Aggressive crystalloid administration worsens outcomes in penetrating torso trauma without TBI.
Question 40 of 100
Tranexamic acid (TXA) in trauma is most effective when administered:
Correct Answer: B. Within 3 hours of injury as 1 g IV over 10 minutes
CRASH-2 established TXA 1 g IV over 10 minutes followed by 1 g over 8 hours, administered within 3 hours of injury, reduces all-cause mortality in bleeding trauma patients. Beyond 3 hours, benefit is lost and possibly harmful. Most EMS protocols administer the first 1 g in the field.
Question 41 of 100
A patient with a hemothorax develops progressive hypoxia and worsening respiratory distress. Lung sounds are absent on the affected side with dull percussion. The most likely pathophysiologic contribution is:
Correct Answer: C. Large hemothorax physically preventing lung expansion plus intrapulmonary shunting
A large hemothorax mechanically restricts lung expansion on the affected side, creating both restrictive physiology and V/Q mismatch (perfusion of non-ventilated lung = shunt). Oxygenation does not improve with supplemental O2 beyond a point because shunted blood never contacts ventilated alveoli. Definitive management is tube thoracostomy.
Question 42 of 100
Which of the following is NOT a feature of a hemopneumothorax?
Correct Answer: D. Completely relieved by needle thoracostomy alone
A hemopneumothorax is the combined collection of blood and air. A single-bore needle thoracostomy may decompress the air but does not adequately drain the blood. Definitive management requires tube thoracostomy — and sometimes two separate drains. Field needle decompression is a temporizing measure only.
Question 43 of 100
A flail chest is defined as:
Correct Answer: B. Two or more adjacent ribs fractured in two or more places, producing a free segment
A flail segment requires at least two adjacent ribs fractured in at least two places each, creating a 'free-floating' segment that moves paradoxically. Isolated rib fractures, even multiple non-adjacent ones, do not produce a flail.
Question 44 of 100
Paradoxical chest wall motion associated with flail chest is described as:
Correct Answer: A. The flail segment moves inward on inspiration and outward on expiration
Normal chest wall moves OUT with inspiration (negative intrathoracic pressure pulls). The flail segment, disconnected from the rest of the cage, is instead SUCKED IN by that negative pressure — opposite of normal. On expiration it pushes back out. This paradoxical motion reduces tidal volume and impairs ventilation.
Question 45 of 100
The primary cause of hypoxia in flail chest is:
Correct Answer: B. Underlying pulmonary contusion
Modern trauma literature has shifted the focus: the paradoxical motion is a marker of severity, but the underlying PULMONARY CONTUSION is what actually kills. The lung beneath the flail segment is bruised, filled with blood and edema, and cannot oxygenate. Treat the contusion, not just the chest wall.
Question 46 of 100
Initial prehospital management of a flail chest with adequate respiratory effort and SpO2 of 94% is:
Correct Answer: C. Analgesia, supplemental oxygen, monitoring, and position of comfort
A flail chest with adequate spontaneous ventilation is managed supportively: pain control (fentanyl), O2 to maintain SpO2 ≥94%, and monitoring for deterioration. Taping a flail segment is OUT (worsens ventilation). Prophylactic intubation is not indicated if the patient is ventilating adequately — but have a low threshold if they tire out.
Question 47 of 100
Which patient with rib fractures is at HIGHEST risk for morbidity and mortality?
Correct Answer: C. An 84-year-old on warfarin with fractures of ribs 4-5-6 on the left
Geriatric rib fractures carry a mortality penalty: each additional rib fracture in a patient >65 increases mortality by ~19% and pneumonia risk by ~27%. Add anticoagulation (warfarin), and the risk of occult hemothorax and delayed bleeding climbs dramatically. 'Just a rib' is a lie in this population.
Question 48 of 100
Fractures of the first or second rib are clinically significant because:
Correct Answer: B. They indicate extreme force and are associated with aortic/great vessel and brachial plexus injuries
Ribs 1-2 are short, thick, and protected by the clavicle and musculature. Fracturing them requires massive force — think great-vessel injury, tracheobronchial disruption, spinal injury, brachial plexus injury. These patients get a workup even if they 'look fine.'
Question 49 of 100
Fractures of the lower ribs (9-12) should raise suspicion for:
Correct Answer: C. Abdominal solid organ injury (liver on the right, spleen on the left)
Ribs 9-12 protect the upper abdomen. Right lower rib fractures → think liver laceration. Left lower rib fractures → think splenic injury. Patients with lower rib fractures and hemodynamic instability need abdominal evaluation, not just chest imaging.
Question 50 of 100
The MOST effective prehospital intervention to improve outcomes in multiple rib fractures is:
Correct Answer: C. Adequate analgesia to prevent splinting and atelectasis
Pain drives splinting, splinting drives atelectasis, atelectasis drives pneumonia, and pneumonia kills elderly rib fracture patients. Aggressive, titrated analgesia (fentanyl is the prehospital workhorse) is the single most outcome-changing intervention. In-hospital, this extends to regional blocks and epidurals.
Question 51 of 100
Beck's triad, classically associated with cardiac tamponade, consists of:
Correct Answer: B. Hypotension, muffled/distant heart sounds, jugular venous distention
Beck's triad = hypotension + muffled heart sounds + JVD. Remember it's classically incomplete in the field (heart sounds are hard to hear in a moving ambulance, JVD may be absent in hypovolemia). Absence of Beck's does NOT rule out tamponade.
Question 52 of 100
Pulsus paradoxus in cardiac tamponade is defined as:
Correct Answer: A. A drop in systolic BP of >10 mmHg with inspiration
Pulsus paradoxus is an exaggeration of a normal phenomenon: during inspiration, BP normally drops a few mmHg. In tamponade, the fixed pericardial volume means RV filling pushes the septum into the LV, reducing LV output more dramatically. A drop >10 mmHg is paradoxus. Hard to measure in the field — treat it as one more clue, not a requirement.
Question 53 of 100
The pericardial sac in an acutely traumatized heart can typically accumulate how much blood before tamponade physiology develops?
Correct Answer: B. 150-200 mL
The pericardium is inelastic in acute settings. As little as 150-200 mL of blood accumulating RAPIDLY can generate enough pressure to impair ventricular filling. (Chronic effusions can reach a liter or more because the pericardium stretches over time — traumatic tamponade does not give that luxury.)
Question 54 of 100
A penetrating wound to which anatomic region should raise the highest suspicion for cardiac injury?
Correct Answer: C. The 'cardiac box' — sternal notch to xiphoid, bilaterally to the midclavicular lines
The cardiac box = vertical strip over the anterior mediastinum (above the costal margin, between the mid-clavicular lines, from sternal notch to xiphoid). Any penetrating injury in this zone is cardiac until proven otherwise. Wounds near but outside the box can still reach the heart, but the box is the high-yield trigger.
Question 55 of 100
Prehospital definitive treatment for traumatic cardiac tamponade is:
Correct Answer: C. Rapid transport to a trauma center capable of surgical intervention (pericardial window / ED thoracotomy)
There is NO definitive prehospital fix for traumatic tamponade. Needle pericardiocentesis in the field is rarely within paramedic scope and is a temporary, often failing, measure. The real intervention is surgical — get the patient to a trauma center FAST. A cautious fluid bolus can buy preload; otherwise, drive.
Question 56 of 100
Which finding BEST differentiates cardiac tamponade from tension pneumothorax on exam?
Correct Answer: C. Breath sounds are EQUAL bilaterally in tamponade but DIMINISHED/ABSENT on one side in tension pneumothorax
Both diagnoses present with obstructive shock + JVD + tachycardia + hypotension. The discriminator is the LUNG EXAM. Tension pneumo = unilateral diminished/absent breath sounds and hyperresonance. Tamponade = clear, equal breath sounds. If breath sounds are equal and the patient is crashing with JVD, think tamponade (or a central pump problem).
Question 57 of 100
Why is IV fluid administration a 'bridge' rather than a definitive treatment in cardiac tamponade?
Correct Answer: B. Fluid increases right atrial filling pressure, briefly maintaining diastolic filling against the rising pericardial pressure
Tamponade crushes the heart from outside. A fluid bolus briefly raises central venous pressure enough to overcome pericardial pressure and keep the ventricles filling. It buys minutes — not cure. Definitive treatment is drainage of the pericardial blood.
Question 58 of 100
In a hypotensive trauma patient with a penetrating left parasternal wound, which of the following should the paramedic prioritize FIRST en route?
Correct Answer: B. Rapid transport to a trauma center, permissive hypotension, and early notification
A parasternal penetrating wound in shock screams 'cardiac box → tamponade or cardiac laceration.' Drive, don't dawdle. Early trauma center notification so they can have a surgeon and blood ready. Permissive hypotension (MAP ~65) to avoid popping fresh clot. Needle thoracostomy is not indicated unless breath sounds are absent.
Question 59 of 100
Commotio cordis is best described as:
Correct Answer: B. Sudden cardiac arrest from a low-energy blow to the precordium during a vulnerable phase of the T-wave, inducing ventricular fibrillation
Commotio cordis = structurally normal heart + low-energy blow (baseball, hockey puck, fist) + timing in the 10-30 ms window on the upslope of the T wave = v-fib. It is an ELECTRICAL event, not a structural injury. Autopsy is normal. Treatment is immediate CPR and defibrillation — survival depends on how fast the AED arrives.
Question 60 of 100
The single most important intervention for a suspected commotio cordis arrest is:
Correct Answer: B. Rapid defibrillation with a manual defibrillator or AED
V-fib demands electricity. The survival curve in commotio cordis is brutal — every minute without defibrillation drops survival by ~10%. CPR and shock, shock, shock. This is why AEDs on youth sports fields save lives.
Question 61 of 100
Which chamber of the heart is MOST commonly injured in blunt cardiac trauma?
Correct Answer: C. Right ventricle
The right ventricle sits directly behind the sternum — it takes the hit in steering-wheel and CPR injuries. Left ventricle injuries occur but are less common. Atrial injuries are rarer still.
Question 62 of 100
The MOST common ECG finding in blunt cardiac injury is:
Correct Answer: B. Sinus tachycardia
Sinus tachycardia is by far the most common finding and is nonspecific — it's just 'something's wrong.' More concerning BCI findings include new RBBB (due to RV location), PVCs, and arrhythmias. Significant BCI can also present with ST changes, but sinus tach is the most frequent early sign.
Question 63 of 100
A 16-year-old baseball player is struck in the chest by a fastball. He collapses and is pulseless on your arrival. Bystander CPR is in progress. Your FIRST action is:
Correct Answer: A. Apply the AED/monitor and deliver a shock if indicated
Commotio cordis is the leading cause of sudden death in youth baseball. The rhythm is almost certainly v-fib. Get pads on, shock. Defibrillation first — airway and drugs are secondary. Continue CPR. Shocks + CPR + early transport to PCI/cardiac center.
Question 64 of 100
Which of the following is MOST associated with significant blunt cardiac injury (BCI)?
Correct Answer: B. A high-speed MVC with steering wheel deformity or a direct sternal blow with sternal fracture
Mechanism matters. Steering wheel deformity, crushed sternum, or high-energy anterior chest impact with sternal fracture should trigger concern for BCI — cardiac contusion, septal rupture, or even valve injury. These patients need ECG, troponin, and monitoring even if they initially 'look fine.'
Question 65 of 100
The MOST common location for traumatic aortic disruption from deceleration injury is:
Correct Answer: C. The aortic isthmus, just distal to the left subclavian artery at the ligamentum arteriosum
The aorta is fixed at the ligamentum arteriosum (remnant of fetal ductus arteriosus) and free-floating elsewhere. In sudden deceleration, the mobile descending aorta swings forward while the arch stays tethered — tearing right at the isthmus. ~90% of traumatic aortic injuries happen here. The rest scatter at other tether points.
Question 66 of 100
A widened mediastinum on chest radiograph in a blunt trauma patient should prompt immediate concern for:
Correct Answer: C. Traumatic aortic disruption
A widened mediastinum is the classic radiographic shadow of aortic injury — the contained hematoma pushes the mediastinal silhouette wider than ~8 cm. Other signs: loss of aortic knob, deviated NG tube, left pleural cap, depressed left main bronchus. CT angiography is definitive.
Question 67 of 100
A patient survives the initial aortic injury only because:
Correct Answer: A. The aorta has three layers, and usually only the intima tears, leaving the adventitia intact to contain the hematoma
Complete aortic rupture is almost uniformly fatal at scene. The ~15% who make it alive to the ED are those whose adventitia (outer layer) held when intima/media tore, containing the blood in a contained pseudoaneurysm. This is time-limited — secondary rupture can happen any moment. Get them to surgery.
Question 68 of 100
Blood pressure management in a patient with suspected traumatic aortic disruption aims to:
Correct Answer: B. Achieve permissive hypotension with SBP ~90-100 mmHg to reduce shear stress on the aortic wall
Higher BP = higher shear stress = higher rupture risk. Aim for SBP ~90-100, HR <100 (beta-blockade in hospital). Avoid crystalloid overdose. If intubated, avoid spikes during laryngoscopy. Goal: get them to the OR or endovascular repair with their adventitia still intact.
Question 69 of 100
Which clinical finding is SUGGESTIVE (though not diagnostic) of traumatic aortic injury?
Correct Answer: A. Differential blood pressure between upper extremities or upper-vs-lower extremities
A BP difference >20 mmHg between arms, or a radial pulse stronger than a femoral pulse, hints at aortic injury distorting branch perfusion. Other clues: interscapular murmur, hoarseness (recurrent laryngeal stretch), left flail chest or left rib fractures. None are diagnostic — imaging is.
Question 70 of 100
Which mechanism carries the HIGHEST risk for traumatic aortic disruption?
Correct Answer: C. Frontal MVC at highway speed with rapid deceleration
Classic TAI mechanism = sudden, severe deceleration. Highway-speed MVC, fall from great height (>10 ft), ejection, pedestrian-struck-by-car. The numbers: ~70% of TAI comes from MVCs. Low-energy mechanisms rarely cause TAI.
Question 71 of 100
Pulmonary contusion is BEST described as:
Correct Answer: C. Alveolar hemorrhage and edema within the lung parenchyma, causing a V/Q mismatch and hypoxia
Pulmonary contusion = bruised lung tissue. Alveoli fill with blood and inflammatory edema. Those alveoli are perfused but not ventilated — a V/Q mismatch that impairs oxygenation. Distinguish from pneumothorax (air OUTSIDE the lung in the pleural space) and hemothorax (blood in the pleural space).
Question 72 of 100
Pulmonary contusion classically worsens:
Correct Answer: B. Over 24-72 hours post-injury
The evil thing about pulmonary contusion is its slow burn. A patient who looks OK at scene can deteriorate over 24-72 hours as inflammation and edema accumulate. Initial chest x-ray may underestimate the injury. High-risk mechanism = admit and watch, even without florid findings early.
Question 73 of 100
A patient with a significant pulmonary contusion who requires intubation benefits MOST from:
Correct Answer: B. Low tidal volume, lung-protective ventilation (6 mL/kg IBW) with PEEP
Contused lung behaves like ARDS — fragile, stiff, leaks with pressure. Lung-protective strategy: 6 mL/kg IBW, PEEP 5-10 to keep alveoli open, FiO2 titrated to SpO2 94-98%. High tidal volumes rupture alveoli and worsen the injury.
Question 74 of 100
Overly aggressive crystalloid fluid resuscitation in a patient with pulmonary contusion is harmful because:
Correct Answer: B. It worsens alveolar edema in already leaky lung tissue, increasing hypoxia
A contused lung's capillary membranes are broken. Flooding them with crystalloid is pouring water into a sieve — alveoli drown. Use balanced resuscitation: blood products for bleeding, judicious crystalloid (TXA, permissive hypotension). All of the listed answers are real crystalloid problems in trauma, but lung drowning is the contusion-specific one.
Question 75 of 100
Pulmonary contusion is MOST commonly associated with:
Correct Answer: B. Rib fractures, flail chest, or high-energy blunt thoracic trauma
Pulmonary contusion rides with rib fractures, flail chest, and major blunt thoracic mechanism. See a flail segment? Assume there's a contusion under it. High-velocity penetrating injuries can also cavitate lung tissue and contuse surrounding parenchyma.
Question 76 of 100
A tracheobronchial injury should be suspected when:
Correct Answer: B. A persistent, large air leak continues after chest decompression, or massive subcutaneous emphysema is present
A torn major airway pours air into the pleural space faster than a chest tube can evacuate it — persistent pneumothorax despite decompression, massive subcutaneous emphysema, hemoptysis, and hoarseness are the red flags. Bronchoscopy confirms. Surgery repairs. These patients need tertiary-care intervention — don't delay transport.
Question 77 of 100
Traumatic diaphragmatic rupture MOST commonly occurs on which side and why?
Correct Answer: B. Left, because the liver protects the right hemidiaphragm and transmits force to the weaker left side
Left-sided diaphragmatic ruptures outnumber right ~3:1 in blunt trauma. The liver is a big protective cushion on the right; the left has only the spleen/stomach. Rupture allows abdominal contents (stomach, colon, spleen) to herniate into the chest — look for decreased breath sounds on the left, bowel sounds in the chest, and a stomach-gas shadow on CXR that shouldn't be there.
Question 78 of 100
A patient with suspected diaphragmatic rupture is at INCREASED risk for which complication during positive-pressure ventilation?
Correct Answer: A. Worsening of the mediastinal shift and compression of thoracic structures by herniated abdominal contents
PPV pushes more air into the lungs AND — via abdominal pressure and altered mechanics — can drive herniated contents further into the chest. Be prepared for tension-physiology-without-air (mediastinal shift from gut). Definitive treatment is surgical repair.
Question 79 of 100
Esophageal injury in chest trauma is:
Correct Answer: B. Rare but life-threatening; mediastinitis is the feared complication
Traumatic esophageal injury is uncommon but deadly. Soilage of the mediastinum with saliva and gastric contents → fulminant mediastinitis and sepsis. Most commonly from penetrating trauma (cervical region). Suspect with mediastinal air, unexplained pneumothorax, fever, or sepsis out of proportion to visible injuries.
Question 80 of 100
Which of the following is a classic delayed presentation of missed diaphragmatic rupture?
Correct Answer: B. Years later, bowel incarceration and strangulation in the chest
Small diaphragmatic tears can hide for years. The problem: bowel slowly migrates through the defect, and one day it incarcerates — the patient shows up with obstruction or strangulation in the 'chest.' History of remote blunt trauma + GI symptoms + abnormal CXR should trigger suspicion.
Question 81 of 100
A 40-year-old male is stabbed in the right lateral chest. He is tachypneic, anxious, and SpO2 is 88%. Breath sounds are DIMINISHED on the right with hyperresonance. BP is 100/70. The MOST appropriate first intervention is:
Correct Answer: B. Occlusive chest seal over the wound and prepare for needle decompression if tension develops
This is an open pneumothorax (sucking chest wound). First: vented chest seal over the wound, monitor. If SpO2 drops, BP falls, or tension develops — needle decompress. Intubation without first relieving the tension/air leak can convert a simple open pneumo into a tension pneumo fast.
Question 82 of 100
A patient with a suspected tension pneumothorax on the LEFT after needle decompression at the 2nd ICS midclavicular line shows no improvement. The MOST likely reasons include all EXCEPT:
Correct Answer: D. The needle was placed on the wrong side
This is an 'all EXCEPT' question — identify what is LEAST likely. Wrong-side placement is a gross error but relatively rare. Common failures: catheter too short (obesity, muscular chest — use 8 cm / 3.25-inch catheter, or move to 5th ICS midaxillary), wrong diagnosis, catheter kinked or clotted with blood/tissue. If first decompression fails, try a second catheter or switch site.
Question 83 of 100
Which of the following patients is MOST likely to present with OBSTRUCTIVE shock?
Correct Answer: C. A patient with a tension pneumothorax and JVD
Shock categories: hypovolemic (femur bleed), distributive (spinal shock), cardiogenic, obstructive (tension pneumo, tamponade, massive PE). Tension pneumo with JVD is a textbook obstructive shock. Chronic pericardial effusions slowly stretch and rarely tamponade; acute ones can cause obstructive shock, but this chronic one is less likely.
Question 84 of 100
Subcutaneous emphysema ('crepitus' on palpation) suggests:
Correct Answer: A. Pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum, or tracheobronchial injury
Air under the skin came from somewhere — usually a pleural leak (pneumothorax), mediastinal leak (pneumomediastinum), airway disruption (trach/bronchus), or esophageal rupture. Massive subcutaneous emphysema from neck to groin = big airway problem until proven otherwise. Simple rib fractures DON'T typically produce crepitus unless associated pneumothorax is present.
Question 85 of 100
In the primary survey of a thoracic trauma patient, which component is assessed under 'B' (Breathing)?
Correct Answer: B. Observing chest rise/fall, auscultating breath sounds, checking for flail/tension/open pneumo
B = Breathing. Look (rise/fall, paradoxical motion, wounds), Listen (breath sounds equal?), Feel (crepitus, tenderness, flail). Identify and treat the six rapid killers during the primary survey — tension pneumo, open pneumo, massive hemothorax, flail chest, cardiac tamponade, airway obstruction.
Question 86 of 100
A patient with chest trauma is DIAPHORETIC, ANXIOUS, and COMPLAINING OF 'DOOM.' These findings:
Correct Answer: B. Are early signs of compensated shock and demand immediate action
'Feeling of impending doom' in a trauma patient is a powerful physiologic signal — cortical perfusion is precarious. Combined with diaphoresis and anxiety, this is early compensated shock. Don't dismiss it. Full workup, aggressive resuscitation, rapid transport.
Question 87 of 100
Which vital sign pattern is MOST consistent with EARLY compensated shock from hemothorax?
Correct Answer: B. HR 120, BP 118/88, narrow pulse pressure, cool extremities
Compensated shock: tachycardia masks a dropping pulse pressure as SVR clamps down. BP still 'looks OK,' but the narrowing PP and cool extremities scream compensated. The third answer (180, 60/palp) is decompensated. The first is inconsistent (bradycardia with shock is late or vagal).
Question 88 of 100
End-tidal CO2 (EtCO2) in a patient with significant traumatic hemorrhage typically shows:
Correct Answer: B. Low EtCO2 (<30 mmHg) due to decreased pulmonary perfusion
Low EtCO2 in trauma = poor pulmonary perfusion (shock state) or hyperventilation. In major hemorrhage, cardiac output drops, less CO2 reaches the alveoli, EtCO2 falls. Trending EtCO2 is a surprisingly useful continuous marker of perfusion in a resuscitation. A sudden drop during transport may mean arrest is imminent.
Question 89 of 100
The rationale for Tranexamic Acid (TXA) in traumatic hemorrhagic shock is:
Correct Answer: B. It inhibits fibrinolysis (antifibrinolytic), stabilizing clot formation; effective within 3 hours of injury
TXA blocks plasmin from breaking down fibrin. In the CRASH-2 trial, 1 g IV over 10 min + 1 g over 8 hours, given within 3 hours, reduced mortality. Given >3 hours may INCREASE mortality. Dose in the first dose bolus must be slow (not IV push) to avoid hypotension.
Question 90 of 100
Permissive hypotension is AVOIDED in which of the following patients?
Correct Answer: B. A patient with a concurrent traumatic brain injury (TBI)
Permissive hypotension (SBP ~90, MAP ~65) limits clot disruption — GOOD for pure hemorrhagic trauma. But a TBI needs adequate cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP - ICP), so the brain-injured patient gets a higher target (SBP ≥110 for TBI patients). Avoid hypotension in the head-injured — one drop can do damage.
Question 91 of 100
Which airway adjunct is the preferred induction agent for RSI in a hypotensive trauma patient?
Correct Answer: B. Ketamine (dissociative anesthetic with hemodynamic stability)
Ketamine 1-2 mg/kg IV preserves BP (catecholamine release) and provides analgesia and dissociation simultaneously. Etomidate is another option with hemodynamic stability. Propofol and thiopental drop BP hard — dangerous in an already-hypotensive patient. Midazolam/fentanyl can also drop BP and provide incomplete anesthesia.
Question 92 of 100
Fentanyl is preferred over morphine in most trauma analgesia because:
Correct Answer: B. It does not cause histamine release, has minimal hemodynamic effects, and has a rapid onset
Fentanyl: rapid onset (2-3 min), short half-life, minimal histamine release, less hypotension. Morphine releases histamine → hypotension and itching, and its longer duration means you can't easily 're-dose' or chase a changing exam. Start fentanyl at 1-2 mcg/kg IV, titrate.
Question 93 of 100
The maximum recommended crystalloid (NS/LR) bolus for a trauma patient before shifting to blood products is typically:
Correct Answer: B. 1 liter (with ongoing balanced blood product resuscitation for continued bleeding)
Modern trauma resuscitation: minimize crystalloid (max ~1 L), then switch to blood products in balanced 1:1:1 ratio (PRBC:FFP:platelets) or whole blood. Crystalloid dilutes clotting factors, causes coagulopathy, worsens edema, and triggers the 'lethal triad' (coagulopathy + hypothermia + acidosis). Early blood is the modern paradigm.
Question 94 of 100
Calcium chloride is given in the setting of massive transfusion because:
Correct Answer: B. Citrate preservative in stored blood products binds calcium, causing hypocalcemia and cardiac depression
Stored blood uses citrate as anticoagulant. Citrate chelates calcium → ionized hypocalcemia → decreased contractility, hypotension, worsening coagulopathy. 1 g calcium chloride IV (central line preferred; ~3 g calcium gluconate through peripheral line as alternative) per several units of blood. Known as 'the forgotten killer' in massive transfusion.
Question 95 of 100
Which of the following is TRUE about chest seal application in an open pneumothorax?
Correct Answer: A. Dry the skin thoroughly before applying; consider a vented seal to allow air escape
Dry the skin (seal WILL NOT adhere to wet/bloody chest — no adherence = no seal). Vented seals (Halo, HyFin Vent) allow air out but not in, reducing tension risk. Non-vented seals taped on 3 sides work in a pinch. Fully occlusive (all 4 sides) risks tension. No seal at all risks ongoing open pneumothorax.
Question 96 of 100
A patient with a chest tube in place (already inserted in the ED) becomes hypotensive with worsening respiratory distress during ground transport. The FIRST thing to check is:
Correct Answer: B. Ensure tubing is not kinked, clamped, or disconnected; verify water seal/Heimlich valve is functional
A kinked or clamped chest tube = recurrent tension pneumo. Always inspect the whole length of the tubing during transport. Listen for breath sounds. If the tube is functioning but patient still crashes, consider another injury. Never remove a chest tube in the field — manage its drainage.
Question 97 of 100
A pediatric patient with chest trauma is MORE likely than an adult to have:
Correct Answer: B. Significant internal injury without rib fractures (compliant chest wall transmits force to underlying organs)
Pediatric chest walls are cartilaginous and compliant — they bend rather than break, transmitting force to lungs and heart. So you see a child with pulmonary contusion and NO rib fractures. Conversely, rib fractures in kids = MASSIVE force — high suspicion for major internal injury. Kids also compensate longer, then crash suddenly.
Question 98 of 100
Geriatric patients with chest trauma have higher mortality because:
Correct Answer: B. Decreased physiologic reserve, stiffer/osteoporotic ribs, higher rates of anticoagulation, and reduced pulmonary function predispose to complications
The elderly trauma patient carries multiple hits: chest wall compliance drops, ribs fracture on minor force, pre-existing lung/cardiac disease reduces reserve, anticoagulants are common, and pneumonia risk from splinting is high. A 'minor' rib fracture in a 70-year-old carries real mortality risk — aggressive pain control matters.
Question 99 of 100
In a pregnant trauma patient with chest injury, the MOST effective maneuver to optimize hemodynamics during transport is:
Correct Answer: B. Left lateral tilt (15-30°) or manual displacement of the uterus to the left to relieve IVC compression
A gravid uterus (after ~20 weeks) compresses the IVC when supine, reducing preload by up to 30%. Tilt the backboard left 15-30° or manually displace the uterus leftward. The fetus is best resuscitated by resuscitating the mother — maternal CPR + left tilt is the cornerstone.
Question 100 of 100
You respond to a 26-year-old construction worker struck by falling beam. He is awake, tachypneic (RR 28), SpO2 88% on RA, BP 92/60, HR 128. There is a flail segment on the right chest with obvious paradoxical motion and diminished breath sounds on the right. The MOST appropriate early intervention sequence is:
Correct Answer: B. Apply O2, monitor, obtain IV access, provide analgesia, prepare for needle decompression if tension develops, rapid transport to trauma center with destination notification
This is the capstone-integration answer. The flail segment has underlying contusion, there is probable pneumo/hemothorax (diminished right breath sounds), compensated shock is evident. Sequence: oxygenate, IV access, titrated analgesia (pain control IS a ventilation intervention here), watch for tension, don't over-resuscitate with crystalloid (drowns the contusion), fast transport with trauma team activation. Intubation is ON THE TABLE but not automatic — if ventilating adequately, support; if tiring, intubate with ketamine and lung-protective settings.
