📋 Patient History ▾
Patient
Jaylen M., 7-year-old male
Chief Complaint
Severe dyspnea, unresponsive to home bronchodilator therapy
PMHx
Moderate persistent asthma (dx age 4), allergic rhinitis, eczema. Two prior intubations (age 5 and 6).
Medications

Allergies

Onset/Trigger

Pertinent Negatives
No fever, no stridor, no foreign body ingestion reported. No URI symptoms this week. No recent steroid use.
🔬 Physical Examination Findings ▾
  • General: Anxious, diaphoretic male in severe respiratory distress. Skin pale with circumoral cyanosis.
  • Airway: Patent, no foreign body, no stridor. Severe expiratory wheeze bilateral, diminished RLL.
  • Breathing: Tachypneic at 36/min, severe intercostal/suprasternal retractions. Accessory muscle use. Tripod positioning. Paradoxical breathing noted.
  • Circulation: Tachycardic 138 bpm, peripheral pulses weak. Cap refill 3 seconds. Skin pale, diaphoretic.
  • Neuro: Alert but extremely anxious. GCS 13. Eye contact appropriate. Moving all extremities.
  • Abdomen: Non-distended. No guarding. No bowel sounds auscultated (due to respiratory effort).
  • Peak Flow: Unable to perform — patient too distressed.
📚 Background — Pathophysiology Review ▾
📖 Research This Status asthmaticus is defined as a severe asthma attack that fails to respond to standard bronchodilator therapy. Review the following topics: bronchospasm cascade, mucus plugging, air trapping and dynamic hyperinflation, the role of hypoxia-driven tachycardia, and why ETCO₂ rising in an asthmatic is a late and ominous sign. Consider Heliox, magnesium sulfate, and ketamine indications in pediatric status asthmaticus.
  • Pathophysiology: Bronchospasm → air trapping → V/Q mismatch → hypoxemia → hypercarbia (late)
  • Pulsus paradoxus: SBP drops >10 mmHg with inspiration — a marker of severity
  • Silent chest: Loss of wheeze in severe attack = airflow too limited to produce wheeze — DANGER SIGN
  • Fatigue leads to hypercapnia, respiratory failure, and apnea without intervention
  • Pediatric considerations: smaller airway diameter = disproportionate resistance increase
💊 Key Pharmacology (Reference) ▾
  • Albuterol (Salbutamol): 2.5mg NEB or MDI — β2-agonist bronchodilator. Continuous NEB in severe cases. Peds: 0.15 mg/kg min 2.5mg q20min × 3
  • Ipratropium Bromide: 0.5mg NEB — Anticholinergic, inhibits bronchoconstriction. Used adjunct to albuterol in first hour only.
  • Methylprednisolone: 1–2 mg/kg IV (max 60mg) — Corticosteroid to reduce airway inflammation. Onset 4–6 hours.
  • Magnesium Sulfate: 25–75 mg/kg IV (max 2g) over 20 min — Smooth muscle relaxant. Used in severe refractory cases.
  • Epinephrine 1:1000: 0.01 mg/kg IM — Reserved for impending respiratory failure or anaphylaxis component.
  • Ketamine: 1–2 mg/kg IV for RSI if intubation needed — Bronchodilatory properties; anesthetic of choice in status asthmaticus.

📋 Patient History ▾
Patient
Sofia A., 4-year-old female
Chief Complaint
Severe sore throat, stridor, drooling, high fever — 4-hour progression
PMHx

Medications
None
Allergies
Unknown
Onset
Parents noticed sore throat and fever this morning. Over 4 hours, child progressed from mild throat pain to inability to swallow, drooling, and stridor.
Key Observations
Classic "3 D's" potentially present: Drooling, Dysphagia, Distress. Child prefers sitting forward — becomes more distressed when repositioned supine.
🔬 Physical Examination Findings ▾
  • General: Toxic-appearing child, markedly anxious, in classic "tripod" (sniffing) position. Refuses to lie down.
  • Airway: Inspiratory stridor — high-pitched, progressive. Drooling. Muffled "hot potato" voice quality. DO NOT examine oropharynx — risk of complete obstruction.
  • Breathing: Tachypneic, mild intercostal retractions. SpO₂ 91% on 2L NC. No wheeze. Inspiratory noise predominates.
  • Circulation: Tachycardic 152 bpm, warm flushed skin (febrile), cap refill <2 sec. Peripheral pulses strong.
  • Neuro: GCS 14 — alert, extremely anxious and frightened. Recognizes parents. Moving all extremities.
  • Neck: Tender on anterior palpation. Subtle fullness noted at the level of the hyoid. No lymphadenopathy palpated (neck exam brief to avoid agitation).
📚 Background — Pathophysiology Review ▾
⚠ Research Priority Epiglottitis is historically caused by Haemophilus influenzae type B (HiB) — now rare due to vaccination but re-emerging in undervaccinated populations. Review: anatomy of the supraglottic airway, why the epiglottis swells so rapidly, why stimulation can cause complete obstruction, and the "thumb sign" on lateral neck X-ray. Research the management hierarchy: BVM vs. surgical airway in complete obstruction.
  • Etiology: HiB (classic), Group A Streptococcus, Staphylococcus aureus, thermal/chemical burns
  • Anatomy: Epiglottis sits at C3-C4 level; edema causes rapid supraglottic narrowing
  • The "4 D's": Drooling, Dysphagia, Dysphonia (muffled voice), Distress
  • Danger: Manipulation, crying, or agitation can trigger laryngospasm and complete obstruction
  • "Thumb sign": Enlarged epiglottis on lateral neck XR — looks like an adult thumb
  • Management: Keep child calm, allow parent to hold, blow-by O2, prepare for emergent airway
💊 Key Pharmacology & Airway Adjuncts (Reference) ▾
  • Dexamethasone: 0.6 mg/kg IV/IM (max 10mg) — Reduce epiglottic edema; controversial in field but useful during transport
  • Racemic Epinephrine: 0.5mL of 2.25% solution via NEB — Vasoconstriction to temporarily reduce edema (bridge to definitive airway)
  • Midazolam: 0.05–0.1 mg/kg IV — For anxiolysis if agitation escalates and intubation is imminent; use cautiously
  • Ketamine + Succinylcholine (RSI): Preferred agents if intubation is required — Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV, Succinylcholine 2 mg/kg IV (pediatric dose)
  • Surgical Airway: Needle cricothyrotomy or surgical cric — Last resort for cannot-intubate cannot-oxygenate scenario
  • Avoid: IV access attempts that agitate the child, throat exam, laying child flat until definitive airway is secured

📋 Patient History ▾
Patient
Marcus T., 11-year-old male
Chief Complaint
Syncope during exertion, chest tightness, dyspnea on exertion
PMHx
Known congenital aortic stenosis (bicuspid aortic valve — diagnosed infancy). Followed by pediatric cardiology. No prior surgeries. Parents report cardiologist suggested "watching it" until symptoms develop.
Medications
Atenolol 25mg daily (beta-blocker). No other medications.
Allergies
Penicillin (rash)
Onset
During 60-yard dash in PE class. Brief LOC approximately 30 seconds per coach. Pre-syncopal symptoms of "tunnel vision" and nausea. Similar (milder) episode 3 weeks ago — not reported to parents.
Pertinent History
Mother states Marcus has had decreased exercise tolerance recently — "can't keep up with his teammates." Reports occasional chest pain with exertion over the past 2 months. No prior syncope documented in medical records.
🔬 Physical Examination Findings ▾
  • General: Alert male, pale, anxious, diaphoretic. Reports chest tightness and shortness of breath. Oriented × 4.
  • Airway: Patent. Speaking full sentences but breathless with minimal exertion.
  • Cardiac Auscultation: Harsh 4/6 systolic ejection murmur — RUSB, radiation to bilateral carotids. S3 or S4 may be present (listen carefully). Diminished S2.
  • Pulses: Pulsus parvus et tardus — weak, delayed radial pulse rise compared to carotid. Peripheral pulses weak bilaterally.
  • Circulation: BP 72/50 (narrow pulse pressure). Cap refill 3 seconds. Skin pale and cool.
  • Respiratory: Mild basilar crackles (early pulmonary edema). No overt wheeze.
  • Extremities: No clubbing, no edema. Slight cyanosis in nail beds.
📚 Background — Pathophysiology Review ▾
📖 Research This Aortic stenosis (AS) is the most common obstructive valvular lesion in children. The classic triad of severe AS is: Angina, Syncope, and Heart Failure ("ASH"). The appearance of syncope — especially exertional syncope — marks a critical turning point in the disease. Research: bicuspid aortic valve anatomy, compensatory LVH, the Gorlin formula, and why afterload reduction agents (nitroglycerin, morphine) are DANGEROUS in severe AS.
  • Bicuspid aortic valve: Present in 1–2% of population; most common congenital cardiac anomaly requiring intervention
  • Obstruction → increased afterload → LVH → increased O2 demand → subendocardial ischemia → angina
  • Exertional syncope: During exercise, peripheral vasodilation + fixed cardiac output → cerebral hypoperfusion → syncope
  • Narrow pulse pressure: Small difference between SBP and DBP = low stroke volume (a critical sign)
  • Pulsus parvus et tardus: Weak and slow-rising arterial pulse — hallmark of severe AS
  • CONTRAINDICATED: Preload-reducing agents (nitrates), afterload reduction (vasodilators) — will cause cardiovascular collapse
💊 Key Pharmacology & Considerations (Reference) ▾
  • Oxygen: High-flow O2 — reduces hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, supports myocardial oxygenation
  • IV Fluid (Cautious): 10 mL/kg NS bolus — maintain preload carefully. Over-resuscitation risks pulmonary edema.
  • Dopamine: 5–10 mcg/kg/min — for hypotension and poor perfusion. Maintain SVR.
  • Avoid Nitroglycerin: ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED — preload reduction → catastrophic hypotension in severe AS
  • Avoid Morphine: May cause vasodilation — use extreme caution
  • Transcutaneous Pacing: Available if bradyarrhythmia contributes to hypotension
  • Definitive: Surgical aortic valve replacement or balloon valvuloplasty — hospital procedure

Angioedema visible — lips markedly swollen, tongue edema noted. Audible expiratory wheeze and inspiratory stridor. No IV access. Stinger may still be present at the left forearm.
📋 Patient History ▾
Patient
Amara J., 9-year-old female
Chief Complaint
Bee sting → anaphylaxis with respiratory compromise and circulatory collapse
PMHx
Known severe bee venom allergy — previous anaphylaxis at age 6 (required epinephrine, hospitalized). Otherwise healthy. No asthma. No other allergies documented.
Medications
EpiPen Jr (0.15mg auto-injector) — prescribed but parent unable to administer. Cetirizine (Zyrtec) daily for seasonal allergies.
Allergies
Bee venom (SEVERE — anaphylaxis); Peanuts (mild — rash only, no systemic)
Onset/Mechanism
Stung left forearm while playing approximately 10 minutes ago. Immediate local pain. Hives appeared within 3 minutes. Shortness of breath 5 minutes post-sting. Altered consciousness in last 2 minutes.
Parent Reports
"She said her throat felt like it was closing." Child's last oral intake was 30 min prior (snacks at the park). No other medications given. Stinger may be embedded.
🔬 Physical Examination Findings ▾
  • General: Limp, lethargic child. Responds to painful stimuli. Skin warm and flushed (massive vasodilation). Widespread raised, blanching urticaria — trunk, arms, legs.
  • Airway: Lips markedly edematous. Tongue swollen. Stridor audible — progressive supraglottic edema. Drooling. Voice muffled/hoarse.
  • Breathing: Tachypnea 34/min. Bilateral expiratory wheeze — bronchospasm. SpO₂ 87% on room air. Moderate accessory muscle use.
  • Circulation: Profound hypotension 68/40. Tachycardia 148 bpm. Peripheral pulses faint and thready. Cap refill >4 seconds. Distributive shock pattern.
  • Skin: Urticaria generalized. Angioedema face/lips/tongue. Left forearm: local wheal and flare at sting site. Stinger visible.
  • Neuro: GCS 11 (E3V3M5). Altered — decreasing LOC. Was verbal 2 minutes ago, now only groaning.
📚 Background — Pathophysiology Review ▾
📖 Research This Anaphylaxis is a life-threatening, IgE-mediated systemic allergic reaction causing massive mast cell and basophil degranulation. This releases histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, and leukotrienes causing simultaneous vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and bronchospasm. Research: the role of epinephrine as the ONLY first-line treatment, biphasic anaphylaxis, and why antihistamines (Benadryl) are NOT first-line treatment despite common misconception.
  • IgE-mediated: Prior sensitization → IgE antibodies on mast cells → re-exposure → massive degranulation
  • Histamine effects: H1 → bronchospasm, urticaria; H2 → vasodilation, gastric; combined → distributive shock
  • Angioedema: Deep tissue edema from vascular permeability — can cause complete airway obstruction
  • Distributive shock: Massive vasodilation + fluid shift → low SVR → hypotension despite normal/high HR
  • Biphasic anaphylaxis: Recurrence 8–12 hours later (in 20% of cases) even after initial resolution — requires monitoring
  • Epinephrine MOA: α1 → vasoconstriction (reverses shock); β1 → increased HR/contractility; β2 → bronchodilation; also mast cell stabilization
💊 Key Pharmacology (Reference) ▾
  • Epinephrine 1:1,000 IM (FIRST-LINE): 0.01 mg/kg IM lateral thigh (max 0.5mg). Peds: EpiPen Jr 0.15mg for <30kg, EpiPen 0.3mg for ≥30kg. Repeat q5–15 min if needed.
  • Epinephrine 1:10,000 IV: 0.1 mL/kg slow IV push — for refractory anaphylaxis with cardiovascular collapse. Use extreme caution.
  • Normal Saline: 20 mL/kg IV bolus — for distributive shock, repeat as needed for hemodynamic instability
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl): 1 mg/kg IV/IM (max 50mg) — H1 blocker, adjunct ONLY — NOT a substitute for epinephrine. Does not reverse shock.
  • Methylprednisolone: 1–2 mg/kg IV — for preventing biphasic reaction and reducing ongoing inflammation
  • Albuterol NEB: 2.5mg NEB — for bronchospasm component that persists after epinephrine
  • Glucagon: 1 mg IV — for anaphylaxis in patients on beta-blockers (reverses β-blocker effects on myocardium)
