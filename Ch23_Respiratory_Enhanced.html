Section 01

Anatomy & Physiology Review

Understanding respiratory anatomy and the physiology of gas exchange is the foundation for recognizing and managing respiratory emergencies.

🔼 Upper Airway Structures

Located above the glottis. Primary functions include filtering, warming, and humidifying inspired air.

  • Nasopharynx — posterior nasal passage, lined with ciliated mucosa
  • Oropharynx — posterior oral cavity, shared by digestive/respiratory tracts
  • Laryngopharynx — inferior pharynx; divides into larynx and esophagus
  • Larynx — voice box; contains the epiglottis, thyroid cartilage, vocal cords, and glottic opening
🔽 Lower Airway Structures

Located below the glottis. Site of gas exchange and bronchial conduction.

  • Trachea — 10–12 cm rigid tube; 16–20 C-shaped cartilage rings; bifurcates at the carina (T4–T5)
  • Bronchial tree — right/left main bronchi → lobar → segmental → terminal bronchioles
  • Alveoli — ~300 million; primary site of gas exchange; coated with surfactant to prevent collapse
  • Lungs — right (3 lobes), left (2 lobes); covered by visceral pleura
🔄 Physiology of Gas Exchange

The essence of respiratory physiology is the exchange of gases between body cells and the outside environment. This involves two interconnected processes:

🌬
External Respiration Transfer of O₂ and CO₂ between inspired air and the pulmonary capillaries at the alveolar-capillary membrane.
🩸
Internal Respiration Transfer of O₂ and CO₂ between capillary red blood cells and tissue cells at the systemic capillary level.
🧠 Control of Breathing

Central Control

  • Medullary respiratory center — primary rhythm generator
  • Pneumotaxic center (pons) — regulates rate
  • Apneustic center (pons) — prolongs inspiration

Chemoreceptors

  • Central: respond to ↑ CO₂/↓ pH in CSF (primary driver)
  • Peripheral (carotid/aortic bodies): respond to ↓ PaO₂, ↑ PaCO₂, ↓ pH
  • COPD patients may rely on hypoxic drive

Normal Values

  • PaO₂: 80–100 mmHg
  • PaCO₂: 35–45 mmHg
  • SpO₂: 95–100%
  • ETCO₂: 35–45 mmHg
  • RR: 12–20 /min (adult)

Section 02

Pathophysiology of Respiratory Emergencies

Respiratory failure can result from problems with ventilation, diffusion, or perfusion — or any combination. Understanding which process is impaired guides treatment.

⚠️
Clinical Pearl Gas exchange requires all three systems to work: Ventilation moves air in/out, Diffusion transfers gases across the alveolar membrane, and Perfusion carries oxygenated blood to tissues. A defect in any one of these impairs cellular oxygenation.
ProcessDefinitionRequirementsCauses of FailurePrehospital Tx
Ventilation Air movement in/out of lungs Intact neurologic control, patent airways, functional respiratory muscles, non-collapsed alveoli CNS injury/depression, obstruction, paralysis, flail chest, pneumothorax, diaphragm injury Open/clear airway; BVM/PPV; advanced airway if indicated
Diffusion Gas movement from high to low concentration across alveolar-capillary membrane Intact alveolar/capillary walls; non-thickened interstitium Pulmonary edema, ARDS, pneumonia, asbestosis, blebs, submersion, EVALI High-concentration O₂; reduce interstitial inflammation; urgent transport
Perfusion Circulation of blood through pulmonary capillaries Adequate blood volume and Hgb; patent pulmonary capillaries; effective cardiac output Pulmonary embolism, hypovolemia, anemia, cardiac failure IV fluid resuscitation; adequate Hgb; treat underlying cause
⚠️ Intrinsic Risk Factors

Genetic predisposition may influence development of:

  • Asthma and reactive airway disease
  • Obstructive lung disease (alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency)
  • Pulmonary malignancy
  • Pulmonary edema (cardiac etiology)
  • Pulmonary emboli (coagulation disorders)
🚬 Extrinsic Risk Factors

Environmental and behavioral factors that increase respiratory disease risk:

  • Cigarette smoking (COPD, cancer, ↑ severity of all respiratory disease)
  • Indoor/outdoor air pollution (↑ COPD prevalence; ↑ obstructive disease severity)
  • Occupational exposures (asbestos, silica, coal dust)
  • Passive smoke exposure
  • Obesity (restrictive effects, sleep apnea)

He has a barrel chest and tells you he has "COPD real bad." His wife says he's been gradually getting worse for 2 days with increased sputum production.

❓ Which process (ventilation, diffusion, or perfusion) is primarily impaired? What does the ETCO₂ of 58 tell you?

🔍 Clinical Analysis

This patient has a combined ventilation and diffusion problem. The elevated ETCO₂ of 58 mmHg indicates ventilatory failure (hypercapnia) — CO₂ is not being adequately removed. The severe hypoxia (SpO₂ 84%) suggests concurrent diffusion impairment from the COPD exacerbation with likely pneumonia (fever, increased sputum, worsening over 2 days).

Management priority: Low-flow O₂ titrated to SpO₂ 88–92% (avoid suppressing hypoxic drive), consider NIPPV (CPAP/BiPAP), bronchodilators (albuterol + ipratropium), IV access, cardiac monitoring, and priority transport. The elevated fever warrants investigation for infection as the exacerbating trigger.

Section 03

Respiratory Failure

Respiratory failure is a syndrome in which the respiratory system fails to maintain adequate gas exchange — leading to hypoxemia with or without hypercapnia.

📉 Type I — Hypoxemic Failure

Criteria: PaO₂ <60 mmHg

Failure of oxygenation — lungs cannot adequately transfer O₂ to blood. Often results from V/Q mismatch or diffusion problems.

  • Perfusion defect / V̇/Q̇ mismatch
  • Pneumonia, pulmonary edema
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Pulmonary contusion
  • Sepsis, ARDS
📈 Type II — Hypercapnic Failure

Criteria: PaCO₂ >55 mmHg

Failure of ventilation — lungs cannot eliminate CO₂ adequately. Results in respiratory acidosis.

  • Asthma, COPD exacerbation
  • Bronchiectasis
  • CNS injury / drug overdose
  • Respiratory muscle fatigue
  • Pump failure (myasthenia gravis, GBS)
🚨 Key Signs of Impending Respiratory Failure

Objective Signs

  • ↓ SpO₂ despite O₂ therapy
  • ↑ ETCO₂ (ventilatory failure)
  • Seesaw (paradoxical) ventilation
  • Nasal flaring, severe retractions
  • Cyanosis (central)
  • Arrhythmias (hypoxia-driven)

Subjective/Clinical Signs

  • Altered/decreasing mental status
  • Evidence of respiratory fatigue
  • Inability to speak more than 2–3 words
  • Tripod positioning
  • Diaphoresis and pallor
  • Paradoxical chest movement
🔴
Immediate Action Triggers Any patient with altered mental status + respiratory distress requires aggressive intervention — BVM, CPAP/BiPAP, or RSI. Do not delay for scene time. Respiratory failure that progresses to arrest carries <5% survival to discharge.
📊 Hemodynamic Monitoring in Respiratory Failure

Continuous ECG monitoring is essential — hypoxia and hypercapnia rapidly cause arrhythmias. These are the typical ECG patterns seen during respiratory compromise:

Sinus Tachycardia — Early Hypoxia Response
Arrhythmia Risk — Advanced Hypoxia

⚠ Any arrhythmia in a respiratory patient should be treated by fixing the underlying hypoxia/hypercapnia first — antiarrhythmics will not work until oxygenation improves.

💊 Medication History

Medications provide critical diagnostic clues. Specifically ask about:

  • Inhalers (rescue vs. maintenance — albuterol, salmeterol, tiotropium)
  • Home oxygen and CPAP/BiPAP usage
  • Corticosteroids (chronic lung disease, immunosuppression)
  • Diuretics (loop diuretics → CHF/pulmonary edema)
  • ACE inhibitors (bradykinin-mediated cough)
  • Beta-blockers (may worsen bronchospasm)
  • Anticoagulants (PE history)
  • Compliance with prescribed medications
👁️ Visual Inspection
  • Position — Tripod, upright, recumbent (can't lay flat = orthopnea → CHF)
  • Pursed-lip breathing — COPD; splints airways open during exhalation
  • Barrel chest — Air trapping (COPD/emphysema)
  • Grunting — Peds; PEEP effect; attempts to maintain alveolar patency
  • Nasal flaring — Accessory muscle use; high work of breathing
  • Retractions — Subclavicular, intercostal, substernal; high WOB
  • Accessory muscle use — SCM, scalenes engaged in inspiration

Section 05

Diagnostic Testing

Prehospital diagnostic tools provide objective data to guide treatment decisions and monitor response to interventions.

📡 Pulse Oximetry (SpO₂)

SpO₂ measures the percentage of hemoglobin saturated with oxygen using infrared light absorption. It represents the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve — note the steep portion (60–90%) where small PaO₂ changes cause large saturation drops.

SpO₂ RangeInterpretationAction
97–100%NormalMonitor
94–96%Low-normal/mild hypoxemiaSupplemental O₂ PRN
90–93%Moderate hypoxemiaO₂ therapy required
<90%Severe hypoxemiaAggressive O₂/NIPPV
⚠️
SpO₂ Limitations CO poisoning (false high), methemoglobinemia (falsely reads ~85%), anemia (can be normal SpO₂ with severe hypoxia), poor perfusion/vasoconstriction (unreliable), dark nail polish (interference).
📌
COPD O₂ Target For known COPD patients, titrate O₂ to SpO₂88–92% to avoid abolishing the hypoxic drive and precipitating hypercapnic respiratory failure.

💨 Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR)

Peak flowmeters measure the maximum speed of forced exhalation, reflecting the degree of airway obstruction. Most useful for quantifying asthma severity and tracking bronchodilator response.

% of Personal BestSeverityAction
≥80%MildPRN bronchodilator

⚠️
Limitations Requires patient cooperation and adequate effort. Unreliable in very young children, severely obtunded patients, or those in extreme distress. A normal PEFR does not rule out significant pathology if effort was poor.

Section 06

COPD: Chronic Bronchitis & Emphysema

COPD is a major health problem affecting nearly 41 million Americans, consisting of a triad of chronic bronchitis, emphysema, and chronic obstructive asthma — diseases that often coexist.

🔵 Chronic Bronchitis

CRITERIA: Productive cough ≥3 months/year × 2 consecutive years

Characterized by hypertrophy and hyperplasia of mucus-secreting goblet cells, leading to excess mucus production, ciliary dysfunction, and chronic airway obstruction. Often called "Blue Bloater" — cyanotic due to low PaO₂, edematous from cor pulmonale.

  • ↑ number and size of mucus-producing glands
  • Chronic productive cough (often purulent)
  • Frequent respiratory infections
  • Low PaO₂; compensatory polycythemia
  • Can progress to: emphysema, bronchiectasis
💨 Emphysema

MECHANISM: Alveolar wall destruction → air trapping

Results from destruction of alveolar walls beyond terminal bronchioles, leading to permanently enlarged air spaces, loss of elastic recoil, and dynamic airway collapse on exhalation. Often called "Pink Puffer" — breathes hard to maintain oxygenation, rarely cyanotic until late.

  • Permanent abnormal air space enlargement
  • Destruction and collapse of alveoli
  • Barrel chest from chronic hyperinflation
  • Pursed-lip breathing (auto-PEEP effect)
  • Progressively worsening dyspnea on exertion
🔍 COPD Exacerbation — Assessment

Acute exacerbations most commonly triggered by respiratory infections, environmental pollutants, or medication non-compliance.

Typical Presentation

  • Worsening dyspnea at rest (beyond baseline)
  • Increased sputum production or change in character
  • Malaise, fatigue
  • Wheezes, rhonchi, crackles on auscultation
  • Prolonged expiratory phase
  • Tripod position, pursed-lip breathing
  • ↑ ETCO₂ (CO₂ retention)

Severe/Late Signs

  • SpO₂ <88% despite O₂
  • Altered mental status (CO₂ narcosis)
  • Asterixis (flapping tremor)
  • Paradoxical breathing
  • Inability to speak even one word
  • Cyanosis
  • Bradycardia (very late/ominous)
💊 COPD Management & Pharmacology
Albuterol (Salbutamol)
Beta₂ Agonist — Bronchodilator
Mechanism
Stimulates β₂ receptors → smooth muscle relaxation → bronchodilation; also inhibits mast cell degranulation
Dose (Adult)
2.5 mg in 3 mL NS via nebulizer; 2 puffs (90 mcg/puff) via MDI q20min × 3 then q4–6h PRN
Onset/Duration
5–15 min / 3–6 hours
Side Effects
Tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, hypokalemia (high doses), headache
Ipratropium Bromide (Atrovent)
Anticholinergic — Bronchodilator
Mechanism
Blocks muscarinic receptors → inhibits bronchoconstriction + reduces mucus secretion; synergistic with albuterol
Dose (Adult)
0.5 mg in 2.5 mL NS via nebulizer (can mix with albuterol); q6–8h
Use in COPD
Particularly effective in COPD (more cholinergic tone); often combined as DuoNeb (albuterol + ipratropium)
Caution
Avoid spraying in eyes (worsens glaucoma); use with caution in BPH (urinary retention)
Methylprednisolone / Dexamethasone
Corticosteroid — Anti-inflammatory
Mechanism
Inhibits inflammatory cytokine release; reduces airway edema; upregulates β₂ receptors; decreases mucus production
Dose
Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV; Dexamethasone 10 mg IV/IM; Solu-Medrol per protocol
Onset
4–6 hours for full anti-inflammatory effect — give early, benefits accrue over time
Use
Moderate-to-severe COPD/asthma exacerbations; reduces hospital admission when given early
🫁
NIPPV (Non-Invasive Positive Pressure Ventilation) CPAP/BiPAP is the preferred intervention for moderate-to-severe COPD exacerbations prior to intubation. BiPAP (two-level pressure) is preferred over CPAP for hypercapnic COPD because the inspiratory pressure support helps augment tidal volume and reduce CO₂. Target: IPAP 10–20 cmH₂O / EPAP 4–8 cmH₂O.

Section 07

Asthma & Status Asthmaticus

Asthma is a reactive airway disease characterized by reversible bronchospasm, airway inflammation, and mucus hypersecretion — and is the most common chronic disease of childhood.

🔬 Pathophysiology Triad
  • Bronchospasm — Smooth muscle contraction narrows airways; triggered by allergens, irritants, exercise, infections
  • Mucosal edema — Inflammatory mediators (histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins) → airway wall swelling
  • Mucus hypersecretion — Goblet cell activation → thick mucus plugging
⚡
Air Trapping Narrowed airways allow air in on inspiration but collapse during exhalation → hyperinflation → auto-PEEP → ↑ work of breathing → respiratory muscle fatigue.
🎯 Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic Triggers

EXTRINSIC (children)

  • Allergens (pollen, dust mites, pet dander)
  • Mold spores
  • Food additives (sulfites)
  • Often improves with age

INTRINSIC (adults)

  • Infections (URTI)
  • Exercise (cold dry air)
  • NSAIDs/ASA (aspirin-exacerbated)
  • Stress/emotions
  • Usually persistent

19-year-old female college student found by roommate sitting in tripod position, speaking only 1–2 words at a time. She has known asthma and used her albuterol inhaler 3 times with no relief. Began about 2 hours ago after visiting a friend's house with cats.
HR
128 bpm
RR
32 /min
SpO₂
88%
BP
108/72
ETCO₂
52 mmHg
PEFR
40% PB
❓ How do you classify this asthma attack? What does the elevated ETCO₂ of 52 tell you in the context of asthma?

🔍 Clinical Analysis — Status Asthmaticus

This is severe asthma / approaching status asthmaticus. The ETCO₂ of 52 mmHg is critically significant: early asthma causes hyperventilation and low ETCO₂. A rising or high ETCO₂ in an asthmatic means they are too tired to hyperventilate — they are fatiguing and progressing toward respiratory arrest. The PEFR of 40% personal best confirms severe obstruction.

Treatment: (1) High-flow O₂; (2) Nebulized albuterol 2.5 mg continuous or every 20 minutes; (3) Ipratropium 0.5 mg × 1; (4) IV methylprednisolone 125 mg; (5) Consider nebulized magnesium sulfate 151 mg/3 mL; (6) IV access + cardiac monitor; (7) Prepare for intubation if no response — but use ketamine for RSI induction to preserve bronchodilation. Transport priority IMMEDIATELY.

💊 Asthma Pharmacology
Magnesium Sulfate
Bronchodilator — Smooth Muscle Relaxant
Mechanism
Ca²⁺ antagonist → inhibits smooth muscle contraction → bronchodilation; also reduces ACh release and histamine secretion
Dose (Nebulized)
151 mg in 3 mL NS via nebulizer; add to albuterol in severe cases
Dose (IV)
2 g in 50 mL NS over 20 min IV (check local protocol)
Use
Severe / refractory asthma not responding to initial bronchodilators; safe in pregnancy
🔴 Status Asthmaticus

A severe, prolonged asthma attack that does not respond to repeated doses of bronchodilators — a true respiratory emergency with risk of death.

  • Same treatment as acute attack, but more urgent and aggressive
  • IV fluids for rehydration (dehydration worsens mucus plugging)
  • High-concentration oxygen — do not withhold
  • Anticipate need for intubation; use ketamine (bronchodilatory) for RSI induction
  • Consider heliox (helium-oxygen mixture) if available
  • Post-intubation: permissive hypercapnia strategy; avoid auto-PEEP

Section 08

COVID-19

An infectious disease caused by SARS-CoV-2 — the fifth documented pandemic since the 1918 influenza. Attributed to more than 6.4 million deaths worldwide.

🦠 Pathophysiology
  • Spreads via aerosolized droplets entering eyes, nose, mouth
  • Binds to ACE2 receptors on epithelial cells of respiratory tract
  • Reproduces in upper airway; migrates to lower respiratory tract
  • Triggers immune response → cytokine storm (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β)
  • Systemic complications: DVT, PE, limb ischemia, stroke
📋 Assessment Criteria

Most patients experience mild flu-like illness; physician evaluation indicated for:

  • Severe dyspnea
  • SpO₂ ≤90% on room air (regardless of dyspnea)
  • Mental status changes
  • Advanced age or significant comorbidities
  • "Happy hypoxia" — surprisingly low SpO₂ with minimal distress (common in COVID)
🏥 COVID-19 Management
SeveritySpO₂Prehospital Management
Mild≥95%Supportive; oral hydration; home care; physician follow-up
Moderate90–94%Supplemental O₂ (prefer mask over NC to reduce aerosolization); consider transport
Severe<90%High-flow O₂; NIPPV if available; awake proning position; transport priority
Critical (intubation)<85%Preoxygenate thoroughly; RSI preferred (minimize aerosolization); video laryngoscopy recommended; all PPE
😷
Infection Control N95 respirator (or higher), eye protection, gown, and gloves for all suspected COVID patients. Prefer mask O₂ delivery over nasal cannula. Use HEPA filters on BVM and ventilator circuits. Minimize aerosol-generating procedures.

Section 09

Pneumonia

A group of specific infections causing acute inflammatory process of respiratory bronchioles and alveoli — one of the leading causes of infectious death in the US.

🦠 Viral Pneumonia

Inflammation of the pulmonary parenchyma caused by viral pathogens. Most common types and their associated viruses:

  • Influenza (most common cause in adults) — Influenza A/B; seasonal; can be severe in elderly/immunocompromised
  • RSV (most common in infants/young children) — Respiratory Syncytial Virus; can cause severe bronchiolitis
  • Parainfluenza — Croup in children; walking pneumonia in adults
  • Adenovirus — Military recruits; immunocompromised
  • SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19)
📋
Signs & Symptoms (Viral) Chest pain, cough, fever, dyspnea, occasional hemoptysis, general malaise. Auscultation: wheezing, fine crackles. Symptoms typically resolve 7–10 days.
🔵 Bacterial Pneumonia

Pneumococcal (most common)

Streptococcus pneumoniae — most common cause; incidence ↓ due to PCV13/PPSV23 vaccines. Presents with acute onset, rust-colored sputum, lobar consolidation on CXR.

Mycoplasmal (atypical)

Mycoplasma pneumoniae — "walking pneumonia"; mild upper respiratory illness in school-age children and young adults; transmitted by infected respiratory secretions.

📋
Signs & Symptoms (Bacterial) Acute shaking chills, fever, tachypnea/tachycardia, productive cough (rust-colored/yellow/green/gray sputum), anorexia, malaise, flank/back pain, vomiting, hypoxemia. Symptoms typically resolve 3–5 days with antibiotics.
🍄 Fungal Pneumonia
  • Caused by environmental fungi (Histoplasma, Coccidioides, Aspergillus, Pneumocystis)
  • Accounts for small percentage of community-acquired pneumonia
  • Most common in immunocompromised (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy, steroids)
  • PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia) — common AIDS-defining illness
  • Treated with antifungal agents (fluconazole, amphotericin B, TMP-SMX for PCP)
  • Prehospital management: supportive; O₂; transport
⚠️ Aspiration Pneumonia/Pneumonitis

Inflammation of lung tissue resulting from aspiration of foreign material into the tracheobronchial tree. High-risk patients:

  • Altered level of consciousness (alcohol, sedatives, seizures, stroke)
  • Intubated patients (tube bypasses protective reflexes)
  • Esophageal disorders (GERD, dysphagia)
  • Patients with foreign body aspiration
⚠️
Signs & Symptoms (Aspiration) Dyspnea, cough, bronchospasm; wheezes, rhonchi, crackles; cyanosis; pulmonary and cardiac insufficiency. Monitor for sepsis: HR >90, RR >20, Temp >38.3°C or <36.1°C. Poor prognosis especially with acidic gastric content (Mendelson syndrome).
🏥 Prehospital Management — All Pneumonia Types
  • Provide airway support and suction as needed
  • Administer oxygen — titrate to SpO₂ ≥94%
  • Provide ventilatory assistance as needed (BVM, NIPPV)
  • Establish IV access — administer fluids to support BP and thin mucus
  • Apply cardiac monitor
  • Transport for evaluation by physician (antibiotics, cultures, CXR, definitive care)
🔬
Prehospital Note Paramedics cannot diagnose specific pneumonia type (viral vs. bacterial vs. fungal) in the field. Management is supportive regardless of type. Accurate documentation of presentation, vital trends, and O₂ requirements is critical for in-hospital management decisions.

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

ARDS is a form of hypoxemic respiratory failure characterized by diffuse alveolar damage, non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema, and refractory hypoxemia.

🔬 Pathophysiology

ARDS results from direct or indirect lung injury triggering inflammatory cascade:

  • Release of inflammatory mediators damages alveolar-capillary membrane
  • Protein-rich fluid floods alveoli → non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Surfactant destruction → alveolar collapse (atelectasis)
  • Hyaline membrane formation → diffusion impairment
  • Intrapulmonary shunting → severe, refractory hypoxemia
  • Reduced lung compliance → high airway pressures to ventilate
⚠️ Common Causes (ARDS)

DIRECT INJURY

  • Pneumonia (bacterial, viral)
  • Aspiration
  • Submersion/drowning
  • Inhalation injury
  • Pulmonary contusion

INDIRECT INJURY

  • Sepsis (most common)
  • Severe trauma
  • Burns
  • Massive transfusion (TRALI)
  • Drug overdose
🏥 ARDS Management

ARDS management is primarily supportive and is directed at the underlying cause while providing lung-protective ventilation.

  • Identify and treat the underlying cause
  • Provide high-flow nasal O₂ or NIPPV for awake patients
  • Lung-protective ventilation: low tidal volumes (6 mL/kg IBW), high PEEP, permissive hypercapnia
  • IV fluid management — conservative fluids to minimize pulmonary edema (diuretics if fluid-overloaded)
  • Consider prone positioning (improves V/Q matching)
  • Pharmacologic agents to stabilize pulmonary, capillary, and alveolar walls
🔴
Prognosis ARDS carries a mortality of 30–40%. Refractory hypoxemia (SpO₂ <88% despite high FiO₂) is the hallmark. High-flow O₂ alone is insufficient — mechanical ventilation is required for survival in severe cases.

Pulmonary Embolism

Blockage of a pulmonary artery by a clot or other foreign material — sudden death is the first sign in approximately 25% of patients.

🔺 Virchow's Triad — Risk Factors for Clot Formation
🩸
Hypercoagulability Active cancer (especially metastatic); oral contraceptives; pregnancy; autoimmune disorders; congenital coagulation disorders (Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency)
🛑
Venous Stasis Extended travel (long flights/car rides); prolonged bed rest; obesity; advanced age; varicose veins; immobilization post-surgery
💉
Vessel Injury Surgery of chest, abdomen, pelvis, or legs; burns; long bone fractures; fractures of pelvis or legs; trauma
🔍 Signs & Symptoms — Classic vs. Atypical

Classic Presentation

  • Sudden onset pleuritic chest pain
  • Dyspnea — often out of proportion to findings
  • Tachycardia (sinus) — most common ECG finding
  • Tachypnea
  • Hemoptysis (classic but uncommon)
  • Signs of DVT: unilateral leg swelling, tenderness

Additional Findings

  • Anxiety and sense of impending doom
  • Syncope (massive PE)
  • Hypotension (obstructive shock → massive PE)
  • Diaphoresis
  • Distended neck veins (massive PE → R heart strain)
  • Pleural friction rub (pulmonary infarction)
  • Fever, localized wheezing, crackles
🚨
ECG in PE — S₁Q₃T₃ Pattern S wave in lead I + Q wave in III + inverted T in III = classic PE pattern (only ~20% sensitive). More commonly: sinus tachycardia, RBBB, ST changes, or new atrial fibrillation. A normal ECG does NOT rule out PE.
🏥 Prehospital Management

Prehospital care for PE is primarily supportive — definitive treatment (anticoagulation, thrombolytics, catheter-directed therapy, surgical embolectomy) requires hospital resources.

  • Supplemental high-concentration oxygen — titrate to SpO₂ ≥94%
  • IV access — large bore × 2 if hemodynamically unstable
  • Cardiac monitoring — 12-lead ECG (look for S1Q3T3, sinus tachycardia, RBBB)
  • Position of comfort — semi-Fowler's usually preferred
  • IV fluid bolus (250 mL NS) if hypotensive — judiciously (avoid fluid overload into impaired RV)
  • Vasopressors (norepinephrine) if refractory hypotension per protocol
  • Priority transport with early notification to receiving facility

Upper Respiratory Infections & Spontaneous Pneumothorax

Upper respiratory infections are common and rarely life-threatening, but can exacerbate underlying pulmonary conditions. Spontaneous pneumothorax requires rapid recognition and can progress to tension pneumothorax.

🤧 Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URI)

Can affect nose, throat, sinuses, and larynx. Includes common cold, pharyngitis, tonsillitis, sinusitis, and laryngitis. May significantly exacerbate underlying pulmonary conditions (asthma, COPD).

Signs & Symptoms:

  • Sore throat, fever, chills, headache
  • Facial pain/pressure (sinusitis)
  • Purulent nasal drainage
  • Halitosis (bad breath)
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Erythematous pharynx
📋
Prehospital Management Primarily symptomatic relief; follow-up with physician recommended; monitor for signs of lower respiratory tract involvement.
💨 Spontaneous Pneumothorax

Usually results from rupture of a bleb (thin-walled air-filled sac), allowing air to enter the pleural space. Primary spontaneous pneumothorax occurs in otherwise healthy individuals, typically tall/thin males aged 20–40.

MILD-MODERATE

  • Sudden onset chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Pallor, diaphoresis
  • Tachypnea

SEVERE / TENSION

  • Altered mental status
  • Cyanosis
  • Absent breath sounds (affected side)
  • Tracheal deviation (late)
  • Hypotension (obstructive shock)
🚨
Tension Pneumothorax Treatment Immediate needle decompression: 2nd ICS, MCL (or 4th–5th ICS, AAL) with 14–16 gauge needle. Do NOT wait for CXR. Follow with chest tube at hospital. In cardiac arrest, bilateral needle decompression.

Hyperventilation Syndrome

Abnormally deep or rapid breathing resulting in excessive CO₂ loss, hypocapnia, and respiratory alkalosis — with important masquerading conditions to rule out.

🌀 Pathophysiology

Hyperventilation → ↓ PaCO₂ (hypocapnia) → cerebrovascular constriction → ↓ cerebral perfusion → neurological symptoms. Hypocapnia also causes respiratory alkalosis (↑ pH) → altered calcium binding → neuromuscular excitability.

Signs & Symptoms:

  • Dyspnea with rapid breathing, high minute volume
  • Chest tightness/pain
  • Perioral and facial tingling/numbness
  • Carpopedal spasm (Trousseau's sign — hypocalcemia from alkalosis)
  • Dizziness, near-syncope
  • Feelings of euphoria or panic

Underlying Causes (RULE OUT FIRST!)

  • Hypoxia — Do NOT suppress breathing
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Metabolic acidosis (DKA, sepsis) — breathing compensates
  • Cardiovascular disorders
  • Neurologic disorders
  • Anxiety/panic attacks (diagnosis of exclusion)
  • Fever, infection, pain, pregnancy
  • Drug use (salicylates cause hyperventilation)
🏥 Management
🚫
NEVER Use a Paper Bag The classic "paper bag" technique for hyperventilation is CONTRAINDICATED. Rebreathing CO₂ can worsen hypoxia and has caused deaths in patients with underlying hypoxic conditions (PE, MI, pneumothorax) that were mistakenly diagnosed as anxiety.
  • Rule out life-threatening causes (hypoxia, PE, MI, DKA, sepsis)
  • Administer oxygen — do not withhold
  • Provide airway/ventilatory support if needed
  • Calm the patient; provide reassurance; verbal coaching of ventilations
  • Do NOT attempt to forcibly slow ventilations if patient is physiologically compensating for metabolic acidosis or hypoxia
  • If severe, complicated, or no clear cause — transport for physician evaluation

Section 14

Lung Cancer

Most cases develop in people aged ≥65 years, and most new cases are fatal within one year. Cigarette smoking is the most common cause — responsible for ~85% of all lung cancers.

⚠️ Risk Factors
  • Cigarette/tobacco smoking (primary cause)
  • Passive/secondhand smoke exposure
  • Radon gas exposure (second leading cause)
  • Asbestos exposure (synergistic with smoking)
  • Occupational: coal products, chromium, arsenic, nickel
  • Radiation therapy to chest
  • Pulmonary fibrosis (scarring → malignant change)
  • HIV infection (↑ immune surveillance failure)
  • Genetic predisposition/family history
  • Alcohol consumption
🔬 Pathophysiology & Cell Types

🔍 Signs & Symptoms
  • Persistent or worsening cough
  • Hemoptysis (blood in sputum)
  • Dyspnea
  • Hoarseness or voice change (recurrent laryngeal nerve)
  • Dysphagia (esophageal involvement)
  • Unexplained weight loss and anorexia
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Chest pain (pleural involvement)
  • Superior vena cava syndrome (facial/arm swelling)
  • Pancoast syndrome (shoulder/arm pain — superior sulcus)
🏥 Prehospital Management
  • Provide airway, ventilatory, and circulatory support
  • Administer oxygen if indicated (SpO₂-guided)
  • Establish IV access; administer fluids if needed
  • Provide analgesics if patient is in pain (per protocol)
  • Drug therapy as indicated for symptom management
  • Transport for physician evaluation
📋
Advance Directives End-stage lung cancer patients often have advance directives or DNR/DNI orders. Always ask and document before initiating resuscitative measures. Comfort-focused care may be the appropriate priority.
