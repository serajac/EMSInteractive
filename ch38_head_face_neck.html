Module 1 · Foundation & Real-World Context

Head, Face & Neck Trauma

Traumatic brain injury is the leading cause of trauma mortality in adults under 45. Your next call could change everything.

Opening Scenario — Scene Size-Up
Unit 7, respond to a high-speed MVC on Route 58...
You arrive to find a 22-year-old male ejected from a pickup truck. Bystanders state he was unresponsive initially, then "woke up" and is now confused and combative. He has significant facial lacerations, is bleeding from both ears, and has a markedly swollen neck. His GCS is 11 (E3V3M5). BP 142/90, HR 58, RR 12. His left pupil is 6mm and sluggish; the right is 3mm and brisk. You have a 12-minute transport to the Level II trauma center.

What does his presentation tell you? What are your priorities? What interventions could kill him if done incorrectly? Hold these questions — this module will arm you with the answers.
⚡ Why Every Paramedic Must Master This
📊 The Reality Check Traumatic brain injury (TBI) accounts for approximately 30% of all injury-related deaths in the U.S. — over 61,000 annually. Of those who survive, half will have permanent disability. The critical window for intervention is measured in minutes, and prehospital decisions directly alter neurological outcomes.
⚠️ The Cost of Missed Assessment A "lucid interval" — the hallmark of epidural hematoma — can mask rapidly rising ICP. The patient who tells you "I feel fine" may herniate before arrival. Failing to recognize this pattern is a career-defining, life-altering error.
🏆 Your Impact as a Paramedic Studies consistently show that airway management, oxygenation, and prevention of secondary brain injury in the prehospital phase directly correlate with improved neurological outcomes. You are the first neuroprotective intervention.
🎯 Career Relevance Head, face, and neck trauma competency is evaluated in NREMT Paramedic Psychomotor Exam Station 3, appears heavily in cognitive exams, and is a clinical cornerstone for ACLS, ATLS, and specialty certifications (TPATC, CFRN, FP-C).

⚖️ The Monroe-Kellie Doctrine — The Central Concept of TBI

The skull is a rigid, fixed-volume container. Its contents are:

ComponentNormal Volume% of Total
Brain tissue~1,200–1,400 mL80%
CSF~100–150 mL10%
Blood~100–150 mL10%
Key Principle: Volume is fixed. If one component increases, another must decrease — or ICP rises. Normal ICP = 5–15 mmHg. ICP >20 mmHg requires treatment; >40 mmHg is incompatible with life.
Compensatory Mechanisms (Temporal Buffer)
Phase 1
CSF displaced — CSF shifts from cranium to spinal subarachnoid space. ICP remains normal. Patient may appear asymptomatic.
Phase 2
Venous blood expelled — cerebral venous blood compressed out of the cranium. ICP begins to rise incrementally. Headache, nausea, subtle mental status changes.
Phase 3
Decompensation — compensatory reserve exhausted. Small volume increases → large ICP spikes. Herniation begins. Rapid deterioration. Emergency intervention required.

Head Trauma — Skull & Brain Injuries

From scalp to brainstem — understanding the spectrum of traumatic brain injury.

🩹 Scalp Injuries

The scalp has 5 layers (mnemonic: SCALP):

LetterLayerClinical Note
SSkinExternal; hair-bearing
CConnective tissue (dense subcutaneous)Contains blood vessels, nerves — highly vascular
AAponeurosis (galea aponeurotica)Tough fibromuscular layer; when lacerated, vessels cannot constrict → profuse bleeding
LLoose areolar tissue"Dangerous layer" — infection spreads easily; subgaleal hematoma forms here
PPericraniumPeriosteum of skull; cephalohematoma in neonates
🩸
Hemorrhagic Shock from Scalp Lacerations — Scalp lacerations can cause significant blood loss, especially in elderly patients on anticoagulants or pediatric patients. A single scalp laceration can produce >1 liter of blood loss. Direct pressure is the priority — do NOT explore or probe. Do NOT remove impaled objects.
Subgaleal Hematoma: Bleeding in the loose areolar layer can cross suture lines and expand over the entire calvarium — up to 40% of an infant's or 10% of an adult's blood volume can be sequestered here.

Skull Fractures

💀 Types & Clinical Significance
Linear Fracture
Most Common (80%)

Non-displaced crack through full thickness. Benign in isolation, but watch for underlying epidural hematoma if over temporal region. Plain radiographs may miss it — CT is gold standard.

Depressed Fracture
Moderate Risk

Bone fragment displaced inward. Risk of cortical laceration and seizure. Often associated with underlying parenchymal injury. Surgical elevation required if >1 skull-thickness depressed.

Basilar Fracture
High Risk

Fracture of skull base. Often not visible on plain radiographs. Diagnosis is clinical. Do NOT insert NPA or NGT — risk of intracranial passage.

Diastatic Fracture
Pediatric

Fracture along suture line, widening it. Primarily in children <3 years where sutures are not fused. May present as "ping-pong ball" deformity in infants.

Halo Test: Place drop of bloody fluid on white linen. CSF will diffuse outward leaving a clear "halo" around the blood — suggests CSF contamination. Not definitive but clinically useful.
🚫
Absolute Contraindication: Nasogastric tube and nasopharyngeal airway insertion are contraindicated with suspected basilar skull fracture. Report to receiving facility. Use oropharyngeal approach or oral ET tube.

Traumatic Brain Injuries

🧠 Primary vs. Secondary Brain Injury
Primary Brain Injury
Occurs at moment of impact. Irreversible tissue destruction from mechanical forces. Nothing prehospital can reverse it. Your role: PREVENT secondary injury.
Secondary Brain Injury
Develops hours to days after primary injury. Caused by hypoxia, hypotension, hypercapnia, hyperthermia, hypoglycemia, herniation. 100% preventable with proper management.
💡
The 6 H's of Secondary Brain Injury: Hypoxia · Hypotension · Hypercapnia (↑CO₂) · Hypocapnia (↓CO₂ from aggressive ventilation) · Hyperthermia · Hypoglycemia. Memorize these — your entire TBI management strategy revolves around preventing them.

Concussion (mTBI)

Transient neurological dysfunction without structural injury. Most common TBI. No anatomic lesion on imaging.

FeatureDescription
MechanismAcceleration-deceleration; rotational forces
LOCBrief or absent (<30 minutes)
AmnesiaRetrograde and/or anterograde; duration varies
SymptomsHeadache, confusion, nausea, photophobia, cognitive "fog"
CT scanNormal — diagnosis is clinical
GCS13–15 typically
⚠️
Second Impact Syndrome: A second concussion before the first resolves → diffuse cerebral swelling → catastrophic herniation. Has occurred in young athletes. Absolute return-to-play protocol required.
Prehospital Management: Scene safety. Prevent further injury. Serial neurological assessment. Appropriate transport. Do NOT clear for return to activity. Facilitate ED evaluation — CTs ordered on clinical grounds (PECARN, Canadian CT Head Rule).

Cerebral Contusion

Bruising of brain parenchyma — microhemorrhages and edema within brain tissue. More severe than concussion; may have lasting deficits.

FeatureDescription
CoupInjury at site of impact
ContrecoupInjury opposite the impact (brain rebounds into skull)
LOCMinutes to hours; variable
GCSVariable; often 9–12
DeficitsFocal neurological based on location — speech, motor, behavioral
CT scanHyperdense areas within parenchyma; edema
Coup-Contrecoup: A classic example — fall backward hitting occiput. Occipital impact (coup) + frontal lobe contusion (contrecoup) from the brain sloshing forward. Frontal lobe = personality, executive function, motor cortex.
Management: Airway protection a priority if GCS ≤8. Avoid hypoxia/hypotension. Careful fluid management — avoid hyperosmolar states. Neurosurgery consult on arrival.

Epidural Hematoma (EDH)

Blood accumulates between the skull and dura mater. Classic cause: rupture of middle meningeal artery from temporal bone fracture.

FeatureDescription
SourceArterial (middle meningeal artery) — high-pressure
LocationTemporal lobe (most common); biconvex (lenticular) shape on CT
Classic historyDirect blow to temporal region (e.g., ball, fist, fall)
LUCID INTERVALLOC → regains consciousness → rapid deterioration. Present in ~50% of cases.
DeteriorationMinutes to hours; arterial bleed expands rapidly
GCSInitially normal → rapid drop → herniation
🚨
The Classic Presentation: Patient knocked out → wakes up talking → "Doc I feel fine" → 30 minutes later: blown pupil, decerebrate posturing, GCS 6. This is a surgical emergency. Mortality approaches 100% without immediate craniotomy — time is brain. Lights and siren transport.
Blown Pupil: CN III (oculomotor) runs along the tentorium. As the temporal lobe herniates downward (uncal herniation), CN III is compressed → ipsilateral pupil dilates and becomes unresponsive. This is a HERNIATION SIGN — emergency.

Subdural Hematoma (SDH)

Blood accumulates between dura and arachnoid. Typically venous — slower bleeding from bridging veins.

TypeOnsetPopulation
Acute SDH<24–48 hrs; high mortalityHigh-energy trauma; younger
Subacute SDH2 days – 2 weeksVariable
Chronic SDHWeeks to monthsElderly, alcoholics, anticoagulated patients
Chronic SDH Red Flags: "Foggy" elderly patient with headaches and mild cognitive decline. May not recall any injury (minor fall weeks ago). Bilateral chronic SDH → personality changes, dementia-like presentation.
Why elderly are at risk: Brain atrophy creates more "space" between brain and dura — bridging veins stretch longer and are under more tension. Minimal trauma (rolling out of bed) can tear them. Anticoagulation (warfarin, eliquis, xarelto) dramatically increases risk and magnitude.
CT Appearance: Acute SDH → hyperdense crescent on CT. Chronic SDH → isodense or hypodense crescent (hard to see). Midline shift indicates brain compression.

Intracerebral Hematoma (ICH)

Bleeding within the brain parenchyma itself. Associated with severe contusions and high-energy mechanisms.

FeatureDescription
MechanismHigh-energy trauma; penetrating injury; severe acceleration
LocationFrontal/temporal most common in trauma; basal ganglia/thalamus in spontaneous HTN bleed
PresentationFocal deficits; altered consciousness; may have signs of ↑ICP
ManagementSupportive; neurosurgical evaluation; some require evacuation
🔬
Differentiation Summary: Epidural = arterial, biconvex, temporal, lucid interval, younger. Subdural = venous, crescent, diffuse, elderly, anticoagulated. Intracerebral = inside brain, worst prognosis, most severe mechanism.
🌊 Diffuse Brain Injuries

Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI)

Widespread shearing of axons throughout white matter from rotational/angular acceleration. The most severe diffuse injury.

GradeDescription
Mild DAIPost-traumatic coma 6–24 hrs; usually good recovery
Moderate DAIComa >24 hrs; brainstem involvement; disability likely
Severe DAIProlonged coma; decerebrate posturing; often fatal or vegetative
CT scan is often NORMAL in DAI — MRI (especially diffusion tensor imaging) is the diagnostic tool. If patient is unconscious after high-energy mechanism with a normal CT, suspect DAI.

Herniation Syndromes

Uncal
Temporal lobe herniates over tentorium → CN III compression → ipsilateral blown pupil, contralateral hemiparesis. Most common.
Transtentorial
Generalized downward pressure → bilateral pupil changes, Cushing's triad, Cheyne-Stokes breathing.
Tonsillar
Cerebellar tonsils through foramen magnum → brainstem compression → respiratory arrest, death. "Coning."
Cushing's Triad (Late ICP Sign):
↑ Blood pressure (widened pulse pressure) + ↓ Heart rate (bradycardia) + Irregular respirations = Imminent herniation. This is a very late finding — patient is about to die.

Face Trauma

Controlling the airway when the anatomy that usually protects it is destroyed.

🎯
The Paramedic's Priority: In facial trauma, the airway is the primary concern — always. Hemorrhage control is second. Aesthetics and fracture management are hospital concerns. Your job at the scene is: keep them breathing and stop the bleeding.
🩹 Soft Tissue Facial Injuries
Injury TypeManagement Considerations
LacerationsDirect pressure; irrigate if possible; do NOT remove embedded objects; approximate edges loosely with bulky dressing if transport is prolonged
AvulsionsPreserve avulsed tissue in saline-moistened gauze; potential for reimplantation; control hemorrhage
Impaled objectsStabilize in place EXCEPT objects impaling cheek with oral airway access concern
Ear lacerationsEar is poor for direct pressure — pad and wrap circumferentially
Nasal injuriesEpistaxis control: lean forward, pinch soft nose 10–15 min; do NOT pack if basilar fracture suspected
👁️
Eye Injuries: Blunt orbital trauma → hyphaema (blood in anterior chamber), vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment. Penetrating injuries: stabilize impaled object, apply eye shield (NOT pressure patch). Both eyes should be covered to prevent sympathetic movement.
Avulsed tooth: If tooth is avulsed and patient is conscious/cooperative: rinse, replace in socket, bite down on gauze. If not: store in milk, saliva (buccal pouch), or saline. Transport tooth with patient. Time to reimplantation is critical.

Facial Fractures

💀 Mandibular Fractures

Second most common facial fracture. The mandible is a ring structure — it typically fractures in two places simultaneously.

FindingSignificance
Dental malocclusionTeeth don't fit together properly — patient "can't bite right"
Step deformityPalpable along lower jaw
TrismusInability to open mouth — muscle spasm or condylar fracture; complicates intubation
Tongue displacementBilateral mandible fracture → posterior tongue displacement → airway obstruction
✈️
Airway Alert — Bilateral Mandible Fracture: The genioglossus muscle attaches to the mandibular symphysis. With bilateral fractures, the mandible can float posteriorly, carrying the tongue with it. The patient may ONLY be able to breathe while sitting upright (orthopneic). DO NOT lay supine without airway control.
🦴 Midface Fractures — LeFort Classification
LF I
Horizontal / Guérin
Fracture separates maxillary teeth from upper face. Floating palate. Punch to mouth/chin. Dental malocclusion, palate mobility on palpation.
LF II
Pyramidal
Fracture through maxillary sinuses, orbit floor, nasal bones. Nose and palate move as unit. Raccoon eyes, CSF rhinorrhea possible. Massive facial swelling.
LF III
Craniofacial Dissociation
Complete separation of facial skeleton from cranial base. High-energy mechanism. Massive hemorrhage. Airway devastated. Surgical airway likely required. Life-threatening.
🚨
LeFort III Airway Strategy: The entire face can be manually repositioned anteriorly to briefly open the airway (by grasping the maxillary teeth and pulling forward). This buys 30 seconds. Use this time to perform RSI or surgical cricothyrotomy. These patients frequently require surgical airways.

Other Facial Fractures

Zygomatic (Cheek) Fracture: "Tripod fracture" — zygomatic arch, orbital floor, and zygomaticofrontal suture. Facial asymmetry, enophthalmos (sunken eye), periorbital edema. Usually no immediate airway threat unless combined with other injuries.
Orbital Blowout Fracture: Direct blow to globe increases intraorbital pressure → blows out the thin orbital floor or medial wall. Eye may sink inward (enophthalmos). CN IV/VI traction → diplopia on upgaze. NOT an emergent airway issue — but document findings.

Neck Trauma

Carotid arteries, jugular veins, larynx, and spinal cord — all within 5 centimeters of each other.

🗺️ Anatomical Zones — Clinical Decision Making
I
Clavicles → Cricoid cartilage
Key structures: Great vessels (subclavian, carotid origin), trachea, esophagus, brachial plexus, thoracic duct, cervicothoracic junction
Injuries often require thoracic surgery; mortality from vascular injury is high; proximal control may require sternotomy
II
Cricoid → Angle of Mandible
Key structures: Carotid arteries (bifurcation at C3-4), internal jugular, trachea, esophagus, larynx, thyroid, vagus nerve, hypoglossal nerve
Most commonly injured; best surgical access; mandatory surgical exploration traditionally (now CT angiography first)
III
Angle of Mandible → Skull Base
Key structures: Distal ICA, vertebral arteries, pharynx, CN IX-XII, parotid gland, jugular vein proximally
Difficult surgical access; often managed with endovascular techniques (coil embolization)
🗡️ Penetrating Neck Trauma
⚠️
High-Index Suspicion: Any penetrating trauma that violates the platysma muscle is a serious injury until proven otherwise. The platysma is the thin muscle immediately under the skin — penetration beyond it puts all critical structures at risk.

Vascular Injury Signs

Prehospital Hemorrhage Control

Carotid Laceration: Direct manual pressure. Do NOT use tourniquet on neck. Consider wound packing with hemostatic gauze (XStat, QuikClot) if manual pressure is insufficient. Transport immediately — this is not a scene where "you fix it" alone.
Jugular Vein Injury: Risk of air embolism — place patient in left lateral decubitus Trendelenburg position. Occlude wound on exhalation. Keep wound covered/sealed.
Air Embolism Prevention: Venous injuries in the neck: avoid patient sitting upright (increases venous negativity). Cover with non-permeable dressing. Do NOT leave wound exposed to air.
💥 Blunt Neck Trauma

Laryngeal Trauma

The larynx is protected by the mandible in flexion and the sternum in extension — blunt force to the midline neck (dashboard, steering wheel, clothesline injury) can fracture thyroid/cricoid cartilage.

Sign/SymptomSignificance
Hoarseness / dysphoniaVocal cord or cricoarytenoid injury
Subcutaneous emphysemaAirway disruption; air tracking into tissues — pathognomonic
StridorPartial airway obstruction — hematoma, edema, displaced cartilage
DysphagiaEsophageal injury or hematoma
Loss of laryngeal landmarksSevere cartilaginous disruption
🚫
Intubation Precaution: Laryngeal fracture with cricoid involvement is a relative contraindication to oral intubation — passing tube may further displace fractured cartilage, converting partial obstruction to complete obstruction. Surgical cricothyrotomy may be the primary airway choice. If you must intubate: video laryngoscope, smallest tube that works, no BURP/Sellick.
"Clothesline Injury": Classic mechanism — pedestrian/cyclist hits wire or cable at neck level. Can create complete laryngeal disruption, carotid injury, and cervical spine fracture simultaneously. Maintain extreme suspicion.

Blunt Cerebrovascular Injury (BCVI)

Carotid/Vertebral Dissection: High-energy blunt trauma can shear the intima of cervical arteries → intimal flap → thrombosis → stroke. Presentation may be delayed hours to days. Screen for: high-energy mechanism, seatbelt sign across neck, cervical spine fracture, LeFort II/III, basilar skull fracture.
Denver Criteria: Arterial dissection should be suspected in patients with cervical spine fracture extending into foramen transversarium, Horner syndrome after blunt trauma, or focal neurological deficits inconsistent with CT findings. Anticoagulation/antiplatelet therapy reduces stroke risk.
🦴 Cervical Spine Injury

Mechanisms of Injury

MechanismInjury Pattern
Axial loading (diving)Burst fracture, C4-5 most common

Neurogenic Shock: SCI above T6 → disruption of sympathetic chain → loss of vasomotor tone → hypotension + relative bradycardia (no compensatory tachycardia). Treat with judicious fluid, consider vasopressors (norepinephrine preferred). Do NOT confuse with hemorrhagic shock.
NEXUS Criteria (Clearance Rule):
No C-spine imaging needed if ALL: No midline tenderness · No altered mental status · No focal neurological deficit · No intoxication · No distracting injury. If ANY criterion positive → immobilize and image.

🏥 Prehospital TBI Management — Priority Order
1. Scene
BSI, scene safety, MOI/NOI. Identify number of patients. Cervical spine precautions for blunt trauma with significant MOI. Time is brain — minimize scene time.
2. Airway
GCS ≤8 → definitive airway (RSI preferred). Maintain SpO₂ ≥94%. Position of comfort if conscious. Suction blood/vomit. Hypoxia is the most preventable cause of secondary TBI.
3. Breathing
Target ventilations at NORMAL rate: 10–12/min adult. ETCO₂ target: 35–40 mmHg. Avoid hyperventilation (causes vasoconstriction → ischemia). Exception: herniation signs → brief permissive hyperventilation to ETCO₂ 30–35 mmHg as temporizing measure ONLY.
4. Circulation
Target SBP ≥90 mmHg (ideally 110–120 in TBI). 2 large-bore IVs. Isotonic crystalloid (NS or LR). Avoid hypotension absolutely. A single episode of SBP <90 mmHg doubles mortality.
5. Disability
GCS with subscores. Pupils (PERRL, size, reactivity). Blood glucose — correct hypoglycemia aggressively (target 80–180 mg/dL). Temperature control — prevent/treat hyperthermia (>38°C worsens outcomes).
6. Transport
Expedite transport to appropriate trauma center. Notify receiving. Serial assessments en route. Document trends. In TBI, hospital choice matters — Level I trauma center preferred.

Dose: 0.3 mg/kg IV
MOA: GABA modulation
Advantage: Minimal hemodynamic effect; ↓ ICP; ↓ CMRO₂
Succinylcholine
Class: Depolarizing neuromuscular blocker
Dose: 1.5 mg/kg IV (RSI)
Onset: 30–60 seconds
Caution: Transient ↑ICP (brief); avoid if crush injury >72h, burns, SCI >24h (hyperkalemia)
Rocuronium
Class: Non-depolarizing NMB
Dose: 1.2 mg/kg IV (RSI dose)
Reversal: Sugammadex 16 mg/kg
Advantage: Preferred when succinylcholine contraindicated
Tranexamic Acid (TXA)
Class: Antifibrinolytic
Indication: TBI within 3 hrs of injury
Dose: 1 g IV over 10 min, then 1 g over 8 hrs
MOA: Inhibits fibrinolysis; reduces hematoma expansion
Evidence: CRASH-3 trial — mortality benefit in mild-moderate TBI

Case 1: The Lucid Interval
Scene: Highway MVC · Unit 7 · Urban ALS
You respond to a single-vehicle crash. A 28-year-old male struck his head on the side window during a rollover. Bystanders report 30 seconds of LOC. When you arrive, the patient is A&Ox4, GCS 15, complaining of a headache. He has a 4 cm scalp laceration over his left temporal region. Vital signs: BP 122/74, HR 72, RR 16, SpO₂ 99%. He is asking to sign a refusal.
Which injury are you MOST concerned about, and what sign must you monitor for during transport?
A Subdural hematoma — monitor for bilateral pupil changes
B Epidural hematoma — monitor for deteriorating GCS and ipsilateral pupil dilation (lucid interval)
C Diffuse axonal injury — monitor for CT abnormalities on arrival
D Concussion only — the GCS 15 and short LOC rule out serious injury
✅ Correct! This is the classic epidural hematoma presentation. Temporal blow + brief LOC + "normal" now = lucid interval. The middle meningeal artery in the temporal epidural space is likely torn. Arterial bleeding will expand the hematoma rapidly. This patient needs immediate transport to CT — do NOT let him sign a refusal. Serial GCS every 2 minutes. Any deterioration = immediate notification of trauma team, lights and siren, consider ALS intercept if needed.

Case 2: The Penetrating Neck
Scene: Industrial Site · Dispatch: Laceration · ALS Unit
A 44-year-old construction worker was struck in the neck by a piece of rebar. The rebar is no longer in place — it has been removed by coworkers. You find an actively bleeding Zone II neck wound. An expanding hematoma is visible. The patient is conscious but in obvious distress with a hoarse voice. SpO₂ 94%, BP 88/60, HR 124. His airway is patent but with audible stridor.
What is your IMMEDIATE priority and airway strategy for this patient?
A Apply direct pressure, establish 2 IVs, fluid resuscitate to BP >100, then reassess airway
B Begin BVM ventilation and call for ALS intercept before moving the patient
C Secure the airway NOW (RSI with video laryngoscope) while simultaneously applying direct pressure — the expanding hematoma will occlude the airway within minutes
D Surgical cricothyrotomy is the only safe option given the neck injury
✅ Correct! An expanding Zone II hematoma with stridor is an immediate surgical airway warning. The hematoma will compress the trachea within minutes. Act now while the airway is still accessible. RSI with video laryngoscope (GlideScope/C-MAC) is preferred — direct view is critical. Have your surgical airway kit open and ready. The hoarseness suggests laryngeal involvement — use a smaller-than-usual ET tube (7.0 vs 8.0). Direct pressure to the wound simultaneously. Two large-bore IVs en route. Permissive hypotension acceptable here to avoid dislodging clot — target SBP ~80–90 until hemorrhage controlled.

Case 3: The Confused Elder
Scene: Assisted Living Facility · Dispatch: Altered Mental Status
An 81-year-old female on warfarin and clopidogrel has had "increased confusion" over the past 3 weeks per staff. History of "minor fall" about 4 weeks ago. No report of LOC. She is A&Ox1 (person only), GCS 12 (E3V3M6). Vitals: BP 156/94, HR 86, RR 16. No focal deficits. No scalp laceration, no obvious trauma. Family denies any recent fall.
What is your leading diagnosis, and why is her medication history critical?
A Acute ischemic stroke — warfarin provides no protection against clot formation in the brain
B Epidural hematoma — elderly patients develop rapid arterial bleeds after minor trauma
C Chronic subdural hematoma — minor/forgotten fall + anticoagulation + brain atrophy + slow progressive decline over weeks
D Hypertensive encephalopathy — BP 156/94 is the causative factor
✅ Correct! This is chronic subdural hematoma until proven otherwise. The triad: elderly + anticoagulation + gradual cognitive decline over weeks after minor/forgotten head trauma. Brain atrophy stretches bridging veins. Warfarin + clopidogrel severely impair clotting — even tiny venous bleeds expand slowly. CT is diagnostic. Management includes warfarin reversal (Vitamin K + PCC or FFP), and neurosurgical burr hole drainage. Document all medications. Communicate anticoagulation status immediately to receiving ED.

Case 4: The Herniation Decision
Scene: Sports Field · High School Football · BLS First / ALS Responding
A 17-year-old male was involved in a helmet-to-helmet collision. He was unresponsive for 2 minutes. BLS crew reports GCS was 13 on their arrival. You arrive 4 minutes later. GCS is now 7 (E1V2M4). Right pupil is 7mm and non-reactive. Left pupil is 3mm and reactive. BP 168/92, HR 54, RR 8 and irregular. BVM being held.
Identify this triad and describe your COMPLETE management plan for this patient.
A This is neurogenic shock — initiate norepinephrine infusion, avoid IVF overload, allow permissive hypotension
B This is sympathetic storming — administer propranolol IV and hyperventilate aggressively to ETCO₂ 25 mmHg
C This is Cushing's Triad with uncal herniation — RSI, controlled ventilation ETCO₂ 30–35 mmHg (temporizing), mannitol or 3% NaCl, immediate trauma center transport
D Await on scene for physician guidance — this patient should not be moved due to suspected cervical spine injury
✅ Correct! Cushing's Triad = ↑BP (168/92, widened pulse pressure) + ↓HR (54 bradycardia) + Irregular respirations. This is a LATE sign of massively elevated ICP with imminent brainstem herniation. Unilateral blown pupil (right 7mm) = right uncal herniation from likely right-sided mass lesion (epidural or acute SDH).

Your plan: (1) RSI — etomidate 0.3 mg/kg + succinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg; (2) Secure ET, confirm placement, ETCO₂ 30-35 temporarily; (3) Mannitol 1g/kg IV or 3% NaCl 250 mL if available; (4) Two IVs — NS/LR to maintain SBP ≥110; (5) HOB 30°; (6) Immediate transport — Level I Trauma Center, neurosurgery alert; (7) Serial GCS and pupils every 2 minutes.
