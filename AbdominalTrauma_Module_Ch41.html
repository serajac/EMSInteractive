● Andragogy Principle 1 — Need to Know

Why does this matter to YOU, right now? Abdominal trauma kills quietly. The peritoneal cavity will accommodate 40% of circulating blood volume before classical shock signs appear. By the time the abdomen is distended, your patient is already in decompensated hemorrhagic shock. Intraperitoneal vs. Retroperitoneal — Why It Matters

This distinction changes your suspicion list and the type of bleeding you expect:

SpaceMajor organsBleeding behavior
Intraperitoneal Liver, spleen, stomach, most of small/large bowel, transverse colon Free blood produces peritoneal irritation (rigidity, rebound). Detected by FAST. Surgical bleeding.
Retroperitoneal Kidneys, ureters, pancreas, duodenum, adrenal glands, abdominal aorta & IVC Often occult — can hide several liters before a sign appears. Frequently linked to pelvic/lumbar fractures.
Pelvic Bladder, urethra, rectum, reproductive organs Pelvic-ring fractures cause severe retroperitoneal bleeding (mortality 6.4%–19%).

Mechanisms of Injury

Blunt Trauma

Caused by compression or shearing forces. Severity depends on force, duration, and the type of structure injured (fluid-filled, gas-filled, solid, hollow).

  • Compression: organs crushed between two solid objects (e.g., liver between steering wheel and spine).
  • Shearing: rupture or tear at points of attachment when tissues are stretched (classic deceleration injury).
  • Common sources: MVCs, falls, sports impacts, assaults.

Penetrating Trauma

Stab wounds, gunshot wounds, impalement. Higher mortality rate than blunt trauma. The major complication is hemorrhage from a major vessel or solid organ.

  • Internal bleeding correlates with type and number of vessels injured + organ vascularity.
  • May perforate bowel → bacterial contamination → peritonitis.
  • Track of injury matters more than the entry wound — assume the worst trajectory.
Clinical pearl — speed and severity:

Blunt trauma frequently involves multi-system injury. Penetrating trauma carries higher single-event mortality. In both, your kinematic awareness during the call (vehicle damage, intrusion, ejection, restraint use, weapon caliber/length) is more predictive of internal injury than your physical exam.

The content below is sequenced the way you'll encounter it: solid organ → hollow organ → retroperitoneal → pelvic → vascular, followed by the practical exam findings that distinguish them.

Solid Organ Injury — Liver & Spleen

Solid-organ injury produces rapid, significant blood loss. The liver and spleen are the two most-often injured solid organs and the most common sources of life-threatening hemorrhage in abdominal trauma.

Liver — RUQ

  • Suspect with steering-wheel injury, lap-belt injury, or any history of epigastric trauma.
  • Blood + bile escape into peritoneal cavity → shock + peritoneal irritation (pain, tenderness, rigidity).
  • Mortality from uncontrolled bleeding: 54% — the leading cause of liver-injury death.
Field cue: RUQ tenderness + right lower rib fractures + hypotension after frontal MVC = liver injury until proven otherwise.

Spleen — LUQ

  • Common mechanisms: MVC, falls, sports impact to the upper-left abdomen.
  • Many patients initially have only minor symptoms — that is the trap.
  • Frequently associated with other intra-abdominal injuries.
Kehr Sign — referred pain:

Left shoulder pain after blunt trauma to the LUQ is splenic injury / hemoperitoneum until proven otherwise. The blood is irritating the diaphragm; the phrenic nerve refers it to the shoulder.

Right shoulder pain can suggest liver or gallbladder irritation.

Tie back to the scenario:

Remember the 24-year-old driver with left-shoulder pain and a bent steering wheel? You are now looking at it through the right lens. He is not just a "shoulder pain after MVC." He is potentially a Kehr-sign positive splenic injury masquerading as benign musculoskeletal pain — exactly the pattern that gets overlooked at the scene and dies on the recliner two hours later.

Hollow Organ Injury — Stomach, Small Bowel, Colon

Hollow-organ rupture spills luminal contents (acid, enzymes, bacteria, stool) into the peritoneum. The result: peritonitis, sepsis, wound infection, or abscess. Onset can be delayed hours to days, which is why undiagnosed bowel injury is so dangerous.

OrganMechanismField findings
Stomach Rarely injured by blunt (protected location). Penetrating trauma → transection or laceration. Rapid peritonitis from acidic gastric leak; severe epigastric pain, rigidity.
Small intestine More common in penetrating trauma. Can shear from restraints in deceleration. Considerable force required → other injuries usually present. Delayed peritonitis.
Colon Penetrating > blunt. Compression in high-speed MVC. Heavy bacterial contamination → high infection rate. Often associated injuries.
Peritonitis (Box 41-3): Inflammation of the peritoneum, usually acute and very painful. Onset may be delayed by hours or days. Field signs: rigid, board-like abdomen; the patient lies still with knees drawn up; movement worsens pain.

Retroperitoneal Organ Injury

Retroperitoneal injury is the occult bleed. Hemorrhage may be massive, often results from pelvic or lumbar fractures, and may produce no peritoneal signs because the blood is behind the peritoneum.

Kidney

  • Range from minor (contusion, small laceration) to major (deep medullary laceration, fracture, laceration into the collecting system).
  • Result in hemorrhage, urine extravasation, or both.
  • Contusions are self-limiting — bed rest, fluids. Major lacerations may need surgery.
  • Look for: flank ecchymosis, hematuria, lower rib fractures, mid-back tenderness.

Pancreas, Duodenum, Ureters, Esophagus

  • Pancreas — more often injured by penetrating trauma than blunt.
  • Duodenum — crushed or lacerated by great force; usually associated with concurrent pancreatic injury, confirmed at surgery.
  • Ureters — flexible, rarely injured by blunt trauma.
  • Esophagus / adrenals — possible with severe blunt or penetrating trauma to anterior, posterior abdomen, or thoracic spine.
Why retroperitoneal bleeding kills quietly:

A patient can lose two or more liters into the retroperitoneum with minimal external sign. By the time you see flank discoloration (Grey-Turner sign) or pain that radiates to the back, the patient has already lost significant volume. Mechanism + unexplained tachycardia + back/flank pain is a retroperitoneal bleed until ruled out.

Pelvic Organ Injury

The pelvis supports and protects multiple organ systems, so when it fractures, associated injuries are almost guaranteed. Pelvic fractures are often associated with severe retroperitoneal hemorrhage. Mortality ranges from 6.4% to 19% depending on severity.

Urinary Bladder

  • Rupture more likely if the bladder was distended at the time of injury.
  • Peritoneal integrity may be disrupted → urine spilled into peritoneal cavity.
  • Gross hematuria may be present; patient may complain of being unable to void.

Urethra

  • Patient complains of abdominal pain and inability to urinate.
  • Blood at the meatus is highly suggestive of urethral injury.
  • Indwelling urinary catheter is contraindicated — you can convert a partial tear into a complete one. Definitive diagnosis is by retrograde urethrogram in the ED.

Vascular Structure Injury

Injury to abdominal arterial and venous vessels can be life-threatening because of massive hemorrhage. Vascular injury is usually marked by hypovolemia and, in some cases, a palpable abdominal mass.

The most-often injured vessels with the highest mortality:

  • Aorta — exsanguinating hemorrhage; near-100% pre-hospital mortality with full transection.
  • Inferior vena cava — large-volume venous bleeding.
  • Renal arteries & veins — retroperitoneal hemorrhage.
  • Mesenteric arteries & veins — bowel ischemia + intraperitoneal bleeding.
  • Iliac arteries & veins — frequently injured with pelvic fractures.

Prompt 1 — Mechanism Mapping

An unrestrained driver in a head-on collision strikes the steering wheel. Predict three injuries by mechanism (one solid-organ, one hollow-organ, one retroperitoneal). Justify each.

Model answer:

(1) Liver laceration (solid) — RUQ compression between steering column and spine; expect RUQ tenderness, rigidity, hypotension; mortality from uncontrolled bleeding ~54%. (2) Small-bowel injury (hollow) — deceleration shears bowel at points of attachment; expect delayed peritonitis if undiagnosed. (3) Pancreatic / duodenal injury (retroperitoneal) — direct epigastric blow; often occult, confirmed only at surgery; suspect with persistent epigastric pain + tachycardia.

Prompt 2 — The Apparently Stable Patient

Why is "I'm fine" the most dangerous sentence in abdominal trauma? Use what you know about peritoneal volume reserve and compensated shock to explain.

Model answer:

The peritoneal cavity will accommodate large volumes of blood before producing the visible distention or rigidity that drives lay-person concern. Healthy young patients compensate with tachycardia and peripheral vasoconstriction; hypotension and AMS may not appear until >40% of circulating volume is lost. By that point, the patient has crossed from compensated to decompensated shock and the window for fluid/blood resuscitation is closing. Reliance on the patient's self-report or a "normal" first set of vitals is the most common error in field abdominal trauma.

Branching Decision Exercise · Operation Suffolk Crossing

You are the lead paramedic. Each decision below changes what happens next. The "best" path is not always the most obvious — read carefully.

Step 1 — Scene Arrival

Single-vehicle MVC, 24-year-old unrestrained driver, ambulatory, complaining of left-shoulder pain only. Steering wheel is bent. Vitals: BP 118/76, HR 102, RR 18, SpO₂ 98%, GCS 15. He says, "I don't need to go." What is your priority action?

Step 2 — En Route Reassessment (10 minutes later)

You convinced him to come. En route: BP 96/62, HR 128, RR 22, SpO₂ 96%, skin pale and diaphoretic, mild LUQ tenderness, persistent left-shoulder pain. He is now anxious and confused. What is the best next step?

Step 3 — Destination Decision

You are 8 minutes from a community ED and 19 minutes from a Level I trauma center. The patient is now hypotensive with a positive Kehr sign and rigid abdomen.

Case Resolution

The patient arrived at the Level I trauma center with SBP 84. FAST exam was positive. He was taken directly to the OR and underwent splenectomy for a Grade IV splenic laceration with hemoperitoneum of 2.1 L. He survived. The first paramedic who almost let him refuse transport sent him a thank-you card.

Why you got him there alive: you trusted mechanism over the patient's self-report, recognized Kehr sign, used permissive hypotension instead of aggressive fluid resuscitation, and chose surgical capability over distance.

Multi-System Trauma — Lap-Belt-Only Restraint

Synthesis Tasks

Integrating all three modules, work through the following — type each answer briefly, then expand the model rationale to compare.

Task 1 · Differential — At least four likely injuries

(1) Hollow-organ injury (small bowel, mesentery) — classic "seat-belt syndrome" from lap-only restraint compressing bowel against the spine. Expect delayed peritonitis. (2) Lumbar Chance fracture — flexion-distraction injury → mid-back pain. (3) Splenic injury — lateral impact to LUQ; watch for Kehr sign. (4) Pelvic / retroperitoneal hemorrhage — explains hypotension out of proportion to visible injury. Consider also (5) bladder rupture if she had recently voided.

Task 2 · Prehospital Plan — Sequence and justify each action

Airway/C-spine: spinal precautions (full-body); monitor airway. Breathing: O₂ to keep SpO₂ ≥ 94%. Circulation: two large-bore IVs; titrate fluid to SBP 80–90 / MAP 60–65 (blunt trauma + no documented TBI = permissive hypotension target per chapter). Pelvic binder if pelvic instability suspected. Reassess abdomen, vitals, mental status every 5 minutes. Destination: Level I trauma center — surgical capability is the limiting factor. Notification: early radio report to activate trauma team before arrival. Document mechanism in detail — lap-only restraint is critical history.

Task 3 · Fluid Resuscitation — Defend your target

Per the chapter: for blunt abdominal trauma with hypotension, target SBP 80–90 (MAP 60–65). The goal is organ perfusion, not pre-injury pressure. Aggressive fluid resuscitation in active hemorrhage dilutes clotting factors, dislodges soft clots, and worsens bleeding. Permissive hypotension is the strategy. Note: in penetrating trauma, titration is to mental status, not a number.

One-Page Recap · The Things You Cannot Forget

  • Penetrating trauma → higher mortality. Blunt trauma → more multi-system injury.
  • Liver injury–related death from uncontrolled bleeding: 54%. RUQ pain + frontal MVC = liver until proven otherwise.
  • Kehr sign: left shoulder pain after blunt LUQ trauma → splenic injury / hemoperitoneum.
  • Hollow-organ injury → peritonitis, delayed by hours to days. The patient who "looked OK" can still die at home.
  • Retroperitoneal bleeding is occult — pelvic and lumbar fractures are the most common drivers; mortality 6.4%–19% for pelvic fractures.
  • Blood at the urethral meatus → no Foley. Definitive Dx via retrograde urethrogram.
  • Most significant sign of abdominal trauma: unexplained shock. Distention is a late finding.
  • Clinical signs of shock may not appear until >40% of circulating volume is lost — do not be reassured by an early "normal" set of vitals.
  • Fluid strategy: blunt + hypotension → SBP 80–90 / MAP 60–65. Penetrating → titrate to mental status. SpO₂ ≥ 94%.
  • Auscultation of bowel sounds in the field is informational only — never delay transport for it.
