I
Anatomy & Physiology Review
206 bones · 3 joint types · 3 muscle types

The Musculoskeletal System

The musculoskeletal system consists of bones, muscles, tendons, ligaments, and articulating surfaces. Bones provide structural support and protection; muscles generate movement and maintain posture; tendons anchor muscle to bone; ligaments bind bone to bone; and cartilage cushions articulating surfaces.

Skeletal System

Axial Skeleton

  • Skull & hyoid bone
  • Vertebral column (cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral, coccyx)
  • Thoracic cage (sternum + 12 pairs of ribs)
  • Forms the central axis of the body

Appendicular Skeleton

  • Upper extremities & shoulder girdle
  • Lower extremities & pelvic girdle
  • 126 bones total
  • Most joints are synovial (freely moveable)

Joint Classification

🔩 Fibrous

Bones joined by dense fibrous tissue with little or no movement. Example: skull sutures, distal tibiofibular joint.

💠 Cartilaginous

Bones united by hyaline cartilage/fibrocartilage. Slightly moveable. Example: pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs.

💧 Synovial

Fluid-filled joint capsule allows for free movement. Most joints of the appendicular skeleton. Example: knee, hip, shoulder.

Muscular System

Skeletal Muscle

Voluntary, striated. Attached to bone via tendons. Generates locomotion and postural control through contraction/relaxation cycles of muscle fibers.

Cardiac Muscle

Involuntary, striated. Found only in the heart. Self-excitatory with intrinsic pacemaker activity. Highly resistant to fatigue.

Smooth Muscle

Involuntary, non-striated. Found in viscera, blood vessels, and airways. Controls internal organ function under autonomic regulation.

🔦
Muscle Tone Analogy: Think of muscle tone like the idle speed of a car engine — it's not full throttle, but the engine is running, keeping the body upright and ready to move. Without proper tone, the "engine stalls" and posture collapses. Conditions like myopathies or stroke reduce this idle, while spasticity revs it too high.

General Assessment Strategies
Extremity · Spine · Vascular · Motor Examination

Assessment Framework

A systematic musculoskeletal assessment includes evaluation of the extremities, spine, vascular status, and motor/neurological function. The goal is to identify abnormal findings that guide prehospital management decisions.

Abnormal Findings to Identify

Abnormal movement

Extremity Exam

  • Hands/wrists: contour, alignment, tenderness
  • Elbows: palpate flexed and extended
  • Shoulders: structural integrity
  • Pelvis/hips: stability, no deformity
  • Knees: patella midline, non-tender
  • Ankles/feet: contour, alignment, no nodules

Spine Exam

  • Check cervical, thoracic, lumbar curves
  • Neck: flex/extend/lateral without pain
  • Inspect thoracic/lumbar for swelling, discoloration
  • Note abnormal curvature (kyphosis, scoliosis, lordosis)

Vascular Exam

  • Skin color and texture (pallor, cyanosis)
  • Pulse quality distal to injury
  • Temperature (cold = poor perfusion)
  • Pitting edema
  • Lymph node assessment

Motor Exam

  • Gait observation (antalgic gait?)
  • Agility, flexion, extension, abduction
  • Point-to-point coordination
  • Sensation: pain, vibration, touch
  • Involuntary movements (tremor, spasm)
III
General Management Guidelines
Prehospital Principles · Pain Management · Transport

Prehospital Management Framework

Prehospital care for musculoskeletal disorders is primarily supportive. The foundation includes scene safety, primary assessment (ABCs), secondary assessment, pain management (pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic), and careful transport for physician evaluation.

Non-Pharmacologic Measures

  • Immobilization — splint affected extremity in position of comfort
  • Ice/Cryotherapy — reduce swelling and inflammation (15–20 min on/off)
  • Elevation — reduce edema by promoting venous drainage
  • Rest — prevent further injury
  • Gentle transport — minimize jolting and patient movement

Pharmacologic Measures

  • Analgesics — morphine, fentanyl, ketorolac (per protocol)
  • NSAIDs — ibuprofen, ketorolac (anti-inflammatory)
  • Muscle relaxants — cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol
  • Corticosteroids — methylprednisolone (severe inflammation)
  • Antibiotics — when infectious etiology is present (hospital-initiated)
⚠️

Cultural Sensitivity: Not all cultures embrace Western pharmacologic pain management. Be aware of non-pharmaceutical pain management preferences and remain non-judgmental. Pediatric patients are frequently undertreated for pain — use validated pediatric pain scales and treat accordingly.

IV
Osteomyelitis
Acute and chronic bone infection

Pathophysiology

Osteomyelitis is an acute or chronic infection of bone caused most commonly by Staphylococcus aureus (70–80% of cases). The infection typically reaches bone via hematogenous spread (bloodborne), direct inoculation (puncture wound, open fracture), or contiguous spread from adjacent infected tissue.

Once bacteria seed the bone, they colonize the highly vascular metaphysis of long bones in children. The resulting inflammatory response causes increased intraosseous pressure, leading to vascular compromise and ischemic necrosis. Dead bone (sequestrum) becomes a nidus for persistent infection that antibiotics struggle to penetrate — the mechanism behind chronic osteomyelitis.

🏰
Fortress Under Siege Analogy: Imagine bone as a fortress with its own blood supply (the moat). Bacteria that breach the walls (via a puncture or bloodstream) set up camp inside. The body's immune response floods the area, but increased pressure cuts off the moat — essentially starving the fortress's own defenders. Dead bone becomes a "castle ruin" — structurally compromised and sheltering bacteria that antibiotics cannot easily reach through the blocked supply lines.

V
Bone Tumors
Benign & Malignant · Primary & Secondary

Pathophysiology

Bone tumors arise from abnormal proliferation of bone-forming cells. Benign tumors are locally expansile without metastatic potential; malignant tumors invade locally and disseminate via the hematogenous or lymphatic route. Primary bone cancers originate in bone; secondary (metastatic) tumors arise elsewhere (breast, prostate, lung, kidney, thyroid) and seed to bone — the most common form of bone malignancy overall.

Tumor growth disrupts normal bone architecture, weakening cortical and trabecular structure. This leads to pathologic fractures — fractures occurring with minimal or no trauma through bone that has been structurally compromised by tumor invasion.

🏗️
Building Demolition Analogy: Healthy bone is like reinforced concrete. A bone tumor is like a demolition crew quietly working from the inside — replacing structural beams with defective material. The building looks normal on the outside until one day a minor stress (stepping off a curb) causes catastrophic collapse — a pathologic fracture. The taller the building (longer bone like femur), the more force transmitted and the greater the fracture risk.

Primary Bone Tumors

🔴 Osteosarcoma

Most common primary bone cancer. Peak incidence in teens & young adults. Occurs near knee (distal femur, proximal tibia) or proximal humerus. Highly aggressive — early metastasis to lungs.

🟡 Ewing Sarcoma

Ages 5–20 years. Affects diaphysis of long bones (femur, tibia, fibula), also pelvis, ribs, spine. Second most common primary bone tumor in children. "Onion skin" appearance on x-ray.

🔵 Chondrosarcoma

Arises from cartilaginous tissue. Primarily adults > 20 years. Occurs in hip, pelvis, and shoulder. Slower-growing than osteosarcoma; relatively resistant to chemo/radiation — surgery is primary treatment.

Any patient with known or suspected cancer who sustains a fracture with trivial trauma should be immobilized gently, pain managed aggressively, and transported to a trauma-capable facility. Do not attribute skeletal pain in young patients to "growing pains" without thorough evaluation.

VI
Low Back Pain, Disc Disorders & Cauda Equina
Most common musculoskeletal complaint · Potential surgical emergency

Intervertebral Disc Pathophysiology

Each intervertebral disc has a nucleus pulposus (gelatinous center) surrounded by the annulus fibrosus (fibrous outer ring). Discs act as hydraulic shock absorbers distributing axial load. With repetitive mechanical stress or aging, the annulus fibrosus dehydrates and weakens. Progressive annular tears allow the nucleus pulposus to bulge outward (disc herniation), impinging adjacent nerve roots.

90% of disc herniations occur at L4–L5 or L5–S1, producing radicular symptoms along the sciatic nerve distribution (sciatica). The lumbar spine bears the greatest compressive load, making it the most vulnerable segment.

🍩
Jelly Donut Analogy: An intervertebral disc is like a jelly-filled donut — the jelly is the nucleus pulposus, and the pastry ring is the annulus fibrosus. When you squeeze the donut unevenly (poor lifting mechanics, repetitive bending), the jelly pushes toward the weakest spot in the ring. A small push = disc bulge. A bigger push that breaks through = disc herniation. The jelly then squirts onto the nerve root next door, causing the burning, shooting pain of sciatica. The fix? Reduce the pressure and let the donut "dry out" through rest and PT — or surgically remove the errant jelly (discectomy).

Herniated Disc — Signs & Symptoms

  • Low back ache radiating to buttock and leg
  • "Shooting" / electric pain down leg (sciatica)
  • Numbness or weakness in lower extremities
  • Deep muscle pain and spasms
  • Pain worsened by Valsalva (coughing, sneezing)
  • Positive straight-leg raise test

Sprains & Strains

  • Strain — injury to muscle or tendon (overstretching)
  • Sprain — stretching/tearing of ligament beyond normal ROM
  • Causes: twisting, pulling, improper lifting
  • S/S: pain, warmth, muscle spasms, swelling
  • Tx: 24–48 hrs bed rest, NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, PT
🚨

CAUDA EQUINA SYNDROME — Surgical Emergency
Rare compression of the sacral nerve root bundle at the lumbar spine. Hallmark: saddle anesthesia (loss of sensation in perineum, inner thighs, genitals), bladder/bowel dysfunction, bilateral leg weakness/pain. Without emergent decompression, permanent paralysis and incontinence result. This is a time-sensitive surgical emergency — transport immediately.

VII
Arthritis & Joint Disorders
58.5 million Americans affected · Leading cause of disability in the US

Osteoarthritis (OA)

Pathophysiology

OA is a degenerative joint disease characterized by progressive breakdown of articular cartilage. Chondrocytes (cartilage cells) are lost, the underlying subchondral bone becomes sclerotic and forms osteophytes (bone spurs). The joint space narrows, synovial fluid decreases, and bone-on-bone contact causes severe pain. OA primarily affects weight-bearing joints (knees, hips) and the spine.

⚙️
Worn Brake Pad Analogy: OA is like worn-down brake pads on a car — the protective material erodes over decades of use. Eventually you get metal on metal (bone on bone) contact — grinding, squealing, and pain. The body tries to compensate by laying down extra material (osteophytes/bone spurs) — like welding reinforcement plates onto the worn brake assembly — but this just makes the joint stiffer and less functional.

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Pathophysiology

RA is an autoimmune synovitis — the immune system mistakenly targets the synovial membrane. Activated lymphocytes (T and B cells) infiltrate the synovium, releasing cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6) that cause synovial hyperplasia and pannus formation. The pannus is a layer of granulation tissue that aggressively invades and destroys cartilage and subchondral bone. RA follows a symmetrical distribution (bilateral wrists, MCPs, PIPs) and can affect extra-articular tissues (lungs, heart, eyes).

🔥
Friendly Fire Analogy: RA is the immune system's "friendly fire" — the body's soldiers (lymphocytes) receive bad intelligence and attack their own base (the synovium). They set up a permanent occupation force (pannus) that dismantles the joint from the inside out. The harder the immune system fights, the more structural damage accumulates. DMARDs and biologics work by calling off the soldiers or disrupting their chemical communication lines (cytokines like TNF-α).

Septic Arthritis

Pathophysiology

Direct invasion of the joint space by bacteria (most commonly Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus, or Neisseria gonorrhoeae in young sexually active adults). Bacteria trigger a massive immune response within the synovial space. Proteolytic enzymes destroy cartilage within hours to days if untreated. The knee is most commonly affected, followed by hip, shoulder, ankle, and wrist. Septic arthritis of the hip in children is a true emergency — avascular necrosis of the femoral head can result.

VIII
Gout
Crystal arthropathy · Monoarticular · Hyperuricemia

Pathophysiology

Gout results from hyperuricemia — elevated serum uric acid levels. Uric acid is the end product of purine metabolism (purines are found in high concentrations in meat, seafood, alcohol, and fructose). When uric acid supersaturates synovial fluid, it crystallizes as monosodium urate (MSU) crystals — needle-shaped and negatively birefringent on polarized light microscopy.

Macrophages engulf the MSU crystals, triggering NLRP3 inflammasome activation and massive IL-1β release. This causes the sudden, excruciating inflammation characteristic of acute gout attacks. Classic presentation: red, hot, swollen, exquisitely tender first MTP joint (podagra).

🪡
Broken Glass Analogy: MSU crystals in a joint are like broken glass shards scattered on the floor of your knee or toe. Every movement of the joint grinds the shards into the synovium, triggering a massive inflammatory response. The macrophages (the cleanup crew) try to vacuum up the glass but are cut in the process, releasing a flood of inflammatory chemicals (IL-1β) that cause the intense redness, heat, and agonizing pain. Allopurinol works by stopping the production of new "glass" (uric acid) at the source — blocking xanthine oxidase.

Acute Gout Features

  • Sudden onset (often nocturnal)
  • Monoarticular (1st MTP = classic "podagra")
  • Intense, excruciating pain
  • Red, hot, swollen joint
  • Exquisitely tender — even bedsheet contact painful
  • Resolves in 7–14 days without treatment

Risk Factors

  • Male sex (women post-menopause)
  • Diets rich in meat, shellfish, beer
  • Obesity and metabolic syndrome
  • Hypertension and heart failure
  • Diuretics (thiazide, loop)
  • Cyclosporine (transplant patients)
  • CKD (impaired uric acid excretion)

IX
Myalgia & Inflammatory Myopathies
Autoimmune · Progressive · Multi-system

Pathophysiology

Myalgia is generalized muscle pain that can be acute (infection, overuse, rhabdomyolysis) or chronic (myopathy, fibromyalgia). Inflammatory myopathies are autoimmune conditions in which immune cells attack skeletal muscle fibers, causing chronic inflammation, fiber necrosis, and progressive proximal muscle weakness. There are four major inflammatory myopathies: dermatomyositis, polymyositis, inclusion body myositis, and necrotizing autoimmune myopathy.

🔴 Dermatomyositis

Skin rash (heliotrope — blue-purple eyelid rash, Gottron's papules over joints) + progressive proximal muscle weakness. Can be paraneoplastic (associated with underlying malignancy). Treat with corticosteroids + immunosuppressants.

🟡 Polymyositis

Bilateral proximal muscle weakness (shoulder/hip girdle) without skin involvement. Ages > 20. May cause dysphagia, respiratory muscle weakness, and cardiac dysrhythmias. No cure — manage with steroids, azathioprine.

🔵 Inclusion Body Myositis

Most common inflammatory myopathy in adults > 50. Slowly progressive weakness of wrist/finger flexors and quadriceps. Inclusion bodies visible on muscle biopsy. Poorly responsive to immunosuppression.

⚫ Necrotizing Autoimmune Myopathy

Rapid onset (weeks). Associated with statin use, viral infections (HIV, HTLV-1), or connective tissue disorders. High CK levels. Requires aggressive immunosuppression; may be paraneoplastic.

🛡️
Autoimmune Analogy: Think of inflammatory myopathy as the body's security system going rogue — the antivirus software starts deleting healthy muscle files instead of viral ones. In dermatomyositis, the complement system attacks the microvasculature of muscle. In polymyositis, cytotoxic T-cells directly invade muscle fibers. Corticosteroids work by essentially "turning down the sensitivity" of the security system, while DMARDs change the underlying software code to prevent recurring false attacks.

Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS/ME)
Myalgic Encephalomyelitis · 2.5 million Americans

Pathophysiology

CFS (Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome, ME/CFS) is a complex multi-system disorder characterized by profound, unexplained fatigue lasting ≥ 6 months that is not improved by rest and worsens with physical or mental exertion (post-exertional malaise). The exact pathophysiology is incompletely understood but evidence points to immune dysregulation (elevated pro-inflammatory cytokines), autonomic nervous system dysfunction (orthostatic intolerance), mitochondrial dysfunction (impaired energy production), and potential viral triggers (EBV, HHV-6, SARS-CoV-2 in Long COVID).

Profound fatigue (> 6 months)

ℹ️

Prehospital Note: CFS patients often call EMS during "crashes" (severe post-exertional relapses). Treat symptomatically — pain, dehydration, tachycardia from orthostatic intolerance. Avoid dismissive language like "it's just stress." These patients face significant diagnostic invalidation. Perform standard workup to rule out other emergent causes of fatigue (sepsis, cardiac, endocrine).

XI
Overuse Syndromes
Bursitis · Muscle Strains · Tendonitis · PRICEM Method

Bursitis

Inflammation of bursae (fluid-filled cushioning sacs) from repetitive compression, overuse, trauma, or infection. Common sites: olecranon, subacromial, trochanteric, prepatellar, Achilles bursa.

Tx: Corticosteroid injection, ice, rest, NSAIDs, needle aspiration, PT.

Muscle Strains

Microscopic to macroscopic tears in muscle fibers or myotendinous junction from excessive force or overstretching. Grades 1–3.

Common: Hamstrings, quadriceps, hip flexors, lower back.

Tx: PRICEM, surgery for Grade 3.

Tendonitis

Tendon inflammation from repetitive mechanical loading. Affects wrist (de Quervain's), Achilles, patellar, rotator cuff, and common extensor origin (tennis elbow).

Tx: PRICEM method, eccentric PT, corticosteroid injection, PRP.

Peripheral Nerve Syndromes
Entrapment Neuropathies · Compression · Ischemia

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome — Pathophysiology

CTS occurs when the median nerve is compressed within the carpal tunnel — a narrow, rigid canal bounded by the carpal bones dorsally and the transverse carpal ligament volarly. Compression causes myelin sheath damage and axonal ischemia, initially demyelinating (reversible), then axonal (permanent) if untreated. Increased pressure from edema, hypertrophy, or structural changes reduces epineural blood flow — causing the classic nocturnal paresthesias (when wrist is held in flexion during sleep).

🚰
Kinked Garden Hose Analogy: The median nerve is like a water hose running through a narrow tunnel. When the tunnel swells (from pregnancy, hypothyroidism, RA, repetitive wrist use), the hose gets kinked. Water (nerve signals) can still trickle through initially — producing the tingling and burning of early CTS. Over time, the sustained kinking damages the hose itself — first the outer coating (myelin), then the inner tube (axon) — causing permanent numbness and thenar wasting. Wrist splinting keeps the hose unkinked; surgery widens the tunnel.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

  • Median nerve compression at wrist
  • 3× more common in women
  • Nocturnal paresthesias (thumb, index, middle fingers)
  • Thenar atrophy (advanced)
  • Positive Tinel's sign, Phalen's test
  • Tx: wrist splint, corticosteroid injection, surgical carpal tunnel release

Ulnar Nerve Entrapment (Cubital Tunnel)

  • Ulnar nerve compressed behind medial epicondyle
  • Numbness/tingling in ring and little finger
  • Weakness of intrinsic hand muscles ("claw hand")
  • Worsens with elbow flexion (leaning on elbow)
  • Diagnosed by nerve conduction studies
  • Tx: padding, splinting, surgical decompression

XIII
Necrotizing Fasciitis
"Flesh-Eating Bacteria" · Rapid progression · Surgical emergency
🚨

TRUE EMERGENCY: Necrotizing fasciitis is a rapidly progressive, life-threatening infection. Mortality approaches 30–70% without prompt surgical debridement. Time from onset to surgery is the most important determinant of outcome. Transport immediately — do not delay for extended on-scene management.

Pathophysiology

NF is a polymicrobial or monomicrobial necrotizing infection of the deep fascia that spreads along fascial planes with sparing of overlying skin (initially). Group A Streptococcus (Strep pyogenes) is the classic causative organism in Type II NF. Bacteria release exotoxins and superantigens that trigger a massive cytokine storm — causing microthrombi in fascial blood vessels, leading to ischemia, necrosis, and tissue liquefaction. The infection tracks far beyond the visible skin changes — the area of tenderness almost always exceeds the area of visible skin abnormality.

🧨
Underground Fire Analogy: NF is like a fire burning in the walls and floors of a building, not on the surface. The outside walls (skin) look only mildly discolored while the interior (fascia, subcutaneous tissue) is completely ablaze. By the time the surface shows obvious changes — bullae, skin necrosis, violaceous discoloration — the fire has already spread through the entire interior. Surgical debridement means tearing out the walls to expose and extinguish the fire, even if it means sacrificing structurally intact-looking material around it.

Signs & Symptoms (Temporal Progression)

  • Early: Intense pain disproportionate to appearance, erythema, warmth, swelling
  • Mid: Skin discoloration (dusky, violet-purple), blistering/bullae, "wooden" texture
  • Late: Skin necrosis, crepitus (gas), frank anesthesia (nerve destruction), septic shock

Risk Factors

  • Immunocompromise (HIV, DM, malignancy)
  • IV drug use (injection site entry)
  • Recent surgery or trauma
  • Skin infections (infected wounds, insect bites)
  • Cirrhosis / ESRD
  • NSAIDs may mask early warning pain

XIV
Gangrene
Tissue Necrosis · Dry vs. Wet · Vascular & Infectious Etiology

Pathophysiology

Gangrene is necrosis and decay of body tissue resulting from critical ischemia or overwhelming infection. The two major types have distinct mechanisms. Dry gangrene results from progressive arterial insufficiency (atherosclerosis, Buerger's disease, frostbite) — the tissue desiccates and mummifies without infection. Wet gangrene results from bacterial infection of ischemic or traumatized tissue; anaerobes produce gas (crepitus on palpation) — giving rise to the rare but deadly gas gangrene caused by Clostridium perfringens.

🌿
Plant Analogy: Dry gangrene is like a branch cut off from the tree in dry conditions — it slowly desiccates, turns brown, hardens, and eventually falls off on its own. Wet gangrene is the same branch in a warm, moist environment — bacteria colonize and decompose it rapidly, producing foul odors and toxins that can infect the entire tree. Gas gangrene is the most aggressive form — like a fast-acting blight that produces gas bubbles as it digests tissue at alarming speed. Amputation is sometimes the only way to save the tree.

Dry Gangrene

  • Gradual onset; arterial occlusion
  • Tissue: dry, shrunken, discolored (black/brown)
  • Well-demarcated boundary
  • Less immediate threat — may be managed medically
  • Associated with PVD, diabetes, frostbite, arterial embolism
  • Tx: revascularization or amputation when indicated

Wet Gangrene

  • Rapid onset; infected tissue
  • Tissue: moist, swollen, oozing pus, malodorous
  • Poorly demarcated; spreads rapidly
  • Systemic sepsis common
  • May present with crepitus (gas-forming organisms)
  • Tx: immediate surgery + IV antibiotics + ICU support

Paronychia & Flexor Tenosynovitis
Hand infections · Kanavel Signs · Orthopedic Emergency

Paronychia

Infection of the lateral nail fold caused by injury (nail biting, pulled hangnail, manicure trauma) allowing entry of bacteria (Staph aureus, most common), yeast (Candida), or fungus.

  • Acute: bacterial — pain, redness, purulent discharge
  • Chronic: fungal — thickened, discolored nail, less pain
  • Treatment: I&D (incision & drainage), antibiotics (clindamycin, TMP-SMX), warm soaks, nail removal if subungual abscess
  • Watch for systemic signs: red streaks (lymphangitis), fever, malaise — indicates spreading cellulitis

Flexor Tenosynovitis — Orthopedic Emergency

Infection within the closed flexor tendon sheath of the hand. When bacteria enter the sheath (puncture, bite), the resulting inflammatory edema disrupts the gliding mechanism, causing tendon rupture, permanent contracture, or vascular compromise if untreated within hours.

Kanavel's Four Cardinal Signs:

  • 1️⃣ Severe pain on passive extension of the finger
  • 2️⃣ Uniform fusiform swelling of the digit
  • 3️⃣ Finger held in slight flexion (position of comfort)
  • 4️⃣ Tenderness along the flexor tendon sheath

Prehospital Tx: Elevate, ice for comfort, rapid transport. Hospital: surgical drainage + IV antibiotics + splinting.

🚨

Kanavel's Signs = Immediate Transport: Any patient presenting with Kanavel's four cardinal signs has pyogenic flexor tenosynovitis until proven otherwise — this is an orthopedic emergency. Delay in surgical drainage results in permanent tendon destruction and loss of hand function.
