📊 Epidemiology of Trauma

⚠ Why This Matters to You

Unintentional injury is the leading cause of death for ages 1–44 and the fourth-leading cause of death overall. The majority of these deaths are preventable — and your ability to apply kinematic principles is one of the tools that saves them.

The Three Phases of Trauma Care

PhaseDefinitionParamedic Role
Pre-EventFactors present before injury: intoxication, vision impairment, cardiac disease, fatigueInjury prevention education, community awareness
EventBegins at first contact with injuring force; ends when injuring force is no longer appliedScene reconstruction, MOI analysis
Post-EventEmergency care delivery to the patientGather info, lifesaving maneuvers, appropriate transport

The Trauma System

  • Comprehensive trauma systems integrate prevention, prehospital care, emergency department care, definitive care, rehabilitation, and data collection
  • Level I Trauma Center: Full specialists 24/7; regional resource for severely injured patients
  • Level II–V: Progressively limited capability — know your regional system

Transport Decisions

  • Ground transport: Default if destination reachable in reasonable time
  • Aeromedical transport: Consider when ground time threatens survival, road conditions delay care, or critical care equipment is needed en route
💡 Clinical Pearl

Time is the most critical resource in trauma. The "golden hour" concept means your field time and transport decision directly affects mortality. Always ask: Where does this patient need to be, and how do I get them there fastest?

⚡ Kinematics: The Physics of Trauma

Kinematics is the process of predicting injury patterns based on the forces and energies involved in a traumatic event. It is your most powerful diagnostic tool — it works before you ever touch the patient.

The Laws of Physics Every Paramedic Must Know

KE = ½ × m × v²
Kinetic Energy = ½ × Mass × Velocity²  |  Velocity is the dominant factor
LawPrincipleClinical Application
Newton's 1st LawObjects at rest/motion stay that way until acted on by outside forceRestrained patient: body stops with car. Unrestrained: body continues forward.
Newton's 2nd LawF = m × a (or deceleration)Heavier patient or faster deceleration = greater force on organs
Conservation of EnergyEnergy cannot be created or destroyed — only transformedCrash energy transfers to patient's tissues as crush, stretch, heat
Kinetic EnergyKE = ½mv² — velocity has exponential effectDoubling speed = 4× the energy — explains why high-speed crashes are so deadly
🔑 Key Insight

Doubling mass doubles kinetic energy. Doubling velocity quadruples it. Speed kills exponentially. This is why a 60 mph crash is not twice as bad as a 30 mph crash — it is four times more lethal.

🚗 Blunt Trauma: Motor Vehicle Crashes

Every MVC involves three separate impacts as energy transfers through the system:

1st
Vehicle Impact
Vehicle strikes object or another vehicle. Energy transfers to the chassis.
2nd
Occupant Impact
Occupant collides with interior of vehicle: dashboard, steering wheel, windshield.
3rd
Organ Impact
Internal organs collide against each other, body wall, or anchoring structures.

MVC Impact Patterns & Expected Injuries

Impact TypeMechanismPredicted Injuries
Frontal (Head-On)Down-and-under OR Up-and-over pathwayKnee/femur fractures, chest wall, aortic shear, C-spine, facial trauma
LateralSide compression; organ moves away from impactClavicle, ribs, pelvic fractures; aortic tear; C-spine lateral flexion
Rear-EndVehicle accelerates forward under occupant; head lags behindCervical hyperextension injury (whiplash), C-spine fracture
RotationalOff-center impact causes vehicle rotationCombination of frontal and lateral patterns
RolloverUnrestrained occupant tumbles inside vehicle; possible ejectionMultiple-system trauma; all patterns possible; ejection is 30× higher fatality risk

🔒 Restraints: Injury Prevention & Pattern Modification

📊 The Evidence

Properly used safety belts reduce fatal injury risk by 51% and serious/critical injury by 65%. Unrestrained occupants are 30× more likely to be ejected, and 75% of ejected patients die.

Restraint TypeBenefitInjury Pattern When Improperly Used
Lap BeltPrevents ejection; distributes force to pelvisWorn too high: abdominal compression → "seat belt syndrome" (bowel, mesenteric, lumbar spine injuries)
Shoulder BeltPrevents upper torso impact; holds occupant in seatWorn improperly under arm: rib fractures, clavicular injury, lacerated spleen
3-Point BeltBest protection; distributes force across bony pelvis and chestStill allows some forward flexion — assess for abdominal/lumbar injuries
Frontal AirbagCushions forward momentum with lap/shoulder beltDeflates rapidly — does NOT protect from side, rear, or subsequent impacts. Risk group: infants <13 yrs, adults <4'6", elderly

🫀 Organ Injuries from Deceleration & Compression

Deceleration Injuries — "The Snap"

When the body decelerates, organs continue to move — stretching or tearing from their attachment points:

RegionOrgan MotionResult
HeadBrain continues moving as cranium stops; strikes inside of skullConcussion, contusion, DAI, coup-contrecoup
ThoraxHeart & aorta continue forward; shear at ligamentum arteriosumTraumatic aortic dissection/rupture — often immediately fatal
AbdomenKidneys shear from vascular pedicle; liver separates at hilum; mesenteric tearsExsanguinating hemorrhage — may be clinically silent initially

Compression Injuries — "The Crush"

RegionMechanismResult
HeadExternal force compresses skullDepressed fracture, brain contusion/laceration, hemorrhage
ThoraxChest wall compressed; increased intrathoracic pressurePaper bag effect → lung rupture; pneumothorax; flail chest
AbdomenHollow/solid organ compressed between external force and spineBladder rupture (especially if full), splenic/hepatic laceration

⚠ Vertical Falls

Falls from 3× patient's height (15–20 ft) are associated with severe injury. Classic finding: bilateral calcaneus (heel) fractures. Energy then travels up: pelvis → lumbar spine → thoracic spine. Infants fall headfirst (proportionally large head) → skull fractures, intracranial bleeding. Elderly sustain high numbers of hip fractures and head injuries from low-level falls.

💥 Blast Injuries: 5 Phases

1°
Primary
Supersonic blast wave → gas-containing organs: lungs, bowel, sinuses, tympanic membranes. Unique to high-order explosives.
2°
Secondary
Flying debris (glass, metal, shrapnel) → penetrating trauma. Most common blast injury mechanism.
3°
Tertiary
Patient propelled through space by blast wind → strikes stationary object. Similar pattern to vertical falls/MVCs.
4°
Quaternary
All other explosion injuries: burns, toxic gas inhalation, crush injuries, exacerbation of existing conditions.
5°
Quinary
Injuries from contaminants in explosion: chemical, biological, radiologic substances ("dirty bomb" contamination).

🔫 Penetrating Trauma

All penetrating objects — regardless of velocity — cause tissue disruption via two mechanisms: crushing and stretching.

Cavitation

Cavitation is the opening produced when a projectile pushes body tissues laterally away from its tract. Temporary cavitation can be massive even without permanent visible damage — especially in abdominal injuries.

Energy Levels of Penetrating Trauma

Energy LevelWeaponsMuzzle VelocityInjury Pattern
LowKnives, needles, ice picksN/ACutting and crushing along track only; minimal cavitation
MediumHandguns, some rifles~1,000 fps (304 m/s)Larger permanent cavity; temporary cavitation; unpredictable tract
HighMilitary rifles, high-powered rifles>2,000 fps (610 m/s)Massive cavitation; tissue disruption far from wound tract; fragmentation

Wounding Factors for Medium/High-Energy Projectiles

AbdomenGSW to abdomen = surgery; always assume serious injury even if patient appears stable

📋 Trauma Assessment: The 9-Component Framework

  1. Standard precautions — PPE before everything
  2. Scene size-up & resource identification — unsafe scene cannot be entered
  3. General impression — Is this patient sick or not sick?
  4. MOI — Reconstruct the event before you assess the patient
  5. Primary assessment — ABCDE; identify and treat life threats
  6. Baseline vitals — BP, HR, RR, SpO₂, EtCO₂, ECG
  7. Patient history & event history — SAMPLE, OPQRST, pre-event history
  8. Secondary assessment — Head-to-toe physical exam
  9. Reassessment — Continuous monitoring; trends matter
📝 Documentation Is Treatment

Your patient care report recreates the scene for every provider who sees this patient after you. Include the MOI, scene findings, anatomic wound locations (on a body diagram), and a timeline. Poor documentation = lost clinical information = compromised care.

📍 Capstone Scenario: Operation Westhaven

Multi-Patient Trauma Scene

You are the first ALS unit on scene of a high-speed MVC on a rural two-lane highway. A pickup truck (estimated 65 mph) crossed the centerline and struck a passenger sedan head-on. The sedan has severe front-end intrusion. Your unit is alone; the next unit is 8 minutes out.

Patient A: Male, ~40s, driver of sedan. Unrestrained. Found partially ejected through windshield. GCS 8. HR 140, BP 70/40, RR 6 and labored. Obvious head laceration with facial deformity.

Patient B: Female, ~35, passenger of sedan. Restrained (3-point belt). Alert and oriented. Complaining of severe left chest pain and left shoulder pain. HR 110, BP 100/70, RR 22, SpO₂ 94%.

Patient C: Male, ~50s, driver of pickup. Restrained, airbag deployed. Confused (GCS 14). Minor facial lacerations. HR 95, BP 130/80, RR 18, SpO₂ 98%.

🧩 Capstone Analysis: Apply Your Framework

Step 1: Scene Size-Up & MOI Analysis

⚠ Critical Decision Point

Head-on collision at 65 mph = combined closing speed of ~130 mph. This is an extreme kinetic energy event. Expect multisystem trauma in all patients. Call for additional resources immediately — you cannot manage three patients alone.

Step 2: Triage Priority (Using Your MOI Assessment)

PatientMOI / Predicted InjuriesTriage PriorityRationale
A (Sedan driver)Ejection (30× fatal risk) + head-on + unrestrained. Expected: TBI, C-spine, pneumothorax, aortic shear, internal abdominal hemorrhageIMMEDIATE (Red)Multisystem trauma, altered LOC, hypotension = hemorrhagic shock, likely airway compromise
B (Sedan passenger)Restrained 3-point + head-on. Belt syndrome possible. Left lateral chest: rib fractures, pneumothorax, aortic dissection, splenic injuryURGENT (Yellow)Compensated; hemodynamically borderline — can deteriorate rapidly; monitor closely
C (Pickup driver)Restrained + airbag. GCS 14 — possible TBI, airbag abrasions. Lower kinetic force absorption due to truck massDELAYED (Green)Ambulatory, stable — monitor for delayed deterioration

Step 3: Transport Decisions

  • Patient A: Requires Level I Trauma Center — request aeromedical now if ground time >15 minutes. Do not delay on scene for anything but airway control and IV access.
  • Patient B: Level I preferred — left lateral chest pain with hypoxia may represent aortic injury or tension pneumothorax in evolution.
  • Patient C: Level II or I — monitor GCS trend; confused patient with head-on MOI warrants CT of head.

Step 4: Integration — The MOI Algorithm

🔄 Your Clinical Algorithm

Scene → MOI → Physics → Anatomy → Predicted Injuries → Assessment → Transport. This is the loop you run on every trauma patient. Kinematics does not replace physical assessment — it directs your assessment so you know where to look and what to treat before the injury declares itself.
