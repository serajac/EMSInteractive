🎯 Jackson Thermal Wound Theory — Zones of Injury

Full-thickness burns exhibit three concentric zones in a "bull's-eye" pattern. Understanding these zones guides your resuscitation priorities:

Zone of Hyperemia

Outermost — increased blood flow, minimal damage; will recover

Zone of Stasis

Middle — decreased perfusion; salvageable with appropriate fluid resuscitation

Zone of Coagulation

Core — maximum heat contact; coagulation necrosis; irreversible

Clinical Pearl: The Zone of Stasis is where YOUR resuscitation efforts matter. Delay in IV fluids, hypothermia, or hypotension can convert the stasis zone into coagulation necrosis — worsening the wound depth and TBSA.

⚡ Burn Shock — Pathophysiology You Must Understand

Critical Concept: Within minutes of a major burn, ALL capillaries — not just those at the burn site — lose the ability to retain fluid. This systemic capillary leak is the mechanism of burn shock.

Mechanism of burn shock:

⚠️Thermal injury → massive local edema → fluid loss at burn site (local response)
⚠️Systemic capillary permeability increases → intravascular fluid shifts to interstitium (systemic response)
⚠️Hypovolemia: fluid evaporates from injured skin, sodium is lost, K⁺ released into extracellular fluid, blood concentrates
⚠️Compensatory mechanisms: ADH, aldosterone, renin-angiotensin — attempt to retain sodium and water but are overwhelmed in major burns
✅Capillary permeability normalizes at ~24 hours post-burn — this guides fluid resuscitation timing

Multi-system effects of major burn injury:

Pulmonary: ARDS risk, increased work of breathing
GI: Ileus, Curling's ulcer, mucosal compromise
Metabolic: Hypermetabolic state; glucose dysregulation
Immune: Compromised; risk of sepsis
Neuroendocrine: Catecholamine surge
Emotional: Acute stress; fear; pain

✅Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints
✅Full-thickness burns in any age group
✅Electrical burns, including lightning injury
✅Chemical burns
✅Inhalation injury
✅Burns + concomitant trauma where burn poses greatest risk
✅Burns in children — transfer to pediatric-verified burn center
✅Burns in patients with preexisting medical disorders

🚑 General Principles of Prehospital Burn Management

1️⃣Stop the burning process — ensure scene safety; for small burns, cool (not ice-cold) tap water; do NOT apply ice, butter, ointments, or toothpaste
2️⃣Airway, O₂, Ventilation — evaluate every patient; humidified high-concentration O₂ for severe burns/inhalation; intubate early — vocal changes and stridor signal impending obstruction
3️⃣Circulation & Fluid Resuscitation — based on burn severity, VS, and transport time; crystalloid (LR) preferred
4️⃣Remove jewelry, rings, watches from extremity burns — prevent vascular compromise as edema develops
5️⃣Pain management — burns are excruciating; treat pain per protocol; morphine/fentanyl; IV preferred
6️⃣Prevent hypothermia — burn patients lose heat rapidly; cover burns with dry, sterile dressings; warm the ambulance
7️⃣Circumferential burns — can act like a tourniquet on extremities; monitor distal pulses carefully

💧 Fluid Resuscitation Formulas

Parkland Formula (First 24 Hours)
Total Volume = 4 mL × body weight (kg) × % TBSA burned
50% given in first 8 hours from time of injury · 25% in next 8 hrs · 25% in final 8 hrs · Fluid: Lactated Ringer's or Normal Saline
Rule of 10 (≥40 kg patients — simplified field formula)
Initial Rate (mL/hr) = %TBSA (rounded to nearest 10) × 10
Add 100 mL/hr for every 10 kg over 80 kg · Faster to calculate in the field
Pediatric Fluid Resuscitation: Indicated for burns ≥10–20% TBSA in children. Add maintenance D5LR (children have low glycogen stores → hypoglycemia risk). Check blood glucose levels regularly.

Example Calculation: 80 kg patient, 40% TBSA burn (Parkland formula):

Total = 4 × 80 × 40 = 12,800 mL in 24 hours
First 8 hrs: 6,400 mL (800 mL/hr) · Second 8 hrs: 3,200 mL (400 mL/hr) · Third 8 hrs: 3,200 mL (400 mL/hr)
Note: Clock starts at time of injury, not EMS arrival.

🫁 Inhalation Burn Injuries

Incidence: Pulmonary complications cause 77% of deaths in residential fires. The majority involve carbon monoxide poisoning and/or inhalation injury. Every burn patient must be evaluated for this.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

CO is colorless, odorless, tasteless. Does not harm lung tissue directly — displaces O₂ from hemoglobin (carboxyhemoglobin). Pulse oximetry reads FALSELY NORMAL (cannot differentiate HbO₂ from HbCO).

COHb LevelSymptoms
<10%Usually asymptomatic; mild headache in sensitive patients
10–20%Headache, nausea, dizziness — often mistaken for flu
20–40%Confusion, syncope, tachycardia, visual changes
40–60%Severe neurological impairment, seizures, cardiovascular collapse
>60%Fatal in most cases
Treatment: 100% O₂ via NRB or intubation. High-flow O₂ reduces CO half-life from 4–5 hours (room air) to ~60–90 minutes. Consider hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) for severe cases.

Signs of Upper Airway (Supraglottic) Injury

🔴Facial burns / singed nasal/facial hair
🔴Carbonaceous sputum (black soot)
🔴Edema of face / oropharynx
🔴Hoarse voice — intubate now
🔴Stridor — impending obstruction
🔴Brassy cough / grunting respirations

Lower Airway (Infraglottic) Injury

Results from toxic chemical inhalation; symptoms may be delayed hours after exposure:

⚠️Wheezes / crackles / rhonchi
⚠️Productive cough
⚠️Signs of hypoxemia
⚠️Bronchospasm

☣️ Chemical Burn Injuries — Assessment & Management

Determine at scene: Type, concentration, volume, mechanism of injury, time of contamination, first aid given, pain level, appearance.

General Chemical Burn Management:

✅Remove ALL clothing and shoes — can trap concentrated chemicals
✅Brush off powdered chemicals before irrigation
✅Irrigate with copious water — large areas: use shower if available
✅Eyes: flush with water (Morgan lens for prolonged irrigation)
✅Irrigation priority over transport; continue en route if possible

Specific Chemical Injuries:

AgentMechanismSpecial Treatment
Hydrofluoric AcidH⁺ + F⁻ ions damage tissue; fluoride inhibits cell metabolism; can cause systemic hypocalcemiaIrrigate ≥15 min; apply calcium gluconate gel ≥20 min; consider IV calcium gluconate
Phenol (Carbolic Acid)Tissue coagulation + systemic absorption riskWater irrigation; watch for systemic toxicity
AmmoniaStrong alkali; very soluble; severe eye hazard — corneal burns, ulcers, blindnessCopious water; immediate eye irrigation
ChlorineSimilar to ammonia; more likely bronchiolar burns and wheezingRemove from exposure; O₂; bronchodilators
White PhosphorusIgnites spontaneously in air; releases smoke that irritates respiratory/GI tractSubmerge/cover with wet dressings to stop burning; do NOT allow to dry out
Alkali Metals (Na, K)React with water and generate heatRemove metal physically or cover with oil — do NOT use water

⚡ Electrical Burn Injuries

Electrical injuries have three basic types: Direct-contact burns (penetrates skin/tissue), Arc injuries (current overcomes air resistance between contact points), and Flash/flame burns (ignites nearby combustibles).

Hidden Injury Principle: Electrical injuries cause far more internal damage than visible wounds suggest. The entry wound may be small while massive coagulation necrosis exists along the current pathway. ALWAYS suspect hidden injuries.

Assessment & Management:

✅Scene safety first — do not approach until power is confirmed off
✅Search for entry AND exit wounds; remove all clothing and jewelry
✅Examine joints and creases — multiple current pathways possible
✅Obtain: voltage, amperage, duration of contact, LOC history, significant PMH
✅Early IV fluids — vital for severe electrical injury (myoglobinuria risk)
✅Analgesia per protocol — pain can be severe
✅ECG monitoring — dysrhythmias are common

Lightning Injury — Special Considerations: 40 million lightning strikes/year in U.S.; ~43 deaths/year. Temperature up to 50,000°F. Unlike other electrical injury, current often travels OVER rather than through skin (flashover phenomenon). If storm still active — all care in sheltered area. Reverse triage may apply: apparent "dead" patients may respond to resuscitation (lightning causes cardiac/respiratory arrest).

☢️ Radiation Injury

Radiation injuries are rare but require specific safety and management protocols. Ionizing radiation (gamma rays, X-rays) is most dangerous; non-ionizing (radio waves, microwaves) is generally safe at normal levels.

Dose (rem)Expected Effects
<100 remNo significant acute problems
100–200 remMild symptoms; not life-threatening; fatigue, nausea
~200 remNausea, vomiting, diarrhea within 2–4 hours
450 remCognitive impairment occurs
≥600 remHigh mortality

Radiation Protection Principles (TDS-Q):

⏱ Time
Minimize exposure time
📏 Distance
Maximize distance from source
🛡 Shielding
Use appropriate lead/barriers
📦 Quantity
Limit radioactive material handled
Clean vs. Dirty: A "clean" exposure = patient exposed but not contaminated. A "dirty" exposure = patient contaminated with radioactive material. Contaminated patients require decontamination — remove clothing and irrigate skin — before transport to prevent secondary contamination of EMS personnel and facilities.

📋 Module Summary — Critical Takeaways

🔥Burns kill by airway compromise, burn shock, and CO poisoning — not just by tissue damage. Address all three simultaneously.
🔥Intubate early — airway edema is progressive. Once you hear stridor, the window for elective intubation may be closing.
🔥SpO₂ is unreliable in CO poisoning — treat empirically with 100% O₂.
🔥Decontaminate chemical burns before transport — irrigation priority over transport unless it can continue en route.
🔥Electrical burns have hidden internal injury — visible wounds don't represent the true extent of damage.
🔥Fluid resuscitation saves the Zone of Stasis — your resuscitation determines whether wounds worsen or stabilize.
🔥Know burn center criteria cold — you make the transport decision; make it early and communicate clearly.
