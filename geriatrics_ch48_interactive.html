Physiologic Changes of Aging by Body System

🫁 Respiratory System

Reduced pulmonary function from decreased elastic recoil, diminished respiratory muscle strength, and reduced chest wall compliance. PaO₂ slowly declines; chemoreceptor response to hypoxia and hypercapnia is blunted. Loss of cilia, diminished cough reflex, and impaired gag reflex increase infection risk.

❤️ Cardiovascular System

Diminished ability to raise HR; decreased ventricular compliance; prolonged contraction; decreased catecholamine response. Myocardial hypertrophy and CAD predispose to dysrhythmias, CHF, and sudden cardiac arrest. Conduction system fibrosis leads to AFib, sick sinus syndrome, bradycardias, and blocks.

🫘 Renal System

Decreased renal blood flow → reduced GFR. Loss of concentrating ability, sodium conservation, free water clearance. Reduced drug clearance → drug toxicity and adverse events more likely. Decreased liver/kidney function increases electrolyte disturbances.

🧠 Nervous System

11% reduction in brain weight by age 70. Decreased cerebral blood flow, altered neurotransmitter levels. Peripheral nerve conduction slows → delayed reaction time and motor responses. Intellectual functioning deteriorates selectively, not globally.

💪 Musculoskeletal System

Muscles shrink; ligaments calcify; intervertebral disks thin. Osteoporosis common (⅓ loss of bone density by age 70). Kyphosis alters balance and mobility. Decreased total muscle mass increases fall risk. Falls are the leading traumatic injury in this population.

🌡️ Thermoregulation & Immunity

Declining thermoregulatory mechanisms increase risk of both hypothermia and hyperthermia. Decreased primary antibody response and cellular immunity. Age is the greatest cancer risk factor — 60% of cancer patients are ≥65. Decreased lean body mass and increased body fat affect drug pharmacokinetics.

Key Geriatric Assessment Principles

🕵️ Atypical Presentations

Pain may be diminished or absent. Multiple simultaneous illnesses. Chronic symptoms mask acute conditions. Social and emotional factors heavily influence health. Patient may minimize or deny symptoms. Confusion may be the ONLY presenting sign of pneumonia or UTI.

💊 Medication History

Obtain detailed medication list including OTC drugs and herbals. Polypharmacy: prescriptions from multiple physicians, OTC + Rx combinations. Verify patient is taking medications as prescribed — forgetfulness and vision impairment are major barriers. Bring ALL medications to ED.

📋 Functional Assessment

Assess changes in Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) — decline signals acute illness or need for social intervention. Use six-item cognitive screener for cognitive impairment early in assessment. Ask discreetly about advance directives and DNR status.

🏠 Environmental Assessment

Assess immediate surroundings for alcohol or medication use, presence of food, housing condition, and hygiene. Poverty affects 15 million older adults. Fixed income limits heating — hypothermia risk. 25% forgo food, utilities, or medicine to pay for health care.

Sensory Impairments in the Geriatric Patient

👁️ Vision — Cataracts

Loss of transparency of the lens from protein fiber changes. Progressive loss of clarity and detail — never causes full blindness. Very common — surgical removal is routine. Vision changes begin ~age 40 and worsen gradually.

👁️ Vision — Glaucoma

Elevated intraocular pressure damages the optic nerve → partial or complete loss of peripheral then central vision. Rarely seen before age 40. Treated with oral medications and eye drops to reduce pressure if detected early.

👂 Hearing — Presbycusis

Age-related degeneration of the hearing mechanism — the most common cause of hearing loss in older adults. Ménière disease, ototoxic drugs, tumors, and viral infections can also cause hearing loss. Hearing aids and surgical implants may restore or improve hearing.

🔔 Tinnitus

Perception of noise in the ear (ringing, buzzing, whistling) — almost always associated with hearing loss from aging. Present at all times with intermittent awareness. May change in nature and intensity but usually permanent.

🗣️ Speech & Communication

Difficulty in word retrieval, decreased fluency, slowed speech rate, changes in voice quality. Causes include damage to language centers, CNS degeneration, hearing loss, laryngeal disorders, and poor-fitting dentures. Critical for EMS communication strategy.

Elder Abuse — Recognition & Reporting

📖 Definition

Infliction of physical pain, injury, debilitating mental anguish, unreasonable confinement, or willful deprivation of services necessary to maintain mental and physical health of a geriatric person by a caregiver.

🚨 Types of Abuse

  • Physical abuse
  • Sexual abuse
  • Emotional/psychological abuse
  • Neglect
  • Abandonment
  • Financial/material exploitation
  • Self-neglect

📊 Epidemiology

Estimated to affect 5 million older adults each year. May occur in the home or residential care facilities. A growing and increasingly recognized problem. Affects all socioeconomic levels.

⚖️ Mandatory Reporting

ALL 50 states have elder abuse statutes. Reporting of suspected elder abuse is MANDATORY. Follow established local protocol for reporting. Paramedics are mandated reporters — document and report any suspicion.

Suicide Risk Assessment in Geriatric Patients

📊 Epidemiology

Patients ≥65 account for ~18% of all US suicides. Rate of completed suicides is HIGHER than the general population. Most visited their primary care physician in the month before. Most were suffering from their FIRST episode of major depression — unrecognized and untreated.

⚠️ Warning Signs

  • Talking about death or "getting affairs in order"
  • Stating benefits of dying ("My family would be better off")
  • Giving away prized possessions
  • Not adhering to medical regimen
  • Acquiring a weapon (especially firearm)

🔫 Method of Choice

Most suicides by older adults involve FIREARMS. Scene safety and crew safety are the priority when caring for a patient with suicidal tendencies. Secure the scene before approaching — law enforcement may be needed.

🤝 Approach

Many depressed patients will discuss suicidal thoughts if asked directly. Establish rapport and encourage open discussion. Interview family about mental state and depression history. Refer to physician for evaluation — depression is treatable!

Polypharmacy & Adverse Drug Events

💊 Most Common Culprits

Cardiovascular medications (first), then diuretics, non-opioid and opioid analgesics, hypoglycemics, and anticoagulants — these categories cause the most preventable ADEs leading to hospital admission.

⚠️ Why Geriatric Patients Are Vulnerable

  • Increased gastric pH, decreased GI motility (altered absorption)
  • Decreased cardiac output and total body water
  • Decreased liver blood flow (slower metabolism)
  • Decreased renal clearance (drug accumulation)
  • Altered CNS sensitivity

🔄 Causes of Drug Mishaps

  • Dispensing errors
  • Noncompliance / forgetfulness
  • Vision impairment
  • Self-selection of OTC drugs
  • Multiple prescribers
  • Resuming old medications alongside new ones

📉 Dose Adjustment

Drug doses may need to be reduced by as much as 50% in older adults due to changes in metabolism and elimination. Fat-soluble drugs accumulate in increased fat tissue. Water-soluble drugs become more concentrated with decreased total body water. Always consider dose-related ADE.

STEADI Fall Prevention Program

❓ Three Screening Questions

  • Have you fallen in the past year?
  • Do you feel unsteady when standing or walking?
  • Do you worry about falling?

Yes to ANY = increased fall risk

🔍 Identifiable Risk Factors

  • Lower body weakness
  • Gait and balance problems
  • Postural dizziness
  • History of previous falls
  • Home safety hazards
  • Medications (sedatives, diuretics, antihypertensives)

🚑 EMS Role

Paramedics assess fall risk during lift-assist and medical calls. Provide educational materials on fall reduction. Assess for postural hypotension. Refer to mobile integrated health or social services for follow-up. Document fall circumstances thoroughly.
