Incidence, Morbidity & Mortality
Why This Matters at Scale
Clinical Reality: Spinal cord injuries affect thousands of Americans annually. Motor vehicle crashes are the leading cause (~39%), followed by falls (~31%), acts of violence (~13%), and sports injuries (~8%). Injury prevention strategies have measurable impact — your education of patients matters beyond the call.

💀 Mortality Drivers

  • High cervical injuries (C1–C4) carry the highest mortality due to respiratory muscle involvement
  • Associated injuries (TBI, chest trauma) compound mortality significantly
  • Secondary injury from hypotension, hypoxia, and movement is preventable

📉 Morbidity Impact

  • Approximately 17,000 new SCIs per year in the US
  • Average age at injury: 43 years
  • ~80% of SCI patients are male
  • Lifetime care costs can exceed $5 million for high cervical injuries

🛡️ Your Role in Prevention

  • Proper SMR technique prevents secondary cord injury
  • Avoiding unnecessary immobilization reduces harm (pressure sores, airway compromise)
  • Early recognition of neurogenic shock prevents missed diagnosis

Spinal Anatomy Review
Building Your Clinical Foundation
Prior Experience Bridge: Think about the last time you intubated or managed a difficult airway in a trauma patient. Your decision to maintain in-line stabilization was already applying this anatomy — now let's understand why.

🔢 Vertebral Column (33 Vertebrae)

  • Cervical (C1–C7): 7 vertebrae — most mobile, most vulnerable
  • Thoracic (T1–T12): 12 vertebrae — rib attachments provide stability
  • Lumbar (L1–L5): 5 vertebrae — large, weight-bearing
  • Sacral (S1–S5): 5 fused vertebrae (sacrum)
  • Coccygeal (Co1–Co4): 4 fused (coccyx)

🧠 Spinal Cord Landmarks

  • Originates at the base of the brain (medulla oblongata)
  • Runs through cervical and thoracic spine
  • Terminates at L1–L2 in adults, L3 in infants
  • Below L2 → Cauda equina ("horse's tail") — nerve roots, not cord
  • Clinical significance: Cauda equina injuries may be incomplete/recoverable

⚡ Anterior vs. Posterior Elements

  • Anterior: Vertebral bodies + intervertebral discs + longitudinal ligaments
  • Posterior: Neural arch, spinous processes, facet joints
  • Anterior elements provide compressive strength
  • Posterior elements protect the cord and guide motion

🗺️ Key Dermatome Landmarks

C2–C4
Collar of sensation — neck to below clavicles
T4
Nipple line
T10
Umbilicus (belly button)
S1
Soles of the feet

Most Common Injury Levels — Know These Cold

Clinical Pearl: The most frequently injured spinal regions in descending order are: C5–C7, then C1–C2, then T12–L2. This distribution reflects the highest-mobility regions. C5–C7 injuries are disproportionately common in diving accidents.

Mechanisms of Spinal Injury
Forces → Pathology → Clinical Presentation
Problem-Centered Lens: You don't memorize mechanisms — you recognize them at scene. When you see the deformed steering wheel, the pool with the shallow end, the football tackle, your mind automatically runs this analysis.
MechanismForce DirectionTypical InjuryClassic Scene
Axial LoadingVertical compression along spinal columnBurst fractures, wedge-shaped compression fracturesDiving into shallow water (head hits bottom), cave-in, fall landing on feet/head
HyperflexionAnterior flexion beyond normal ROMWedge-shaped compression fractures, cord compressionHead-on MVC — head flies forward; whiplash forward component
HyperextensionPosterior extension beyond normal ROMLigament injury, cord stretching, posterior element fractureRear-end MVC — head snaps backward; fall with face strike

DistractionLongitudinal stretching — pulling apartCord laceration, vertebral separation, ligament ruptureHanging, high-velocity ejection, sudden halt with momentum

Common Fracture Patterns

🔺 Wedge-Shaped Compression Fractures

  • Result of hyperflexion + compression
  • Anterior vertebral body is crushed
  • Generally more stable than teardrop
  • Most common in cervical and thoracolumbar junction

💧 Teardrop Fractures

  • Result of severe hyperflexion + compression
  • Highly unstable injuries
  • Fragment driven posteriorly into canal
  • High association with complete SCI
  • Named for the triangular fragment shape

🔀 Subluxation

  • Partial dislocation of vertebral bodies
  • Canal is narrowed → cord at risk
  • May be "self-reducing" — initially appears normal on plain film
  • High index of suspicion required

Spinal Cord Injury Classification
Complete vs. Incomplete Lesions
Primary vs. Secondary Injury — Critical Distinction: Primary injury occurs at the moment of impact — you can't undo this. Secondary injury (swelling, ischemia, bony fragment movement, hypoxia, hypotension) IS PREVENTABLE. Your interventions target secondary injury exclusively.

Autonomic Dysfunction — T6 and Above

Why T6? The major sympathetic splanchnic nerves originate between T5–T9. Injury at or above T6 disrupts the entire sympathetic outflow to cardiovascular and thermoregulatory systems.

❤️ Cardiovascular

  • Bradycardia (loss of sympathetic cardiac fibers)
  • Hypotension (loss of vasomotor tone / vasodilation)
  • In neurogenic shock: bradycardia + hypotension (unlike hemorrhagic shock where HR ↑)

🌡️ Thermoregulatory

  • Loss of sweating and shivering below injury
  • Poikilothermia — patient takes on ambient temperature
  • Monitor temperature in cold environments

🔴 Other Autonomic

  • Priapism — sustained penile erection (T6 and above)
  • Loss of bowel and bladder control
  • In field: priapism = strong SCI indicator in male trauma patient

Incomplete Cord Syndromes — The Three You Must Know

🔵 Central Cord Syndrome

Most common incomplete cord syndrome. Results from hyperextension injury (especially in patients with pre-existing cervical spondylosis / stenosis). Affects central portion of cord — the motor tracts serving upper extremities are medial.

  • Upper extremity weakness > lower extremity weakness
  • Hands affected most severely (fine motor first to go)
  • Bladder dysfunction common
  • Variable sensory loss below injury
💡 "Hands worse than feet"

🔴 Anterior Cord Syndrome

Injury to the anterior spinal artery or direct compression of the anterior cord. Affects the corticospinal (motor) tracts and spinothalamic (pain/temp) tracts in the anterior cord.

  • Loss of motor function below injury
  • Loss of pain and temperature below injury
  • Preserved proprioception and vibration (posterior columns intact)
💡 "Motor + pain gone; proprioception spared"

🟢 Brown-Séquard Syndrome

Hemisection of the cord — half the cord is injured. Classic cause: penetrating trauma (knife, GSW). Creates a paradoxical presentation across both sides of the body.

  • Ipsilateral: Motor paralysis (corticospinal) + proprioception loss
  • Contralateral: Pain and temperature loss (spinothalamic crosses)
💡 "Opposite sides — opposite deficits"

Spinal Assessment in the Field
Clinical Criteria + MOI = Decision Framework

Clinical Criteria for SCI — Apply ALL of These

Key Principle: ANY single criterion present + positive MOI = Spinal Motion Restriction indicated. The absence of symptoms does NOT rule out injury if the MOI is significant.

Field Neurologic Exam — Quick Reference

💪 Upper Extremity Motor Testing

  • C7: Have patient extend wrist against resistance
  • T1: Have patient spread fingers against resistance
  • Compare bilateral — asymmetry is significant
  • Grip strength assessment (squeeze your fingers)

🦵 Lower Extremity Motor Testing

  • L5: Dorsiflexion of foot and great toe
  • S1–S2: Plantar flexion of foot (push down like a gas pedal)
  • Test both sides simultaneously if possible

👋 Sensory Testing

  • Light touch to each hand and each foot (eyes closed)
  • Mark dermatome level if deficit found
  • Babinski sign: stroke outer sole → great toe bends UP = abnormal in adults
  • Priapism = involuntary indicator of SCI

Spinal Motion Restriction (SMR) — Management Framework
Evidence-Based Approach (Post-2013 Consensus)
Paradigm Shift: The language has changed from "spinal immobilization" to Spinal Motion Restriction (SMR). Long backboards are no longer the default — they are a tool, not a reflex. The goal is minimizing unnecessary movement, not achieving zero movement.

Manual In-Line Stabilization (MILS) — The Foundation

MILS is initiated as soon as potential SCI is recognized. Applied WITHOUT traction on the head. Maintained until mechanical devices are in place.

CONTRAINDICATIONS to MILS / Returning to Neutral Position:

🚫 Absolute Contraindications

  • Resistance to movement
  • Neck muscle spasm / rigidity
  • Increased pain with movement
  • New or worsening neurologic deficits during movement
  • Airway or ventilation compromise
  • Severe head misalignment from body axis (rare)

📦 Device Selection Guide

  • Cervical collar: First device applied; does NOT immobilize alone
  • Scoop stretcher: Preferred for moving patients to stretcher
  • Vacuum splint: Good alternative for immobilization
  • Long backboard: Selected situations only; NOT for penetrating trauma
  • KED (Kendrick): Extrication from confined spaces

⚠️ Penetrating Trauma Rule

  • NO role for SMR in penetrating spinal trauma
  • Research shows significantly higher mortality with SMR in penetrating trauma
  • Apply cervical collar ONLY if secondary blunt MOI exists
  • Prioritize rapid transport over spinal precautions

Special Populations

👶 Pediatric Considerations

  • Padding under shoulders to maintain neutral position (larger occiput)
  • Do NOT transport in car seat if SCI suspected
  • Use appropriate pediatric stabilization device
  • Same SMR criteria as adults apply
  • Torticollis in children = SMR indicator

⛑️ Helmet Issues

  • Full-face helmets: Remove early to access airway
  • Sports helmets + shoulder pads: Remove together or not at all (if both fit well)
  • Firm-fitting helmet may provide additional cervical support
  • Two-rescuer technique required for helmet removal

🏊 Diving Incidents

  • C5–C7 axial loading is the classic mechanism
  • Supine immobilization in water if patient is still submerged
  • Float patient to edge → extract with spinal precautions
  • Hypothermia risk: minimize time in water

Capstone Synthesis: Spinal Shock vs. Neurogenic Shock vs. Autonomic Hyperreflexia
Three presentations — one common cause — different management
Characteristic Spinal Shock Neurogenic Shock Autonomic Hyperreflexia
DefinitionTemporary loss of all spinal reflex activity below injuryLoss of sympathetic vasomotor control → vasodilation + bradycardiaExaggerated sympathetic response in chronic SCI patients to stimulus below injury
TimingAcute — immediately post-injuryAcute — immediately post-injuryWeeks to years after injury (after spinal shock resolves)
Injury LevelAny level of SCIT6 and above (loss of splanchnic sympathetics)T6 and above (chronic)
HRVariable (flaccid paralysis dominates)↓ Bradycardia↓ Bradycardia (30–40 bpm)
BPMay be hypotensive↓ Hypotension↑↑ Paroxysmal hypertension (up to 300 mmHg)
Key FeatureFlaccid paralysis + loss of autonomic function (priapism, hypotension, vasodilation)Hypotension + bradycardia WITHOUT compensatory tachycardia (unlike hemorrhagic)Severe pounding headache + flushing above injury + pallor/piloerection below injury
TriggerPrimary SCIPrimary SCI at T6+Noxious stimulus below injury (full bladder most common)

Critical Differentiator: In neurogenic shock, you see bradycardia + hypotension together. In hemorrhagic shock, hypotension triggers compensatory tachycardia. If your trauma patient is hypotensive AND bradycardic → think neurogenic shock until proven otherwise. Do not reflexively bolus fluids as you would for hemorrhage.

Nontraumatic Spinal Conditions
The other spinal emergencies you'll encounter

🔴 Low Back Pain

  • Most common musculoskeletal complaint in EMS
  • Vast majority: mechanical (muscle strain)
  • Red flags: bowel/bladder dysfunction, saddle anesthesia, fever, progressive deficit = emergent (Cauda equina syndrome)

🔵 Herniated Intervertebral Disc

  • Nucleus pulposus herniates through annulus fibrosus
  • Compresses nerve root → radicular pain
  • Classic L4–L5 or L5–S1; cervical C6–C7
  • Sciatica = most common presentation

🟡 Degenerative Conditions

  • Spondylosis: Degenerative arthritis of spine (osteophytes)
  • Degenerative Disc Disease: Loss of disc height, flexibility, shock absorption
  • Predispose to central cord syndrome with minor trauma

⚫ Spinal Cord Tumors

  • Primary (rare) or metastatic (common — breast, lung, prostate, kidney)
  • Progressive neurologic deficit over days to weeks
  • Back pain worse at night = red flag
  • Pathologic fracture risk with metastatic disease
