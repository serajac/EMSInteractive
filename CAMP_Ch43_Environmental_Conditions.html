Why this chapter, and why now

Environmental emergencies are time-critical, often misread, and disproportionately fatal when prehospital decision-making is slow or wrong. Drowning is the leading cause of death in children ages 1 to 4. Heatstroke kills within hours of presentation. The hypothermic heart will not respond to the drugs your protocols normally lean on. A diver pulled from the water with chest pain may be having an arterial gas embolism, not a STEMI — and the wrong position will kill them. This module turns each of those clinical realities into a decision you can rehearse before it lands in your truck.

1 · Thermoregulation — The Set Point Behind Every Decision
Why 98.6°F matters and what the body does to defend it
▼

Bedside Frame

Before you can decide "is this heat illness" or "is this hypothermia," you must own one fact: the human body is a chemical reactor that runs reliably only within a narrow temperature window. Push it 6°F up or 6°F down and proteins denature, enzymes stall, and the central nervous system goes offline. The body knows this and fights hard to defend the set point — and the way it fights tells you what is happening.

Core Physiology

The posterior hypothalamus is the body's thermostat. Its set point is approximately 98.6°F (37°C). The hypothalamus receives temperature input from peripheral skin receptors and from the temperature of blood circulating through it, and adjusts heat production and heat loss to defend that set point.

Four Mechanisms of Heat Loss — memorize cold
  • Radiation — direct transfer of heat to cooler surrounding objects. Largest source of resting heat loss.
  • Conduction — direct contact transfer. Water conducts heat ~32× faster than air at the same temperature — this is the single most important number in cold-water drowning physiology.
  • Convection — heat carried away by moving air or water (wind, current).
  • Evaporation — sweat phase change. The body's primary defense in heat stress, but it fails when ambient humidity is high.

Why It Matters in Your Truck

If you understand which mechanism is dominating, you know which intervention works. A submerged patient is losing heat by conduction and convection — your job is to break the contact (remove from water, dry, insulate). A heatstroke patient in 100% humidity has lost evaporation as a defense — and is now losing the ability to dump heat at all. A frostbite patient on a metal stretcher in a cold ambulance is conducting heat into the metal.

Field Pearl Your first rapid assessment in any environmental call is two-pronged: where is heat going, and which compensatory mechanism is exhausted?

2 · Hyperthermia — Cramps, Exhaustion, Stroke
Three diagnoses on one continuum, one of them a true emergency
▼

Why You Care

Heatstroke has a mortality of 10–80% depending on time-to-cooling. Every minute of delay above 104°F core temperature increases neuronal injury. The discriminator from heat exhaustion — altered mental status — is also the most commonly missed sign in athletes and laborers because providers attribute the confusion to "fatigue" or "dehydration."

The Continuum

StageCore TempMental StatusSkinField Action
Heat crampsNormalNormalSweatyRest, oral electrolytes, stretch
Heat exhaustionUp to 103°FIntact (may feel weak/dizzy)Pale, cool, diaphoreticCool environment, oral or IV fluids, monitor
Heatstroke≥104°F (40°C)ALTERED — confusion, seizure, comaHot; classic = dry, exertional = often sweatyAGGRESSIVE COOLING NOW
Critical Discriminator Altered mental status with a hot body = heatstroke until proven otherwise. Do not wait for the rectal temperature to confirm. Begin cooling on suspicion.

Classic vs Exertional Heatstroke

  • Classic (passive) heatstroke — elderly, chronically ill, no air conditioning during a heat wave. Often dry, hot skin (sweating mechanism failed). Slow onset over days.
  • Exertional heatstroke — young athletes, military recruits, laborers in heat/humidity. May still be sweating profusely. Rapid onset over hours. Higher rhabdomyolysis and DIC risk.

Prehospital Treatment of Heatstroke

Active Cooling Protocol
  1. Move to cool environment immediately. Strip clothing.
  2. Ice-water immersion when available is the most effective cooling method for exertional heatstroke. If immersion is not feasible: ice packs to neck, axilla, groin; cold wet sheets; fans for evaporative cooling.
  3. Establish IV access. Administer cool isotonic crystalloid 20 mL/kg boluses, reassess; titrate to perfusion.
  4. Treat seizures with benzodiazepines (lorazepam, diazepam, or midazolam per protocol).
  5. Continuous core temperature monitoring. Stop active cooling at approximately 102°F (38.9°C) to prevent overshoot hypothermia.
  6. Anticipate cardiac dysrhythmias, rhabdomyolysis, and acute kidney injury — destination should be a facility capable of ICU-level care.
Field Error to Avoid Antipyretics (acetaminophen, ibuprofen) do not work in heatstroke and may worsen coagulopathy and renal injury. The hypothalamic set point is normal — the problem is excess heat the body cannot dump, not a fever to suppress.

3 · Hypothermia — The Heart That Won't Respond to Drugs
Mild → moderate → severe → profound, and the cardiac arrest you cannot give up on
▼

Why You Care

The hypothermic patient breaks several rules you've built your practice on. Their heart will not respond to epinephrine the way you expect. Their bradycardia is protective and should not be paced. Their pulse may be undetectable for a full minute. And the dictum is "no one is dead until they are warm and dead" — survivors with full neurologic recovery from core temperatures below 60°F have been documented.

Definition and Classification

Hypothermia is core temperature below 95°F (35°C). Sanders divides it into four stages:

ClassCore TempClinicalCardiac
Mild90–95°F (32–35°C)Shivering vigorous, alert, "umbles" (stumbles, mumbles, fumbles)Tachycardia early, then sinus brady
Moderate82–90°F (28–32°C)Shivering ceases, lethargy, dysarthria, paradoxical undressing possibleAtrial fibrillation common, J/Osborn waves appear
Severe68–82°F (20–28°C)Stuporous to comatose, fixed dilated pupils possible, rigidVF risk extreme; spontaneous arrhythmias
Profound<68°F (<20°C)Apparent death, asystoleAsystole; CPR with prolonged resuscitation
ECG Finding to Recognize J wave (Osborn wave) — a positive deflection at the junction of the QRS complex and the ST segment. Classically appears at core temperatures below ~32°C. Most prominent in leads II, V5, V6.

The 60-Second Pulse Check

In a hypothermic patient, peripheral vasoconstriction and bradycardia can make pulses imperceptible. Palpate the carotid for a full 60 seconds before declaring pulselessness and starting CPR. Starting compressions on a bradycardic but perfusing hypothermic heart can convert the rhythm to VF.

Cardiac Arrest in Hypothermia — What's Different

Drug Pharmacology Changes Below approximately 86°F (30°C), the hypothermic myocardium is largely unresponsive to pharmacologic intervention. Per current resuscitation guidance, withhold ACLS drugs and limit defibrillation attempts (often a single shock, then resume CPR and rewarming) until core temperature rises. Drugs given to a frozen heart will accumulate and produce toxicity once perfusion resumes during rewarming.
Sinus Bradycardia is Protective A bradycardic rhythm in moderate-to-severe hypothermia represents the heart's adaptation to reduced metabolic demand. Do not pace. Do not give atropine. Rewarming will resolve it.

Field Treatment

  1. Move to warm environment, gently. Rough handling can precipitate VF.
  2. Remove wet clothing by cutting it off. Insulate with warm dry blankets — head, neck, trunk especially.
  3. Passive external rewarming for mild cases. Active external rewarming (warm packs to trunk axilla groin) for moderate. Active core rewarming (warm humidified O2, warmed IV fluids 42°C) for severe.
  4. Handle gently; avoid CPR if any organized rhythm with pulse.
  5. Transport to facility with extracorporeal rewarming capability if cardiac arrest or profound hypothermia.
Afterdrop Returning warm peripheral blood to the cold core can transiently lower core temperature and worsen the patient. This is why active external rewarming is applied to the trunk preferentially, not the extremities first.

4 · Frostbite — Tissue That Crystallized
Frostnip, superficial, and deep — and the rewarming decision you cannot reverse
▼

Why You Care

Frostbite outcomes depend almost entirely on how the prehospital and early ED phase is managed. Premature rewarming followed by refreezing causes worse tissue loss than continued cold. Massage and rubbing — what well-meaning bystanders do — drives ice crystals through cell membranes and shears tissue.

Pathophysiology in One Sentence

Cold causes vasoconstriction, then ice-crystal formation in tissue, then cell membrane disruption, then microvascular thrombosis on rewarming — the second wave of injury is reperfusion, not the freezing itself.

Two-Tier Field Classification

StageFindingsTissue Depth
FrostnipPale, numb, tingling skin. Reversible with warming. No tissue loss.Surface only
Superficial frostbiteWhite or grayish skin, soft underneath when palpated. Clear blisters on rewarming. Painful thawing.Skin and subcutaneous
Deep frostbiteHard, woody, white-to-black tissue. Hemorrhagic blisters or no blisters at all on rewarming. Anesthetic.Muscle, tendon, bone

Field Treatment

Do
  • Remove from cold environment.
  • Remove wet, constricting clothing and jewelry.
  • Treat hypothermia first if present — core before periphery.
  • Pad and protect the affected part. Splint without compression.
  • Transport. Definitive rewarming (warm-water immersion at 37–39°C) is performed in hospital where the part can be kept warm afterward.
Do NOT
  • Do not rub or massage the affected area.
  • Do not rewarm in the field if there is any chance the part can refreeze before definitive care.
  • Do not allow the patient to walk on a frostbitten foot if any other transport is available.
  • Do not use dry heat (heater vents, fires, hand warmers directly on tissue).

5 · Drowning — Hypoxia, Hypercapnia, Acidosis
The #1 cause of death in children 1–4 years old
▼

Why You Care

Drowning is the leading cause of unintentional death in children ages 1 to 4 in the United States. The pathophysiology is hypoxia first, aspiration second — meaning a child pulled from a pool who never appeared to swallow water can still die from drowning. The intervention with the highest survival benefit is ventilation, delivered fast and well.

Modern Definition

Drowning is defined as the process of experiencing respiratory impairment from submersion or immersion in liquid. Outcomes are now classified as: drowning with morbidity, drowning without morbidity, or drowning with death. The terms "near-drowning," "wet" and "dry" drowning are obsolete.

Sequence of Events

  1. Initial breath-hold — voluntary, lasts seconds to a minute.
  2. Laryngospasm — water hits the larynx, vocal cords clamp shut. Hypoxia and hypercapnia accumulate.
  3. Loss of consciousness from hypoxia.
  4. Aspiration — laryngospasm relaxes; water enters the lungs. Surfactant is washed out, alveoli collapse, V/Q mismatch and pulmonary edema follow.
  5. Cardiac arrest — usually from hypoxia, presenting most commonly as bradyasystolic arrest, not VF.
Heat Loss in Water Water conducts heat 32× faster than air at the same temperature. Even "warm" water (75°F) will produce hypothermia in prolonged submersion. Cold-water drowning patients with prolonged submersion have a known small but real chance of intact survival because the cold protects the brain — this drives the "warm and dead" doctrine for drowning arrest as well.

Modified ABCDE for Drowning

Resuscitation Priority

Drowning is a hypoxic arrest. The standard adult cardiac arrest sequence (compressions first) is modified:

  • Airway — open. Suction if obstructed.
  • Breathing — give 5 initial rescue breaths before compressions if a non-breathing victim is encountered. This is the key deviation from BLS-as-usual and reflects the hypoxic etiology.
  • Circulation — start compressions if no pulse after the rescue breaths.
  • Disability — assume cervical spine injury only if mechanism (diving into shallow water, water-skiing, surfing) suggests it. Routine c-spine immobilization in all drowning victims is not warranted and can delay airway management.
  • Exposure / Environment — remove wet clothing, prevent further heat loss, manage hypothermia.
Heimlich Maneuver — NO Abdominal thrusts are not indicated to "clear" water from the lungs. They delay ventilation, increase aspiration risk, and have no evidence of benefit. Open the airway and ventilate.

Transport Decision

Per AHA guidance, all drowning victims who have required any resuscitation, including rescue breathing, should be transported to a hospital for evaluation. Pulmonary edema and ARDS can develop hours after a seemingly benign event.

Local Frame For your service area: James River, Nansemond River, Chesapeake Bay tributaries, residential pools. Pediatric pool drownings cluster in summer. Adult open-water drownings frequently involve alcohol. Both populations benefit equally from a paramedic who delivers ventilation fast.

6 · Diving Emergencies & Dysbarism — Boyle, Dalton, Henry
Barotrauma on descent, expanded gas on ascent, dissolved gas after the dive
▼

Why You Care

A diver who surfaces and then collapses with chest pain, hemiparesis, or sudden confusion is having an arterial gas embolism (AGE) until proven otherwise — and your positioning of that patient changes their neurologic outcome. Decompression sickness ("the bends") can present up to 48 hours later, often misattributed to musculoskeletal pain. Both conditions are treated with hyperbaric oxygen, and the closest chamber may not be the closest hospital.

Three Gas Laws You Already Know

LawWhat it saysWhat it does to a diver
Boyle'sP × V = constant. Pressure up → volume down, and vice versa.On descent, gas in body cavities (middle ear, sinuses, bowel, lungs if breath-holding) compresses → barotrauma of descent (squeeze). On ascent, gas expands → barotrauma of ascent. Holding the breath on ascent ruptures alveoli.
Dalton'sTotal pressure = sum of partial pressures of constituent gases.At depth, partial pressures of O₂ and N₂ both rise. High pO₂ → oxygen toxicity. High pN₂ → nitrogen narcosis ("rapture of the deep").
Henry'sThe amount of gas dissolved in a liquid is proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid.At depth, more nitrogen dissolves into blood and tissues. On ascent, if too rapid, dissolved nitrogen comes out of solution as bubbles — decompression sickness.
Numbers to Memorize Atmospheric pressure at sea level: 1 atm = 14.7 psi = 760 mmHg. Every 33 ft of seawater adds another atmosphere.

Pulmonary Over-Pressurization Syndrome (POPS)

Caused by ascending while breath-holding (panicked diver, equipment failure). Expanding alveolar gas ruptures alveoli, producing pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum, subcutaneous emphysema, and — most lethally — arterial gas embolism (AGE).

Arterial Gas Embolism (AGE)

Time-Critical Recognition AGE typically presents within minutes of surfacing with sudden focal neurologic deficits — hemiparesis, vision loss, confusion, seizure — that mimic stroke. May present with chest pain that mimics cardiac etiology. Hallmark: onset moments after surfacing.
Field Management of AGE
  • High-flow oxygen by non-rebreather (100% FiO₂) — washes nitrogen from the bubble.
  • Position: left lateral recumbent (Durant's maneuver) traditionally taught to keep gas in right ventricle/apex; modern practice favors supine with no head-down tilt. Follow your local protocol.
  • IV access, isotonic crystalloid for hydration.
  • Transport to a facility with hyperbaric oxygen (HBOT) capability. The Divers Alert Network (DAN) Emergency Hotline can advise: 919-684-9111.

Decompression Sickness (DCS, "The Bends")

Onset is delayed — most cases within 1 hour, but can be up to 48 hours after the dive. Type I (musculoskeletal): joint pain ("bends"), skin rash. Type II (neurologic/pulmonary): paresthesias, weakness, vertigo, "the chokes" (cough, dyspnea), spinal cord involvement (paraparesis). Treatment is the same: high-flow O₂, hydration, transport for HBOT.

Nitrogen Narcosis — The Martini Rule

At depth, increased pN₂ produces a CNS-depressant effect resembling alcohol intoxication. Rule of thumb: each 50 feet of depth is equivalent to one martini on an empty stomach. Reverses on ascent — usually a dive incident, not a prehospital call.

7 · High-Altitude Illness — AMS, HACE, HAPE
Three syndromes on a single hypoxic spectrum
▼

Why You Care

Less common in your immediate service area, but increasingly relevant as patients fly to ski resorts, hike Blue Ridge and Appalachian high points, and travel internationally. Altitude illness is also a board-relevant topic and a reasonable assumption for any unexplained dyspnea or confusion in a patient who has gained altitude in the past 24–72 hours. The single intervention that always works: descend.

Threshold

High-altitude illness can occur above approximately 8,200 feet (2,500 m), with risk and severity rising with altitude and rate of ascent.

Three Syndromes

SyndromeOnsetHallmarksSeverity
AMS — Acute Mountain Sickness4–6 hours after ascentHeadache + one of: anorexia/nausea/vomiting, fatigue, dizziness, sleep disturbanceMild–moderate; may progress
HACE — High-Altitude Cerebral EdemaHours to days, often progression of AMSAtaxia, altered mental status, severe headache, vomiting, eventual comaMost severe; life-threatening
HAPE — High-Altitude Pulmonary EdemaUsually 2–4 days after ascentProgressive cough, dyspnea, hypoxia, hyperpnea, weakness above 8,000 ft. Pink frothy sputum late.Life-threatening
Pathophysiology — One Sentence Each

AMS/HACE: hypoxic cerebral vasodilation → increased cerebral blood flow and edema → headache, ataxia, ALOC.

HAPE: hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction is uneven → high pressure in patent capillary beds → noncardiogenic pulmonary edema, with leukotriene release contributing.

Treatment Hierarchy

Field Approach
  1. Descend. Even 1,000–3,000 ft of descent is often curative. This is the single most important intervention for HACE and HAPE.
  2. Oxygen — supplemental O₂ corrects hypoxia and bridges to descent.
  3. Pharmacology:
    • Acetazolamide — prevention and treatment of AMS; accelerates acclimatization.
    • Dexamethasone — reduces cerebral edema in HACE.
    • Nifedipine — pulmonary vasodilation in HAPE.
  4. Portable hyperbaric chamber (Gamow bag) when descent is delayed.
Pediatric High-Risk Groups Children with respiratory infections or chronic respiratory disease, congenital heart disease, Down syndrome, full-term infants under 6 weeks of age, and premature infants with respiratory insufficiency — all are at higher risk for high-altitude illness.

Case Analysis

Case 1 · Heat

The Marine Recruit at Parris Island

Dispatch: 0930, July, you are responding to a military training facility for "heat casualty." Ambient 92°F, humidity 84%.

On scene: 19-year-old male, 5'11", 195 lb, in PT uniform. Was running the obstacle course when he became confused mid-run, stumbled, and dropped. Now somnolent, mumbling incoherently. Skin is hot and still diaphoretic. Rectal temp 105.1°F. HR 158 sinus tach, BP 92/58, SpO₂ 96% on room air, RR 24. Capillary glucose 88.

Your decision: What is this, and what is your single highest-yield next intervention?

Diagnosis: Exertional heatstroke. The discriminator is altered mental status with a hot body — sweating may persist in exertional heatstroke, so dry skin is not required for the diagnosis. Temperature is well above the 104°F threshold.

Highest-yield intervention: Aggressive cooling — preferably ice-water immersion if available, or ice packs to neck/axilla/groin plus cold wet sheets and fanning. Every minute of delay above 104°F adds neuronal injury. Establish IV access, give cool isotonic crystalloid 20 mL/kg, monitor for seizures, plan a destination with ICU capability for likely rhabdomyolysis and renal injury. Stop active cooling at approximately 102°F to avoid overshoot.

What junior providers get wrong here: attributing the confusion to dehydration, withholding cooling pending a "confirmed temperature," or giving acetaminophen.

Case 2 · Cold

The Lost Hunter Found at Dawn

Dispatch: 0640, January, search-and-rescue has located a missing hunter in the woods. Overnight low was 28°F. He was found supine, lightly dressed in a flannel jacket, last seen approximately 14 hours prior.

On scene: 58-year-old male, unresponsive to voice. Cold to touch, rigid posture. Pupils 4 mm, sluggish. You palpate the carotid for a full 60 seconds and feel a slow, weak pulse around 28. ECG monitor shows sinus bradycardia with prominent J waves at the QRS-ST junction. SpO₂ unreadable. Estimated core temp by tympanic 78°F.

Your decision: Does this patient need CPR? What rewarming strategy, and what should you NOT do?

This patient does not need CPR. He has a perfusing rhythm — slow, but present, confirmed by a 60-second pulse check and consistent with the ECG. Compressions on a perfusing hypothermic heart can convert to VF. The bradycardia is protective.

Rewarming: Severe hypothermia (78°F is in the severe-to-profound range). Active core rewarming is needed: warm humidified oxygen, warmed IV fluids (42°C / 108°F isotonic), warm packs to the trunk (axilla, chest wall, groin) — not the extremities, to avoid afterdrop. Handle gently. Transport to a facility capable of extracorporeal rewarming.

Do NOT: pace the bradycardia, give atropine, give epinephrine. The hypothermic myocardium below ~86°F is unresponsive to drugs and accumulating doses become toxic on rewarming. Do not rub the extremities.

Doctrine: "No one is dead until they are warm and dead." Even with apparent death, full neurologic survival from temperatures below 60°F has been documented.

Case 3 · Water

The Toddler in the Backyard Pool

Dispatch: 1715, June, "child not breathing, pool." Mother performing CPR per dispatcher instruction.

On scene: 22-month-old female, found floating face-down in residential pool. Estimated submersion 3–5 minutes. On arrival: apneic, pulseless, cyanotic, asystolic on monitor. Skin cool but not cold. No obvious trauma; no diving mechanism.

Your decision: What is the resuscitation sequence? Do you immobilize the c-spine? What is your transport disposition if you achieve ROSC?

Sequence: Drowning is hypoxic arrest. Modified ABCDE: open airway, give 5 initial rescue breaths before starting compressions, then standard pediatric BLS/ALS. Bradyasystolic arrest is the most common rhythm — VF is uncommon in pediatric drowning.

C-spine: No mechanism suggests cervical injury (no diving, no shallow-water impact). Routine c-spine immobilization in this drowning patient is not warranted and would delay airway management. Reserve immobilization for cases where mechanism — diving, water-skiing, surfing — suggests cervical trauma.

Transport: Per AHA guidance, all drowning patients requiring any resuscitation, including isolated rescue breaths, should be transported to the hospital. Pulmonary edema and ARDS can develop hours after a seemingly recovered event. Even if you achieve ROSC and the child appears well, transport.

Heimlich: Not indicated. Abdominal thrusts will not "drain" the lungs and will delay ventilation.
