Core anatomy & mechanism

Andragogy 3 · Prior Experience You already know bone, joint, and gross anatomy from your EMT and A&P coursework. Here we are going to use that foundation to predict injury — anatomy is no longer a fact to memorize, it is a map to read on scene.
ABone biology — why bone bleeds, breaks, and heals the way it does
▾

Architecture

Bone is living, vascular, neurally innervated tissue. Cortical (compact) bone forms the dense outer shaft and resists bending; cancellous (trabecular) bone fills the metaphysis and epiphysis with a lattice that absorbs compression. The periosteum is a richly vascular and innervated outer membrane — strip it and you destroy bone's blood supply; tear it and the patient feels deep, sickening pain.

Why this matters in the field

  • Bone bleeds significantly because cancellous bone houses sinusoidal capillaries and marrow vessels with poor ability to vasoconstrict.
  • Pediatric bone bends before it breaks (greenstick, torus, plastic deformation) because immature cortex is more porous and the periosteum is thicker.
  • Geriatric bone snaps with minimal force because osteoporotic cortex loses tensile strength — a "trip-and-fall" fracture is a real fracture.
  • Bone heals through hematoma — splinting that controls motion preserves the fracture hematoma and is the patient's first contribution to bony union.
Clinical pearl
The "feel" of crepitus is broken cortex grinding on broken cortex, with periosteum dragged across both. Producing it deliberately is malpractice. Hearing it once is unforgettable; producing it intentionally tells the patient you don't know what you're doing.
BJoint structure & the geometry of dislocation
▾

Synovial joints (knee, hip, shoulder, elbow) are the ones that dislocate. Each is a balance between mobility (provided by lax capsule, ball-and-socket geometry, and accessory motion) and stability (provided by ligaments, joint congruity, and surrounding muscle). Hip = most stable, hardest to dislocate, requires the most force. Shoulder = least stable, easiest to dislocate, often atraumatic in recurrent cases.

Subluxation vs. dislocation

A subluxation is partial loss of articular contact that often spontaneously reduces. A dislocation is complete loss of contact — the joint is locked out of position and will not move through normal range. Both stretch capsule, ligaments, and surrounding neurovascular structures.

DFracture nomenclature — speaking the language
▾

Open vs. closed

An open ("compound") fracture communicates with the outside through any break in the skin — a dime-sized puncture from a bone end is open, even if the bone is no longer protruding. Treat as contaminated. A closed fracture is intact skin over the injury.

Complete vs. incomplete

  • Complete — disruption through both cortices.
  • Incomplete — one cortex intact (greenstick, torus/buckle, plastic deformation in pediatrics).

Pattern (the line of fracture)

Transverse
Perpendicular to long axis — direct blow
Oblique
Diagonal — angulated force
Spiral
Curving — twisting force; child abuse red flag in non-ambulatory child
Comminuted
≥3 fragments — high-energy
Segmental
Two complete fractures isolating a middle segment
Impacted
Driven into itself — fall from height onto axial bone
Avulsion
Tendon/ligament tears off bone fragment
Pathologic
Through abnormal bone (tumor, cyst, osteoporosis)

Salter–Harris (pediatric only)

Fractures involving the growth plate (physis), graded I–V. Higher grades carry higher risk of growth arrest and limb-length discrepancy. You don't grade them in the field, but you do recognize that any pediatric fracture near a joint is presumed to involve the physis until imaging proves otherwise.

Gustilo classification of open fractures

(Documentation pearl, not a field grade.) Type I <1 cm clean wound; Type II 1–10 cm without extensive soft-tissue damage; Type III >10 cm or extensive soft-tissue/neurovascular involvement. Type III carries the highest infection and amputation risk — describe what you saw, do not estimate the grade.

EThe 6 P's — neurovascular assessment as a verb, not a checkbox
▾

Document the 6 P's before any movement, splinting, or transport — and again after every intervention. Documenting "intact" once means you don't know what changed.

Pain
Out of proportion is the earliest sign of compartment syndrome
Pallor
Pale, cool extremity = vascular compromise
Paresthesia
Tingling, numbness — early nerve sign
Paralysis
Inability to move — late and ominous
Pulselessness
Absent or asymmetric distal pulse — late vascular sign
Poikilothermia
Limb takes ambient temperature — vascular compromise
Warning
Pulses can be present in a limb that is dying. Pulses last; tissue does not. Pain out of proportion, tense compartment, and paresthesia precede pulse loss in compartment syndrome by hours.

Pelvic ring fractures — the silent exsanguination

The problem

The pelvis is a vascular crime scene waiting to happen. Disruption of the ring opens a retroperitoneal space that can hold 2–4 liters of blood without producing a single drop visible to you. By the time the abdomen looks distended, the patient is in late shock. Mortality for unstable pelvic fractures with hemodynamic instability is reported at 30–50%.

Three main patterns

Anterior–Posterior (Open Book)
Frontal compression "opens" the symphysis & SI joints. Massive arterial & venous bleeding. Single highest mortality. Mechanism: head-on MVC, motorcycle, ped struck.
Lateral Compression
Most common. T-bone MVC, fall onto side. Bleeding usually less than APC but can still be life-threatening. Look for crush injury and bladder/urethral involvement.
Vertical Shear
Axial loading drives one hemipelvis upward (fall from height, ejection). Highest neurologic injury. Limb shortening + asymmetric pelvis.

Field assessment — gentle & once

  • Look — leg-length discrepancy, asymmetric rotation, perineal/scrotal hematoma, blood at urethral meatus, flank ecchymosis (Grey-Turner).
  • Palpate — single, gentle bilateral compression of the iliac wings once. Pain, instability, or crepitus = positive. Do not rock the pelvis. Repeated examination dislodges clot and worsens bleeding.
  • Suspect — any patient with the right mechanism + hypotension + femur or back pain has a pelvic injury until imaging says otherwise.

Pelvic binder — apply early, apply correctly

  1. Indicated for any suspected unstable pelvic ring injury, especially with hemodynamic instability.
  2. Place at the level of the greater trochanters — not the iliac crests. A binder placed too high does not close the ring.
  3. Internally rotate the lower extremities (knees/feet together) before tightening — reduces pelvic volume.
  4. Tension to manufacturer specification or until the symphysis closes; over-tension can cause overcompression of lateral compression injuries.
  5. Document distal neurovascular status before and after. Do not remove for transfer of care unless the receiving team is ready to take over hemorrhage control.
Cannot miss
  • An "open" pelvic fracture (skin, vagina, rectum, or perineum disrupted) carries mortality up to 50%. Pack any obvious external wound, transport priority one.
  • Blood at the urethral meatus, perineal hematoma, or high-riding prostate = do not insert a urinary catheter.

Femoral shaft & hip injuries

Femoral shaft fracture

The femur is the strongest bone in the body — fracturing it requires significant force, so the patient also has another injury until proven otherwise. Closed femur fracture sequesters 1,000–1,500 mL of blood; open or bilateral, more.

Findings

  • Shortened, externally rotated thigh (often)
  • Pain, swelling, deformity at midshaft
  • Tense, doughy thigh — expanding hematoma
  • Possible distal neurovascular compromise

Traction splint — when, when not, how

Indicated: isolated, closed, midshaft femur fracture without contraindications.

Contraindicated:

  • Suspected pelvic fracture
  • Hip dislocation or fracture (intracapsular, intertrochanteric, subtrochanteric)
  • Knee injury or fracture distal to the midshaft
  • Lower-leg, ankle, or foot injury that prevents ankle hitch placement
  • Partial amputation with bone-on-soft-tissue connection
  • Pediatric patients smaller than the device permits

Mechanism of benefit: realigns overlapping bone ends, reduces pain, reduces internal hemorrhage by tamponade against the femoral fascia, and prevents secondary injury to the neurovascular bundle in Hunter's canal.

Caution
Apply traction only until pain decreases, alignment is restored, and the limb feels stable — not "as much as the ratchet will give." Excess traction injures the nerve.

Hip fracture (proximal femur)

The classic geriatric mechanism: a ground-level fall, the patient cannot weight-bear, the affected leg is shortened and externally rotated, and pain localizes to the groin. Mortality at one year approaches 20–30% — not from the fracture itself, but from immobility, pneumonia, and DVT.

  • Femoral neck (intracapsular) — risk of avascular necrosis of the head; surgical urgency.
  • Intertrochanteric / subtrochanteric (extracapsular) — significant blood loss, often surgically stabilized.

Field management

  • Position of comfort — usually slightly flexed and externally rotated. Pillows under the knee.
  • Vacuum splint or scoop-and-pad. Do not apply a traction splint to a hip fracture.
  • Aggressive pain control — these patients hurt and underdosing is a real harm.
  • Always assess for the cause of the fall (syncope, stroke, dysrhythmia, sepsis) — the fracture may be the second event, not the first.

Hip dislocation

True orthopaedic emergency. Posterior dislocation (≈90%) classically results from knee-strikes-dashboard MVC: the limb is shortened, adducted, and internally rotated. The femoral head sits on the sciatic nerve. Reduction within 6 hours dramatically reduces the rate of avascular necrosis.

Time = function
  • Immobilize in position found, generous pain control, expedite transport — do not attempt field reduction.

Compartment syndrome — the limb-killer

Bleeding or edema in a closed fascial compartment raises pressure beyond capillary perfusion. Tissue ischemia causes more edema, which raises pressure further — a closed-loop failure that produces irreversible muscle and nerve death in 4–8 hours. Treatment is fasciotomy. Recognition is your job.

Risk profile

  • Long-bone fractures, especially tibia & forearm
  • Crush injuries, especially with prolonged extrication
  • High-energy soft-tissue trauma without fracture
  • Burns, especially circumferential
  • Reperfusion after vascular repair
  • Tight casts, splints, or dressings — including the one you applied

The 5 P's of compartment syndrome (in order of appearance)

1. Pain
Out of proportion to injury, worsened by passive stretch — earliest, most reliable
2. Paresthesia
Burning, tingling — early nerve sign
3. Pallor
Pale, cool, tense compartment
4. Paralysis
Late and ominous
5. Pulselessness
Latest sign — pulses last; tissue does not
Field truth
  • If you wait for pulselessness, the limb is gone. Pain out of proportion + tense compartment + paresthesia = compartment syndrome until proven otherwise.

Field actions

  1. Loosen any constrictive dressing or splint immediately, even one you placed.
  2. Position limb at heart level (not elevated, not dependent).
  3. Aggressive pain control as protocol allows.
  4. Notify the receiving facility — fasciotomy is time-critical.
  5. Document: onset of symptoms, interventions, and serial NV checks.

Amputations & near-amputations

  • Hemorrhage control first — direct pressure, then tourniquet 2–3 inches above the wound, on bare skin, tightened until bleeding stops. Mark "TQ" and the time on the patient's forehead, the device, or both.
  • Wound — saline-moistened sterile gauze, dry sterile cover, splint to prevent further injury.
  • Amputated part — wrap in moist sterile gauze, place in a sealed plastic bag, then bag on ice. Never place tissue in direct contact with ice or in water — frostbite destroys it.
  • Partial amputation — splint as a unit, do not complete the amputation. Even a thread of perfusion can preserve the limb.
  • Transport priority one to the appropriate replantation-capable trauma center.

Splinting — principles before product

  1. Expose the injury and assess distal neurovascular status before splinting.
  2. Cover open wounds with sterile dressing first.
  3. Splint the joint above and the joint below the fracture site; for joint injuries, splint the bones above and below.
  4. Pad bony prominences and any voids in the splint.
  5. Realign severely angulated long-bone fractures with gentle longitudinal traction only if there is distal neurovascular compromise — and per local protocol. Stop if you meet resistance or pain dramatically increases.
  6. For dislocations and joint injuries, splint in the position found unless distal pulse is absent and protocol authorizes a single reduction attempt.
  7. Reassess and document distal neurovascular status after splinting and at every change of care.
  8. Elevate (when permitted by injury), apply cold pack with a barrier (do not place ice directly on skin).
  9. Manage pain — splinting without analgesia is incomplete care.

Pain management — your most reliable intervention

Untreated pain is harmful: tachycardia, hypertension, increased oxygen demand, and patient-reported anxiety that worsens hemodynamics. Per protocol, options commonly include:

Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg IV/IO/IN Morphine 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO/IM Ketamine 0.1–0.3 mg/kg IV (analgesic dose) Acetaminophen IV/PO Ondansetron 4 mg IV (antiemetic)
  • Fentanyl: rapid onset (1–2 min IV), short duration, less histamine release than morphine — favored for hemodynamically borderline patients.
  • Ketamine: maintains airway reflexes and BP, effective in opioid-naïve and opioid-tolerant patients; watch for emergence at higher doses.
  • Reassess pain numerically and functionally — the patient who can tolerate the splint and answer questions is adequately treated.
  • Document analgesia given, response, and any side effects.
Caution
Hypotension is not an absolute contraindication to analgesia. It is a contraindication to over-dosing. Titrate, treat the cause of hypotension, and don't leave a patient screaming because the BP is 90.

Six interventions, in order

  1. Hemorrhage control + tourniquet readiness — open tibia/fibula fracture is contaminated and may bleed; control with direct pressure, sterile dressing, tourniquet ready proximally if pulsatile bleeding develops.
  2. Pelvic binder applied at the greater trochanters — bilateral pelvic tenderness + significant mechanism + tachycardia. Apply early, do not wait for hypotension.
  3. Recognize and act on right-leg compartment syndrome — tense calf, pain on passive stretch, paresthesia, pulses present but diminished. Loosen any constrictive dressing, position at heart level, expedite transport, alert receiving team that fasciotomy may be needed.
  4. Splint each long-bone injury appropriately — vacuum or rigid splint to right tibia (open: sterile dressing first, do not push bone back); align and splint left femur (consider traction splint only if pelvic binder placement and exam exclude pelvic ring injury — in this case the pelvic findings make a traction splint contraindicated; use a vacuum splint or rigid alignment instead).
  5. Vascular access ×2 + measured fluid + analgesia — large-bore IV/IO, permissive hypotension goal (mentation + radial pulse + SBP ~90), fentanyl titrated to effect; consider ondansetron.
  6. Reassess and document NV status of all four extremities before, during, and after interventions; every 5 minutes en route. Notify the receiving trauma center of suspected pelvis, open tibia, contralateral femur, and developing compartment syndrome.

Three time-critical issues

  1. Pelvic ring instability with developing shock — minutes-to-hours.
  2. Right-leg compartment syndrome — 4–8 hour irreversible window.
  3. Open contaminated tibial fracture — every minute increases infection risk; antibiotics and operative washout are time-sensitive.

Destination & transport

Level I or highest available trauma center, direct triage. Air medical if ground time exceeds the trauma system's threshold and helicopter ETA shortens definitive care arrival. Pre-arrival notification: trauma activation, "high-energy crush, suspected pelvis, open tibia/fibula with developing compartment syndrome, contralateral femur, age 34, vital signs trending."
