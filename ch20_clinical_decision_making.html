WHY CLINICAL DECISION MAKING MATTERS

The Paramedic's Core Skill

Protocols tell you what to do when a patient fits a textbook presentation. Clinical decision making tells you what to do when they don't. Most real patients are messier, vaguer, and more complex than any algorithm. Your brain is the most advanced diagnostic tool on the truck — this chapter teaches you how to use it.

🧠 ANALOGY — THE CHESS MASTER

A beginner chess player memorizes openings (protocols). A grandmaster understands why each move works — so when the opponent plays something unexpected, they don't freeze. A paramedic who only knows protocols will fail the moment a patient doesn't read the textbook. Clinical decision making is your grandmaster-level thinking.

Clinical Reasoning

The Process

The thought process used to evaluate the patient's problems. How you think through the situation from dispatch to hospital.

Clinical Decision Making

The Choice

The actual decision or choice made at the end of clinical reasoning. What treatment, transport destination, and priority you select.

Clinical Judgment

The Whole Picture

The combined process of reasoning + decision making. Your overall ability to assess, reason, decide, and act correctly.

💡 CLINICAL PEARL

The history gives you ~80% of the diagnosis. The physical exam adds ~19%. Diagnostic tests (12-lead, glucose, SpO₂) contribute only about 1%. Learn to listen — the patient is usually telling you what's wrong.

ASSESSMENT-BASED MANAGEMENT

What Is ABM?

Assessment-based management (ABM) means your treatment decisions are driven by what you find, not by what dispatch said or what the patient looks like. Every action should trace back to a specific finding in the history or physical exam.

🔦 ANALOGY — THE DETECTIVE

Sherlock Holmes doesn't decide the culprit before examining the crime scene. He gathers clues, forms a theory, and tests it. You are the detective. The patient is the crime scene. Gather evidence before you convict a diagnosis — and be willing to change your theory when new evidence appears.

Differential Diagnosis

The Short List

Using assessment findings + knowledge of pathophysiology, generate a list of potential causes for the patient's symptoms. Rank them by likelihood — but never forget the worst-case scenario even if it's less probable.

📋 EXAMPLE

Chest pain differential: ACS, pulmonary embolism, aortic dissection, tension pneumothorax, pericarditis, GERD. You can't rule out dissection in the field — so your treatment must not harm that possibility.

Pattern Recognition

The Template Match

Your brain matches the patient's presentation against stored patterns from training and experience. Classic STEMI pattern: crushing chest pain + diaphoresis + radiation to arm. When it matches — you move fast.

⚠️ PITFALL

New graduates have few stored patterns. Don't assume you've seen everything — every patient is a learning opportunity, not a confirmation of your assumptions.

The Action Plan Flow

📡 Dispatch Information
↓
Scene Size-Up + Initial Assessment
↓
History + Physical Examination
↓
Pattern Recognition → Differential Diagnosis
↓
Field Impression (Working Diagnosis)
↓
Action Plan (BLS/ALS Treatment + Transport Decision)
↓
Reassessment → Modify Plan as Needed

Two Approaches to the Patient

LIFE THREAT

Resuscitative Approach

Recognize and immediately intervene. You are treating before you have the full picture.

  • Cardiorespiratory arrest
  • Coma / altered LOC
  • Major trauma / hemorrhage
  • Respiratory distress/failure
  • Seizures
  • Sepsis / shock / hypotension
  • STEMI / unstable dysrhythmias
  • Stroke
📋 EXAMPLE

Unresponsive patient → you don't get a history before starting CPR. Treat the threat, gather data simultaneously.

STABLE

Contemplative Approach

History and physical exam are gathered before treatment begins. You have time to think.

This is where ABM really shines. You have space to build a complete picture, form a differential, and select targeted treatment rather than reflexive protocols.

📋 EXAMPLE

85-year-old with 3-day history of fatigue and mild dyspnea. No immediate threat — get the full story. What meds? What history? What changed 3 days ago?

THE CRITICAL THINKING PROCESS

Critical thinking is the ability to quickly focus your thinking to get the desired results — regardless of how messy, ambiguous, or stressful the situation is. It requires creative, reflective, and analytic thinking, plus the willingness to question your own assumptions.

🎯 ANALOGY — THE FIGHTER PILOT

Fighter pilots use the OODA Loop: Observe → Orient → Decide → Act — then loop back. You can't just observe once; conditions change, and the pilot who can complete the loop faster wins. Paramedic critical thinking is the same loop, applied to medicine at 70 mph.

1. Concept Formation
Building the Initial Impression
+

Definition: Gathering ALL elements needed to form a general impression of the patient. This begins at dispatch and continues through your initial assessment.

  • Scene size-up (mechanism, hazards, number of patients)
  • Chief complaint (in the patient's own words)
  • Patient history (OPQRST, SAMPLE)
  • Patient affect (anxious? calm? confused?)
  • Initial assessment (ABCs, LOC)
  • Physical examination findings
  • Diagnostic tests (12-lead, glucose, SpO₂, EtCO₂)
🎭 ANALOGY — THE PLAYWRIGHT

Before writing the story, a playwright gathers characters, setting, conflict, and tone. You are collecting the "ingredients" of the patient's story. Miss an ingredient, and the diagnosis — your story — will be incomplete or wrong.

📋 FIELD EXAMPLE

You arrive to find a 58M slumped in a recliner. Scene: pill bottles nearby, empty whiskey glass, wife crying. Chief complaint: "He won't wake up." Affect: unresponsive. You've already formed a concept — possible polypharmacy overdose vs. stroke vs. hypoglycemia. All three investigations will now be run simultaneously.

2. Data Interpretation
Making Sense of What You Found
+

Definition: Using your knowledge of anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology to interpret the data you gathered. This is where your classroom knowledge meets the real world. Quality of interpretation depends on knowledge depth + clinical experience.

🔬 ANALOGY — THE LAB TECHNICIAN

Raw data is meaningless without interpretation. A blood glucose of 38 is just a number until you know that normal is 70–110 mg/dL and that 38 causes altered mental status, diaphoresis, and seizures. Data interpretation is converting raw numbers into clinical meaning.

📋 FIELD EXAMPLE

SpO₂ 91%, respiratory rate 28, accessory muscle use, tripod position, history of COPD. Data interpretation: patient is in moderate-to-severe respiratory distress likely from COPD exacerbation with possible hypoxic drive risk if supplemental O₂ is titrated incorrectly. Your knowledge of COPD physiology changes your treatment plan.

💡 KEY INSIGHT

Knowing what's normal is the only way to recognize what's abnormal. Every lab value, vital sign, and assessment finding should be evaluated against a mental baseline of normal human physiology.

3. Application of Principles
Turning Knowledge Into Action
+

Definition: Based on your conceptual understanding and data interpretation, you apply the appropriate medical principles to treat the patient. This is where protocols, pharmacology, and pathophysiology all converge into a treatment plan.

🔧 ANALOGY — THE MASTER MECHANIC

A novice mechanic follows the repair manual step by step. A master mechanic understands why each step works — so when the car is a nonstandard model, they can still fix it. "Application of principles" means you understand the underlying mechanism, not just the recipe.

📋 FIELD EXAMPLE

Suspected hypoglycemia in a diabetic patient. Application: Dextrose 50% IV or glucagon IM — but which? Patient has an IV → D50. Patient refuses IV, is combative → glucagon IM. Principles applied: treatment is matched to the clinical situation, not blindly followed.

⚠️ PROTOCOL LIMITATION

Protocols work for classic presentations. The patient who doesn't fit the protocol isn't wrong — they need a paramedic who can think beyond the algorithm. Protocols are a floor, not a ceiling.

4. Evaluation
Is the Treatment Working?
+

Definition: Ongoing reassessment of the patient's response to treatment. Is the patient improving, staying the same, or deteriorating? Your plan must evolve with the patient's response.

🧪 ANALOGY — THE SCIENTIST

A scientist proposes a hypothesis, runs an experiment, and evaluates the results. If the results don't support the hypothesis, they revise the theory. Your treatment IS the experiment. If the patient doesn't improve as expected, your working diagnosis may be wrong — adjust.

📋 FIELD EXAMPLE

You treat wheezing patient with albuterol for suspected bronchospasm. After 2 treatments, SpO₂ drops further and breath sounds become equal bilaterally with decreased chest rise. Evaluation: this is not responding as bronchospasm should. Is this actually anaphylaxis? Pneumothorax? Revise the diagnosis and plan accordingly.

  • Reassess ABCs after every intervention
  • Repeat vitals at minimum every 5 min (critical) / 15 min (stable)
  • Note trends — a BP dropping from 110 to 95 to 80 is significant even if 80 is "not that bad"
  • Ask: "Is this patient better, same, or worse than when I arrived?"
5. Reflection on Action
Learning After the Call
+

Definition: After the call, reflecting on what you did — what worked, what didn't, and what you would do differently. This is how paramedics build experience and improve their pattern library over time.

📹 ANALOGY — THE GAME FILM

Elite athletes watch game film after every game — not to beat themselves up, but to find patterns, correct errors, and improve performance before the next competition. Your post-call reflection is your game film. The best paramedics are always students.

💡 METACOGNITION

Metacognition = thinking about your thinking. Evaluating your own decision-making process for cognitive errors is one of the highest-level paramedic skills. National EMS Standard: "Evaluate decision-making strategy for cognitive errors to enhance future critical-thinking skills."

📋 REFLECTION QUESTIONS

• Did I focus too quickly on one diagnosis and miss alternatives?
• Was I affected by the patient's appearance or demeanor?
• Did I use all available resources (partner, medical direction)?
• What would I do differently on the next similar call?

THE SIX Rs OF CRITICAL THINKING

The Six Rs are a tactical framework for applying critical thinking at the scene. Click each Rs card to expand the full explanation, clinical examples, and practice tips.

1
READ THE PATIENT
FIRST IMPRESSION · GLOBAL LOOK

Your first 30 seconds at the patient's side. Before you touch them, before you speak — what does the gestalt tell you? Sick or not sick?

What to assess: Appearance (work of breathing, skin color, position), LOC (AVPU), environment (clues in the room).

ANALOGY — THE POKER PLAYER

Great poker players read the table before they look at their cards. Read the patient before you open your drug bag. Your eyes and nose are data collection tools too.

EXAMPLE

You walk in: 70M leaning forward on a table, pursed-lip breathing, barrel chest, nicotine-stained fingers. Before he speaks, you've already read: COPD exacerbation. High probability.

2
READ THE SCENE
ENVIRONMENT · CONTEXT · SAFETY

The scene provides context that the patient can't always tell you. Environmental cues confirm or challenge your working hypothesis.

What to look for: Medication bottles (what conditions is this patient being treated for?), food/drink (alcohol? diabetic supplies?), hazards, mechanism of injury, position of patient when found.

ANALOGY — THE ARCHAEOLOGIST

The environment is the dig site. Every artifact (pill bottle, EpiPen, walker, O₂ concentrator) tells you a story about who this patient is and what may have gone wrong.

EXAMPLE

Altered patient with an insulin pen, candy wrappers, and a glucometer on the table next to them. Scene says: diabetic who may have over-treated a high blood sugar. Check glucose before doing anything else.

3
REACT
IMMEDIATE INTERVENTIONS · PRIORITIZE

Based on what you've read from the patient and scene, take appropriate immediate action. This is where resuscitative vs. contemplative approach splits.

Key principle: Treat life threats as you find them. Don't finish your head-to-toe before addressing an airway obstruction.

ANALOGY — THE FIREFIGHTER

Firefighters don't do a full building survey before putting out a visible flame. They suppress the immediate threat, then complete their assessment. React to what's in front of you — don't let thoroughness cause delay in an emergency.

EXAMPLE

Patient with severe respiratory distress and SpO₂ 82%. React immediately: high-flow O₂, positioning, consider CPAP — then complete your assessment. The SpO₂ tells you what to do before the history tells you why.

4
REEVALUATE
ONGOING REASSESSMENT · TRENDS

The patient is a dynamic system. A snapshot assessment is never enough. Constantly loop back: Is the patient improving? Trending worse? Has new information changed the working diagnosis?

Red flags: Vitals trending in the wrong direction, new symptoms appearing, interventions not working as expected.

ANALOGY — THE NAVIGATOR

Ships don't set a course and switch off the navigation system. They continuously plot position relative to destination and correct drift. Reevaluation is your continuous navigation — staying on course toward the correct diagnosis and treatment.

EXAMPLE

Patient treated for suspected hypoglycemia with D50. Two minutes later: glucose now 185, but still altered. Reevaluate — the hypoglycemia is corrected but the altered mental status persists. New differential: stroke? Postictal? Intoxication?

5
REVISE THE PLAN
ADAPT · MODIFY · ITERATE

When reevaluation shows the plan isn't working — or new data changes the picture — update the treatment plan. Commitment to a wrong diagnosis is more dangerous than changing your mind.

Signs you need to revise: Patient deteriorating despite treatment, new history contradicts working diagnosis, vitals not responding as expected.

ANALOGY — THE GPS REROUTE

When the road is blocked, GPS doesn't keep routing you down the same road. It calculates a new route. When your treatment plan is "blocked" by lack of response, reroute. Stubbornness is not a clinical virtue.

EXAMPLE

Working diagnosis: CHF exacerbation. Given nitroglycerin for preload reduction. BP drops to 68/40. Revise: this patient may have a right-sided MI or severe hypovolemia. Stop the NTG, reassess — maybe the real problem is not fluid overload.

6
REVIEW PERFORMANCE
DEBRIEF · METACOGNITION · GROW

After every call — especially the hard ones — review your performance. This is the fuel for professional growth. No paramedic reaches mastery without honest self-reflection and peer feedback.

Tools: Post-call debrief, QA/QI review, hospital outcome feedback, simulation review.

ANALOGY — THE SURGEON'S M&M

Surgery departments hold Morbidity & Mortality (M&M) conferences — not to blame, but to learn. Every adverse event is a curriculum. The best EMS providers hold their own internal M&M after every call: what did I do well? What would I change?

EXAMPLE

You treated a patient for suspected stroke only to find out at the hospital it was a subdural hematoma from a fall. Review: did I miss head trauma? Were there scene clues I overlooked? This review builds the pattern that catches the next one.

FACTORS, BIAS & COGNITIVE PITFALLS

Three categories of factors affect every assessment: Paramedic factors (your own knowledge, biases, and state of mind), Patient factors (cooperation, distracting injuries), and Environmental factors (noise, chaos, access). Understanding them helps you compensate.

PARAMEDIC FACTOR

Tunnel Vision

Focusing on one finding so intensely that you miss others. You see the leg fracture and miss the internal abdominal hemorrhage.

⚠️ REAL CONSEQUENCE

Patient with obvious ankle fracture after a fall → you splint and transport. But the fall was because of a syncopal episode from a serious cardiac dysrhythmia. Tunnel vision = missed diagnosis.

PARAMEDIC FACTOR

Labeling

Assuming a diagnosis based on appearance or previous encounters with this patient. "He's always drunk" → misses the subdural.

ANALOGY

Every time you call someone "the frequent flyer" instead of "the patient," you risk not seeing their acute emergency. Labels are the enemy of assessment.

PARAMEDIC FACTOR

Confirmation Bias

Seeking only data that confirms your initial impression. You've decided it's anxiety — so you stop looking for pulmonary embolism.

⚠️ ANTIDOTE

Always ask: "What's the worst thing this could be?" If you can't rule it out in the field, treat for it or get the patient to a facility that can.

PATIENT FACTOR

Distracting Injuries

A painful, obvious injury draws attention away from a more serious but less visible problem. Classic trauma: the screaming open femur fracture distracts from the quiet tension pneumothorax.

💡 RULE

In trauma, always complete your primary survey before getting drawn into secondary injuries. The screaming patient may not be the sickest patient.

ENVIRONMENTAL FACTOR

Scene Chaos & Noise

Crowded, loud, emotionally charged scenes degrade assessment quality. Family members shouting, bystanders interfering, and poor lighting all compromise your ability to gather accurate data.

📋 STRATEGY

Designate roles early. Limit voices at the patient's side. Consider moving the patient to a controlled environment when possible. You cannot think clearly in chaos without training and deliberate practice.

💡 CULTURAL CONSIDERATIONS

Cultural and religious backgrounds affect both the paramedic's decision-making AND the patient's cooperation. In some cultures, male providers may not perform physical assessments on female patients; family members may make treatment decisions. Work within these frameworks while identifying and managing life threats.

INTUITIVE vs. ANALYTIC THINKING

Paramedics and all healthcare providers make decisions using two distinct cognitive processes. Understanding both — and knowing when to use each — is a core professional skill.

FAST · AUTOMATIC · PATTERN-BASED
System 1 Thinking

"Intuitive" mode. Rapid, unconscious, based on pattern recognition and prior experience. The experienced medic who walks in and says "this patient is sick" before taking a single vital sign.

  • Instant recognition of classic presentations
  • Efficient under time pressure
  • Relies on experience — weak in novices
  • Prone to bias and error when patterns don't fit
  • Can miss atypical presentations
SLOW · DELIBERATE · ANALYTICAL
System 2 Thinking

"Analytic" mode. Slow, deliberate, logical. Step-by-step analysis. Used for complex, unfamiliar, or unclear presentations where System 1 may lead you astray.

  • Methodical differential diagnosis building
  • Better for atypical or ambiguous patients
  • Can override faulty intuitive conclusions
  • Requires time — difficult under extreme pressure
  • Requires broad knowledge base to be effective
🏎️ ANALOGY — F1 DRIVER vs. ROAD TEST STUDENT

An F1 driver's System 1 has been trained to react to a spin in milliseconds — their pattern recognition IS survival. A student driver uses System 2 for everything: check mirror, signal, check speed. Neither mode is wrong — the F1 driver still uses System 2 for race strategy. The danger is when an experienced provider's System 1 fires incorrectly on an atypical case, and they don't engage System 2 to check.

⚠️ THE EXPERT TRAP

As paramedics become more experienced, System 1 thinking dominates. This increases efficiency but creates a vulnerability: anchoring bias. The more experienced you are, the more important it is to deliberately trigger System 2 when a patient isn't responding to treatment as expected. Expertise is not infallibility.

When to Force System 2

  • When the patient isn't improving with expected treatment
  • When the presentation doesn't fit your initial impression
  • When new information contradicts your working diagnosis
  • When multiple providers disagree on the impression
  • When something "just doesn't feel right"

CREW RESOURCE MANAGEMENT

What Is CRM?

Crew Resource Management (CRM) is a structured approach to communication, teamwork, situational awareness, workload management, and decision making borrowed from aviation. Core philosophy: patient care responsibility belongs to everyone on the crew — not just the medic.

✈️ ANALOGY — THE COCKPIT

Aviation adopted CRM after finding that most crashes were caused by communication failures, not mechanical failures. The co-pilot who didn't speak up when the captain made a fatal error. EMS had the same problem — the EMT who said nothing while the medic made a wrong call. CRM fixes this by making silence unacceptable.

CRM Core Components

  • Clear leadership — one designated team leader per call
  • Role acknowledgment — each member knows their assignment
  • Situational awareness — everyone shares the mental model
  • Workload management — tasks distributed appropriately
  • Honest communication — the core of CRM
  • Closed-loop communication — confirm, verify, repeat back

Why CRM Fails

  • Authority gradient — junior staff won't challenge senior staff
  • Communication breakdown under stress
  • Assumed understanding — "I thought you were going to do that"
  • No pre-established role clarity
  • Ego overriding team input

THE PACE ESCALATION MODEL

PACE gives any team member a structured, safe way to challenge a decision that concerns them. It escalates progressively — use the minimum level needed.

LETTER ACTION WHEN TO USE EXAMPLE
P
 Probe Something feels off — you want to better understand the situation without challenging directly. "Hey, what's your thinking on not giving the nitroglycerin here?"
A
 Alert You have a specific concern. Communicate it clearly to the team leader. "I'm concerned — this patient has a right bundle branch block on the 12-lead. NTG may drop his pressure."
C
 Challenge The alert was dismissed. You believe the action is wrong and need to challenge it directly. "I need you to stop — I'm not comfortable giving this medication without checking with medical direction first."
E
 Emergency The plan will clearly cause harm. Intervene. This is used rarely but is always available. Physically preventing an unsafe medication from being administered, with simultaneous contact to medical direction.
💡 CRM IN PRACTICE

Good CRM doesn't mean challenging everything your partner does. It means building a relationship where safety concerns can always be voiced without fear. Pre-shift role discussions and mutual respect build the foundation for this.

THINKING UNDER PRESSURE

The fight-or-flight hormonal response (adrenaline, cortisol) has both positive and negative effects on cognitive performance. Understanding this helps you harness the positive and counter the negative.

POSITIVE EFFECTS

The Boost

  • Enhanced visual acuity
  • Improved auditory keenness
  • Better reflexes
  • Increased muscle strength
  • Heightened alertness

These are survival advantages in a physical threat — and they help when fast decisive action is needed.

NEGATIVE EFFECTS

The Cost

  • Reduced analytical thinking
  • Decreased concentration
  • Narrowed attentional focus
  • Impaired working memory
  • Reduced assessment thoroughness

High stress + high stakes = highest risk for cognitive error. Mental conditioning is the countermeasure.

🏋️ ANALOGY — MENTAL CONDITIONING

You train your body so it can perform under stress without thinking. Mental conditioning works the same way — you rehearse the decision-making process until the mental checklist runs automatically, even under extreme adrenaline load. Simulation training IS mental conditioning.

MENTAL CHECKLIST FOR PRESSURE SITUATIONS

1
STOP AND THINK
Force a pause. Even one second of deliberate pause can break the panic cycle and re-engage prefrontal cortex function. "Stop. What do I actually see?"
2
SCAN THE SITUATION
Get the full picture. Resist tunnel vision. Mentally categorize: threats, resources, patient status, crew status.
3
DECIDE AND ACT
Make a decision. A wrong action executed well is often better than no action at all. Commit to the plan with conviction.
4
MAINTAIN CLEAR CONTROL
Keep communication clear and direct. Calm voice tone matters — it calms both the patient and your team. Volume is not clarity.
5
CONTINUOUSLY REEVALUATE
No call is static. Keep the evaluation loop running. If the situation changes, your response must change with it.

PATIENT HANDOFF & REPORTING

Patient transitions of care are among the most common sources of medical errors in healthcare. A strong handoff protects the patient from information loss, ambiguity, and duplicated or missed treatment.

Effective Handoff Characteristics

  • Concise — less than 1 minute for stable patients
  • Jargon-minimal — clear language, not abbreviation soup
  • Consistent structure — same format every time
  • Pertinent positives AND negatives — what's there AND what's absent
  • Respectful tone — toward the receiving team

Begin With the End in Mind

Anticipate what the receiving team will want to know. What will the ED physician immediately ask? Frame your report around those anticipated questions. Don't just report what happened — tell the story that leads to the clinical decision the next provider needs to make.

ANALOGY — THE RELAY RACE

In a relay race, the baton handoff is the most critical moment. A dropped baton loses the race. A dropped clinical detail can cost a patient's life.

DISPATCH // 14:32 hrs
Unit 47 — respond to 814 Waterfall Dr for a SICK PERSON. Caller is the patient's adult son. States his 67-year-old father "seems confused and isn't acting right." Patient has a history of diabetes and high blood pressure. No other information at this time. Code 2 response.

🧠 CLINICAL DECISION MAKING ANALYSIS

What the scene tells you: The glucometer, OJ glass, and diabetic medication suggest active blood sugar management. Empty OJ may mean someone tried to treat a low already — or it may mean nothing. Diaphoresis + pallor + altered mental status in a known diabetic = hypoglycemia until proven otherwise.

Your working concept: Altered diabetic, likely hypoglycemia. But you're also noting unilateral lean + slurred speech → stroke is on the differential. You cannot confirm either diagnosis from the doorway.

⚠️ COGNITIVE TRAP TO AVOID

Don't commit to hypoglycemia just because you see a glucometer. Labeling this "just a sugar" before checking vitals + glucose + neuro exam could miss a concurrent ischemic stroke.

🧠 DATA INTERPRETATION — THE CRITICAL QUESTION

You now have a dilemma that separates the good paramedic from the great one. Glucose is 38 (severe hypoglycemia). Cincinnati Stroke Scale is positive. These findings can coexist — hypoglycemia CAUSES stroke-like symptoms. But acute ischemic stroke can ALSO present this way. Which came first?

💡 THE KEY PRINCIPLE

Treat the reversible cause first. Hypoglycemia can mimic stroke. Correct the glucose — if neurological deficits resolve, you've found your answer. If they persist after normoglycemia, you have a stroke until proven otherwise. This is Systems 2 thinking at its finest.

📋 CLINICAL DECISION

Establish IV access. Administer D50W 25g IVP. Reassess neurological status in 2 minutes. Meanwhile, record time of symptom onset from son (critical for stroke thrombolytics window). Notify receiving hospital of possible stroke alert — simultaneously treating the hypoglycemia.

⚡ CRITICAL DECISION POINT

Glucose is now normal (142). GCS improved slightly (12→13). But the right-sided drift and facial asymmetry PERSIST. The patient is more verbal but still confused. Blood pressure has risen to 168/98.

This is the moment for plan revision: The neurological deficits are not from hypoglycemia alone. The elevated BP in the context of persistent focal deficits and symptom onset within 4.5 hours = STROKE ALERT.

⚠️ DO NOT LOWER THE BP

In acute ischemic stroke, the hypertension is protective — it maintains perfusion pressure to the ischemic penumbra. Lowering BP prehospitally can worsen the infarct. This is an Application of Principles decision — knowing the pathophysiology changes your treatment.

SON'S HISTORY (obtained while treating):

  • Symptoms noted approximately 90 minutes ago
  • Patient took his usual metformin and metoprolol this AM
  • Patient skipped breakfast ("trying to cut carbs")
  • No recent illness, no fall, no head trauma
  • History: HTN × 12 years, T2DM × 8 years, hyperlipidemia
  • No previous strokes or TIAs
  • No blood thinners. No known allergies.
💡 CLINICAL PEARL — SYMPTOM ONSET TIME

In ischemic stroke, tPA (alteplase) is most effective within 3 hours of symptom onset and can be used up to 4.5 hours. At 90 minutes, this patient is in the optimal thrombolytic window — if a hemorrhagic stroke can be ruled out by CT. Time is brain.
